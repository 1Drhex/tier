Guest Question.

Evaluate shear design resistance, according to AISC 360-10, of connection illustrated in Figure E13.4.1 (all dimensions are in inches). The connection is slip-critical. Bolts have a \(^{3}/_{4}\) in. (19 mm) diameter, ASTM A490, preloaded. Holes have a 13/16 in. (20.6 mm) diameter. Each bolt has two friction surfaces. Elements to be connected and cover plates are made of ASTM A36 steel: \(F_{y}\)= 36 ksi (248 MPa), \(F_{u}\)= 58 ksi (400 MPa). Friction surfaces are class B: uncoated blast-cleaned steel surfaces.

Admin
Solution

Check of positioning of the holes
The distance between centres of standard holes shouldn’t be less than 2–2/3 times the nominal diameter:
d =3/4 in.=0.75 in (19.1 mm)
min {2;2.7}/d =2/d =2/0.75 = 2.67 > 2−2/3=2.667 OK
Minimum edge distance from centre of standard hole to every edge of connected cart (see Table 13.7a) for 3/4
bolt: 1 in. (25.4 mm)
min (1.4;1.4)=1.4 in. >1 in. OK

Minimum bolt pretension (from Table 13.9a):

For class B faying surfaces assume μ = 0.5 for computing bolt design slip resistance (Eq. (13.39)) and consider: two slip planes per bolt, one filler and no tension action applied to the connection.
\(R_{n}=\mu D_{u}h_{f}T_{b}n_{s}k_{sc}\)=0.5×1.13×1×35×2×1= 39.55 kips (176 kN)
Compute design slip resistance of the connection:
LRFD (ϕ= 1.00):
\(4\cdot \phi R_{n}\)=4×1.00 × 39.55 = 158.2 kps (703.7 kN)
ASD (Ω = 1.50):
\(4.R_{n}\)/1.50 = 4 × 39.55/1.50 = 105.5 kips (469.3 kN)
Check of design shear resistance
Nominal shear strength of a bolt in bearing-type connection (A490 bolts when threads are excluded from shear planes, see Table 13.11):
\(F_{nv}\)= 84 ksi (579 MPa)
Nominal unthreaded bolt area: \(A_{b}=0.442 in.^{2} (285 mm^{2})\)

LRFD approach:
Design shear strength of a bolt (two shear planes):
\(\phi R_{n}=\phi F_{nv}A_{b}\)=0.75 × 84 × (2 × 0.442) = 55.7 kips (247.8 kN)
Design bearing strength at the bolt hole (deformation at service loads is a design consideration):
edge row bolt:
\(\phi .1.2l_{c}tF_{u}\)=0.75 × 1.2 ×[1.4−0.5× (13/16)] ×0.7 × 58 = 36.31 kips (161.5 kN)
\(\phi.2.4dtF_{u}\)=0.75 × 2.4 × (3/4) ×0.7 × 58 = 54.81 kips (243.8 kN)
\(\phi R_{n}=min \left \{ \phi.1.2l_{c}tF_{u};\phi .2 4dtF_{u} \right \}\)=min{36.31;54.81} = 36.31 kips (161.5 kN)
internal row bolt:
\(\phi .1.2l_{c}tF_{u}\)=0.75 × 1.2 ×[2.0- (13/16)] ×0.7 × 58 = 43.39 kips (161.5 kN)
\(\phi.2.4dtF_{u}\)=0.75 × 2.4 × (3/4) ×0.7 × 58 = 54.81 kips (243.8 kN)
\(\phi R_{n}=min \left \{ \phi.1.2l_{c}tF_{u};\phi .2 4dtF_{u} \right \}\)=min{43.39;54.81} = 43.39 kips (193 kN)
Design strength for an edge bolt (minimum between shear and bearing strength):
\(\phi R_{n}\) =min {55.7;43.39} = 43.39 kips (193 kN)
Design strength for an internal bolt (minimum between shear and bearing strength):
\(\phi R_{n}\) =min {55.7;36.31}= 36.31 kips (161.5 kN)
Design strength of the connection:
36.31 × 2 + 43.39 × 2 = 159.4 kips (709 kN)
ASD approach:
Allowable shear strength of a bolt (two shear planes):
\(R_{n}/\Omega= F_{nv}A_{b}/\Omega\)=84 × (2×0.442)/2.00 = 37.12 kips (165.2 kN)
Design bearing strength at the bolt hole (deformation at service loads is not a design consideration):
edge row bolt:
\(1.2l_{c}tF_{u}/\Omega\)=1.2× [1.4−0.5× (13/16)] ×0.7×58/2.00 = 24.21 kips (48.4 kN)
\(2.4dtF_{u}/\Omega\)=2.4× (3/4) ×0.7×58/2.00 = 36.54 kips (162.5 kN)
\(R_{n}/\Omega =min\left \{ 1.2l_{c}tF_{u}/\Omega ;2.4dtF_{u}/\Omega \right \}\)=min {24.21;36.54} = 24.21 kips (107.7 kN)
internal row bolt:
\(1.2l_{c}tF_{u}/\Omega\)=1.2× [2.0-(13/16)] ×0.7×58/2.00 = 28.93 kips (128.7 kN)
\(2.4dtF_{u}/\Omega\)=2.4× (3/4) ×0.7×58/2.00 = 36.54 kips (162.5 kN)
\(R_{n}/\Omega =min\left \{ 1.2l_{c}tF_{u}/\Omega ;2.4dtF_{u}/\Omega \right \}\)=min {28.93;36.54} = 28.93 kips (128.7 kN)
Design strength for an edge bolt (minimum between shear and bearing strength):
\(R_{n}/\Omega \)=min {37.12;24.21} = 24.21 kips (107.7 kN)
Design strength for an internal bolt (minimum between shear and bearing strength):
\(R_{n}/\Omega \)=min {37.12;28.93} = 28.93 kips (128.7 kN)
Design strength of the connection:
24.21 × 2 + 28.93 × 2 = 106.3 kips (472.8 kN)
Resistance of the connection.
LRFD approach:
Design slip resistance: 158.2 kps (703.7 kN)
Design shear and bearing resistance : 159.4 kips (709 kN)
ASD approach:
Design slip resistance: 105.5 kps (469.3 kN)
Design shear and bearing resistance: 106.3 kips (472.8 kN)
The design resistance of the connection is the minimum resistance between slip resistance and shear and bearing resistance. So in this case we have:
LRFD approach:
Design connection resistance : 158.2 kps (703.7 kN)
ASD approach:
Design connection resistance 105.5 kips (469,3 kN)
Design connection resistance means that the connection does not slip at service loads and it does not fail for bearing or bolt shear at ultimate loads. But the maximum tensile force that can be sustained by the connected elements depends on their tensile resistance and on cover plates’ tensile resistance.
Connected elements or cover plates tensile strength
Connected elements area is equal to cover plate area, so verify just connected elements.
Tensile yielding in gross section:
\(A_{g}\)=5.5×0.7=3.85 in\(.^{2}\) (24.8 \(cm^{2}\))
LFRD: \(\phi A_{g}F_{y}\)=0.90 × 3.85 × 36 = 124.7 kips (555 kN)
ASD : \(A_{g}F_{y}/\Omega \)=3.85 × 36/1.67 = 83 kips (369 kN)
Tensile rupture in net section:
\(A_{e}\)=[5.5 –2× (13/16 + 1/16)] ×0.7=2.63 in\(.^{2}\) (17 \(cm^{2}\))
LFRD: \(\phi A_{e}F_{u}\)=0.75 × 2.63 × 58 = 113.4 kips (509 kN)
ASD: \(A_{e}F_{u}/\Omega \)=2.63 × 58/2.00 = 76.3 kips (339 kN)
Tensile strength:
LFRD: min {124.7;113.4} = 113.4 kips (509 kN)
ASD : min {83;76.3}=76.3 kips (339 kN)
So the connection is stronger than the connected plates.
