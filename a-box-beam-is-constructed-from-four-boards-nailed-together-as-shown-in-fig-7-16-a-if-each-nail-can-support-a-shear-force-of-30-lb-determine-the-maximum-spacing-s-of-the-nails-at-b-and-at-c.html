Guest Question.

A box beam is constructed from four boards nailed together as shown in Fig. 7–16 a . If each nail can support a shear force of 30 lb, determine the maximum spacing s of the nails at B and at C so that the beam will support the force of 80 lb.

Admin
Solution

Internal Shear. If the beam is sectioned at an arbitrary point along its length, the internal shear required for equilibrium is always \(V = 80 lb\), and so the shear diagram is shown in Fig. 7–16 b .

Section Properties. The moment of inertia of the cross-sectional area about the neutral axis can be determined by considering a \(7.5-in. × 7.5-in\) square minus a \(4.5-in. × 4.5-in\). square.

\(I=\frac{1}{12}(7.5in.)(7.5in.)^3-\frac{1}{12}(4.5in.)(4.5in.)^3=229.5in^4 \)

The shear flow at B is determined using \(Q_B\) found from the darker shaded area shown in Fig. 7–16 c . It is this “symmetric” portion of the beam that is to be “held” onto the rest of the beam by nails on the left side and by the fibers of the board on the right side. Thus,

\(Q_B=\bar{y}^\prime A^\prime =[3in.](7.5in.)(1.5in.)=33.75in^3\)

Likewise, the shear flow at C can be determined using the “symmetric” shaded area shown in Fig. 7–16 d . We have

\(Q_C=\bar{y}^\prime A^\prime =[3in.](4.5in.)(1.5in.)=20.25in^3\)

Shear Flow.

\(q_B=\frac{VQ_B}{I}=\frac{80lb(33.75in^3)}{229.5in^4}=11.76lb/in. \)

 

\(q_C=\frac{VQ_C}{I}=\frac{80lb(20.25in^3)}{229.5in^4}=7.059lb/in. \)

These values represent the shear force per unit length of the beam that must be resisted by the nails at B and the fibers at B′, Fig. 7–16 c , and the nails at C and the fibers at C′, Fig. 7–16 d , respectively. Since in each case the shear flow is resisted at two surfaces and each nail can resist 30 lb, for B the spacing is

\(s_B=\frac{30lb}{(11.76/2)lb/in.}=5.10in. \quad \quad Use \quad s_B=5in. \)

And for C ,

\(s_C=\frac{30lb}{(7.059/2)lb/in.}=8.50in. \quad \quad Use \quad s_C=8.5in. \)
