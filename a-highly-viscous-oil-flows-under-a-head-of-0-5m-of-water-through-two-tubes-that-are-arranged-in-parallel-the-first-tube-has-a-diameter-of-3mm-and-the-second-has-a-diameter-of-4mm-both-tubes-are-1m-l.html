Guest Question.

A highly viscous oil flows under a head of 0.5m of water through two tubes that are arranged in parallel. The first tube has a diameter of 3mm and the second has a diameter of 4mm. Both tubes are 1m long. Determine the volume flow rates in the two tubes. The viscosity of oil may be taken as 3 times the viscosity of water and the relative density of oil is 0.8. Take water properties at 30 ◦C.

Admin
Solution

Step 1 Water properties at 30 ◦C are taken from table of properties of water. They are

\(\rho_{w}=995.7 kg / m ^{3}, \mu_{w}=7.97 \times 10^{-4} kg / m s\)

The flowing fluid is oil with the following properties:

Viscosity: \(\mu_{ oil }=3 \times \mu_{w}=3 \times 7.97 \times 10^{-4}=2.39 \times 10^{-3} kg / m s\)

Density: \(\rho_{\text {oil }}=0.8 \times \rho_{w}=0.8 \times 995.7=797 kg / m ^{3}\)

Step 2 The given data is written down as below

Tube 1: Diameter: \(D_{1}=0.003 m \text { Length: } L_{1}=1 m\)

Tube 2: Diameter: \(D_{2}=0.004 m \text { Length: } L_{2}=1 \)

Step 3 Available pressure drop is given to be equal to a head ofwater of h = 0.5m.
The corresponding pressure drop is given by

\(\Delta p=\rho_{w} g h=995.7 \times 9.81 \times 0.5=4884 Pa\)

where we have used the standard value for the acceleration due to gravity of \(g = 9.81 m/s^2\) We shall assume that the flow through both tubes is laminar. Of course we shall verify it later on.

Step 4 The flow resistance of the tubes may be obtained using Eq. 12.64.

\(R_{f}=\frac{128 \mu L}{\pi \rho D^{4}}\) (12.64)

Tube 1: \(R_{f 1}=\frac{128 \times 2.39 \times 10^{-3} \times 1}{\pi \times 797 \times 0.003^{4}}=1.510 \times 10^{6} Pa s / kg\)

Tube 2: \(R_{f 2}=\frac{128 \times 2.39 \times 10^{-3} \times 1}{\pi \times 797 \times 0.004^{4}}=4.777 \times 10^{5} Pa s / kg\)

Step 5 Using the definition of flow resistance, themass flow rates in the two tubes may be calculated now.

Tube 1: \(\dot{m}_{1}=\frac{\Delta p}{R_{f 1}}=\frac{4884}{1.510 \times 10^{6}}=3.235 \times 10^{-3} kg / s\)

Tube 2: \(\dot{m}_{2}=\frac{\Delta p}{R_{f 2}}=\frac{4884}{4.777 \times 10^{5}}=1.022 \times 10^{-2} kg / s\)

The corresponding oil velocities in the two cases are given by

Tube 1: \(U_{1}=\frac{\dot{m_{1}}}{\rho_{\text {oil }} A_{1}}=\frac{4 m_{1}}{\rho_{\text {oil }} \pi D_{1}^{2}}=\frac{4 \times 3.235 \times 10^{-3}}{797 \times \pi \times 0.003^{2}}=0.575 m / s\)

Tube 2: \(U_{2}=\frac{\dot{m_{2}}}{\rho_{\text {oil }} A_{2}}=\frac{4 m_{2}}{\rho_{\text {oil }} \pi D_{2}^{2}}=\frac{4 \times 1.022 \times 10^{-2}}{797 \times \pi \times 0.004^{2}}=1.021 m / s\)

Step 6 We now verify whether the flow is laminar in the two cases. This is done by making sure that the larger of the two Reynolds numbers is less than 2300. The Reynolds number in the case of 4mm tube is the larger of the two and is

\(R e_{D_{2}}=\frac{\rho_{ oil } U_{2} D_{2}}{\mu_{ oil }}=\frac{797 \times 1.021 \times 0.004}{2.39 \times 10^{-3}}=1361\)

The flow is indeed laminar and the use of laminar flow resistance formula is justified.

Step 7 The volume flow rates are obtained now.

Tube 1: \(v_{1} = \frac{m_{1} }{p_{oil} }=\frac{3.235\times 10^{-3} }{797} = 4.06\times 10^{-6} m^3/ s\)

Tube 2: \(v_{2} = \frac{m_{1} }{p_{oil} }=\frac{1.022\times 10^{-2} }{797} = 12.83\times 10^{-6} m^3/ s\)
