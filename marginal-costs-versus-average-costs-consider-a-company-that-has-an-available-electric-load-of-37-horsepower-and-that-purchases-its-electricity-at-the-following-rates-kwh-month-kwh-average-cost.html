Guest Question.

Marginal Costs versus Average Costs

Consider a company that has an available electric load of \(37\) horsepower and that purchases its electricity at the following rates:

kWh/Month @$/kWh Average Cost ($/kWh)
First 1,500 $0.050 $0.050
Next 1,250 0.035 \(\frac{\$75 + 0.0350(X - 1,500)}{X}\)
Next 3,000 0.020 \(\frac{\$118.75 + 0.020(X - 2,750)}{X}\)
All over 5,750 0.010 \(\frac{\$178.25 + 0.010(X - 5,750)}{X}\)
Admin
Solution

Given: Marginal cost schedule for electricity; operating volume \(= 3,200 kWh\).

Find: Marginal and average cost per \(kWh\).

The marginal cost of adding one more \(kWh\) is \(\$0.020\). The average variable cost per \(kWh\) is calculated as follows:

kWh Rate ($/kWh) Cost
First 1,500 0.050 $75.00
Next 1,250 0.035 43.75
Remaining 450 0.020 9.00
Total $127.75

The average variable cost per \(kWh\) is \(\$127.75/3,200 kWh = \$0.0399 kWh\). Or we can find the value by using the formulas in the third column of the rate schedule:

\(\frac{\$118.75 + 0.020(3,200 – 2,750)}{3,200}=\frac{\$127.75}{3,200 kWh}=\$0.0399 kWh\).

Changes in the average variable cost per unit are the result of changes in the marginal cost. As shown in \(Figure 8.7\), the average variable cost continues to fall because the marginal cost is lower than the average variable cost over the entire volume.
