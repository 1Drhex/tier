Guest Question.

In Figure, an electric heater is installed in surface 1 such that a constant heat flux of \(100 kW/m^{2}\) is generated at the surface. The four surrounding surfaces are in radiant balance with surface 1 and a large surrounding room at \(20^{\circ }C\). The surface properties are \( \epsilon _{1}=0.8 \) and \( \epsilon _{2} = \epsilon _{3} = \epsilon _{4} = \epsilon _{5} = 0.4 \). Determine the temperatures of all surfaces. The back side of surface 1 is insulated. Repeat the calculation assuming surfaces 2, 3, 4, and 5 are just one surface uniform in temperature.°

Admin
Solution

In reality, surfaces 2, 3, 4, and 5 have two surfaces each; an inside and an outside surface. We thus have nine surfaces plus the room, so a 10-body problem is involved. Of course, from symmetry we can see that \(T_{2} = T_{4}\) and \(T_{3} = T_{5}\), but we set up the problem in the general numerical formulation. We designate the large room as surface 6 and it behaves as if \(\epsilon_{6} = 1.0\). So, it is as if the opening were covered with a black surface at \(20^{\circ}C\). The shape factors of the inside surfaces are obtained from Figures:

\(F_{16} = F_{61} = 0.285 F_{13} = F_{15} = 0.24 = F_{31} = F_{51}\)
\(F_{12} = F_{14} = 0.115 F_{24} = F_{42} = 0.068\)
\(F_{35} = F_{53} = 0.285 F_{32} = F_{52} = F34 = 0.115\)
\(F_{25} = F_{23} = F_{45} = F_{43} = F_{21} = F_{41} = F_{26} = F_{46} = 0.23\)
\(F_{11} = F_{22} = F_{33} = F_{44} = F_{55} = 0\)

For the outside surfaces,

\(F _{26}^{\prime }= F_{36}^{\prime } = F_{46}^{\prime } = F_{56}^{\prime } = 1.0\)

where the primes indicate the outside surfaces. We shall also use primes to designate the radiosities of the outside surfaces. For the room,\( J_{6} = E_{b_{6}} = (5.669 × 10^{−8})(293)^{4 }= 417.8 W/m^{2}\).
For surface 1 with constant heat flux, we use Equation \(J_{i}(1-F_{ii})-\sum\limits_{j\neq i}{F_{ij}J_{j}} \frac{q_{i}}{A_{i}}\) and write
\(J_{1 }− (F_{12}J_{2} + F_{13}J_{3} + F_{14}J_{4} + F_{15}J_{5} + F_{16}J_{6}) = 1.0 × 10^{5} \) [a]
Because of the radiant balance condition we have

\((J_{2}-E_{b_{2}})\frac{\epsilon _{2}A_{2}}{1-\epsilon _{2}} =(E_{b_{2}}-J^{\prime }_{2})\frac{\epsilon _{2}A_{2}}{1-\epsilon _{2}}\)

and

\( E_{b_{2}}=\frac{J_{2}+J_{2}^{\prime }}{2} \) [b]

where the prime designates the outside radiosity. A similar relation applies for surfaces 3, 4, and 5. Thus, we can use Equation \(J_{i}[1-F_{ii}(1-\epsilon _{i})]-(1-\epsilon _{i})\sum\limits_{j\neq i}{F_{ij}J_{j}} =\epsilon _{i}E_{b_{i}}\) for inside surface 2

\(J_{2} − (1 − \epsilon_{2})(F_{21}J_{1} + F_{23}J_{3} + F_{24}J_{4} + F_{25}J_{5} + F_{26}J_{6}) =\frac{\epsilon _{2}}{2} (J_{2}+J_{2}^{\prime })\) [c]

and for the outside surface 2

\(J_{2}^{\prime} − (1 −\epsilon_{ 2})(F_{ 26}J_{6}) =\frac{\epsilon _{2}}{2} (J_{2}+J_{2}^{\prime })\) [d]

Equations like (c) and (d) are written for surfaces 3, 4, and 5 also, and with the shape factors and emissivities inserted the following set of equations is obtained:

\( J_{1} − 0.115J_{2} − 0.24J_{3} − 0.115J_{4} − 0.24J_{5} = 1.0012 × 10^{5}\)
\( −0.138J_{1} + 0.8J_{2} − 0.2J_{2}^{\prime} − 0.138J_{3} − 0.0408J_{4} − 0.138J_{5} = 57.66\)
\( 0.2J_{2} − 0.8J_{2} ^{\prime}= −250.68\)
\( −0.144J_{1} − 0.069J_{2} + 0.8J_{3} − 0.2J_{3}^{\prime} − 0.069J_{4} − 0.05J_{5} = 60.16\)
\(0.2J_{3} − 0.8J_{3}^{\prime} = −250.68\)

 

\(−0.138J_{1} − 0.0408J_{2} − 0.138J_{3} + 0.8J_{4} − 0.2J_{4}^{\prime} − 0.138J_{5} = 57.66\)
\(0.2J_{4} − 0.8J_{4}^{\prime} = −250.68\)
\(−0.144J_{1} − 0.069J_{2} − 0.057J_{3} − 0.069J_{4} + 0.8J_{5} − 0.2J_{5}^{\prime} = 60.16\)
\(0.2J_{5} − 0.8J_{5} ^{\prime}= −250.68\)

We thus have nine equations and nine unknowns, which may be solved to give
\(J_{1} = 1.24887 × 10^{5} W/m^{2}\)
\(J_{2} = J_{4} = 37,549\)
\(J _{2}^{\prime} = J_{4}^{\prime} = 9701\)
\(J_{3} = J_{5} = 33,605\)
\(J_{3} ^{\prime}= J_{5}^{\prime} = 8714\)

The temperatures are thus computed from Equation (b):

\(E_{b_{2}}=\frac{37,549 + 9701}{2} = 23,625 T_{2} = T_{4} = 803.5 K\)

 

\( E_{b_{3}}=\frac{33,605 + 8714}{2} = 21,160 T_{3} = T_{5} = 781.6 K \)

For surface 1 we observed that

\( \frac{q}{A} =\frac{\epsilon }{1-\epsilon } (E_{b_{1}}-J_{1})\)

so that

\( E_{b_{1}}=\frac{(1.0 × 10^{5})(1 − 0.8)}{0.8} + 1.24887 × 10^{5} = 1.49887 × 10^{5} \)

and

\( T_{1} = 1275 K \)

We note again that we could have observed the symmetry of the problem and set \(J_{2} = J_{4}, J_{ 2}^{\prime} = J_{4}^{\prime}\),and so on. By so doing, we could have had only five equations with five unknowns.

Surfaces 2, 3, 4, and 5 as one surface

We now go back and take surfaces 2, 3, 4, and 5 as one surface, which we choose to call surface 7. The shape factors are then

\( F_{16} = F_{61} = 0.285 F_{17} = 1 − 0.285 = 0.715\)
\(A_{1} = 2.0 A_{7} = 6.0 \)

Thus

\(F_{71} = (0.715)\left(\frac{2}{6} \right) = 0.2383 = F_{76}\)

 

\( F_{77} = 1 − (2)(0.2383) = 0.5233 F_{76}^{\prime} = 1.0 \)

Then for surface 1 we use Equation \(J_{i}(1-F_{ii})-\sum\limits_{j\neq i}{F_{ij}J_{j}} =\frac{q_{i}}{A_{i}} \) to obtain

\(J_{1} − (F_{17}J_{7} + F_{16}J_{6}) = 1.0 × 10^{5}\)

Using \(E_{b_{7}} = (J_{7} + J_{7}^{\prime})/2\), we have for the inside of surface 7

\( J_{7}[1 − F_{77}(1 −\epsilon _{7})] − (1 − \epsilon _{7})(F_{71}J_{1} + F_{76}J_{6}) = \frac{\epsilon _{7}}{2} (J_{7}+J_{7}^{\prime }) \)

while for the outside we have

\( J_{7}^{\prime } − (1 −\epsilon _{7})F_{76}^{\prime }J_{6} = \frac{\epsilon _{7}}{2} (J_{7}+J_{7}^{\prime }) \)

When the numerical values are inserted, we obtain the set of three equations:

\(J_{1} − 0.715J_{7} = 1.0012 × 10^{5}\)
\(−0.143J_{1} + 0.486J_{7} − 0.2J_{7}^{\prime } = 59.74\)
\(0.2J_{7} − 0.8J_{7}^{\prime } = −250.68\)

which has the solution

\(J_{1} = 1.31054 × 10^{5} W/m^{2}\)
\(J_{7} = 43,264\)
\(J_{7}^{\prime } = 11,129\)

The temperatures are then calculated as before:

\(E_{b_{7}}=\frac{43,264 + 11,129}{2}=27,197 T_{7} = 832.2 K\)

 

\( E_{b_{1}}=\frac{(1.0 × 10^{5})(1 − 0.8)}{0.8}+ 1.31054 × 10^{5} = 1.65054 × 10^{5} \)

 

\( T_{1} = 1306 K \)

So, there is about a 30 K temperature difference between the two methods
