Guest Question.

Formulate the problem of using Lagrange’s equation and derive the equation of motion (a) ignoring friction forces and (b) including friction forces. See Figure 5.11.

Admin
Solution

Solution: Figure 5.11 depicts the system along with the relevant coordinates. Since this is a one degreeof- freedom system, only one generalized coordinate is needed. However, we will select two coordinates, \(r_{1}\) and \(r_{2}\), and show the link between these coordinates and the string that constrains them. (Other coordinates are possible.) Since there is a constraint condition, \(r_{1}=l-r_{2}\),

we will end up with one equation of motion governing one coordinate, which in this case we selected to be \(r_{2}\). (a) The kinetic energy is given by

 

\(T=\frac{1}{2} m_{1} \dot{r}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m_{2} \dot{r}_{2}^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \dot{r}_{2}^{2}\)

The potential energy is given by

\(\begin{aligned}V &=-m_{1} g h_{1}-m_{2} g h_{2} \\&=-m_{1} g\left(l-r_{2}\right) \sin \theta_{1}-m_{2} g r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} .\end{aligned}\)

 

The Lagrangian is the difference between the kinetic and potential energies,

 

\(\begin{aligned}L &=T-V \\&=\frac{1}{2}\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \dot{r}_{2}^{2}+m_{1} g\left(l-r_{2}\right) \sin \theta_{1}+m_{2} g r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} .\end{aligned}\)

 

Now we can determine the terms in the Lagrangian as necessary to derive the equation of motion,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{r}_{2}}\right) &=\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{r}_{2} \\\frac{\partial L}{\partial r_{2}} &=-m_{1} g \sin \theta_{1}+m_{2} g \sin \theta_{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

Substituting these into Equation 5.27, we find the equation of motion for this single degree-of-freedom system to be

 

\(\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_{k}}\right)-\frac{\partial L}{\partial q_{k}}=Q_{k}, \quad k=1,2, \ldots, n\) (5.27)

 

\(\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{r}_{2}=-m_{1} g \sin \theta_{1}+m_{2} g \sin \theta_{2} .\)

 

This is a nonlinear equation for \(r_{2}\) that is valid for large \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{2}\). Note that the same equation will be obtained if Lagrange’s equation is applied to \(r_{1}\).

(b) Here friction is included, and therefore the nonconservative
forces \(Q_{k}\) from Lagrange’s equation are needed. Since this is a two particle one degree-of-freedom system, there is only one dissipative force,

 

\(Q=-\mu\left(m_{1} g \cos \theta_{1}+m_{2} g \cos \theta_{2}\right)\)

 

resulting in the corresponding governing equation

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{r}_{2}=&-m_{1} g\left(\sin \theta_{1}+\mu \cos \theta_{1}\right) \\&+m_{2} g\left(\sin \theta_{2}-\mu \cos \theta_{2}\right),\end{aligned}\)

 

which is the same as Equation 5.14 derived using d’Alembert’s approach.
