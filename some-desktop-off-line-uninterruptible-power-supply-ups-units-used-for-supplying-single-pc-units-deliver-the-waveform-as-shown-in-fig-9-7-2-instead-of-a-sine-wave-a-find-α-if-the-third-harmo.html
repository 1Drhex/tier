Guest Question.

Some desktop off-line uninterruptible power supply (UPS) units used for supplying single \(PC\) units deliver the waveform as shown in Fig. 9.7-2 instead of a sine wave. (a) Find \(α\) if the third harmonic content is to become zero. (b) With this value of \(α\), find \(V\) such that the \(rms\) voltage is \(220 V\). (c) Plot the magnitude and phase spectra with this value of \(α\) and \(V\). (d) The purity of a sine wave is measured in terms of a quantity called ‘Total Harmonic Distortion (THD)’. It is usually quoted in percentage and is defined as follows:

\(\mathrm{THD}=\frac{\sqrt{\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\left|\tilde{v}_{n}\right|^{2}}}{\left|\tilde{v}_{1}\right|} \times 100 \% \)

where \(\tilde{v}_{n}\) is the exponential Fourier series coefficient. The \(rms\) value of all harmonic components together is expressed as a percentage of \(rms\) value of fundamental component in the \(THD \)measure. Amplitudes may be used instead of \(rms\) values since it is a ratio. Calculate the \(THD\) of the waveform in this example.

Admin
Solution

The trigonometric Fourier series of the waveform with \(V = 1\) and \(T = 1\) is determined first. It is an odd half-wave symmetric waveform. Its trigonometric Fourier series will contain only odd sine harmonics. With \(V = 1\) and \(T = 1\),

\(\begin{aligned} \therefore v(t) &=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_{n} \sin n \omega_{o} t \text { and } b_{n}=\frac{4}{T} \int_{0}^{T / 2} v(t) \sin n \omega_{o} t d t \\ b_{n} &=4 \int_{\alpha / 2}^{(1-\alpha) / 2} \sin 2 \pi n t d t \\ &=\frac{4}{2 \pi n}[\cos n \pi \alpha-\cos n \pi(1-\alpha)] \\ &=\frac{4}{\pi n} \sin \frac{n \pi}{2} \sin \frac{n \pi(1-2 \alpha)}{2} \end{aligned}\)

 

The trigonometric Fourier series coefficients tends to zero for even \(n\) as expected. Further, the Fourier series of this waveform approaches that of a unit amplitude square wave as \(α→ 0\) as expected.

(a) If the third harmonic content is to be zero, then \(\sin \frac{3 \pi(1-2 \alpha)}{2}\) must become zero. Therefore, \(\alpha=\frac{1}{6}\) . With this value of a, the waveform will be zero for one-third of a half-cycle.

(b) Therefore \(rms\) value \(=\sqrt{\frac{2}{3} V^{2}}=0.8165 \mathrm{~V} \text { . } \) If this is to be \(220 V\), \(V\) must be \(≈270 V\).

(c) The exponential Fourier series of \(v(t)\) can be constructed from trigonometric Fourier series by noting that coefficients of sine terms will be twice the negative of imaginary part of exponential Fourier series coefficients. Therefore, with \(V = 1\), \(T = 1\) and \(α = 1/6\),

\(\therefore v(t)=\sum_{n=-\infty \atop \text { odd } n}^{\infty}-\frac{j 2}{\pi n} \sin \frac{n \pi}{2} \sin \frac{n \pi}{3} e^{j 2 \pi n t} \quad\quad(9-7-1)\)

 

The two-sided spectrum is plotted in Fig. 9.7-3 with the scaling factor of \(270 V\) incorporated.

(d) Refer to Eqn. 9.7-1. \(\sin \frac{n \pi}{2}\) has a magnitude of \(1\) for all odd \(n\). \(\sin \frac{n \pi}{3}\) has a magnitude of \(0\) for all odd multiples of \(3\) and \(0.866\) for all other odd \(n\) including \(n =1 \cdot \frac{8}{\pi}\) is a common factor.

Therefore, \(\mathrm{THD}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{5^{2}}+\frac{1}{7^{2}}+\frac{1}{11^{2}}+\frac{1}{13^{2}}+\frac{1}{17^{2}}+\frac{1}{19^{2}}+\cdots \times 100} \approx 28.5 \%\)
