Guest Question.

Find \(U, C_{V} , F, p, S, H,\) and G for an ultrarelativistic gas of indistin-guishable particles.

Admin
Solution

The N-particle partition function \(Z_{N}\) is given by\(^{3}\)

\( Z_{N}=\frac{Z^{N}_{1}}{N!},\) (25.10)

and hence

\( \ln Z_{N}=N\ln V+3N\ln T+constants.\) (25.11)

\(^{3}\)This is assuming the density is not so high that this approximation breaks down.

The internal energy U is given by

\( U=-\frac{d\ln Z_{N}}{d\beta }=3Nk_{B}T,\) (25.12)

which is different from the non-relativistic case (which gave \(U=\frac{3}{2}Nk_{B}T).\)The heat capacity \(C_{V}\) is

\( C_{V}=\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_{V},\) (25.13)

and hence is given by\(^{4} C_{V} = 3Nk_{B}.\) The Helmholtz function is

\(F=-k_{B}T\ln Z_{N}=-k_{B}TN\ln V-3Nk_{B}T\ln T-k_{B}T\times constants,\) (25.14)

so that

\( p=-\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V} \right)_{T}=\frac{Nk_{B}T}{V}=n k_{B}T,\) (25.15)

\(^{4}\)Notice that this does not agree with the equipartition theorem, which would predict \(C_{V}=\frac{3}{2}Nk_{B},\) half of the value that we have found. Why does the equipartition theorem fail? Because the dispersion relation is not a quadratic one (i.e., \(E \alpha p^{2}\)), as is needed for the equipartition theorem to hold, but in-stead is a linear one \((E \alpha p).\)

which is the ideal gas equation,\(^{5}\) as for the non-relativistic case. This also gives the enthalpy H via

\( H = U + pV = 4Nk_{B}T. \) (25.16)

\(^{5}\)Note that we have \(p = nk_{B}T\) for both the non-relativistic and ultrarelativis-tic cases. This is because \(Z_{1} \alpha V\) in both cases; hence \(Z_{N} \alpha V^{N}\) and \(F = −k_{B}TN \ln V +\)(other terms not in-volving V ), so that \(p = −(\partial F/\partial V )_{T} = nk_{B}T.\)

As we found for the non-relativistic case, getting the entropy involves bothering with what the constants are in eqn 25.11. Hence, let us write this equation as

\( \ln Z_{N} = N\ln V-3N\ln \Lambda -N\ln N+N\)

 

\( = N\ln \left(\frac{1}{n\Lambda ^{3}} \right)+N,\) (25.17)

where \(n = N/V ,\) so we immediately have (using the usual statistical mechanics manipulations listed in Table 20.1):
