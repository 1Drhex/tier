Guest Question.

Consider a system whose Hamiltonian is given by \(\hat{H}=\alpha (\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid )\), where α is a real number having the dimensions of energy and \(\mid \phi _{1}〉,\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are normalized eigenstates of a Hermitian operator \(\hat{A}\) that has no degenerate eigenvalues.

(a) Is \(\hat{H}\) a projection operator? What about \(\alpha ^{-2}\hat{H}^{2}\)?

(b) Show that \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are not eigenstates of \(\hat{H}\).

(c) Calculate the commutators \([\hat{H},\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid]\) and \([\hat{H},\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid]\) then find the relation that may exist between them.

(d) Find the normalized eigenstates of \(\hat{H}\) and their corresponding energy eigenvalues.

(e) Assuming that \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) form a complete and orthonormal basis, find the matrix representing \(\hat{H}\) in the basis. Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix and compare the results with those derived in (d).

Admin
Solution

(a) Since \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are eigenstates of \(\hat{A}\) and since \(\hat{A}\) is Hermitian, they must be orthogonal, \(〈\phi _{1}\mid\phi _{2}〉=0 \) (instance of Theorem 2.1). Now, since \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are both normalized and since \(〈\phi _{1}\mid\phi _{2}〉=0 \), we can reduce \(\hat {H}^{2}\) to

\(\hat {H}^{2}=\alpha ^{2}(\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid )(\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid )\)

 

\(=\alpha ^{2}(\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid )\), (2.471)

which is different from \(\hat{H}\) ; hence \(\hat{H}\) is not a projection operator. The operator \(\alpha ^{-2}\hat{H}^{2}\) is a projection operator since it is both Hermitian and equal to its own square. Using (2.471) we can write

\((\alpha ^{-2}\hat{H}^{2})^{2}=(\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid) (\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid)\)

 

\(=(\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid ) = \alpha ^{-2}\hat{H}^{2}\). (2.472)

(b) Since \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are both normalized, and since \(〈\phi _{1}\mid\phi _{2}〉=0 \), we have

\(\hat{H}\mid \phi _{1}〉=\alpha \mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid \phi _{1}〉 +\alpha \mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid\phi _{1}〉 =\alpha \mid \phi _{2}〉\), (2.473)

 

\(\hat{H}\mid \phi _{2}〉=\alpha \mid \phi _{1}〉; \). (2.474)

hence \(\mid \phi _{1}〉\) and \(\mid \phi _{2}〉\) are not eigenstates of \(\hat{H}\) . In addition, we have

\(〈\phi _{1}\mid\hat{H}\mid\phi _{1}〉=〈\phi _{2}\mid\hat{H} \mid\phi _{2}〉=0\). (2.475)

(c) Using the relations derived above,\(\hat{H}\mid \phi _{1}〉=\alpha \mid \phi _{2}〉\) and \(\hat{H}\mid \phi _{2}〉=\alpha \mid \phi _{1}〉\), we can write

\([\hat{H},\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid]=\alpha (\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid -\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid )\), (2.476)

 

\([\hat{H},\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid]=\alpha (\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid -\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid )\); (2.477)

hence

\([\hat{H},\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid]=-[\hat{H},\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid]\). (2.478)

(d) Consider a general state \(|\psi〉=\lambda _{1}\mid \phi _{1}〉+\lambda _{2}\mid \phi _{2}〉\). Applying \(\hat{H}\) to this state, we get

\(\hat{H}|\psi〉=\alpha (\mid \phi _{1}〉〈\phi _{2}\mid +\mid \phi _{2}〉〈\phi _{1}\mid )(\lambda _{1}\mid \phi _{1}〉+\lambda _{2}\mid \phi _{2}〉 )\)

 

\(=\alpha (\lambda _{2}\mid \phi _{1}〉+\lambda _{1}\mid \phi _{2}〉 )\). (2.479)

Now, since \(|\psi〉\) is normalized, we have

\(〈\psi\mid \psi〉=\left|\lambda _{1}\right| ^{2}+\left|\lambda _{2}\right| ^{2}=1\). (2.480)

The previous two equations show that \(\left|\lambda _{1}\right| =\left|\lambda _{2}\right|=1/\sqrt{2} \) and that \(\lambda _{1}=\pm \lambda _{2}\). Hence the eigenstates of the system are:

\(\mid \psi\pm 〉=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid \phi _{1}〉\pm \mid \phi _{2}〉)\). (2.481)

The corresponding eigenvalues are ± α:

\(\hat{H}\mid \psi\pm 〉=\pm \alpha \mid \psi\pm 〉\). (2.482)

(e) Since \(〈\phi _{1}\mid \phi _{2}〉=〈\phi _{2}\mid \phi _{1}〉=0\) and \(〈\phi _{1}\mid \phi _{1}〉=〈\phi _{2}\mid \phi _{2}〉=1\), we can verify that \(H_{11}=〈\phi _{1}\mid\hat {H}\mid \phi _{1}〉=0,H_{22}=〈\phi _{2}\mid\hat{H}\mid \phi _{2}〉=0,H_{12}=〈\phi _{1}\mid\hat{H}\mid \phi _{2}〉=\alpha , H_{21}=〈\phi _{2}\mid\hat{H}\mid \phi _{1}〉=\alpha \). The matrix of \(\hat{H}\) is thus given by

\(H=\alpha \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\). (2.483)

The eigenvalues of this matrix are equal to ± α and the corresponding eigenvectors are \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ \pm 1 \end{matrix} \right) \).

These results are indeed similar to those derived in (d).
