Guest Question.

This exercise illustrates the systematic method presented in § 5.4. We apply it to a paramagnetic system. We seek to find the relation between the specific heat at constant magnetic field and the specific heat at constant magnetisation. In order to obtain this result, we need to know the following about this system:

• The magnetisation Μ is related to the magnetic induction field B through the Curie magnetic equation of state (9.106),

\(M=\frac{C}{μ_0T}B\).

where C > 0 and \(μ_0 > 0\) and T is the temperature of the system.

• The internal energy density is a function of the entropy density s and the magnetisation M, i.e. u (s,M) according to relation (9.105),

d u (s,M) = T ds + B · d M.

• The magnetic enthalpy density \( h_m (s,B)\) is obtained by performing a Legendre transformation of internal energy density with respect to the magnetisation, i.e. \(h_m (s,B) = u − B · M \), so that according to relation (9.118),

\( d h_m (s,B) = T d s − M · d B \).

• The free energy density f (T,M) is obtained by performing a Legendre transformation of internal energy density with respect to the temperature, i.e. f \((T,M) = u − T _s \),

d f (T,M) = −s d T + B · d M

Use the method detailed in § 5.4 to determine the difference between the values of the specific heat per unit volume at fixed B, respectively at fixed M, which are defined by,

\(c_B = \frac{\partial h_m}{\partial T} \mid _B \). and \(c_M = \frac{\partial u}{\partial T} \mid _M \).

Admin
Solution

The magnetic enthalpy density is written as,

\(h_m\Bigl(s(T,B),B\Bigr)=u\biggl(s\Bigl(T,M(T,B)\Bigr),M(T,B) \biggr)-B.M(T,B)\).

The temperature derivative of the magnetic enthalpy density is given by,

\(\frac{\partial h_m}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial s}\frac{ds}{dT}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial M}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}-B.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\).

where,

\(\frac{ds}{dT}=\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}+\frac{\partial s}{\partial M}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\). and \(B=\frac{\partial u}{\partial M}\).

This implies that,

\(\frac{\partial h_m}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}=\frac{\partial u}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial M}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\).

Since,

\(\frac{\partial h_m}{\partial T} \mid _B=\frac{\partial h_m}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}\). and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial T} \mid _M=\frac{\partial u}{\partial s}\frac{\partial s}{\partial T}\). and \(T=\frac{\partial u}{\partial s}\).

the previous relation is recast as,

\(\frac{\partial h_m}{\partial T} \mid _B=\frac{\partial u}{\partial T} \mid _M+T\frac{\partial s}{\partial M}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\).

Therefore,

\(c_B=c_M+T\frac{\partial s}{\partial M}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\).

Applying the Schwarz theorem (4.67) to the free energy density f (T,M) we obtain,

\(\frac{\partial }{\partial x} \Bigl(\frac{\partial f(x,y) }{\partial y }\Bigr) =\frac{\partial }{\partial y} \Bigl(\frac{\partial f(x,y) }{\partial x }\Bigr) \).

\(\frac{\partial }{\partial T} \Bigl(\frac{\partial f }{\partial M }\Bigr) =\frac{\partial }{\partial M} \Bigl(\frac{\partial f }{\partial T }\Bigr) \).

which yields the Maxwell relation,

\(\frac{\partial B}{\partial T}=-[\frac{\partial s}{\partial M}\).

and implies that,

\(c_B=c_M-T\frac{\partial B}{\partial T}.\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}\).

According to the Curie magnetic equation of state (9.106),

\(M=\frac{C}{\mu _0T}B\).

\(\frac{\partial B}{\partial T}=\frac{B}{T}\). and \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial T}=-\frac{C B}{\mu _0 T^2}\).

\(c_B=c_M+\frac{C B^2}{\mu _0 T^2}\).
