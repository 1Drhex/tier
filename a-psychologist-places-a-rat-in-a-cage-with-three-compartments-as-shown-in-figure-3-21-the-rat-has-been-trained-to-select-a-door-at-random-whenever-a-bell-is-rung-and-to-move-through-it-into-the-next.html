Guest Question.

A psychologist places a rat in a cage with three compartments, as shown in Figure \(3.21\). The rat has been trained to select a door at random whenever a bell is rung and to move through it into the next compartment.

(a) If the rat is initially in compartment 1, what is the probability that it will be in compartment 2 after the bell has rung twice? three times?

(b) In the long run, what proportion of its time will the rat spend in each compartment?

Admin
Solution

Let \(P=\left[p_{i j}\right]\) be the transition matrix for this Markov chain. Then

\(p_{21}=p_{31}=\frac{1}{2}, p_{12}=p_{13}=\frac{1}{3}, p_{32}=p_{23}=\frac{2}{3}, \text { and } p_{11}=p_{22}=p_{33}=0 \)

(Why? Remember that \(p_{i j}\) is the probability of moving from \(j\) to \(i\).) Therefore,

\( P=\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{2}{3} & 0 \end{array}\right] \)

and the initial state vector is

\( \mathbf{x}_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right] \)

(a) After one ring of the bell, we have

\( \mathbf{x}_{1}=P \mathbf{x}_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{lll} 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{2}{3} & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0.5 \\ 0.5 \end{array}\right] \)

Continuing (rounding to three decimal places), we find

\( \mathbf{x}_{2}=P \mathbf{x}_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{2}{3} & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{3} \end{array}\right] \approx\left[\begin{array}{l} 0.333 \\ 0.333 \\ 0.333 \end{array}\right] \)

and

\( \mathbf{x}_{3}=P \mathbf{x}_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{lll} 0 & \frac{1}{3} & \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & 0 & \frac{2}{3} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{2}{3} & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{3} \\ \frac{1}{3} \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} \frac{2}{9} \\ \frac{7}{18} \\ \frac{7}{18} \end{array}\right] \approx\left[\begin{array}{l} 0.222 \\ 0.389 \\ 0.389 \end{array}\right] \)

Therefore, after two rings, the probability that the rat is in compartment 2 is \(\frac{1}{3} \approx\) \(0.333\), and after three rings, the probability that the rat is in compartment 2 is \(\frac{7}{18} \approx 0.389 .\) [ Note that these questions could also be answered by computing \(\left(P^{2}\right)_{21}\) and \(\left.\left(P^{3}\right)_{21} \cdot\right]\)

(b) This question is asking for the steady state vector \(\mathbf{x}\) as a probability vector. As we saw above, \(\mathrm{x}\) must be in the null space of \(I-P\), so we proceed to solve the system

\( [I-P \mid 0]=\left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 1 & -\frac{1}{3} & -\frac{1}{3} & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & 1 & -\frac{2}{3} & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{2}{3} & 1 & 0 \end{array}\right] \longrightarrow\left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 1 & 0 & -\frac{2}{3} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right] \)

Hence, if \(\mathbf{x}=\left[\begin{array}{l}x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3}\end{array}\right]\), then \(x_{3}=t\) is free and \(x_{1}=\frac{2}{3} t, x_{2}=t\). Since \(\mathbf{x}\) must be a probability vector, we need \(1=x_{1}+x_{2}+x_{3}=\frac{8}{3} t\). Thus, \(t=\frac{3}{8}\) and

\( \mathbf{x}=\left[\begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{4} \\ \frac{3}{8} \\ \frac{3}{8} \end{array}\right] \)

which tells us that, in the long run, the rat spends \(\frac{1}{4}\) of its time in compartment 1 and \(\frac{3}{8}\) of its time in each of the other two compartments.
