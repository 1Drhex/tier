Guest Question.

Determine an orthogonal matrix \(\mathbf{Q}\) which diagonalizes \(\mathrm{A}=\left(\begin{array}{rrr}2 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 & 4 \\ -2 & 4 & -1\end{array}\right)\)

Admin
Solution

Steps 1 and 2:

This is the same matrix as . Thus we have

\( \left\{\mathbf{u}=\left(\begin{array}{l} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right), \mathbf{v}=\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right)\right\} \text { belong to } \lambda_{1,2}=3 \text { and } \mathbf{w}=\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{array}\right) \text { to } \lambda_{3}=-6 \)

Step 3:

We need to check that eigenvectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) belonging to repeated eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1,2}=3\) are orthogonal.

Remember that in we showed that \(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}=-4\), therefore eigenvectors \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) are not orthogonal. We need to convert these \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) into an orthogonal set, say \(\mathrm{q}_{1}\) and \(\mathrm{q}_{2}\) respectively. How do we convert \(u\) and \(v\) into the orthogonal set \(\mathrm{q}_{1}\) and \(\mathrm{q}_{2}\) ?

By using the Gram-Schmidt process (4.16):

\(\mathbf{q}_{1}=\mathbf{u} \quad \text { and } \quad \mathbf{q}_{2}=\mathbf{v}-\frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}}{\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{q}_{1}\) (*)

How do we evaluate \(\mathrm{q}_{1}\) and \(\mathrm{q}_{2}\) ?

By substituting \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\) and evaluating \(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}\) and \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}\). We have

\(\mathbf{q}_{1}=\mathbf{u}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1 \\0\end{array}\right) \text { and } \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}=\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}=-4 \text { [already evaluated }]\\\) \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}=\left\|\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1 \\0\end{array}\right)\right\|^{2}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1 \\0\end{array}\right) \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1 \\0\end{array}\right)=2^{2}+1^{2}+0^{2}=5 \quad\left({ }^{* *}\right)\)

Substituting the above \(\mathbf{v}=\left(\begin{array}{r}-2 \\ 0 \\ 1\end{array}\right), \mathbf{q}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1 \\ 0\end{array}\right), \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}=-4\) and \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}=5\) into

\(\mathbf{q}_{2}=\mathbf{v}-\frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}}{\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{q}_{1} :\\\) \(\mathbf{q}_{2}=\mathbf{v}-\frac{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{1}}{\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{q}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{r}-2 \\ 0 \\ 1\end{array}\right)-\frac{(-4)}{5}\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\) \( =\left(\begin{array}{r} -2+8 / 5 \\ 0+4 / 5 \\ 1-0 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 / 5 \\ 4 / 5 \\ 1 \end{array}\right)=\frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{array}\right)=\mathbf{q}_{2} \)

Thus we have \(\mathbf{q}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1 \\ 0\end{array}\right), \mathbf{q}_{2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r}-2 \\ 4 \\ 5\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathrm{w}=\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\ 2 \\ -2\end{array}\right)\), which are orthogonal to each other. What else do we need to do?

Step 4:

We need to normalize these eigenvectors by dividing by the norm (length) of each. We have already established above in \(\left({ }^{* *}\right)\) that \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|^{2}=5\). Taking the square root gives \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{1}\right\|=\sqrt{5}\).

 

Remember, we can ignore any fractions (scalars) because vectors are orthogonal independent of scalars. Thus for the eigenvector \(\mathbf{q}_{2}\), we can ignore the fraction \(1 / 5\) and call this \(\mathbf{q}_{2}^{*}\) :

\( \left\|\mathbf{q}_{2}^{*}\right\|^{2}=\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{array}\right) \cdot\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{array}\right)=(-2)^{2}+4^{2}+5^{2}=45 \)

Taking the square root gives \(\left\|\mathbf{q}_{2}^{*}\right\|=\sqrt{45}=3 \sqrt{5}\). Similarly we have

\( \|w\|^{2}=\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{array}\right) \cdot\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{array}\right)=(-1)^{2}+2^{2}+(-2)^{2}=9 \)

Taking the square root gives the norm \(\|\mathrm{w}\|=3\). Normalizing means we divide each vector by its norm (length):

\( \widehat{\mathbf{q}_{1}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{l} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right), \quad \widehat{\mathbf{q}_{2}^{*}}=\frac{1}{3 \sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad \widehat{\mathbf{w}}=\frac{1}{3}\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \\ -2 \end{array}\right) \)

Step 5:

What is the orthogonal matrix \(Q\) equal to?

\( \mathbf{Q}=\left(\begin{array}{lll} \widehat{\mathrm{q}_{1}} & \widehat{\mathbf{q}_{2}^{*}} & \widehat{\mathbf{w}} \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{crr} 2 / \sqrt{5} & -2 / 3 \sqrt{5} & -1 / 3 \\ 1 / \sqrt{5} & 4 / 3 \sqrt{5} & 2 / 3 \\ 0 & 5 / 3 \sqrt{5} & -2 / 3 \end{array}\right) \)

Step 6:

Check \(\mathrm{QD}=\mathrm{AQ}\), where \(\mathrm{D}\) is a diagonal matrix with entries on the leading diagonal given by the eigenvalues of the matrix \(\mathrm{A}\).

What is \(D\) equal to?

\( \mathbf{D}=\left(\begin{array}{ccc} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -6 \end{array}\right) \quad\left[\begin{array}{l} \text { eigenvalues of the given matrix } \\ \mathrm{A} \text { are } \lambda_{1}=\lambda_{2}=3 \text { and } \lambda_{3}=-6 \end{array}\right] \)

process (4.16)

\( \begin{aligned} &\text { Let } \mathbf{p}_{1}=\mathbf{v}_{1} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{2}=\mathbf{v}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{3}=\mathbf{v}_{3}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{3}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{3}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{4}=\mathbf{v}_{4}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{3}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{3}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{3} \\ &\vdots \quad \vdots \quad \vdots \quad \vdots \\ &\mathbf{p}_{n}=\mathbf{v}_{n}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{3}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{3}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{3}-\cdots-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{n-1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{n-1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{n-1} \end{aligned} \)
