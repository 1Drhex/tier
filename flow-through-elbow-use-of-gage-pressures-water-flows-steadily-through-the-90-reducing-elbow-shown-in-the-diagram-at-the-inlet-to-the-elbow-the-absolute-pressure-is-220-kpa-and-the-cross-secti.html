FLOW THROUGH ELBOW: USE OF GAGE PRESSURES

Water flows steadily through the 90° reducing elbow shown in the diagram. At the inlet to the elbow, the absolute pressure is 220 kPa and the cross-sectional area is 0.01 m\({}^2\). At the outlet, the cross-sectional area is 0.0025 m\({}^2\) and the velocity is 16 m/s. The elbow discharges to the atmosphere. Determine the force required to hold the elbow in place.

Admin
Solution

Given: Steady flow of water through \(90^{\circ}\) reducing elbow.
\(p_{1}=220 \mathrm{kPa}(\mathrm{abs}) \quad A_{1}=0.01 \mathrm{~m}^{2} \quad \vec{V}_{2}=-16 \hat{j} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \quad A_{2}=0.0025 \mathrm{~m}^{2}\)
Find: Force required to hold elbow in place.
Solution:
Choose a fixed control volume as shown. Note that we have several surface force computations: \(p_{1}\) on area \(A_{1}\) and \(p_{\text {atm }}\) everywhere else. The exit at section (2) is to a free jet, and so at ambient (i.e., atmospheric) pressure. We can use a simplification here: If we subtract \(p_{\text {atm }}\) from the entire surface (a null effect as far as forces are concerned) we can work in gage pressures, as shown.

Note that since the elbow is anchored to the supply line, in addition to the reaction forces \(R_{x}\) and \(R_{y}\) (shown), there would also be a reaction moment (not shown).

Governing equations:

\(\begin{gathered}\vec{F}=\vec{F}_{S}+\vec{F}_{B}=\cancel{\frac{\partial}{ \partial t}}^{=0(4)} \int_{\mathrm{CV}} \vec{V} \rho d \forall+\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \vec{V} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A} \\\cancel{\frac{\partial}{ \partial t}}^{=0(4)} \int_{\mathrm{CV}} \rho d \forall+\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=0\end{gathered}\)}

 

Assumptions:

(1) Uniform flow at each section.
(2) Atmospheric pressure, \(p_{atm}= 101\) kPa (abs).
(3) Incompressible flow.
(4) Steady flow (given).
(5) Neglect weight of elbow and water in elbow.

Once again (although we didn’t need to) we started with the most general form of the governing equations. Writing the \(x\) component of the momentum equation results in

\(F_{S_{x}}=\int_{C S} u \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=\int_{A_{1}} u \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}\) {\(F_{B_x} = 0\quad \text{and}\quad u_2 = 0\)}

\(\quad p_{1_{g}} A_{1}+R_{x}=\int_{A_{1}} u \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}\)

so
\(\begin{aligned}R_{x} &=-p_{1_{g}} A_{1}+\int_{A_{1}} u \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A} \\&=-p_{1_{g}} A_{1}+u_{1}\left(-\rho V_{1} A_{1}\right) \\R_{x} &=-p_{1_{g}} A_{1}-\rho V_{1}^{2} A_{1}\end{aligned}\)

Note that \(u_{1}\) is the \(x\) component of the velocity, so that \(u_{1}=V_{1}\). To find \(V_{1}\), use the mass conservation equation:
\(\begin{gathered}\int_{\mathrm{CS}} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=\int_{A_{1}} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}+\int_{A_{2}} \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=0 \\\therefore\left(-\rho V_{1} A_{1}\right)+\left(\rho V_{2} A_{2}\right)=0\end{gathered}\)

and

\(V_{1}=V_{2} \frac{A_{2}}{A_{1}}=16 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}} \times \frac{0.0025}{0.01}=4 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

We can now compute \(R_{x}\)

\(\begin{aligned}R_{x} &=-p_{1_{g}} A_{1}-\rho V_{1}^{2} A_{1} \\&=-1.19 \times 10^{5} \frac{\mathrm{N}}{\mathrm{m}^{2}} \times 0.01 \mathrm{~m}^{2}-999 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}} \times 16 \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{~s}^{2}} \times 0.01 \mathrm{~m}^{2} \times \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \\R_{x} &=-1.35 \mathrm{kN} \xleftarrow[]{\quad \quad \quad R_x}\end{aligned}\)

Writing the \(y\) component of the momentum equation gives
\(F_{S_{y}}+F_{B_{y}}=R_{y}+F_{B_{y}}=\int_{C S} v \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A}=\int_{A_{2}} v \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A} \quad\quad\left\{v_{1}=0\right\}\)
or
\(\begin{aligned}R_{y} &=-F_{B_{y}}+\int_{A_{2}} v \rho \vec{V} \cdot d \vec{A} \\&=-F_{B_{y}}+v_{2}\left(\rho V_{2} A_{2}\right) \\R_{y} &=-F_{B_{y}}-\rho V_{2}^{2} A_{2}\end{aligned}\)

Note that \(v_2\) is the y component of the velocity, so that \(v_2 = -V_2\), where \(V_2\) is the magnitude of the exit velocity.
Substituting known values

\(\begin{aligned}R_{y} &=-F_{B_{y}}+-\rho V_{2}^{2} A_{2} \\&=-F_{B_{y}}-999 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}} \times(16)^{2} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{~s}^{2}} \times 0.0025 \mathrm{~m}^{2} \times \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \\&=-F_{B_{y}}-639 \mathrm{~N} \quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad \quad \quad R_y}\end{aligned}\)

Neglecting \(F_{B_{y}}\) gives
\(R_{y}=-639 \mathrm{~N}\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad \quad \quad R_y}\)

 

This problem illustrates how using gage pressures simplifies evaluation of the surface forces in the momentum equation.
