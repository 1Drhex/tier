Guest Question.

Rotations on spin states are given by an expression identical to Equation 6.32, with the spin angular momentum replacing the orbital angular momentum:

\( \hat{R}_{ n }(\varphi)=\exp \left[-\frac{i \varphi}{\hbar} n \cdot \hat{ L }\right] \) (6.32).

\( R _{ n }(\varphi)=\exp \left[-i \frac{\varphi}{\hbar} n \cdot S \right] \) ,

In this problem we will consider rotations of a spin-1/2 state.

(a) Show that

\( ( a \cdot \sigma )( b \cdot \sigma )= a \cdot b +i( a \times b ) \cdot \sigma \),

where the \( σ_i \) are the Pauli spin matrices and and are ordinary vectors.

Use the result of .

(b) Use your result from part (a) to show that

\( \exp \left[-i \frac{\varphi}{\hbar} n \cdot S \right]=\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right) n \cdot \sigma \),

Recall that \( S =(\hbar / 2) \sigma \).

(c) Show that your result from part (b) becomes, in the standard basis of spin up and spin down along the z axis, the matrix

\( R_{ n }=\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \).

where θ and ϕ are the polar coordinates of the unit vector that describes the axis of rotation.

(d) Verify that the matrix \( R_n \) in part (c) is unitary.

(e) Compute explicitly the matrix \( S _{x}^{\prime}= R ^{\dagger} S _{x} R \) where R is a rotation by an angle \( \varphi \) about the z axis and verify that it returns the expected result.
Hint: rewrite your result for \( S _{x}^{\prime} \text { in terms of } S _{x} \text { and } S _{y} \).

(f) Construct the matrix for a π rotation about the x axis and verify that it turns an up spin into a down spin.

(g) Find the matrix describing a 2π rotation about the z axis. Why is this answer surprising?

Solution

(a) We have

\( ( a \cdot \sigma )( b \cdot \sigma )=\sum_{j} a_{j} \sigma_{j} \sum_{k} b_{k} \sigma_{k} \).

\( =\sum_{j} \sum_{k} a_{j} b_{k}\left(\delta_{j k}+i \sum_{n} \epsilon_{j k n} \sigma_{n}\right) \).

\( =\sum_{j} a_{j} b_{j}+i \sum_{n} \sum_{j} \sum_{k} \epsilon_{j k n} a_{j} b_{k} \sigma_{n} \).

\( = a \cdot b +i \sum_{n}[ a \times b ]_{n} \sigma_{n} \).

\( = a \cdot b +i( a \times b ) \cdot \sigma \).

where the critical identity in the second line comes from .

(b) First note that from part (a), for a unit vector n,

\( ( n \cdot \sigma)^{2}= n \cdot n +i( n \times n ) \cdot \sigma=1 \).

and therefore

\( ( n \cdot \sigma)^{2}= \begin{cases} n \cdot \sigma & n \text { odd } \\ 1 & n \text { even }\end{cases} \).

We can then write

\( \exp \left[-i \frac{\varphi}{\hbar} n \cdot S \right]=\exp \left[i \frac{\varphi}{2} n \cdot \sigma\right] \).

\( =\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n !}\left(-\frac{i \varphi}{2}\right)^{k}( n \cdot \sigma)^{k} \).

\( =\sum_{k \text { even }} \frac{1}{k !}\left(-\frac{i \varphi}{2}\right)^{k}+\sum_{k \text { odd }} \frac{1}{k !}\left(-\frac{i \varphi}{2}\right)^{k} n \cdot \sigma \).

\( =\sum_{k \text { even }} \frac{(-1)^{k / 2}}{k !}\left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)^{k}-i \sum_{k \text { odd }} \frac{(-1)^{(k-1) / 2}}{k !}\left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)^{k} n \cdot \sigma \).

\( =\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right) n \cdot \sigma \).

I’ve kept everything as sums but it would also be fine to just write out the first half-dozen or so terms in the series.

(c) In polar coordinates

\( n =\sin \theta \cos \phi \hat{\imath}+\sin \theta \sin \phi \hat{\jmath}+\cos \theta \hat{k} \).

and

\( R _{n}=\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left[n_{x} \sigma_{x}+n_{y} \sigma_{y}+n_{z} \sigma_{z}\right] \).

\( =\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left[\sin \theta \cos \phi\left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array}\right)\right. \)

\( \left.+\sin \theta \sin \phi\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{array}\right)+\cos \theta\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{array}\right)\right] \).

\( =\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)-i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \).

(d) The Hermitian conjugate of \( R _{n} \text { is } \)

\( R _{n}^{\dagger}=\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)+i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right)^{T} \).

\( =\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)+i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \).

which is clearly a rotation by \( -\varphi / 2 \) about the same axis, so it must be the inverse of \( R_n.\) However, lets check it explicitly as well:

\( R _{n} R _{n}^{\dagger}=\cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \) \( +i \cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \) \( -i \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \cos \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \) \( +\sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \)

\( \times \sin \left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos \theta & \sin \theta e^{-i \phi} \\ \sin \theta e^{i \phi} & -\cos \theta \end{array}\right) \).

\( =\cos ^{2}\left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \)

\( +\sin ^{2}\left(\frac{\varphi}{2}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos ^{2} \theta+\sin ^{2} \theta & 0 \\ 0 & \sin ^{2} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta \end{array}\right) \).

\( =\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \).

(e) For a rotation about the z axis, θ = 0 and

\( R =\left(\begin{array}{cc} \cos (\varphi / 2)-i \sin (\varphi / 2) & 0 \\ 0 & \cos (\varphi / 2)+i \sin (\varphi / 2) \end{array}\right) \).

\( =\left(\begin{array}{cc} e^{-i \varphi / 2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i \varphi / 2} \end{array}\right) \).

Then

\( S _{x}^{\prime}= R ^{\dagger} S _{x} R \).

\( =\left(\begin{array}{cc} e^{i \varphi / 2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i \varphi / 2} \end{array}\right) \frac{\hbar}{2}\left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} e^{-i \varphi / 2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i \varphi / 2} \end{array}\right) \).

\( =\frac{\hbar}{2}\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & e^{i \varphi} \\ e^{-i \varphi} & 0 \end{array}\right) \).

This can then be written as

\( S _{x}^{\prime}=\frac{\hbar}{2}\left[\cos \varphi\left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array}\right)-\sin \varphi\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{array}\right)\right] \).

\( = S _{x} \cos \varphi- S _{y} \sin \varphi \).

which is precisely what we expect for the rotation of a vector (Equation 6.31).

\( \left(\begin{array}{c} \hat{x}^{\prime} \\ \hat{y}^{\prime} \\ \hat{z}^{\prime} \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{ccc} \cos \varphi & -\sin \varphi & 0 \\ \sin \varphi & \cos \varphi & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l} \hat{x} \\ \hat{y} \\ \hat{z} \end{array}\right) ? \) (6.31).

(f) For a π rotation about the x axis, \( \theta=\pi / 2, \phi=0 \text {, and } \varphi=\pi \text { so that } \)

\( R =\left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & -i \\ -i & 0 \end{array}\right) \).

and

\( R \left(\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right)=-i\left(\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \).

which transforms an up spin into a down spin, albeit with a different phase factor

(g) For a rotation of 2 π about any axis, \( \varphi=2 \pi \text { and } \sin (\varphi / 2)=0 \text { so that } \)

\( R (2 \pi)=-\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \).

This is surprising because it is not simply the identity matrix!
