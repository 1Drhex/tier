Guest Question.

Hydrogen is often stored by adsorbing it into a metal hydride powder. The hydrogen can be desorbed as needed, by heating the metal hydride throughout its volume. Consider a hydrogen fuel cell–powered automobile cruising at a speed of V = 25 m/s. The car consumes \(\dot{m}_{ H _{2}}=1.35 \times 10^{-4} kg / s\) of hydrogen, which is supplied from a cylindrical, stainless steel canister loaded with metal hydride powder. The canister is of inside diameter \(D_{i}=0.1\) m, length L = 0.8 m, and wall thickness t = 0.5 mm, and is subject to air in cross flow at \(V=25 m / s , T_{\infty}=23^{\circ} C\).

In order for desorption to occur, the metal hydride must be maintained at an operating temperature of at least 275 K. The desorption process is an endothermic reaction corresponding to a thermal generation rate expressed as

\(\dot{E}_{g}=-\dot{m}_{ H _{2}} \times\left(29.5 \times 10^{3} kJ / kg \right)\)

where \(\dot{m}_{ H _{2}}\) is the hydrogen desorption rate (kg/s). Determine how much additional heating, beyond that due to convection from the air, should be supplied to the canister to maintain the required operating temperature.

Admin
Solution

Known: Size and shape of a hydrogen storage canister, hydrogen desorption rate, required hydrogen operating pressure, velocity and temperature of air in cross flow.

Find: The rate of convective heat transfer to the canister and the additional heating needed to sustain \(p_{ H _{2}}>p_{ fc }\).

Assumptions:

1. Steady-state, incompressible flow conditions.

2. Uniform cylinder surface temperature.

3. Negligible heat gain through the ends of the cylinder.

4. Uniform metal hydride temperature.

5. Negligible contact resistance between the canister wall and the metal hydride.

Properties: , air \(\left(T_{f} \approx 285 K \right): v=14.56 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s , k=25.2 \times 10^{-3} W / m \cdot K,\operatorname{Pr}=0.712\). , AISI 316 stainless steel \(\left(T_{ ss } \approx 300 K \right): k_{ ss }=13.4 W / m \cdot K\).

Analysis: The thermal energy generation rate associated with the desorption of hydrogen from the metal hydride at the required flow rate is

 

\(\dot{E}_{g}=-\left(1.35 \times 10^{-4} kg / s \right) \times\left(29.5 \times 10^{6} J / kg \right)=-3982 W\)

 

To determine the convective heat transfer rate, we begin by calculating the Reynolds number:

 

\(R e_{D}=\frac{V\left(D_{i}+2 t\right)}{v}=\frac{23 m / s \times(0.1 m +2 \times 0.005 m )}{14.56 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s }=173,760\)

 

Use of Equation 7.54

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62 \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{1 / 2} \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / P r)^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{R e_{D}}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}\) (7.54)

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62 \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{1 / 2} P r^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / P r)^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{R e_{D}}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}\)

 

yields

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62(173,760)^{1 / 2}(0.712)^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / 0.712)^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{173,760}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}=315.8\)

 

Therefore, the average convection heat transfer coefficient is

 

\(\bar{h}=\overline{N u}_{D} \frac{k}{\left(D_{i}+2 t\right)}=315.8 \times \frac{25.3 \times 10^{-3} W / m \cdot K }{(0.1 m +2 \times 0.005 m )}=72.6 W / m ^{2} \cdot K\)

 

Simplifying Equation 3.34, we find

 

\(q_{r}=\frac{T_{\infty, 1}-T_{\infty, 4}}{\frac{1}{2 \pi r_{1} L h_{1}}+\frac{\ln \left(r_{2} / r_{1}\right)}{2 \pi k_{A} L}+\frac{\ln \left(r_{3} / r_{2}\right)}{2 \pi k_{B} L}+\frac{\ln \left(r_{4} / r_{3}\right)}{2 \pi k_{C} L}+\frac{1}{2 \pi r_{4} L h_{4}}}\) (3.34)

 

\(q_{ conv }=\frac{T_{\infty}-T_{i}}{\frac{1}{\pi L\left(D_{i}+2 t\right) \bar{h}}+\frac{\ln \left[\left(D_{i}+2 t\right) / D_{i}\right]}{2 \pi k_{ ss } L}}\)

or, substituting values,

 

\(q_{ conv }=\frac{296 K -275.2 K }{\frac{1}{\pi(0.8 m )(0.1 m +2 \times 0.005 m )\left(72.6 W / m ^{2} \cdot K \right)}+\frac{\ln [(0.1 m +2 \times 0.005 m ) / 0.1 m ]}{2 \pi(13.4 W / m \cdot K )(0.8 m )}}\)

= 406 W

 

The additional thermal energy, \(q_{ add }\), that must be supplied to the canister to maintain the steadystate operating temperature may be found from an energy balance, \(q_{\text {add }}+q_{\text {conv }}+\dot{E}_{g}=0\). Therefore,

 

\(q_{ add }=-q_{ conv }-\dot{E}_{g}=-406 W +3982 W =3576 W\)

 

Comments:

1. Additional heating will occur due to radiation, conduction from the canister mounting hardware and fuel lines, and possibly condensation of water vapor on the cool canister.

2. The thermal resistances associated with conduction in the canister wall and convection are 0.0014 K/W and 0.053 K/W, respectively. The convection resistance dominates and can be reduced by adding fins to the exterior of the canister.

3. The amount of additional heating that is required will increase if the automobile is operated at a higher speed, since the hydrogen consumption scales as \(V^{3}\), while the convective heat transfer coefficient increases as \(V^{0.7} \text { to } V^{0.8}\). Additional heating is also needed when the automobile is operated in a cooler climate.
