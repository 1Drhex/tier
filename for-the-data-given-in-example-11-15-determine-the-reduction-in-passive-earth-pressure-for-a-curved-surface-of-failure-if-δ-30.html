Guest Question.

For the data given in , determine the reduction in passive earth pressure for a curved surface of failure if δ = 30°.

Admin
Solution

For a plane surface of failure \(P_{p}\) from Eq. (11.76) is

 

\(P_{p}=\frac{P_{ pm }}{\cos \delta}=\frac{1}{2} \gamma H^{2} \frac{K_{p}}{\sin \alpha \cos \delta}\) (11.76)

 

\(P_{p}=\frac{1}{2} \times 18.5 \times 10^{2} \times \frac{21}{\sin 90^{\circ} \cos 30^{\circ}}=22,431 kN / m\)

 

where, \(K_{p}=21\) from Fig. 11.24 for \(\delta=30^{\circ} \text { and } \phi=40^{\circ}\)

From Table 11.3 for \(\delta=30^{\circ} \text { and } \phi=40^{\circ}\)

 

\(K_{p}^{\prime}=\frac{10.38+17.50}{2}=13.94\)

 

From Eq (11.77)

 

\(P_{p}=\frac{1}{2} \gamma H^{2} K_{p}^{\prime}\) (11.77)

 

\(P_{p}=\frac{1}{2} \times 18.5 \times 10^{2} \times 13.94=12,895 kN / m\)

 

\(\text { Reduction in passive pressure }=\frac{22,431-12,895}{22,431}=42.5 \%\)

 

It is clear from the above calculations, that the soil resistance under a passive state gives highly erroneous values for plane surfaces of failure with an increase in the value of δ. This error could lead to an unsafe condition because the computed values of \(P_{p}\) would become higher than the actual soil resistance.
