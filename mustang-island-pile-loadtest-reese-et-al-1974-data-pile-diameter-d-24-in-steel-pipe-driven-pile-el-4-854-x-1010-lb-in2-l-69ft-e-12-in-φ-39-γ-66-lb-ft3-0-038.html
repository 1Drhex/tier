Guest Question.

Mustang Island Pile LoadTest (Reese et al., 1974)

Data:

Pile diameter, d = 24 in, steel pipe (driven pile)

\(E I=4.854 \times 10^{10} lb - in ^{2}\)

L = 69ft

e = 12 in.

\(\phi=39^{\circ}\) \(\gamma=66 lb / ft ^{3}\left(=0.0382 lb / in ^{3}\right)\)

\(M_{y}=7 \times 10^{6}\) in-lbs

The soil was fine silty sand with WT at ground level

Required:

(a) Load-deflection curve \(\left(P_{t} \text { vs. } y_{g}\right) \text { and } n_{h} \text { vs. } y_{g}\) curve

(b) Load-max moment curve \(\left(P_{t} Vs M_{\max }\right)\)

(c) Ultimate load \(P_{u}\)

Admin
Solution

For pile in sand, \(n_{h}=\frac{150 C_{\phi} \gamma^{1.5 }\sqrt{E I d}}{P_{e}}\)

 

For \(\phi=39^{\circ}, \quad C_{\phi}=3 \times 10^{-5}(1.316)^{39^{\circ}}=1.34\)

 

After substitution and simplifying

 

\(n_{h}=\frac{1631 \times 10^{3}}{P_{e}}\) (a)

 

From Eqs( 16.22) and (16.14a), \(P_{e}=P_{t} 1+0.67 \frac{e}{T}\) (b)

 

\(T=\frac{E I}{n_{h}}^{\frac{1}{5}}\) (c)

 

\(T=\left[\frac{E I}{n_{h}}\right]^{\frac{1}{5}}\) (16.14a)

 

\(P_{e}=P_{t}\left(1+0.67 \frac{e}{T}\right)\) (16.22)

 

(a) Calculation of Groundline Deflection, \(y _{g}\)

Step 1

Since T is not known to start with, assume \(e=0, \text { and } P_{e}=P_{t}=10,000 lbs\)

 

Now, from Eq. (a), \(n_{h}=\frac{1631 \times 10^{3}}{10 \times 10^{3}}=163 lb / in ^{3}\)

 

from Eq. (c), \(T=\frac{4.854 \times 10^{10}}{163}^{\frac{1}{5}}=49.5 in\)

 

from Eq. (b), \(P_{e}=10 \times 10^{3} 1+0.67 \times \frac{12}{49.5}=11.624 \times 10^{3} lbs\)

 

Step 2

 

For \(P_{e}=11.62 \times 10^{3} lb , \quad n_{h}=\frac{1631 \times 10^{3}}{11.624 \times 10^{3}}=140 lb / in ^{3}\)

 

As in Step 1 \(T=51 ins , P_{e}=12.32 \times 10^{3} lbs\)

 

Step 3

Continue Step 1 and Step 2 until convergence is reached in the values of T and \(P_{e}\). The final values obtained for \(P_{t}=10 \times 10^{3} lb \text { are } T=51.6 \text { in, and } P_{e}=12.32 \times 10^{3} lbs\)

 

Step 4

The ground line deflection may be obtained from Eq (16.23).

 

\(y_{g}=\frac{2.43 P_{e} T^{3}}{E I}\) (16.23)

 

\(y_{g}=\frac{2.43 P_{e} T^{3}}{E I}=\frac{2.43 \times 12.32 \times 10^{3} \times(51.6)^{3}}{4.854 \times 10^{10}}=0.0845 \text { in }\)

 

This deflection is for \(P_{t}=10 \times 10^{3}\) Ibs. In the same way the values of \(y_{g}\) can be obtained for different stages of loadings. Fig. 16.14(a) gives a plot \(P_{t} \text { vs. } y_{g} . \text { Since } n_{h}\) is known at each stage of loading, a curve of \(n_{h} \text { vs. } y_{g}\) can be plotted as shown in the same figure.

(b) Maximum Moment

The calculations under (a) above give the values of T for various loads \(P_{t}\). By making use of Eq. (16.11) and Table 16.2, moment distribution along the pile for various loads \(P_{t}\) can be calculated. From these curves the maximum moments may be obtained and a curve of \(P_{t} \text { vs. } M_{\max }\) may be plotted as shown in Fig. 16.14b.

 

\(M=[P_{t} T] A_{m}+\left[M_{t}\right] B_{m}\) (6.11)

 

(c) Ultimate Load \(P_{u}\)

Figure 16. 14(b) is a plot of \(M_{\max } \text { vs. } P_{t}\). From this figure, the value of \(P_{u}\) is equal to 100 kips for the ultimate pile moment resistance of \(7 \times 10^{6}\) in-lb. The value obtained by Broms’ method and by computer (Reese, 1986) are 92 and 102 kips respectively

Comments:

Figure 16.14a gives the computed \(P_{t} \text { vs. } y\) curve by the direct approach method (Murthy and Subba Rao 1995) and the observed values. There is an excellent agreement between the two. In the same way the observed and the calculated moments and ultimate loads agree well.
