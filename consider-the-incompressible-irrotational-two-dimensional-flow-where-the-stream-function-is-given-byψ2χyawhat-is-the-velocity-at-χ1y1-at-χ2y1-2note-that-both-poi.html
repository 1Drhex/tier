Guest Question.

consider the incompressible, irrotational, two-dimensional flow, where the stream function is given by:

\(\psi =2\chi y\)

(a) What is the velocity at \(\chi =1 , y=1? \) At \(\chi =2, y=\frac{1}{2}\) ?

Note that both points are on the same streamline, since \(\psi =2\)

for both points.

(b) Sketch the streamline pattern and discuss the significance of the spacing between the streamlines.

(c) What is the velocity potential for this flow?

(d) Sketch the lines of constant potential. How do the lines of equipotential relate to the streamlines?

Admin
Solution

-(a) The stream function can be used to calculate the velocity components:

\(u=\frac{\partial \psi }{\partial y}=2\chi\) \(\nu =-\frac{\partial \psi }{\partial \chi }=-2y \)Now we can find the velocity magnitudes using:

\(\vec{V}=2\chi \hat{i}-2y\hat{j}\)

\(At \chi =1,y=1,\vec{V}=2\hat{i}-2\hat{j} \)and the magnitude of the velocity is

\(U=\mid \vec{V}\mid =\sqrt{\left ( 2 \right )^{2}+\left ( -2 \right )^{2}}=\sqrt{8}=2.8284\)

At \(\chi =2,y=\frac{1}{2},\vec{V}=4\hat{i}-\hat{j} \) and the magnitude of the velocity is

\(U=\mid \vec{V}\mid=\sqrt{\left ( 4 \right )^{2}+\left ( -1 \right )^{2}}=\sqrt{17}=4.1231\)

(b) A sketch of the streamline pattern is presented in F ig. 3 .8. Results are presented only for the first quadrant (\( \chi positive\), y positive). Mirrorimage patterns would exist in the other quadrants. Note that the\( \chi = 0 \)and the y = 0 axes represent the c = 0 “streamline.” These axes, therefore, could be thought of as solid surfaces, and the first quadrant flow field would represent flow in a 90 degree corner. Since the flow is incompressible and steady, the integral form of the continuity equation ( 2.5)indicates that the product of the velocity times the distance between the streamlines is a constant. That is, since

\(\rho=constant\),

\(\oint \vec{V}\cdot \hat{n}dA=0\)

Therefore, the distance between the streamlines decreases as the magnitude of the velocity increases.

(c) Since \(u=\partial \Phi /\partial \chi and \nu =\partial \Phi /\partial y\) , we can integrate both equations to obtain:

\(\int \frac{\partial \Phi }{\partial \chi }=\Phi =\int ud\chi =\int 2\chi d\chi =\chi ^{2}+g\left ( y \right )\) \(\int \frac{\partial \Phi }{\partial y}dy=\Phi =\int udy=\int \left ( -2y \right )dy=-y^{2}+f\left ( \chi \right )\)

The functions \( f(\chi) \) and g(y) appear (rather than constants of integration) because we are integrating partial derivatives. The potential function that satisfies both of these equations, since \( g(y) = -y^{2} and f(\chi) = \chi ^{2}\) , is:

\(\Phi =\chi ^{2}-y^{2}+c\)

where C is an arbitrary constant.

(d) The equipotential lines are included in Fig. 3.8 , where C, the arbitrary constant, has been set equal to zero. The lines of equipotential are everywhere perpendicular to the streamlines.
