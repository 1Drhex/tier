Guest Question.

A tracking radar lies in the vertical plane of the path of a rocket which is coasting in unpowered flight above the atmosphere. For the instant when θ = 30°, the tracking data give \(r=25(10^{4} )\) ft, \(\dot{r} \) = 4000 ft /sec, and \(\dot{θ} \) = 0.80 deg /sec.
The acceleration of the rocket is due only to gravitational attraction and for its particular altitude is 31.4 \({ft}/{sec^{2} }\) vertically down. For these conditions determine the velocity v of the rocket and the values of \(\ddot{r}\) and \(\ddot{θ}\).

Admin
Solution

The components of velocity from Eq. 2 /13 \(v=\dot{r} e_{r} +r\dot{\Theta } e_{\Theta }\)are

\(\left[v_{r} =\dot{r}\right]\) \(v_{r}=4000{ft}/{sec}\)

\(\left[v_{\Theta }=r\dot{\Theta }\right] \) \(v_{\Theta }=25(10^{4} )(0.80)(\frac{\pi }{180} )=3490{ft}/{sec}\)

\(\left[v=\sqrt{v_{r} ^{2}+v_{\Theta } ^{2} } \right]\) \(v=\sqrt{(4000) ^{2}+(3410) ^{2} }=5310{ft}/{sec}\)

 

Since the total acceleration of the rocket is \(g=31.4{ft}/{sec^{2} }\) down, we can easily find its r- and θ-components for the given position. As shown in the figure, they are

\(a_{r} =-31.4\cos 30^{\circ }=-27.2{ft}/{sec^{2} } \)

\(a_{\Theta }=31.4\sin 30^{\circ }=15.70{ft}/{sec^{2} }\)

We now equate these values to the polar-coordinate expressions for \(a_{r}\) and \(a_{\Theta }\) which contain the unknowns \(\ddot{r}\)and \(\ddot{\Theta }\). Thus, from Eq. 2 /14\(a=(\ddot{r} -r\dot{\Theta } ^{2} )e_{r} +(r\ddot{\Theta }+2\dot{r}\dot{\Theta } )e_{\Theta } \)

\( \left[a_{r} =\ddot{r} -r\dot{\Theta } ^{2}\right]\) \(-27.2=\ddot{r}-25(10^{4} )(0.80\frac{\pi }{180})^{2}\)

\(\ddot{r}=21.5{ft}/{sec^{2} }\)

\( \left[a_{\Theta } = r\ddot{\Theta }+2\dot{r}\dot{\Theta } \right] \) \(15.70=25(10^{4} )\ddot{\Theta }+2(4000)(0.80\frac{\pi }{180})\)

\(\ddot{\Theta }=-3.84(10^{-4} ){rad}/{sec^{2} }\)
