Guest Question.

A process similar to that shown in Figure is used to transfer \( CO _{2} \) from a compressed flue gas mixture into a tank of liquid water. The gas space over the liquid inside the tank is considered well mixed, and the \( CO _{2} \) composition exiting the headspace contains 10 mole% \( CO _{2} \) and 90 % \( N_{2} \) at 2.0 atm total system pressure and \( 25^{\circ} C \). Water containing no dissolved \( CO _{2} \) enters the tank at a flow rate \( \left(v_{o}\right) \) of \( 0.20 m ^{3} / h.\) The liquid is well mixed, but the gas is not bubbled into the liquid, so the liquid surface defines the gas–liquid interphase mass-transfer area, S. The diameter of the cylindrical tank (d) is 3.0 m, and the liquid depth is 1.0 m. It can be assumed that the \( N_{2} \) gas does not dissolve appreciably in the liquid, and the solvent is nonvolatile. What is the concentration of dissolved \( CO _{2} \) in the liquid stream exiting the tank, \( C_{A L} \), and what is the maximum dissolved \( CO _{2} \) concentration, \( c_{A L}^{*} \) ?

Admin
Solution

At the conditions of agitation, the gas film mass-transfer coefficient is \( k_{c} = 0.05 m / s, \) and the liquid film mass-transfer coefficient is \( k_{L} = 1.5 \times 10^{-5} m / s \). The Henry’s law constant for dissolution of \( CO _{2} \) gas into liquid water\(^{3} \) at \( 25^{\circ} C \) is 1630 atm, based on the definition \( p_{A, i} = H x_{A, i} \). The density of the liquid water at \( 25^{\circ} C \) is \( 995.2 kg / m ^{3} \). For convenience, the calculations will be based on the liquid phase in the tank, with \( A = CO _{2} \), and \( B = H _{2} O \). The process analysis and solution strategy will require determination of the overall mass-transfer coefficient \( K_{L}\), and the development of a material balance model to predict the dissolved \( CO _{2} \) concentration in the liquid stream exiting the tank. For a linear equilibrium relationship defined by Henry’s law, \( K_{L} \) is given by equation ( \( \frac{1}{K_{G}} = \frac{1}{k_{G}}+\frac{H}{k_{L}} \) )

 

\( \frac{1}{K_{L}} = \frac{1}{k_{L}} + \frac{H}{k_{G}} \)

 

with H defined in terms of \( p_{A, i} = H c_{A L, i}\). The needed conversions for obtaining H and \( K_{G} \) are

 

\( H = \frac{1630 atm }{C_{L}} = 1630 atm \frac{M_{B}}{\rho_{B, l i q}} = 1630 atm \frac{18 kg / kgmole }{995.2 kg / m ^{3}} = 29.5 \frac{ m ^{3} \cdot atm }{ kgmole } \)

 

\( k_{G} = \frac{k_{c}}{R T} = \frac{\left(0.05 \frac{ m }{ s }\right)}{\left(0.08206 \frac{ m ^{3} \cdot atm }{ kgmole \cdot K }\right)(298 K )} = 2.05 \times 10^{-3} \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s \cdot atm }\)

 

and so

\( \frac{1}{K_{L}} = \frac{1}{1.5 \times 10^{-5} m / s }+\frac{29.5 m ^{3} \cdot atm / kgmole }{2.05 kgmole / m ^{2} \cdot s \cdot atm }\)

 

or \( K_{L} = 1.23 \times 10^{-5} m / s \). Note that \( K_{L} < k_{L} \), and that \( K_{L} \) is close to the liquid film mass-transfer coefficient \( K_{L} \). The % of resistance, based on equation ( \( \frac{\text { resistance in the liquid phase }}{\text { total resistance in both phases }} = \frac{\Delta c_{A L, \text { liquid film }}}{\Delta c_{A L, \text { total }}} = \frac{c_{A L, i}-c_{A L}}{c_{A L}^{*} – c_{A L}} = \frac{1 / k_{L}}{1 / K_{L}}\) ) for the liquid phase, is

 

\( \frac{1 / k_{L}}{1 / K_{L}} = \frac{K_{L}}{k_{L}} = \frac{1.23 \times 10^{-5} m / s }{1.5 \times 10^{-5} m / s } \times 100 \% = 82 \% \) liquid phase controlling

 

which is consistent with the observation that the \( CO _{2} \) is relatively insoluble in the solvent water. A material balance on \( CO _{2} \) in the liquid phase of the tank makes the following assumptions: (1) steady-state process, (2) well-mixed liquid in the tank, so that the dissolved \( CO _{2} \) concentration in the tank equals the dissolved \( CO _{2} \) concentration in the outlet liquid stream, (3) constant liquid volume, (4) dilute process, and (5) no reaction of \( CO _{2} \). Under these assumptions, the material balance for \( CO _{2} \) (moles \( CO _{2} \)/time) is

 

( dissolved \( CO _{2} \) in inlet liquid flow) – ( interphase mass-transfer of \( CO _{2} \) in gas to \( CO _{2} \) in liquid (IN) ) + ( dissolved \( CO _{2} \) in outlet liquid flow (OUT)) + ( reaction of \( CO _{2} \) in liquid (GEN) ) = ( accmulation of \( CO _{2} \) in liquid (ACC) )

 

or

\( v_{o} c_{A L, o} + N_{A} S-v_{o} c_{A L} + 0 = 0 \)

 

In an absorption process, the \( CO _{2} \) in the gas serves as the source, and \( CO _{2} \) dissolved in the liquid serves as the sink. The flux \( N_{A} \) from bulk gas phase to bulk liquid phase based on the overall liquid phase driving force is defined by

 

\( N_{A} = K_{L}\left(c_{A L}^{*} – c_{A L}\right) \text { with } c_{A L}^{*} = p_{A} / H \)

 

Combining these relationships gives

 

\( v_{o}\left(c_{A L, o} – c_{A L}\right) + K_{L}\left(\frac{p_{A}}{H} – c_{A L}\right) S = 0 \)

 

And solving for \( C_{A L} \), one obtains

 

\( c_{A L} = \frac{v_{o} c_{A L, o} + \frac{K_{L} S p_{A}}{H}}{v_{o} + K_{L} S} = \frac{v_{o} c_{A L, o} + K_{L} S c_{A L}^{*}}{v_{o} + K_{L} S} \)

 

The material balance equation requires the gas–liquid surface area, S, and bulk gas \( CO _{2} \) partial pressure, \( p_{A}\):

 

\( S = \pi d^{2} / 4 = \pi(3.0 m )^{2} / 4 = 7.07 m ^{2} \)

 

\( p_{A} = y_{A} P = (0.10)(2.0 atm ) = 0.20 atm \)

 

The inlet concentration of \( CO _{2} \) dissolved in the liquid, \( C_{A L, o} \), is 0. Finally the outlet concentration is

 

\( c_{A L} = \frac{\left(0.2 m ^{3} / h \right)(0) + \frac{\left(1.23 \times 10^{-5} m / s \right)\left(7.07 m ^{2}\right)(0.20 atm )}{29.5 m ^{3} \cdot atm / kgmole } \frac{1000 gmole }{1 kgmole }}{\left(0.2 m ^{3} / h \right)(1 h / 3600 s ) + \left(1.23 \times 10^{-5} m / s \right)\left(7.07 m ^{2}\right)} = 4.14 \frac{ gmole A }{ m ^{3}} \)

 

The maximum possible outlet concentration is \( c_{A L}^{*}\), determined by Henry’s law:

 

\( c_{A L}^{*} = \frac{p_{A}}{H} = \frac{0.20 atm }{\left(29.5 m ^{3} \cdot atm / kgmole \right)(1 kgmole / 1000 gmole )} = 6.78 \frac{ gmole A }{ m ^{3}} \)

 

In this analysis, \( c_{A L} \) will approach \( c_{A L}^{*}\) as the liquid flow rate \( v_{o} \) decreases or overall mass-transfer coefficient \( K_{L} \) increases.
