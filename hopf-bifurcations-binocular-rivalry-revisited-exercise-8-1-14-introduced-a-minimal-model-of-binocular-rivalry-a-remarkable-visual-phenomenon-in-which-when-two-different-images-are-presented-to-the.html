Hopf Bifurcations

(Binocular rivalry revisited) Exercise 8.1.14 introduced a minimal model of binocular rivalry, a remarkable visual phenomenon in which, when two different images are presented to the left and right eyes, the brain perceives only one image at a time. First one image wins, then the other, then the first again, and so on, with the winning image alternating every few seconds. The model studied in Exercise 8.1.14 could account for the complete suppression of one image by the other, but not for the rhythmic alternation between them. Now we extend that model to allow for the observed oscillations. (Many thanks to Bard Ermentrout for creating and sharing this exercise and the earlier Exercise 8.1.14.)

Let \(x_{1}\) and \(x_{2}\) denote the activity levels of the two populations of neurons coding for the two images, as in Exercise 8.1.14, but now assume the neurons get tired after winning a while, as adaptation builds up. The governing equations then become

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{x}_{1}=-x_{1}+F\left(I-b x_{2}-g y_{1}\right) \\&\dot{y}_{1}=\left(-y_{1}+x_{1}\right) / T \\&\dot{x}_{2}=-x_{2}+F\left(I-b x_{1}-g y_{2}\right) \\&\dot{y}_{2}=\left(-y_{2}+x_{2}\right) / T\end{aligned}\)

where the \(y\)-variables represent adaptation building up on a time scale \(T\) and provoking tiredness with strength \(g\) in their associated neuronal population. As in Exercise 8.1.14, the gain function is given by \(F(x)=1 /\left(1+e^{-x}\right), I\) is the strength of the input stimuli (the images), and \(b\) is the strength of the mutual antagonism between the neuronal populations. This is now a four-dimensional system, but its key stability properties can be inferred from two-dimensional calculations as follows.

a) Show that \(x_{1}^{*}=y_{1}^{*}=x_{2}^{*}=y_{2}^{*}=u\) is a fixed point for all choices of parameters and that \(u\) is uniquely defined.

b) Show that the stability matrix (the Jacobian) for the linearization about this fixed point has the form

\(\left(\begin{array}{cccc}-c_{1} & -c_{2} & -c_{3} & 0 \\d_{1} & -d_{1} & 0 & 0 \\-c_{3} & 0 & -c_{1} & -c_{2} \\0 & 0 & d_{1} & -d_{1}\end{array}\right)\)

We can write this in block-matrix form as

\(\left(\begin{array}{ll}A & B \\B & A\end{array}\right)\)

where \(A\) and \(B\) are \(2 \times 2\) matrices. Show that the four eigenvalues of the \(4 \times 4\) block matrix are given by the eigenvalues of \(A-B\) and \(A+B\).

c) Show that the eigenvalues of \(A+B\) are all negative, by considering the trace and determinant of this matrix.

d) Show that depending on the sizes of \(g\) and \(T\), the matrix \(A-B\) can have either a negative determinant (leading to a pitchfork bifurcation of \(u\) ) or a positive trace (resulting in a Hopf bifurcation).

e) Using a computer, show that the Hopf bifurcation can be supercritical; the resulting stable limit cycle mimics the oscillations we’re trying to explain. (Hint: When looking for trajectories that approach a stable limit cycle, be sure to use initial conditions that are different for populations 1 and 2 . In other words, break the symmetry by starting with \(x_{1} \neq x_{2}\) and \(y_{1} \neq y_{2}\).)

Solution

\(\begin{array}{ll}\dot{x}_{1}=-x_{1}+F\left(I-b x_{2}-g y_{1}\right) & \dot{y}_{1}=\frac{-y_{1}+x_{1}}{T} \\\dot{x}_{2}=-x_{2}+F\left(I-b x_{1}-g y_{2}\right) & \dot{y}_{2}=\frac{-y_{2}+x_{2}}{T} \\F(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}} &\end{array}\)

a)

Assuming that \(x_{1}^{*}=y_{1}^{*}=x_{2}^{*}=y_{2}^{*}=u\), substituting into the equations gives

\(\begin{array}{ll}\dot{x}_{1}=-u+F(I-b u-g u) & \dot{y}_{1}=\frac{-u+u}{T}=0 \\\dot{x}_{2}=-u+F(I-b u-g u) & \dot{y}_{2}=\frac{-u+u}{T}=0\end{array}\)

The \(\dot{y}_{1}\) and \(\dot{y}_{2}\) are satisfied, and what’s left are the \(\dot{x}_{1}\) and \(\dot{x}_{2}\) equations.

\(\begin{aligned}&0=-u+F(I-b u-g u) \\&u=\frac{1}{1+e^{-(I-b u-g u)}}\end{aligned}\)

The LHS and RHS are zero and positive respectively when \(u=0\). Also, the LHS and RHS are strictly increasing and decreasing respectively, with the LHS greater than the RHS maximum at \(u=0\) within a positive finite \(u\). Therefore the curves intersect and this symmetric solution is unique.

b)

\(A=\left(\begin{array}{llll}\frac{\partial \dot{x}_{1}}{\partial x_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{1}}{\partial y_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{1}}{\partial x_{2}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{1}}{\partial y_{2}} \\\frac{\partial \dot{y}_{1}}{\partial x_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{1}}{\partial y_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{1}}{\partial x_{2}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{1}}{\partial y_{2}} \\\frac{\partial \dot{x}_{2}}{\partial x_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{2}}{\partial y_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{2}}{\partial x_{2}} & \frac{\partial \dot{x}_{2}}{\partial y_{2}} \\\frac{\partial \dot{y}_{2}}{\partial x_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{2}}{\partial y_{1}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{2}}{\partial x_{2}} & \frac{\partial \dot{y}_{2}}{\partial y_{2}}\end{array}\right)\)

\(=\left(\begin{array}{cccc}-1 & \frac{-g e^{-I+b x_{2}+g y_{1}}}{\left(1+e^{\left.-I+b x_{2}+g y_{1}\right)^{2}}\right.} & \frac{-b e^{-I+g x_{2}+g y_{1}}}{\left(1+e^{\left.-I+b x_{2}+g y_{1}\right)^{2}}\right.} & 0 \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T} & 0 & 0 \\\frac{-b e^{-I+b x_{1}+g y_{2}}}{\left(1+e^{\left.-I+b x_{1}+g y_{2}\right)^{2}}\right.} & 0 & -1 & \frac{-g e^{-I+b x_{1}+g y_{2}}}{\left(1+e^{\left.-I+b x_{1}+g y_{2}\right)^{2}}\right.} \\0 & 0 & \frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}\end{array}\right)\)

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{e^{-I+b u+g u}}{\left(1+e^{-I+b u+g u}\right)^{2}} &=\frac{1+e^{-I+b u+g u}}{\left(1+e^{-I+b u+g u}\right)^{2}}-\frac{1}{\left(1+e^{-I+b u+g u}\right)^{2}} \\&=\frac{1}{1+e^{-I+b u+g u}}-\frac{1}{\left(1+e^{-I+b u+g u}\right)^{2}} \\&=F(I-b u-g u)-F^{2}(I-b u-g u) \\&=u-u^{2}\end{aligned}\)

\(A_{(u, u, u, u)}=\left(\begin{array}{cccc}-1 & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) & -b\left(u-u^{2}\right) & 0 \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T} & 0 & 0 \\-b\left(u-u^{2}\right) & 0 & -1 & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) \\0 & 0 & \frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}\end{array}\right)\)

Now to prove the determinant law

\(\left|\begin{array}{cccc}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & b & 0 \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & 0 & 0 \\b & 0 & a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\0 & 0 & a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|\)

(This is in no way a general proof but suffices for our purposes.) Expanding along the last column

\(-a_{1,2}\left|\begin{array}{ccc}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & b \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & 0 \\0 & 0 & a_{2,1}\end{array}\right|+a_{2,2}\left|\begin{array}{ccc}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & b \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & 0 \\b & 0 & a_{1,1}\end{array}\right|\)

And then the bottom row

\(\begin{gathered}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|+a_{2,2} b\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,2} & b \\a_{2,2} & 0\end{array}\right|+a_{1,1} a_{2,2}\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right| \\-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|-\left(a_{2,2} b\right)^{2}+a_{1,1} a_{2,2}\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|\end{gathered}\)

\(\begin{gathered}\left(a_{1,1} a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right)\left|\begin{array}{ll}a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|-\left(a_{2,2} b\right)^{2} \\\left(a_{1,1} a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right)^{2}-\left(a_{2,2} b\right)^{2}\end{gathered}\)

\(\begin{gathered}\left(\left(a_{1,1} a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right)+a_{2,2} b\right)\left(\left(a_{1,1} a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right)-a_{2,2} b\right) \\\left(\left(a_{1,1}+b\right) a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right)\left(\left(a_{1,1}-b\right) a_{2,2}-a_{1,2} a_{2,1}\right) \\\left|\begin{array}{cc}a_{1,1}+b & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|\left|\begin{array}{cc}a_{1,1}-b & a_{1,2} \\a_{2,1} & a_{2,2}\end{array}\right|\end{gathered}\)

Therefore

\(\left|\begin{array}{ll}A & B \\B & A\end{array}\right|=|A+B||A-B|\)

We can use this to compute the eigenvalues of the \(4 \times 4\) matrix.

\(\left|\begin{array}{cccc}-1-\lambda & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) & -b\left(u-u^{2}\right) & 0 \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}-\lambda & 0 & 0 \\-b\left(u-u^{2}\right) & 0 & -1-\lambda & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) \\0 & 0 & \frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}-\lambda\end{array}\right|=0\)

\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}-1-b\left(u-u^{2}\right)-\lambda & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}-\lambda\end{array}\right|\left|\begin{array}{cc}-1+b\left(u-u^{2}\right)-\lambda & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}-\lambda\end{array}\right|=0\)

c)

Looking at the \(A+B\) matrix, we can use that the determinant of a matrix is the product of the eigenvalues and the trace of a matrix is the sum of the eigenvalues.

\(\left|\begin{array}{cc}-1-b\left(u-u^{2}\right) & -g\left(u-u^{2}\right) \\\frac{1}{T} & -\frac{1}{T}\end{array}\right|\)

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta &=\left(-1-b\left(u-u^{2}\right)\right)\left(-\frac{1}{T}\right)-\left(-g\left(u-u^{2}\right)\right) \frac{1}{T} \\&=\frac{1+(b+g)\left(u-u^{2}\right)}{T}=\lambda_{1} \lambda_{2}\end{aligned}\)

Going back to the definition of \(u\)

\(u=F(I-b u-g u)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-(I-b u-g u)}}\)

We also know that \(0<u<1\) because \(0<\left.F\right|_{u=0}<1\) and \(F\) is strictly decreasing. Therefore the intersection occurs for \(0<u<1\). Hence \(u-u^{2}>0\) and

\(0<\frac{1+(b+g)\left(u-u^{2}\right)}{T}=\lambda_{1} \lambda_{2}\)

From this we know that \(\lambda_{1}\) and \(\lambda_{2}\) are both positive or both negative.

Then from the trace

\(\begin{aligned}&\tau=-1-b\left(u-u^{2}\right)-\frac{1}{T} \\&0>-1-b\left(u-u^{2}\right)-\frac{1}{T}=\lambda_{1}+\lambda_{2}\end{aligned}\)

Therefore \(\lambda_{1}\) and \(\lambda_{2}\) are both negative.

d)

However, for the \(A-B\) matrix

\(\begin{gathered}\Delta=\left(-1+b\left(u-u^{2}\right)\right)\left(-\frac{1}{T}\right)-\left(-g\left(u-u^{2}\right)\right) \frac{1}{T}=\frac{1+(g-b)\left(u-u^{2}\right)}{T} \\\tau=-1+b\left(u-u^{2}\right)-\frac{1}{T}\end{gathered}\)

The determinant is positive or negative if \(g>b\) or \(b>g\) respectively, and the trace can be positive or negative depending on the relative sizes of \(b\) and \(T\).

From these facts about the eigenvalues, we can cause either a pitchfork bifurcation or a Hopf bifurcation at \((u, u, u, u)\) by varying \(g\) and \(T\) respectively. If we start with a negative trace, then by changing \(g\), the system will undergo a pitchfork bifurcation at \((u, u, u, u)\) when the determinant switches sign. If we start with a positive determinant, changing \(T\) will cause the system to undergo a Hopf bifurcation at \((u, u, u, u)\) when the trace switches sign.

e)

For small \(T\), the variables settle to the stable fixed point \((u, u, u, u)\).

Increasing \(T\) causes the stable limit cycle to appear.

And increasing \(T\) further enlarges the size of the limit cycle.

We can’t plot the limit cycle since it’s 4-dimensional, but we can plot the individual variables in time. We used \(z\) for the horizontal label because all four of the variables \(x_{1}, y_{2}, x_{3}\), and \(y_{4}\) have indistinguishable graphs. So not only does the system tend toward a symmetric fixed point, the variables even follow the limit cycle symmetrically.
