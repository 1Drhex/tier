Guest Question.

The uniform 7-m steel shaft has a mass of 200 kg and is supported by a balland- socket joint at A in the horizontal floor. The ball end B rests against the smooth vertical walls as shown. Compute the forces exerted by the walls and the floor on the ends of the shaft.

Admin
Solution

The free-body diagram of the shaft is first drawn where the contact forces acting on the shaft at B are shown normal to the wall surfaces. In addition to the weight W = mg = 200(9.81) = 1962 N, the force exerted by the floor on the ball joint at A is represented by its x-, y-, and z-components. These components are shown in their correct physical sense, as should be evident from the requirement that A be held in place. The vertical position of B is found from \(7=\sqrt{2^{2} +6^{2}+h^{2} } , h=3 m. \) Right-handed coordinate axes are assigned as shown.

Vector solution. We will use A as a moment center to eliminate reference to the forces at A. The position vectors needed to compute the moments about A are

\(r_{AG} =-1i-3j+1.5k\) m and \(r_{AB} =-2i-6j+3k\) m

where the mass center G is located halfway between A and B.
The vector moment equation gives

\([\sum{M_{A} }=0 ] r_{AB} \times (B_{x}+B_{y} )+r_{AG} \times W=0\)

 

\((-2i-6j+3k)\times (B_{x} i+B_{y}j )+ (-i-3j+1.5k)\times (-1962k) =0\)

 

\(\left|\begin{matrix} i & j & k \\ -2 & -6 & 3 \\ B_{x} & B_{y} & 0 \end{matrix} \right| +\left|\begin{matrix} i & j & k \\-1 & -3 & 1.5 \\ 0 & 0 & -1962 \end{matrix} \right| =0\)

 

\((-3B_{y}+5890 )i+(3B_{x}-1962 )j+(-2B_{y} +6B_{x} )k=0\)

 

Equating the coefficients of i, j, and k to zero and solving give

\(B_{x} =654 N and B_{y} =1962 N \)

The forces at A are easily determined by

\( [\sum{F=0} ] (654-A_{x} )i+(1962-A_{y} )j+(-1962+A_{z} )k=0 \)

and \( A_{x} =654 N A_{y} =1962 N A_{z} =1962N\)

Finally, \(A=\sqrt{A^{2}_{x} +A^{2}_{y}+A^{2}_{z}}\)

\(=\sqrt{(654)^{2} +(1962)^{2} +(1962)^{2}} =2850 N\)

 

Scalar solution. Evaluating the scalar moment equations about axes through A parallel, respectively, to the x- and y-axes, gives

\([\sum{M_{A_{x} } }=0] 1962(3)-3B_{y} =0 B_{y} =1962 N\)
\([\sum{M_{A_{y} } }=0] -1962(1)+3B_{x} =0 B_{x} =654 N\)

The force equations give, simply,

\([\sum{F_{x} }=0 ] -A_{x} +654=0 A_{x} =654N\)
\([\sum{F_{y} }=0 ] -A_{y} +1962=0 A_{y} =1962 N\)
\([\sum{F_{z} }=0 ] A_{z} -1962=0 A_{z} =1962 N\)

 

Helpful Hints
1) We could, of course, assign all of the unknown components of force in the positive mathematical sense, in which case \( A_{x} and A_{y}\) would turn out to be negative upon computation. The freebody diagram describes the physical situation, so it is generally preferable to show the forces in their correct physical senses wherever possible.
2) Note that the third equation \( -2B_{y}+6 B_{x}=0 \) merely checks the results of the first two equations. This result could be anticipated from the fact that an equilibrium system of forces concurrent with a line requires only two moment equations (Category 2 under Categories of Equilibrium).
3) We observe that a moment sum about an axis through A parallel to the z-axis merely gives us \( 6B_{x}-2 B_{y}=0 \) , which serves only as a check as noted previously. Alternatively we could have first obtained \(A_{z}\)from\(\sum{F_{z} }=0\) and then taken our moment equations about axes through B to obtain\( A_{x}\) and \(A_{y}.\)
