Guest Question.

(a) Consider the Zener diode circuit shown in Fig. 6.30a where D1 and D2 are two Zener diodes with breakdown voltages, forward diode resistances, cut-in voltages as VZ1 , Rf1 , Vr1 and VZ2 , Rf2 , Vr2 respectively. Assume that under reverse bias condition of operations, the diodes act as open switches under normal reverse-biased operations but the D1 and D2 have finite resistances RZ1 and RZ2 in their breakdown regions, respectively. Find the expression for the output voltage vo(t) of the circuit corresponding to all possible values of an arbitrary input signal vi(t).

(b) Assuming ideal Zener diodes with VZ1 = 10 V, VZ2 = 15 V, Vr1 = 0.3 V, Vr2 = 0.6 V and Rf1 = Rf2 = RZ1 = RZ1 = 0, sketch the transfer curve vo(t) versus vi (t) for part (a).

Admin
Solution

(a) Case – I: For vi > 0: In this case, the D1 diode is reverse biased and D2 is forward biased. Assume that D1 is working in the normal reversed-bias condition and voltage appearing across the diode is V1. Since, the diode D1 works as an open switch in this region implying the current i = 0 in the circuit and D2 works as a forward- biased diode, the circuit of Fig. 6. 30a can be simplified as shown in Fig. 6.30b. Now, applying KVL in the input loop, we can obtain

\(V_{1}=v_{i}(t)-V_{\gamma 1}\)

Clearly, for V1 = vi (t) V2 < VZ1 (i.e. vi (t) < (VZ1 + V2)) the diode D1 works as an open switch and hence the output voltage is vo(t) = vi (t). However, for V1 = vi (t) – V2 > VZ1 (i.e. vi (t) > (VZ1 + V2)), the diode D1 works in the break- down region and behaves as a fixed voltage source VZ1 in series with RZ1 but D2 still works as forward-biased diode with cut-in voltage source V2 in series with diode resistance Rf2 as shown in Fig. 6.30c. Applying KVL in the input and output loops of the circuit of Fig. 6.30c and solving the resultant equations, we can obtain the current flowing through the diodes and the output voltage vo(t) as

\(i=\frac{v_{i}-V_{z1}-V_{\gamma 2}}{R+R_{Z1}+R_{f2}} \)

and

\(v_{o}(t)=(V_{Z1}+V_{\gamma 2})+i(R_{z1}+R_{f2}) \)

\(=(V_{Z1}+V_{\gamma 2})+\left\lgroup\frac{R_{Z1}+R_{f2}}{R+R_{Z1}+R_{f2}} \right\rgroup (v_{i}-V_{Z1}-V_{\gamma 2}) \)

Thus, for all values of the input voltage vi (t) > 0, the output voltage can be expressed as

\(v_{o}(t)= \begin{cases} v_{i}(t); for &0 < v_{i}(t)<(V_{Z1}+V_{\gamma 2}) \\ (V_{Z1}+V_{\gamma 2})+\left\lgroup\frac{R_{Z1}+R_{f2}}{R+R_{Z1}+R_{f2}} \right\rgroup (v_{i}-V_{Z1}-V_{\gamma 2}); for & v_{i}(t) > (V_{Z1}+V_{\gamma 2}) \end{cases} \)

Case-II: For vi (t) < 0: In this case, D1 works as a forward-biased diode whereas D2 works as a the reverse-biased diode acting as an open switch as considered earlier for D2. In order to determine the input voltage for transition from the normal reverse bias (i.e. open switch) to breakdown region of D2, we may consider the circuit shown in Fig. 6.30d similar to Fig. 6.30c where the voltage V2 is assumed to be the voltage drop across the diode D2. Since, current i = 0, we obtain

\(V_{2}=v_{i}(t)-V_{\gamma 1}\)

Clearly, for V2< –VZ2 (i.e. vi (t) < –(VZ2 + V1)), D2 works in the breakdown region and for V2 >–VZ2 (i.e.2(VZ2 + V2) < vi (t) < 0) it works as an open switch implying vo (t) = vi (t). Note that D1 works as a forward-biased diode for all values of the input voltage vi (t) < 0. Thus, the equivalent circuit of Fig. 6.30a for vi (t) < –(VZ2 + V1) can be drawn as shown in Fig. 6.30e. Applying KVL in the input and output loop of the circuit and using the result for –(VZ2 + V1) < vi (t) < 0, the output voltage can be expressed for vi (t) < 0 as

\(v_{o}(t)= \begin{cases} v_{i}(t); for &-(V_{Z2}+V_{\gamma 1}) < v_{i}(t)<0 \\ -(V_{Z2}+V_{\gamma 1})+\left\lgroup\frac{R_{Z2}+R_{f1}}{R+R_{Z2}+R_{f1}} \right\rgroup (v_{i}+V_{Z2}+V_{\gamma 1}); for & v_{i}(t) < -(V_{Z2}+V_{\gamma 1}) \end{cases} \)

Note that (vi +VZ2+V1) < 0; for vi (t) <–(VZ2 + V1) which results in vo (t) < 0 for all values of vi (t) < 0.

(b) Using VZ1= 10 V, VZ2 = 15 V, V1 = 3 V, V2 = 0.6 V and Rf1 = Rf1 = RZ1 = RZ2 = 0 in the expressions of the output voltage, vo(t) can be given by

\(v_{o}(t)= \begin{cases} -15.3V & for v_{i}(t)<-15.3V \\ v_{i}(t); & for -15.3V <v_{i}(t)<10.6 V \\ 10.6 V ; & for v_{i}(t)>10.6 V \end{cases} \)

The transfer characteristics is shown in Fig. 6.30f.
