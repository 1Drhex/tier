Guest Question.

Find the response variance and covariance values for an SDF oscillator with a mean-zero, stationary, delta-correlated excitation with autospectral density \( S_{0} \) .

Admin
Solution

The symmetric state-space description of the system is Eq. 9.5

\( A\dot{\vec{Y}} (t)+B\vec{Y}(t)=\vec{Q} (t)\)

with \( n_{Y}=2 \) :

\( \vec{Y}(t)=\left(\begin{matrix} X(t) \\\dot{X}(t) \end{matrix} \right) , A=\left(\begin{matrix} -k & 0 \\ 0 & m \end{matrix} \right) , B=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & k \\ k & c \end{matrix} \right) , \vec{Q} (t)=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ F(t) \end{matrix} \right) \)

Thus, the state-space equation for the covariance, from Eq. 9.17

\(\frac{d}{dt} K _{YY}(t,t)+A^{-1}BK _{YY}(t,t)+K _{YY}(t,t)(A^{-1}B)^{T}=2\pi A^{-1}S_{0}(t)(A^{-1})^{T} \)

is

\( \frac{d}{dt}K_{YY}(t,t)+\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -1 \\ k/m & c/m \end{matrix} \right) K_{YY}(t,t)+K_{YY}(t,t)\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & k/m \\ -1 & c/m \end{matrix} \right)=\frac{2\pi S_{0}}{m^{2}}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 \\0 &1 \end{matrix} \right) \)

Using the change of variables of \( k=m\omega ^{2}_{0}, c=2m\zeta \omega _{0} \) , and \( \omega _{d}=\omega _{0}(1-\zeta ^{2})^{1/2} \) , eigenanalysis of \( A^{-1}B \) gives eigenvalues of \( \lambda _{11}=\zeta \omega _{0}-i\omega _{d} \) and \( \lambda _{22}=\lambda ^{\ast }_{11} \) , with the matrix of eigenvectors being

\( \theta =\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 1 \\ -\lambda _{11} & -\lambda _{22} \end{matrix} \right) \)

Also, \( \hat{A} \equiv \theta ^{T}A\theta \) has nonzero elements of \( \hat{A}_{11}=-2im\omega _{d}\lambda _{11} \) and \( \hat{A}_{22}=2im\omega _{d}\lambda _{22} \) and

\( \theta ^{-1}=\hat{A}^{-1}\theta ^{T}A =\frac{i}{2\omega _{d}}\left(\begin{matrix} -\lambda _{22} & -1 \\ \lambda _{11} & 1 \end{matrix} \right) \)

Using the transformation \( \vec{Y}(t)=\theta \vec{Z} (t) \) and \( \vec{P}(t)=\theta ^{-1}A^{-1}\vec{Q}(t) \) from Eqs. 9.24

\(\vec{Y}(t)=\theta \vec{Z}(t)\)

and 9.26

\(\vec{P}(t)=\theta ^{-1}A^{-1} \vec{Q}(t)\)

allows us to use Eqs. 9.28

\([K_{ZZ}]_{jl}=\frac{2\pi [S_{PP}]_{jl}}{\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l}} \)

9.29

\(\vec{\mu }_{Y}=\theta \vec{\mu }_{Z}\equiv \theta \lambda ^{-1}\theta ^{-1}A^{-1}\vec{\mu }_{Q}=B^{-1}\vec{\mu }_{Q}\), \(K_{YY}\theta K_{ZZ}\theta ^{T}\)

and 9.31

\(K_{ZZ}(t,t)=\exp [-(t-t_{0})\lambda ]K_{ZZ}(t_{0},t{0})\exp [-(t-t_{0})\lambda ]+ 2\pi \int_{t_{0}}^{t}{\exp [-(t-s)\lambda ]S_{PP} (s)\exp [-(t-s)\lambda ] } ds \)

for the response covadance. The excitation spectral density matrix in the \( \left\{\vec{Z}(t)\right\} \) equations is

\( S_{PP}=\frac{S_{0}}{m^{2}}\theta ^{-1}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right) (\theta ^{-1})^{T}=\frac{S_{0}}{4m^{2}\omega ^{2}_{d}}\left(\begin{matrix} -1 &1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right) \)

For stationary response we use Eq. 9.28 to obtain

\( K_{ZZ}=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{4m^{2}\omega ^{2}_{d}} \left(\begin{matrix} -1/\lambda _{11} & 1/(2\zeta \omega _{0}) \\ 1/(2\zeta \omega _{0}) & -1/\lambda _{22} \end{matrix} \right) \)

And

\( K_{YY}=\theta K_{ZZ}\theta ^{T}=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{2m^{2}} \left(\begin{matrix} 1/(\zeta \omega ^{3}_{0}) & 0 \\ 0 & 1/(\zeta \omega _{0}) \end{matrix} \right) =\pi S_{0}\left(\begin{matrix} 1/(ck) & 0 \\ 0 & 1/(cm) \end{matrix} \right) \)

The (1,1) and (2,2) elements, respectively, confirm the values obtained in Chapters 5 and 6 for \( \sigma _{X} \) and \( \sigma _{\dot{X}} \) , and the off-diagonal terms confirm the known fact that \( X(t) \) and \( \dot{X}(t) \) are uncorrelated random variables in stationary response.

Because the dimension of the system is small, it is also feasible to obtain these results without uncoupling the equations. In particular, the (1,1), (2,1), and (2,2) elements of the equation \( A^{-1}BK_{YY}(t,t)+K_{YY}(t,t)(A^{-1}B)^{T}=2\pi A^{-1}S_{0} \) are

\( -2K_{X\dot{X}}(t,t)=0 \) \( -K_{\dot{X}\dot{X}}(t)+\frac{k}{m} K_{XX}(t)+\frac{c}{m}K_{X\dot{X}}(t)=0 \) \( 2\frac{k}{m} K_{X\dot{X}}(t)+2\frac{c}{m}K_{\dot{X}\dot{X}}(t)=\frac{2\pi S_{0}}{m^{2}} \)

and solution of these three simultaneous equations gives the results obtained by matrix manipulation.

Let us now presume that we are given an initial condition of \( K_{YY}(t_{0},t_{0}) \) at some specific time \( t_{0} \) . This will result in a nonstationary \( K_{YY}(t,t) \) matrix, and in order to find this nonstationary solution we will need to use the matrix exponential.

Using the diagonalized equations for \( Z(t)=\theta ^{-1}Y(t) \) and the information that \( S_{PP} \) is independent of time, we find from Eq. 9.31 that

\( [K_{ZZ}(t,t)]_{jl}=\exp [-(t-t_{0})(\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l})][K_{ZZ}(t_{0},t_{0})]_{jl}+2\pi [S_{PP}]_{jl}\int_{t_{0}}^{t}{\exp [-(t-s)(\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l})]} ds \)

Note that \( K_{ZZ}(t_{0},t_{0})=\theta ^{-1}K_{YY}(t_{0},t_{0})(\theta ^{-1})^{T} \) in this expression. The integration may now be performed to obtain

\( [K_{ZZ}(t,t)]_{jl}=\exp [-(t-t_{0})(\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l})][K_{ZZ}(t_{0},t_{0})]_{jl}+2\pi [S_{PP}]_{jl}\frac{1-\exp [-(t-t_{0})(\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l})]}{\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l}} \)

The real parts of both eigenvalues are positive, giving each element of \( K_{ZZ}(t,t) \) an exponential decay of its initial condition, plus an exponential growth toward its previously found stationary value of \( 2\pi [S_{PP}]_{jl}/(\lambda _{j}+\lambda _{l}) \) . Both the decaying and the growing terms, though, also contain trigonometric terms because of the

imaginary parts of the eigenvalues.

For the special case in which the initial condition is \(K_{YY}(0,0)=0\), the results are

\( \sigma ^{2}_{X}(t)=[K_{YY}(t,t)]_{11} \) \( =\frac{\pi S_{0}}{2m^{2}\zeta \omega ^{3}_{0}} \left(1-e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t}\left[\frac{\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}+\frac{\zeta \omega _{0}}{\omega _{d}}\sin (2\omega _{d}t)-\frac{\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}\cos (2\omega _{d}t) \right] \right) \)

Cov \( [X(t),\dot{X}(t)]=[K_{YY}(t,t)]_{12}=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{2m^{2}\omega ^{2}_{d}}e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t} [1-\cos (2\omega _{d}t)] \)

And

\( \sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}(t)=[K_{YY}(t,t)]_{22} \) \( =\frac{\pi S_{0}}{2m^{2}\zeta \omega _{0}}\left(1-e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t}\left[\frac{\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}-\frac{\zeta \omega _{0}}{\omega _{d}} \sin (2\omega _{d}t)-\frac{\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}\cos (2\omega _{d}t) \right] \right) \)

These results are identical to those in Chapter 5, where they were obtained by the indirect method of using the Duhamel integral time-domain solution of the original equation of motion.
