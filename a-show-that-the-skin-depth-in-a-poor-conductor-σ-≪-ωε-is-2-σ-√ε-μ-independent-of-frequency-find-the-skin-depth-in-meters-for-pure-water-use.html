Guest Question.

(a) Show that the skin depth in a poor conductor \((\sigma \ll \omega \epsilon) \text { is }(2 / \sigma) \sqrt{\epsilon / \mu}\) (independent of frequency). Find the skin depth (in meters) for (pure) water. (Use the static values of \(\epsilon, \mu, \text { and } \sigma\) ; your answers will be valid, then, only at relatively low frequencies.)

(b) Show that the skin depth in a good conductor \( (\sigma \gg \omega \epsilon) \text { is } \lambda / 2 \pi\) (where λ is the wavelength in the conductor). Find the skin depth (in nanometers) for a typical metal \( \left(\sigma \approx 10^{7}(\Omega m )^{-1}\right) \text { in the visible range }\left(\omega \approx 10^{15} / s \right) \text {, assuming } \epsilon \approx \epsilon_{0}\) and \(\mu \approx \mu_{0}\) . Why are metals opaque?

(c) Show that in a good conductor the magnetic field lags the electric field by 45◦, and find the ratio of their amplitudes. For a numerical example, use the “typical metal” in part (b).

\(k \equiv \omega \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \mu}{2}}\left[\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon \omega}\right)^{2}}+1\right]^{1 / 2}, \quad \kappa \equiv \omega \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \mu}{2}}\left[\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon \omega}\right)^{2}}-1\right]^{1 / 2}\) (9.126)

\(\kappa \cong \omega \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \mu}{2}}\left[1+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon \omega}\right)^{2}-1\right]^{1 / 2}=\omega \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \mu}{2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon \omega}=\frac{\sigma}{2} \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\epsilon}}\) .

So (Eq. 9.128) \(d=\frac{1}{\kappa} \cong \frac{2}{\sigma} \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon}{\mu}}\) . qed

\(d \equiv \frac{1}{\kappa}\) (9.128)

(b) In this case \((\sigma / \epsilon \omega)^{2} \text { dominates, so (Eq. 9.126) } k \cong \kappa\) , and hence (Eqs. 9.128 and 9.129)

\(\lambda=\frac{2 \pi}{k}, \quad v=\frac{\omega}{k}, \quad n=\frac{c k}{\omega}\) (9.129)

Meanwhile \(\kappa \cong \omega \sqrt{\frac{\epsilon \mu}{2}} \sqrt{\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon \omega}}=\sqrt{\frac{\omega \mu \sigma}{2}}=\sqrt{\frac{\left(10^{15}\right)\left(4 \pi \times 10^{-7}\right)\left(10^{7}\right)}{2}}=8 \times 10^{7} ; \quad d=\frac{1}{\kappa}=\frac{1}{8 \times 10^{7}}=1.3 \times 10^{-8}=13 nm\) . So the fields do not penetrate far into a metal—which is what accounts for their opacity.
