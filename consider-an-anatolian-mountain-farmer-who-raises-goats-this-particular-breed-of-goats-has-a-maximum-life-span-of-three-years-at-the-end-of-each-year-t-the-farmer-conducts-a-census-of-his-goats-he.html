Guest Question.

Consider an Anatolian mountain farmer who raises goats. This particular breed of goats has a maximum life span of three years. At the end of each year t, the farmer conducts a census of his goats. He counts the number of young goats j(t), born in the year t; the middle-aged goats m(t), born the year before; and the old ones a(t), born in the year t – 2. The state of the herd can be described by the vector \(\vec{x}(t)=\left[\begin{array}{c}j(t) \\m(t) \\a(t)\end{array}\right]\).

Suppose that for this breed and this environment the evolution of the system can be modeled by the equation

\(\vec{x}(t+1)=A \vec{x}(t) . \quad \text { where } \quad A=\left[\begin{array}{lll}0 & 0.95 & 0.6 \\0.8 & 0 & 0 \\0 & 0.5 & 0\end{array}\right]\).

For example, m (t + 1) = 0.8 j{ t ), meaning that 80% of the young goats will survive to the next census. We leave it as an exorcise to the reader to interpret the other 3 nonzero entries of A as reproduction and survival rates.

the populations be after t years, according to this model? What will happen in the long term?

We are told that the eigenvalues of A are \(\lambda_{1}=1, \lambda_{2}=-0.6, \text { and }\lambda_{3}=-0.4\).

Admin
Solution

To answer these questions, we have to find an eigenbasis for A. (See Theorem 7.1.3: {\(\vec{x}(t)=c_{1} \lambda_{1}^{t} \vec{v}_{1}+c_{2} \lambda_{2}^{t} \vec{v}_{2}+\cdots+c_{n} \lambda_{n}^{t} \vec{v}_{n}\)})
By Theorem 7.3.5: {An rt x n matrix with n distinct eigenvalues If an n×n matrix A has n distinct eigenvalues, then there exists an eigenbasis for A. We can construct an eigenbasis by finding an eigenvector for each eigenvalue.}, we know that such an eigenbasis does exist, since the 3×3 matrix A has three distinct eigenvalues. Let’s find an eigenvector for each of the eigenvalues. With a little experimentation, we can find the Kyle numbers in each case (or, alternatively, use rref):

. 5 4 2

\(E_{1}=\operatorname{ker}\left[\begin{array}{rrr}-1 & 0.95 & 0.6 \\0.8 & -1 & 0 \\0 & 0.5 & -1\end{array}\right]=\operatorname{span}\left[\begin{array}{l}5 \\4 \\2\end{array}\right]\).

\(E_{-0.6}=\operatorname{span}\left[\begin{array}{r}9 \\-12 \\10\end{array}\right], \quad E_{-0.4}=\operatorname{span}\left[\begin{array}{r}-2 \\4 \\-5\end{array}\right]\).

We have constructed an eigenbasis:\(\vec{v}_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l}5 \\4 \\2\end{array}\right], \vec{v}_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{r}9 \\-12 \\10\end{array}\right], \vec{v}_{3}=\left[\begin{array}{r}-2 \\4 \\-5\end{array}\right]\).

Next, we need to express the initial state vector\(\vec{x}_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{l}j_{0} \\m_{0} \\a_{0}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l}750 \\200 \\200\end{array}\right]\)

as a linear combination of the eigenvectors: \(\vec{x}_{0}=c_{1} \vec{v}_{1}+c_{2} \vec{v}_{2}+c_{3} \vec{v}_{3}\). A somewhat tedious computation reveals that:

\(c_{1}=100, c_{2}=50, c_{3}=100\).

Now that we know the eigenvalues \(\lambda_{i}\), the eigenvectors \(\vec{v}_{i}\), and the coefficients \(c_{i}\) , we are ready to write down the solution:

\(\vec{x}(t)=A^{t} \vec{x}_{0}=c_{1} A^{\prime} \vec{v}_{1}+c_{2} A^{t} \vec{v}_{2}+c_{3} A^{t} \vec{v}_{3}=c_{1} \lambda_{1}^{t} \vec{v}_{1}+c_{2} \lambda_{2}^{t} \vec{v}_{2}+c_{3} \lambda_{3}^{t} \vec{v}_{3}\)

\(=100\left[\begin{array}{l}5 \\4 \\2\end{array}\right]+50(-0.6)^{t}\left[\begin{array}{r}9 \\-12 \\10\end{array}\right]+100(-0.4)^{t}\left[\begin{array}{r}-2 \\4 \\-5\end{array}\right]\).

The individual populations are:

\(j(t)=500+450(-0.6)^{t}-200(-0.4)^{t}\),

\(m(t)=400-600(-0.6)^{t}+400(-0.4)^{t}\),

\(a(t)=200+500(-0.6)^{t}-500(-0.4)^{t}\).

In the long run, the populations approach the equilibrium values
j = 500, m = 400, a = 200.
