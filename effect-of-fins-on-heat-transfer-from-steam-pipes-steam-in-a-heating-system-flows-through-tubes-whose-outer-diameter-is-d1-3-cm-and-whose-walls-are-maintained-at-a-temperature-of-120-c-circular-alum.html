Guest Question.

Effect of Fins on Heat Transfer from Steam Pipes

Steam in a heating system flows through tubes whose outer diameter is \( D_{1} = 3 cm \) and whose walls are maintained at a temperature of \( 120^{\circ} C\). Circular aluminum fins \( \left(k = 180 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right) \) of outer diameter \( D_{2} = 6 cm \) and constant thickness t = 2 mm are attached to the tube, as shown in Fig. The space between the fins is 3 mm, and thus there are 200 fins per meter length of the tube. Heat is transferred to the surrounding air at \( T_{\infty} = 25^{\circ} C\), with a combined heat transfer coefficient of \( h = 60 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \). Determine the increase in heat transfer from the tube per meter of its length as a result of adding fins.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION Circular aluminum fins are to be attached to the tubes of a heating system. The increase in heat transfer from the tubes per unit length as a result of adding fins is to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Steady operating conditions exist. 2 The heat transfer coefficient is uniform over the entire fin surfaces. 3 Thermal conductivity is constant. 4 Heat transfer by radiation is negligible.

Properties The thermal conductivity of the fins is given to be \( k = 180 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C\).

Analysis In the case of no fins, heat transfer from the tube per meter of its length is determined from Newton’s law of cooling to be

\( A_{\text {no fin }} = \pi D_{1} L=\pi(0.03 m )(1 m ) = 0.0942 m ^{2} \)

 

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {no fin }} = h A_{\text {no fin }}\left(T_{b} – T_{\infty}\right) \)

 

\( = \left(60 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(0.0942 m ^{2}\right)(120 – 25){ }^{\circ} C = 537 W\)

 

The efficiency of the circular fins attached to a circular tube is plotted in Fig. Noting that \( L = \frac{1}{2}\left(D_{2} – D_{1}\right) = \frac{1}{2}(0.06 – 0.03) = 0.015 m \) in this case, we have

\( \left.\begin{array}{l} \frac{r_{2}+\frac{1}{2} t}{r_{1}}=\frac{\left(0.03+\frac{1}{2} \times 0.002\right) m }{0.015 m }= 2.07 \\ \left(L+\frac{1}{2} t\right) \sqrt{\frac{h}{k t}}=\left(0.015+\frac{1}{2} \times 0.002\right) m \times \sqrt{\frac{60 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C }{\left(180 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)(0.002 m )}}=0.207 \end{array}\right\} \eta_{\text {fin }=0.95} \)

 

\( A_{\text {fin }} = 2 \pi\left(r_{2}^{2} – r_{1}^{2}\right) + 2 \pi r_{2} t \)

 

\( = 2 \pi\left[(0.03 m )^{2} – (0.015 m )^{2}\right] + 2 \pi(0.03 m )(0.002 m ) \)

 

\( = 0.00462 m ^{2} \)

 

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {fin }}=\eta_{\text {fin }} \dot{Q}_{\text {fin, } \max }=\eta_{\text {fin }} h A_{\text {fin }}\left(T_{b}-T_{\infty}\right) \)

 

\( = 0.95\left(60 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(0.00462 m ^{2}\right)(120 – 25){ }^{\circ} C = 25.0 W \)

 

Heat transfer from the unfinned portion of the tube is

\( A_{\text {unfin }}=\pi D_{1} S=\pi(0.03 m )(0.003 m )=0.000283 m ^{2} \)

 

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {unfin }} = h A_{\text {unfin }}\left(T_{b} – T_{\infty}\right) \)

 

\( = \left(60 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(0.000283 m ^{2}\right)(120 – 25){ }^{\circ} C = 1.60 W\)

 

Noting that there are 200 fins and thus 200 interfin spacings per meter length of the tube, the total heat transfer from the finned tube becomes

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {total fin }} = n\left(\dot{Q}_{\text {fin }}+\dot{Q}_{\text {unfin }}\right) = 200 (25.0 + 1.6) W = 5320 W \)

 

Therefore, the increase in heat transfer from the tube per meter of its length as a result of the addition of fins is

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {inccese }} = \dot{Q}_{\text {total, fin }} – \dot{Q}_{ no fin }\)

 

\( = 5320 – 537 = 4783 W \) (per m tube length)

 

Discussion The overall effectiveness of the finned tube is

\( \varepsilon_{\text {fin, overall }} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text {total, in }}}{\dot{Q}_{\text {total, no fin }}}=\frac{5320 W }{537 W } = 9.9 \)

 

That is, the rate of heat transfer from the steam tube increases by a factor of almost 10 as a result of adding fins. This explains the widespread use of finned surfaces.
