Guest Question.

Develop three possible idealized models of an elevator cable system for the purpose of estimating its natural frequency or frequencies. The models considered are a one-mass, a one-mass with spring correction, and a threemass system. The system parameters are given:

weight of car \(W_{\text {car }}=1000 lb\)

length of cable \(l=750 ft\)

cable weight/length \(\gamma=1 lb / ft\)

cable cross-sectional area \(A=1 in ^{2}\)

cable modulus of elasticity \(E=4.5 \times 10^{7} psi\)

Admin
Solution

Solution: For the one-mass model, shown in Figure 6.15,

the total spring stiffness is

 

\(k_{\text {tot }}=\frac{E A}{l}=\frac{4.5 \times 10^{7} psi \cdot 1 in ^{2}}{750 ft }=6.0 \times 10^{4} \frac{ lb }{ ft }\)

 

The weight of the cable is

 

\(W_{\text {cable }}=\gamma l=1 \frac{ lb }{ ft } \cdot 750 ft =750 lb\)

 

For the one-mass model, the cable weight is added to the car weight in the calculation of the natural frequency of oscillation,

 

\(\begin{aligned}W_{\text {total }} &=W_{\text {car }}+W_{\text {cable }} \\&=1000 lb +750 lb =1750 lb\end{aligned}\)

 

The natural frequency for the one-mass model is then

 

\(\begin{aligned}\omega_{n} &=\sqrt{\frac{k_{\text {tot }}}{m_{\text {tot }}}}=\sqrt{\frac{k g}{W_{\text {total }}}} \\&=\sqrt{\frac{\left(6.0 \times 10^{4} \frac{1 b}{f t}\right)\left(32.2 \frac{ ft }{ s ^{2}}\right)}{(1750 lb )}} \\&=33.3 rad / s\end{aligned}\)

 

Since \(f_{n}=\omega_{n} / 2 \pi\), the cyclic natural frequency is \(f_{n}=5.25\).

Next, in the interest of improving the model we account for the mass of the spring and use an equivalent weight for the mass, as shown in Figure 6.16. Following

 

\(\begin{aligned}W_{\text {equiv }} &=W_{\text {car }}+\frac{1}{3} W_{\text {cable }} \\&=1000 lb +\frac{1}{3}(750 lb )=1250 lb .\end{aligned}\)

 

For this second model, the natural frequency increases as follows,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\omega_{n} &=\sqrt{\frac{k_{\text {tot }} g}{W_{\text {equiv }}}}=\sqrt{\frac{6.0 \times 10^{4} \frac{ lb }{ ft } \cdot 32.2 \frac{ ft }{ s ^{2}}}{1250 lb }} \\&=39.3 rad / s \text { or } f_{n}=6.26 Hz .\end{aligned}\)

 

This is a more accurate representation of the system. Finally, a third model is considered which distributes the cable weight at two discrete locations, at 250 ft and
500 ft from the top, as shown in Figure 6.17. The mass of the cable is distributed in thirds, one third to each mass and the other third to the mass of the car, as before,

 

\(\begin{aligned}&m_{1}=m_{2}=\frac{1}{3} \frac{W_{\text {cable }}}{g}=\frac{250 lb }{g} \\&m_{3}=\frac{W_{\text {car }}+W_{\text {cable }}}{g}=\frac{1250 lb }{g} .\end{aligned}\)

 

The spring stiffness changes due to the shorter lengths,

 

\(k=\frac{E A}{(l / 3)}=\frac{4.5 \times 10^{7} psi \cdot 1 in ^{2}}{250 ft }=1.8 \times 10^{5} \frac{ lb }{ ft }\)

 

To calculate the three natural frequencies, we need the equations of motion,

 

\(\begin{array}{r}m_{1} \ddot{x}_{1}+2 k x_{1}-k x_{2}=0 \\m_{2} \ddot{x}_{2}-k x_{1}+2 k x_{2}-k x_{3}=0 \\m_{3} \ddot{x}_{3}-k x_{2}+k x_{3}=0\end{array}\)

 

Rewriting these equations of motion in matrix-vector form, differentiating and substituting the assumed harmonic responses for each displacement \(A_{i} \exp (i \omega t)\) into the equation of motion, we have

 

\(\begin{gathered}-\omega^{2}\left[\begin{array}{ccc}m_{1} & 0 & 0 \\0 & m_{2} & 0 \\0 & 0 & m_{3}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}A_{1} \exp (i \omega t) \\A_{2} \exp (i \omega t) \\A_{3} \exp (i \omega t)\end{array}\right\} \\+\left[\begin{array}{ccc}2 k & -k & 0 \\-k & 2 k & -k \\0 & -k & k\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A_{1} \exp (i \omega t) \\A_{2} \exp (i \omega t) \\A_{3} \exp (i \omega t)\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0 \\0\end{array}\right\} .\end{gathered}\)

 

Using the fact that \(m_{1}=m_{2}\), combining like terms, and multiplying all terms by \(\exp (-i \omega t)\), we find

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-\omega^{2} m_{1}+2 k & -k & 0 \\-k & -\omega^{2} m_{2}+2 k & -k \\0 & -k & -\omega^{2} m_{3}+k\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A_{1} \\A_{2} \\A_{3}\end{array}\right\} \\=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0 \\0\end{array}\right\},\end{aligned}\)

 

which is the eigenvalue problem. Setting the determinant of the coefficient matrix equal to zero, we obtain the characteristic equation,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\omega^{6}\left(-m_{1}^{2} m_{3}\right) &+\omega^{4}\left[k m_{1}\left(m_{1}+4 m_{3}\right)\right] \\&+\omega^{2}\left[-3 k^{2}\left(m_{1}+m_{3}\right)\right]+k^{3}=0\end{aligned}\)

 

Substituting the parameter values and solving the equivalent cubic equation for \(\omega^{2}\) gives the frequencies of oscillation,

 

\(\omega_{1,2,3}=46.3,165,266 rad / s\)

 

or

 

\(f_{1,2,3}=7.37,26.26,42.34 Hz\)

 

Here, the fundamental frequency (7.37 Hz) is higher than those of the two simpler models and most likely more accurate. The next level of realism would be to use a string model for the cable that represents its physical properties as a continuum. A conclusion from this example is that a range of models can be used to represent a complex system, and the accuracy of numerical results depends on the reasonableness of the underlying assumptions.
