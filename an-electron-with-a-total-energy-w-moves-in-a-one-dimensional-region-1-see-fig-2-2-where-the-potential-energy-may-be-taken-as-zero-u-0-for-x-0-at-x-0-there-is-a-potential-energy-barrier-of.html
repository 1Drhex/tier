Guest Question.

An electron with a total energy W moves in a one-dimensional region 1 (see Fig. 2.2), where the potential energy may be taken as zero, U = 0 for x < 0. At x = 0 there is a potential-energy barrier of height U0 > W, and as indicated in the figure, the potential energy remains constant in region 2 for x > 0.

(a) Verify that the solution of the Schrödinger equation in region 1 is

\(\Psi _{1}\) (x) = C sin(ax) + D cos (ax)

and in region 2 is

\(\Psi _{2}(x)=A exp \left\lgroup-\frac{x}{2d_{o}} \right\rgroup +B exp \left\lgroup \frac{x}{2d_{o}} \right\rgroup \)

where a and d0 are real numbers. Find a and d0 .

(b) Evaluate B, C and D in terms of A.

(c) What is the physical meaning of the fact that |\(\Psi _{2}\)(x)| > 0?

Admin
Solution

(a) If \(\Psi _{1}\)(x) is the wave function in the Region 1, putting U = 0 in Eq. (2.14), \( ∇^2\Psi +\frac{8\pi ^2 m}{h^2}(W-U)\Psi =0 \) the Schrödinger equation for this region can be written as

\(\frac{d^2 \Psi _{1}(x)}{dx^2}+\left\lgroup\frac{8\pi ^2 m W}{h^2} \right\rgroup \Psi _{1}=0 \)

which is a second order linear differential equation. Thus, the general solution an be written as

\(\Psi _{1}(x)=C_{1}exp \left\lgroup j\sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2 m W}{h^2} }x \right\rgroup +D_{1} exp \left\lgroup-j\sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2 mW}{h^2} }x \right\rgroup \)

\(= C \cos \left\lgroup \sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2 m W}{h^2} } \right\rgroup x +D\sin \left\lgroup \sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2 m W}{h^2} } \right\rgroup x\)

\(=C \cos (ax)+D\sin (ax)\)

where C1 and D1 are arbitrary constants, C = C1 + D1 and = j(C1 − D1) and \(a=\sqrt{\left\lgroup \frac{8\pi ^2 m W}{h^2} \right\rgroup} = \frac{2\pi \sqrt{2m} }{h}\sqrt{W}=\sqrt{\frac{2mW}{h^2} } \) where \(h=\frac{h}{2\pi } \) is the modified Planck’s constant.

Similarly, if \(\Psi _{2}\)(x) is the wave function in the Region 2, the Schrödinger equation for this region can be obtained by substituting U = U0 in Eq. (2.14):

\(\frac{d^2 \Psi _{2}(x)}{dx^2} -\left\lgroup\frac{8\pi ^2 m}{h^2}(U_{o} -W) \right\rgroup \Psi _{2} \)

which is also a second order linear differential equation. Hence, the general soution is given as

\(\Psi _{2}(x)= A exp\left\lgroup- \left\lgroup\sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2m}{h^2}(U_{o}-W) } \right\rgroup x\right\rgroup +B exp\left\lgroup \left\lgroup\sqrt{\frac{8\pi ^2m}{h^2}(U_{o}-W) } \right\rgroup x\right\rgroup\)

\(=A exp\left\lgroup-\frac{x}{2d_{o}} \right\rgroup+ B exp\left\lgroup\frac{x}{2d_{o}} \right\rgroup \)

where A and B are two arbitrry constants, and \(d_{o}=\frac{h}{4\pi }\sqrt{\frac{1}{2m(U_{o}-W)} } = \sqrt{\frac{h^2}{8m(U_{o}-W)} } \)

(b) Since \(\Psi _{2}\)(x) →∞ as x →∞ , B must be Because, \(\Psi _{1}\)(x) and \(\Psi _{2}\)(x) must be finite for every value of x. Further, continuity of the wave function at x = 0 implies that \(\Psi _{2}\)(0) = \(\Psi _{2}\)(0). This results in C = A + B = A. Moreover, the continuity of the first derivatives of \(\Psi _{1}\)(x) and \(\Psi \)(x) at x = 0 i.e.

\(\frac{d\Psi _{1}(x)}{dx}|_{x=0} =\frac{d\Psi _{2}(x)}{dx}|_{x=0}\) results in \(D=-\frac{A}{2ad_{0}} \)

(c) We have observed that A = C = C1 + D1 is not equal to zero since C1 and D1 both can’t be This implies that the probability density function |\(\Psi _{2}\) (x) |2 > 0. In other words we can say that there is a finite probability that the incident electron with energy W can penetrate a potential barrier with energy U0 > W. It may be mentioned here that classical physics does not permit the same. According to the classical theory, no particle can be absorbed or transmitted through the barrier whose potential energy is higher than the total energy of the incident particle (see Sec. 3.2). Thus, the finite and nonzero probability of finding the particle in the higher potential barrier region is contradictory to the classical theory.
