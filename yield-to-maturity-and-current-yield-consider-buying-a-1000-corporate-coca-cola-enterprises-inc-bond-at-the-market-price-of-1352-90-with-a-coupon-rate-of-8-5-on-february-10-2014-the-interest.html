Yield to Maturity and Current Yield

Consider buying a $1,000 corporate (Coca-Cola Enterprises Inc.) bond at the market price of $1,352.90 with a coupon rate of 8.5% on February 10, 2014. The interest will be paid semiannually, the interest rate per payment period will be simply 4.25%, and 16 interest payments over eight years are required. We show the resulting cash flow to the investor in Figure 4.24. Find (a) the yield to maturity and (b) the current yield.

Discussion

• Debenture: Coca-Cola Enterprises issued bonds totaling $750 million, and the company backed the bonds with specific pieces of property, such as buildings.

• Par value: Coca-Cola’s bond has a par value of $1,000.

• Maturity date: Coca-Cola’s bonds, which were issued on February 11, 1992, will mature on February 1, 2022; thus, they have a 30-year maturity at the time of their issue. However, if you purchased the bond on February 10, 2014, it has only an 8-year maturity.

• Coupon rate: Coca-Cola’s coupon rate is 2(4.25%) = 8.5%, and interest is payable semiannually. For example, Coca-Cola’s bonds have a $1,000 par value, and they pay $85 in simple interest (8.5%) each year ($42.50 every six months).

• Premium bond: At 135.29%, or a 35.29% premium, Coca-Cola’s bonds are selling at higher than their par value. Price at offering in 1992 was 98.83 with a yield of 8.61%.

Admin
Solution

Given: Purchase price = $1,352.90, coupon rate = 8.5% per year paid semiannually, and 8-year remaining maturity with a par value of $1,000.

Find: (a) Yield to maturity and (b) current yield.
(a) We find the yield to maturity by determining the interest rate that makes the present worth of the receipts equal to the market price of the bond:

$1,352.90 = $42.50(P/A, i, 16) + $1,000(P/F, i, 16)

The value of i that makes the present worth of the receipts equal to $1,352.90 lies between 1.5 and 2%. We could use a linear interpolation to find the yield to maturity, but it is much easier to obtain with Excel’s IRR function. As shown in Table 4.9, solving for i yields i = 1.71%. Note that this 1.71% is the yield to maturity per semiannual period. The nominal (annual) yield is 2(1.71) = 3.42%

compounded semiannually. Compared with the coupon rate of 8.5%, purchasing the bond with the premium paid at 35.29% brings the yield to maturity down significantly below that of the coupon rate of 8.5%. The effective annual interest rate is then

\(i_{a} = \left(1+0.0171\right) ^{2} -1 = 3.45\%\)

The 3.45% represents the effective annual yield to maturity on the bond. Notice that when you purchase a bond at par value and sell at par value, the yield to maturity will be the same as the coupon rate of the bond.

(b) For our example of Coca-Cola Enterprises, we compute the current yield as

\(\frac{\$42.50}{\$1,352.90}\) = 3.14% per semiannual period

3.14% \(\times\) 2 = 6.283% per year (nominal current yield),

\(i_{a} = \left(1+0.0314\right) ^{2} -1 = 6.381\%\)

Comments: This effective current yield is about 2.931% higher than the 3.45% yield to maturity we just computed. If the bond is selling at a discount, the current yield is smaller than the yield to maturity. If the bond is selling at a premium, the current yield is larger than the yield to maturity. A significant difference between the yield to maturity and the current yield of a bond can exist because the market price of a bond may be more or less than its face value. Moreover, both the current yield and the yield to maturity may differ considerably from the stated coupon value of the bond.
