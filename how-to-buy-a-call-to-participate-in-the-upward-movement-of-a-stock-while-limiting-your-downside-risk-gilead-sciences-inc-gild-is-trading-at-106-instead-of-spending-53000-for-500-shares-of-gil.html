Guest Question.

How to Buy a Call to Participate in the Upward Movement of a Stock while Limiting Your Downside Risk

Gilead Sciences, Inc. (GILD) is trading at $106. Instead of spending $53,000 for 500 shares of GILD stock, an investor could purchase a five-month call with a $110-strike price for $8.23. By doing this, the investor is saying that he or she anticipates that GILD will rise above the strike of $110 (which is where GILD can be purchased no matter how high GILD has risen), plus another $8 (the option premium), or $118 by expiration. Without considering commissions and taxes, discuss the amount of risk presented in each of the following scenarios, as opposed to purchasing 500 shares:

  • GILD is trading at or above $118 by expiration.
  • GILD is trading between $100 and $118 at expiration.
  • GILD is trading at or below $100 at expiration.

Discussion: Each call contract represents 100 shares of stock, so five calls could be bought in place of 500 shares of stock. The cost of five calls at $8 is $4,000 (5 calls \(\times\) $8 \(\times\) 100). Thus, instead of spending $53,000 on stock, the investor needs to spend only $4,000 for the purchase of the five calls. The balance of $49,000 could then be invested in short-term instruments. This investor has unlimited profit potential when GILD rises above $118. The risk for the option buyer is limited to the premium paid, which in this example is $4,000.

Admin
Solution

Given: Buying five GILD five-month $110 calls at $8 versus buying 500 shares of GILD at $106
Find: Profit or loss for three possible scenarios at expiration.

(a) GILD is at or above $118 by expiration.

  • If GILD is at $120 at expiration, the option will be worth the difference between the strike and the current price of the stock:

$120 (current price) – $110 (strike price) = $10 (current option value)

The option could be exercised, and a 25% return would be earned on the initial investment. That is,

$10 (current option value) – $8 (premium paid for option)
= $2 (profit if option is sold)

  • Had the stock been purchased at $106 (at a cost of $53,000) and had it risen to $120, it would now be worth $60,000. This would be a 13.21% increase in value over the original cost of $53,000. But the call buyer spent only $4,000 and earned 25% on his options. Table 13.1 compares both scenarios.

(b) GILD is between $110 and $118 at expiration.

  • The investor’s option will still hold some value if GILD is between $110 and $118 but not enough to break even on the position. The option still can be sold to recoup some of the cost. For example, suppose GILD is at $115 on the last day of trading, usually the third Friday of the expiration month. Then the option can be sold to close out the position through the last trading day of the call. What happened is that GILD did rise in value, but not as much as anticipated. The option that cost $8 is now worth just five points. Instead of letting the option expire, you can sell the call and recoup some of your losses:

$8 (Cost) – $5 (Sale) = $3 (Net loss excluding commissions)

  • If just the stock had been bought and it rose to $115, $4,500 would have been earned, while the holder of calls would have lost $1,500. However, the holder of calls would have been earning interest on $49,000, which would offset some of the loss in the options.

(c) GILD is at or below $110 at expiration.

  • Suppose GILD is now at $90 and the option has expired worthless. The premium that was paid for the calls has been lost. However, had the stock been bought, the investor would now be in a losing stock position, hoping to break even. By purchasing a call he or she had limited capital at risk. Now the investor still has most of the money that would have gone into buying the stock, plus interest. Thus, the investor can make another investment decision.
  • If the investor had purchased GILD at $106 and the stock did not move as he or she anticipated, the investor would have had two choices: sell the stock and, after commission costs, incur some losses; or hold onto it and hope that it rises over the long term. Had the investor bought the options and been wrong, the options would expire worthless and the loss would be limited to the premium paid ($4,000).
