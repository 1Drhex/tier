Guest Question.

(a) Determine the concentration of free electrons and holes in a sample of germanium at 300°K which has a concentration of donor atoms equal to \(2\times 10^{14}\) atoms/\(cm^3\) and a concentration of acceptor atom equal to \(3\times 10^{14}\) atoms/\(cm^3\). Is this p– or n-type germanium? In other words, is the conductivity due primarily to holes or to electrons?

(b) Repeat part (a) for equal donor and acceptor concentrations of \(10^{15}\)atoms/\(cm^3\). Is this p-or n-type germanium?

(c) Repeat part (a) for a temperature of 400°K, and show that the sample is essentially

(d) Repeat part (a) for a donor concentration of \(10^{15}\) atoms/\(cm^3\) and zero acceptor

Solution

(a) Substituting \(p=n+(N_{A}-N_{D})\) from Eq. (4.24) , \(N_{D}+p=N_{A}+n\) , in Eq. (4.19) , \(np=n^2_{i}\) , and solving the resultant equation, we can get

\(n=\frac{1}{2}\left\{(N_{D}-N_{A})+\sqrt{(N_{A}-N_{D})^2 +4n^2_{i}} \right\} \)

Using \(N_{D}=2\times 10^{14} cm^{-3} \) , \(N_{A}=3\times 10^{14} cm^{-3} \)3 and \(n_{i}=2.5\times 10^{13} cm^{-3} \) (see Table 4.1) in the above equation, the electron concentration of the given germanium sample at T = 300°K is \(n=5.90\times 10^{12}cm^{-3} \).

Using the value of \(n=5.90\times 10^{12}cm^{-3} \) in Eq. (4.19), the concentration of hole in the sample is given by

\(p=\frac{(2.5\times 10^{13})^2}{5.90\times 10^{12}} =1.06\times 10^{14}cm^{-3} \)

Let \(\sigma _{n} \) and \(\sigma _{p} \) be the conductivity of the sample due to electrons and holes respectively. Using the value of \(\mu _{n} \) from Table 4.1

in Eq. (4.2),\(\sigma =(n\mu _{n}+p\mu _{p})e\) , \(\sigma _{n} \) is obtained as

\(\sigma _{n}=n\mu _{n}e\)

\(= (5.90\times 10^{12}cm^{-3})\times (3800 cm^2/V sec)\times (1.60\times 10^{-19}C)\)

\(= 0.0036(\Omega cm)^{-1}\)

Similarly, \(\sigma _{p} \) is obtained as

\(\sigma _{p}=p\mu _{p}e\)

\(= (1.06\times 10^{14}cm^{-3})\times (1800 cm^2/V sec)\times (1.60\times 10^{-19}C)\)

\(= 0.0305 (\Omega cm)^{-1}\)

It is observed that \(\frac{\sigma _{p}}{\sigma _{n}}=\frac{0.0305}{0.0036}=8.41 \) which is greater than \(\frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}}=\frac{\mu _{p}}{\mu _{n}} =\frac{1800}{3800}=0.47 \) where \(\sigma _{pi} \) and \(\sigma _{ni} \) are conductivity due to holes and electrons respectively in an intrinsic germanium. This implies that the conductivity is
primarily due to holes in the given sample. Hence the sample is a p-type germanium.

(b) Following the similar method as used in part (a), we may find that \(n=p=n_{i}=2.5\times 10^{13}cm^{-3} \) for \(N_{A}=N_{D}=10^{15}cm^{-3} \) In this case, it is found that \(\sigma _{n}=0.0152 (\Omega cm)^{-1} \) and \(\sigma _{p}=0.0072 (\Omega cm)^{-1} \) which gives \(\frac{\sigma _{p}}{\sigma _{n}}=\frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}}=0.47 \) . Hence, the sample is an intrinsic germanium

(c) Let the intrinsic carrier concentrations at two temperatures \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) be \(n_{i}(T_{1})\) and \(n_{i}(T_{2})\) respectively. Then, using Eq. (4.23), \( n^2_{i}=A_{o}T^3 exp-E_{GO}/kT\) , we can write

\(\left\lgroup\frac{n_{i}(T_{1})}{n_{i}(T_{2})} \right\rgroup^2 =\left\lgroup\frac{T_{1}}{T_{2}} \right\rgroup^3 exp\left[-\frac{E_{GO}}{kT_{2}}\left\lgroup 1-\frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}} \right\rgroup \right] \)

Putting \(n_{i}(T_{2}=300^{\circ }K)=2.5\times 10^{13}cm^{-3} \) (i.e. intrinsic carrier concentration of germanium at room temperature) in
the above equation, the intrinsic concentration at \(T_{1}=400^{\circ }K \) can be given by

\(n_{i}(T_{1}=400^{\circ }K)=\left\{\left\lgroup\frac{400}{300} \right\rgroup^3 exp\left[\frac{0.785}{0.0259}\left\lgroup1-\frac{300}{400} \right\rgroup \right]\times (2.5\times 10^{13})^2 \right\}^{\frac{1}{2} } \)

\(=1.7 \times 10^{15}cm^{-3} \)

Using \(N_{D}=2\times 10^{14}cm^{-3} \) , \(N_{A}=3\times 10^{14}cm^{-3} \)

\(n_{i}=n_{i}(T_{1}=400^{\circ }K)=1.7\times 10^{15}cm^{-3} \) in part (a), the hole and electron concentrations are given by \(n= 1.65\times 10^{15}cm^{-3} \) and \(p= 1.75\times 10^{15}cm^{-3} \) The ratio of the conductivities due to holes and electrons is given by

\(\frac{\sigma _{p}}{\sigma _{n}}=\frac{p\mu _{p}}{n\mu _{n}}=\left\lgroup\frac{1.75\times 10^{15}}{1.65\times 10^{15}} \right\rgroup \frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}} =1.08\left\lgroup\frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}} \right\rgroup \approx \frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}} \)

Hence, the germanium sample under consideration is essentially intrinsic.

(d) Using the values of \(N_{D}=10^{15} cm^{-3} \) and \(N_{A}=0 \) in part (a), we may obtain \(n=1.0006\times 10^{15}\approx N_{D}\) and \(p=6.2461\times 10^{11}cm^{-3}\approx \frac{n^2_{i}}{N_{D}} \) Now, the conductivity ratio is given by

\(\frac{\sigma _{p}}{\sigma _{n}}=\frac{p\mu _{p}}{n\mu _{n}}=\left\lgroup\frac{n_{i}}{N_{D}} \right\rgroup^2 \left\lgroup\frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}} \right\rgroup =(6.25\times 10^{-4}) \left\lgroup\frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma _{ni}} \right\rgroup \ll \frac{\sigma _{pi}}{\sigma n_{i}} \)

This shows that the conductivity due to hole is almost negligible as compared to that of electrons in the sample.
In other words, the conductivity of the sample is due primarily to the electrons and hence the sample is essentially
n-type.
