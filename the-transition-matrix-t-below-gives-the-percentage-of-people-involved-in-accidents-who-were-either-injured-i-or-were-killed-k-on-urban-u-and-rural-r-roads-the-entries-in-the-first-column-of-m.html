Guest Question.

The transition matrix \(\mathrm{T}\) below gives the percentage of people involved in accidents who were either injured (I) or were killed (K) on urban (U) and rural (R) roads. The entries in the first column of matrix \(\mathrm{T}\) indicate that \(60 \%\) of road injuries on urban roads and \(40 \%\) on rural roads. The second column of \(\mathrm{T}\) represents \(50 \%\) of road accident deaths occured on urban roads and \(50 \%\) on rural roads. Out of a sample of 100 accidents this year the number of accidents on urban roads was 90 and rural roads 10 and this is represented by the vector \(\mathrm{x}\).

\( I \mathrm{~K} \\\mathrm{T}=\left(\begin{array}{ll}0.6 & 0.5 \\0.4 & 0.5\end{array}\right) \begin{aligned}&\mathrm{U} \\&\mathrm{R}\end{aligned} \text { and } \mathbf{x}=\left(\begin{array}{c}90 \\10\end{array}\right)\)

The vector \(\mathbf{x}_{n}\) given by \(\mathrm{x}_{n}=\mathrm{T}^{n} \mathbf{x}\) gives us the number of accidents on urban and rural roads out of a sample of 100 accidents after \(n\) number of years. Determine to \(2 \mathrm{sf}\)

(i)\(\mathrm{x}_{n}\) for \(n=10\). (ii) \(\mathrm{x}_{n}\) as \(n \rightarrow \infty\)

(This gives us our long term number of injuries and deaths on urban and rural roads out of a sample of 100 accidents.) For a Markov chain, we are interested in the long-term behaviour of \(\mathrm{x}_{n}\).

Admin
Solution

(i) This means that we need to find \(\mathbf{x}_{n}=\mathrm{T}^{n} \mathbf{x}\) when \(n=10\), that is \(\mathrm{x}_{10}=\mathrm{T}^{10} \mathbf{x}\). To evaluate \(\mathrm{T}^{10}\), we diagonalize the matrix \(\mathrm{T}\) by finding the eigenvalue \(\mathrm{D}\) and eigenvector \(\mathrm{P}\) matrices. Verify that the eigenvalues and the corresponding eigenvectors are given by

\(\lambda_{1}=1, \mathbf{u}=\left(\begin{array}{l}5 \\4\end{array}\right) \text { and } \lambda_{2}=0.1, \mathbf{v}=\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\1\end{array}\right)\\\) \(\text { Our eigenvector matrix } \mathbf{P}=(\mathbf{u} \quad \mathbf{v})=\left(\begin{array}{rr}5 & -1 \\4 & 1\end{array}\right) \text { and eigenvalue matrix } \mathbf{D}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & 0 \\0 & 0.1\end{array}\right)\\\)

By result \((7.19) \mathrm{A}^{m}=\mathrm{PD}^{m} \mathrm{P}^{-1}\) with \(m=10\) and \(\mathrm{A}=\mathrm{T}\) we have

\(\mathrm{T}^{10}=\mathrm{PD}^{10} \mathrm{P}^{-1} \) (†)

To evaluate \(\mathrm{T}^{10}\) we need to find \(\mathrm{P}^{-1}\), which is given by

\( \mathrm{P}^{-1}=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ -4 & 5 \end{array}\right) \)

Substituting \(\mathbf{P}=\left(\begin{array}{rr}5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1\end{array}\right), \mathbf{D}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.1\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathrm{P}^{-1}=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{rr}1 & 1 \\ -4 & 5\end{array}\right)\) into \((\dagger)\) gives

\( \begin{aligned} \mathrm{T}^{10}=\mathrm{PD}^{10} \mathrm{P}^{-1} &=\left(\begin{array}{rr} 5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.1 \end{array}\right)^{10} \frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ -4 & 5 \end{array}\right) \\ &=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{rr} 5 & -1 \\ 4 & 1 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{rl} 1 & 1 \\ -4 & 5 \end{array}\right)\left[\begin{array}{l} \text { because } 1^{10}=1 \text { and } \\ 0.1^{10}=1 \times 10^{-10}=0(3 \mathrm{dp}) \end{array}\right] \\ &=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{ll} 5 & 0 \\ 4 & 0 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ -4 & 5 \end{array}\right)=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{ll} 5 & 5 \\ 4 & 4 \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} \)

Substituting \(\mathrm{T}^{10}=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{ll}5 & 5 \\ 4 & 4\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathbf{x}=\left(\begin{array}{c}90 \\ 10\end{array}\right)\) into \(\mathbf{x}_{10}=\mathrm{T}^{10} \mathbf{x}\) gives

\( \begin{array}{r} \mathrm{x}_{10}=\frac{1}{9}\left(\begin{array}{ll} 5 & 5 \\ 4 & 4 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l} 90 \\ 10 \end{array}\right) \underbrace{=}_{ \text { taking out a } \text { factor of } 10 }\frac{10}{9}\left(\begin{array}{ll} 5 & 5 \\ 4 & 4 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l} 9 \\ 1 \end{array}\right)=\frac{10}{9}\left(\begin{array}{l} 50 \\ 40 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 55.5 \\ 44.4 \end{array}\right) \end{array}\begin{array}{l}\mathrm{U}\\\mathrm{R} \end{array} \)

This means that after 10 years the number of people likely to be injured or killed on an urban road is 56 ( \(2 \mathrm{sf}\) ) and on a rural road is 44 (2sf) out of a sample of 100 accidents.

(ii) We have \(\mathrm{D}^{n}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.1\end{array}\right)^{n}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1^{n} & 0 \\ 0 & 0.1^{n}\end{array}\right)\).

How does this change as \(n \rightarrow \infty\) ?

As \(n \rightarrow \infty\) we have \(1^{n} \rightarrow 1\) and \(0.1^{n} \rightarrow 0\). This means that \(\mathrm{D}^{n}=\mathrm{D}^{10}\) correct to \(2 \mathrm{sf}\) which gives the same results as part (i).

(7.19): If an \(n\) by \(n\) matrix \(\mathrm{A}\) is diagonalizable with \(\mathrm{P}^{-1} \mathrm{AP}=\mathrm{D}\) where \(\mathrm{D}\) is a diagonal matrix then

\(\mathbf{A}^{m}=\mathbf{P D}^{m} \mathrm{P}^{-1}\)
