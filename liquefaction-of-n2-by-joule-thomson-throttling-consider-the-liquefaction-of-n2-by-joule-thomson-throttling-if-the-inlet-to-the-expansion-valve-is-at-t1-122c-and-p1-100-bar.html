Guest Question.

Liquefaction of \(N _{2}\) by Joule–Thomson Throttling

Consider the liquefaction of \(N _{2}\) by Joule–Thomson throttling. If the inlet to the expansion valve is at \(T_{1}=-122^{\circ} C \text { and } P_{1}=100\) bar and the outlet is at 1 bar, determine the percentage of \(N _{2}\) that is liquefied.

Admin
Solution

We can solve this problem using the generalized charts for enthalpy departure. Looking up properties for \(N _{2}\) from Appendix A.1, we get:

 

\(T_{c}=126.2[ K ]\)

 

\(P_{c}=33.8 \text { bar }\)

 

\(\omega=0.039\)

 

Since the acentric factor,ω, is small, we will use only the simple fluid term in the generalized correlations. The reduced coordinates for state 1 are:

 

\(T_{1, r}=\frac{T_{1}}{T_{c}}=\frac{151[ K ]}{126.2[ K ]}=1.20 \quad \text { and } \quad P_{1, r}=\frac{P_{1}}{P_{c}}=\frac{100[ bar ]}{33.8[ bar ]}=3.0\)

 

We can then find the values for the enthalpy departure term in Table C.1 in Appendix C:

 

\(\left[\frac{h_{T_{1}, r, P_{1}, r}-h_{T_{1}, r, P_{1}, r}^{\text {ideal gas }}}{R T_{c}}\right]=-2.81\) (E5.10A)

 

For state 2, we have:

 

\(P_{2, r}=\frac{P_{2}}{P_{c}}=\frac{1[\text { bar }]}{33.8[\text { bar }]}=0.03\)

 

Looking at Figure 5.5, we see that the two-phase region is at:

 

\(T_{2, r}=0.61\)

 

so, \(T_{2}=T_{2, r} T_{c}=0.61 \times 126.2[ K ]=77[ K ]\)

 

Applying Equation (5.47) for this isenthalpic process:

 

\(h_{2}-h_{1}=-\Delta h_{T_{1}, P_{1}}^{ dep }+\left[\int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}} c_{P} d T+0\right]+\Delta h_{T_{2}, P_{2}}^{ dep }\) (5.47)

 

\(\frac{h_{2}-h_{1}}{R T_{c}}=0=\frac{-\Delta h_{T_{1}, P_{1}}^{ dep }}{R T_{c}}+\frac{\Delta h_{T_{1} \longrightarrow T_{2}}^{\text {ideal gas }}}{R T_{c}}+\frac{\Delta h_{T_{2}, P_{2}}^{\text {dep }}}{R T_{c}}\) (E5.10B)

 

To find the ideal gas enthalpy change, we need to look up the heat capacity from Appendix A.2:

 

\(\frac{c_{P}}{R}=3.28+0.593 \times 10^{-3} T\)

 

Therefore,

 

\(\frac{\Delta h_{T_{1} \longrightarrow T_{2}}^{\text {ideal gas }}}{R T_{c}}=\frac{1}{T_{c}} \int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}} \frac{c_{P}}{R} d T=\frac{1}{126.2} \int_{151}^{77}\left[3.28+0.593 \times 10^{-3} T\right] d T=-1.28\) (E5.10C)

 

Rearranging Equation (E5.10B) and plugging in values from Equations (E5.10A) and (E5.10C), we get:

 

\(\frac{\Delta h_{T_{2}, P_{2}}^{ dep }}{R T_{c}}=\frac{\Delta h_{T_{1}, P_{1}}^{ dep }}{R T_{c}}-\frac{\Delta h_{T_{1} \longrightarrow T_{2}}^{\text {ideal gas }}}{R T_{c}}=-1.53\)

 

It is useful to view this process on an hP generalized enthalpy chart, as shown in Figure E5.10. The “lever rule” is illustrated. The quality can be found by:

 

\(\Delta h^{\text {dep }}=(1-x) \Delta h_{l}^{\text {dep }}+x \Delta h_{v}^{\text {dep }}\)

 

Solving for x, we get:

 

\(x=\frac{\Delta h^{\text {dep }}-\Delta h_{l}^{\text {dep }}}{\Delta h_{v}^{\text {dep }}-\Delta h_{l}^{\text {dep }}}=\frac{-1.5+5.1}{-0.1+5.1}=0.72\)

 

Since the quality represents the fraction of vapor, we conclude that roughly 28% the inlet stream is liquefied.
