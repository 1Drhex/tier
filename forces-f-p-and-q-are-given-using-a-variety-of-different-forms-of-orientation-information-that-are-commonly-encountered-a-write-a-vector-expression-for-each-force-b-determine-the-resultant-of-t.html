Guest Question.

Forces \(\vec{F}\), \(\vec{P}\) and \(\vec{Q}\) are given using a variety of different forms of orientation information that are commonly encountered.

(a) Write a vector expression for each force.
(b) Determine the resultant of the three forces.
(c) Determine the direction angles for the resultant.

Admin
Solution

Road Map The strategy to be used to write a vector expression depends on the details of the geometry information provided. Once expressions for the three force vectors are obtained, we will add these to obtain the resultant force vector. Once the resultant force vector is known, it will be straightforward to determine the direction angles for the resultant.

Part (a)

Governing Equations & Computation

Force \(\vec{F}\) : We carefully examine Fig. 1 to see if the angles reported for \(\vec{F}\) are direction angles. Indeed, the two \(60^{\circ } \) angles are measured from the positive x and z directions to the direction of \(\vec{F}\) , so both of these are direction angles and thus \(\theta _{x} = 60^{\circ } \) and \(\theta _{z} = 60^{\circ } \). The remaining \(45^{\circ } \) angle is measured from the negative y direction to the vector, so it is not a direction angle. However, the appropriate direction angle, as measured from the positive y direction, is easily found as \(\theta _{y} = 180^{\circ } – 45^{\circ } = 135^{\circ } \).

As a partial check, we evaluate Eq. (2.26) to find \(\cos ^{2} 60^{\circ } + \cos ^{2} 135^{\circ } + \cos ^{2} 60^{\circ } = 1\) , and thus, these angles are indeed direction angles. Note, however, that if we incorrectly took \(\theta _{y}\) to be \(45^{\circ } \) , Eq. (2.26) would still have been satisfied, but the resulting vector \(\vec{F}\) would have a positive y component, rather than the correct value which is negative. Given the direction angles, a vector expression for \(\vec{F}\) may be written as

\(\cos ^{2} \theta _{x} + \cos ^{2} \theta _{y} + \cos ^{2} \theta _{z} = 1\) (2.26)

\(\vec{F} = \left(100 N\right) \left(\cos 60^{\circ } \hat{\imath} + \cos 135^{\circ } \hat{\jmath} + \cos 60^{\circ } \hat{k} \right)\)

\(= \left(50\cdot 00 \hat{\imath} – 70\cdot 71 \hat{\jmath} + 50\cdot 00 \hat{k} \right) N\) (1)

Force \(\vec{P}\) : For this force, we observe the \(30^{\circ } \) angle shown in Fig. 1 is not a direction angle; while it is measured from the positive y direction, it is not measured to the vector’s direction. When geometry is given in this fashion, we use a two-step process to determine the vector’s components. We first use the 8-15 rise-run geometry to compute the components \(P_{z} \) and \(P_{a} \) shown in Fig. 2(a):

\(P_{z} = -\left(200 N\right) \frac{8}{17} = -94 \cdot 12 N\) and \(P_{a} = \left(200 N\right) \frac{15}{17} = 176 \cdot 5 N\) (2)

The negative sign is included in the expression for \(P_{z}\) because this force component acts in the negative z direction. For the second step of this procedure, we use the \(30^{\circ } \) angle to resolve \(P_{a}\) into x and y components, as shown in Fig. 2(b). Thus

\(P_{x} = P_{a} \sin 30^{\circ } = 88\cdot 24 N\) and \(P_{y} = P_{a} \cos 30^{\circ }= 152\cdot 8 N\) (3)

Collecting results for \(P_{x}\) ,\(P_{y}\) and \(P_{z}\) allows us to write

\(\vec{P} = \left(88\cdot 24 \hat{\imath} + 152\cdot 8 \hat{\jmath} – 94\cdot 12 \hat{k} \right) N\) (4)

As a partial check on our work, we compute the magnitude of the above expression to find \(P = \sqrt{\left(88\cdot 24 N\right) ^{2} + \left(152\cdot 8 N\right) ^{2} + \left(- 94\cdot 12\right) ^{2} } = 200\cdot 0 N\) , which is the expected result.

Force \(\vec{Q}\) : For this force, we observe the \(30^{\circ } \) and \(45^{\circ } \) angles are not direction angles; the \(45^{\circ } \) angle is not measured to the vector’s direction, and the \(30^{\circ } \) angle is not measured from a coordinate direction. As with force \(\vec{P}\) , we again use a two-step process to obtain the vector’s components. As shown in Fig. 3(a), we first use the \(30^{\circ } \) angle to resolve \(\vec{Q}\) into z and b direction components

\(Q_{z} = – \left(300 N\right)\sin 30^{\circ } = – 150\cdot0 N\) and (5)

\(Q_{b} = \left(300 N\right) \cos 30^{\circ }= 259\cdot 8 N\) (6)

The negative sign is included in the expressions for \(Q_{z}\) because this component acts in the negative z direction. We could similarly include a negative sign for \(Q_{b}\) because it acts in the negative b direction, but subsequent resolution of \(Q_{b}\) will take into account the appropriate directions in a straightforward manner. As shown in Fig. 3(b), we next use the \(45^{\circ } \) angle to resolve \(Q_{b}\) into the x and y components

\(Q_{x} = Q_{b} \sin 45^{\circ } = 183\cdot 7 N\) and \(Q_{y} =- Q_{b} \cos 45^{\circ }= – 183\cdot 7 N\) (7)

Collecting results for \(Q_{x}\) ,\(Q_{y}\) and \(Q_{z}\) allows us to write

\(\vec{Q} = \left(183\cdot 7 \hat{\imath} – 183\cdot 7 \hat{\jmath} – 150\cdot 0 \hat{k} \right) N\) (8)

As a partial check of correctness, you should evaluate the magnitude of the above expression to verify it is 300 N.

part (b)

Governing Equations & Computation The resultant of the three forces is

\(\vec{R} = \vec{F} + \vec{P} + \vec{Q} = \left(50\cdot 00 \hat{\imath} – 70\cdot 71 \hat{\jmath} + 50\cdot 00 \hat{k} \right) N\)

\(+ \left(88\cdot 24 \hat{\imath} + 152\cdot 8 \hat{\jmath} – 94\cdot 12 \hat{k} \right) N\)

\(+ \left(183\cdot 7 \hat{\imath} – 183\cdot 7 \hat{\jmath} – 150\cdot 0 \hat{k} \right) N\)

\(= \left(321\cdot 9 \hat{\imath} – 101\cdot6 \hat{\jmath} – 194\cdot 1\hat{k} \right) N\) (9)

and the magnitude of the resultant is

\(\left|\vec{R} \right| = \sqrt{\left(321\cdot 9 N\right) ^{2} + \left(-101\cdot 6 N\right) ^{2} + \left(- 194\cdot 1\right) ^{2} } = 389\cdot 4 N\) (10)

part (c)

Governing Equations & Computation Once \(\vec{R}\) is known, obtaining its direction angles is straightforward. Applying the equations \(\cos \theta _{x} = {R_{x} }/{\left|\vec{R} \right| }\), \( \cos \theta _{y} = {R_{y} }/{\left|\vec{R} \right| } \) and \(\cos \theta _{z} = {R_{z} }/{\left|\vec{R} \right| }\) gives

\(\cos \theta _{x} = {321\cdot 9 N}/{389\cdot 4 N}\Rightarrow \theta _{x} = \cos ^{-1} \left({321\cdot 9 }/{389\cdot 4 }\right) =34\cdot 2^{\circ }\), (11)

\(\cos \theta _{y} = {-101\cdot 6 }/{389\cdot 4 N}\Rightarrow \theta _{y} = \cos ^{-1} \left({-101\cdot 6 }/{389\cdot 4 }\right) =105\cdot 1^{\circ }\), (12)

\(\cos \theta _{z} = {-194\cdot 1 N}/{389\cdot 4 N}\Rightarrow \theta _{z} = \cos ^{-1} \left({-194\cdot 1 }/{389\cdot 4 }\right) =119\cdot 9^{\circ }\) . (13)

Discussion & Verification As simple checks of accuracy, you should verify that \(\vec{F}\) , \(\vec{P}\) and \(\vec{Q}\) each point in the proper direction and have the correct magnitude. Depending on the number of forces being added and their relative sizes and magnitudes, it may also be possible to verify that some (or all) of the components of \(\vec{R}\) have reasonable direction and size.
