Adding Sound Levels

In this example we wish to explore how to determine the sound level due to multiple sound sources. We can see in Table 15-4 that if we are 1 m away from a lawnmower, the sound level is 107 dB, while if we are 1 m away from a chainsaw, the sound level is 110 dB. Suppose you are 1 m away from both a lawnmower and a chainsaw. What are the resultant sound intensity and sound level?

Admin
Solution

We need to be careful here—while the sound intensities just add, the sound levels, which are on a logarithmic scale, do not. Our strategy will be to determine the sound intensities for each source, add them together, and use that sound intensity to find the resultant sound level. From Table 15-4, we can see that the sound level for the chainsaw is 110 dB, while the sound level for the lawnmower is 107 dB.

We will first do the intensity level calculation for the chainsaw. We use Equation 15-27 and recall that \(I_{0}=10^{-12} W / m ^{2}\):

 

\(\begin{aligned}110 &=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{\text {chainsaw }}}{I_{0}}\right) \\11.0 &=\log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{\text {chainsaw }}}{10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}}\right) \\10^{11.0} &=\frac{I_{\text {chainsaw }}}{10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}} \\I_{\text {chainsaw }} &=10^{11.0} \times 10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}\end{aligned}\)

 

We can perform a similar calculation to find that

 

\(I_{\text {lawnmower }}=10^{10.7} \times 10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}\)

 

We can find the total sound intensity by adding the two intensities found above:

 

\(I=I_{\text {chainsaw }}+I_{\text {lawnmower }}=\left(10^{10.7}+10^{11.0}\right) \times 10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}\)

 

We put this result back in to Equation 15-27 to get the sound level:

 

\(\beta(I)=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I}{I_{0}}\right)\) {15-27}

 

\(\begin{aligned}\beta &=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{\text {chainsaw }}+I_{\text {lawnmower }}}{I_{0}}\right) \\&=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{\left(10^{10.7}+10^{11.0}\right) \times 10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}}{10^{-12} W \cdot m ^{-2}}\right) \\&=10 \log _{10}\left(10^{10.7}+10^{11.0}\right)=112 dB\end{aligned}\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

This result might seem a bit puzzling. One might have guessed that the result would be 207 dB, obtained by simply adding the two sound levels together. We need to recall that while the intensities add directly, the sound levels (dB) do not, which is a consequence of the logarithmic scale we use to calculate the decibel levels. Let’s do a very simple calculation that illustrates exactly how this works. Suppose we have some source that has intensity \(I_{1}\). We then double the intensity to \(I_{2}=2 I_{1}\). Let’s see how the sound level changes.

We again use Equation 15-27 to find

 

\(\begin{aligned}\beta_{1} &=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{1}}{I_{0}}\right) \\\beta_{2} &=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{2}}{I_{0}}\right) \\&=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{2 I_{1}}{I_{0}}\right) \\&=10 \log _{10}\left(\frac{I_{1}}{I_{0}}\right)+10 \log 2 \\&=\beta_{1}+3 dB\end{aligned}\)

 

(Note that we used the property of logarithms that the log of a product is the sum of the logs.) With that result in mind, we can see that we did not quite double the intensity in our example and we had slightly less than a 3 dB increase in the sound level.
