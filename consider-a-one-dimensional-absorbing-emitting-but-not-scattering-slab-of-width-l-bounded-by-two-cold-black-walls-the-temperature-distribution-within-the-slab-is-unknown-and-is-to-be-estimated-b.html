Guest Question.

Consider a one-dimensional, absorbing-emitting (but not scattering) slab of width L, bounded by two cold, black walls. The temperature distribution within the slab is unknown, and is to be estimated by measuring exit intensities on both bounding walls for various angles. The absorption coefficient of the medium at the detector wavelength, κ , is known and constant.

Admin
Solution

Direct Problem. The direct solution for this simple problem is immediately found from equation (13.20) \(I^{+}(\tau ,\mu )=I_{1}(\mu )e^{-\tau /\mu }+\int_{0}^{\tau }{}S(\tau ^{\prime },\mu )e^{-(\tau -\tau ^{\prime })/\mu } \frac{d\tau ^{\prime }}{\mu } , 0\lt \mu \lt 1 , I^{-}(\tau ,\mu )=I_{2}(\mu )e^{(\tau _{L}-\tau )/\mu } – \int_{\tau }^{\tau_{L} }{}S(\tau ^{\prime },\mu )e^{(\tau ^{\prime }-\tau )/\mu } \frac{d\tau ^{\prime }}{\mu } , -1\lt \mu \lt 0\) as

\(I(x,\mu )= -\int_{x}^{L}{}I_{b}(x^{\prime })e^{\kappa (x^{\prime }-x)/\mu }\kappa \frac{dx^{\prime }}{\mu } , \mu \lt 0,\)

\(= \int_{0}^{x}{}I_{b}(x^{\prime })e^{\kappa (x-x^{\prime })/\mu }\kappa \frac{dx^{\prime }}{\mu } , \mu \gt 0,\)

with \(I_{1} = I_{2} = 0\) (cold walls) and S = \(I_{b}\) (no scattering). Letting \(\tau _{L}=\kappa _{L},\xi =x/L\) , and evaluating only the necessary intensities exiting from the faces at ξ = 0, 1 , leads to

\(I(0,\mu )=-\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu }\int_{0}^{1}{}I_{b}(\xi )e^{\tau _{L}\xi /\mu } d\xi , \mu \lt 0,\)

\(I(1,\mu )=\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu }\int_{0}^{1}{}I_{b}(\xi )e^{-\tau _{L}(1-\xi) /\mu } d\xi , \mu \gt 0 .\)

Inverse Problem. We will again assume that the unknown Planck function field \(I_{b}\)(ξ) can be approximated by an N-th order polynomial, or

\(I_{b}(\xi )=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{}a_{n} \xi ^{n}.\)

Substituting this into the direct solution for exiting intensity gives

\(I(0,\mu )=-\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{}a_{n}f_{n}\left\lgroup\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu } \right\rgroup , \mu \lt 0,\) (22.24a)

\(I(1,\mu )= e^{-\tau _{L}/\mu } \sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{}a_{n}f_{n}\left\lgroup\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu } \right\rgroup , \mu \gt 0,\) (22.24b)

\(f_{n}(\tau )=\tau \int_{0}^{1}{}\xi ^{n}e^{\tau \xi }d\xi =e^{\tau }-\frac{w}{\tau }f_{n-1}(\tau )\) .

Since the temperature (or Planck function) is to be found by measuring I(0, μ) and I(1, μ) for a set of I exit angles – 1 < \(μ_{i}\) < +1, and assuming constant weights, the objective function becomes

\(S=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{I}{} (I_{i}-Y_{i})^{2},\)

where the \(I_{i}\) are evaluated from equation (22.24a) or (22.246), depending on whether \(μ_{i}\) is negative or positive, and the \(Y_{i}\) are the corresponding experimental data.

The sensitivity matrix is readily found by differentiating equations (22.24a) and (2224b) with respect to \(a_{n}\) leading to

\(X_{in}=\begin{cases} -f_{n}\left(\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu _{i}} \right) , & \mu _{i} \\ e^{-\tau _{L}/\mu _{i}} f_{n} \left(\frac{\tau _{L}}{\mu _{i}} \right), & \mu _{i}\gt 0 \end{cases}\)

and

\(I_{i}=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{M}{}a_{n}X_{in}\),

since the problem is linear. In order to use the conjugate gradient method, we again have to evaluate the conjugation coefficient \(\gamma ^{k}\) and step size \(\beta ^{k+1}\) this time from

\(\left|\triangledown _{p}S^{k}\right|^{2}=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{}\left\lgroup\sum\limits_{n=0}^{I}{}(I^{k}_{i}-Y_{i})X_{in}^{k} \right\rgroup ^{2}\),

\(\beta ^{k+1}=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{I}{} (I^{k}_{i}-Y_{i}) \sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{} X_{in}^{k}d^{k}_{n}/\sum\limits_{i=1}^{I}{}\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{}(X_{in}^{k} d^{k}_{n})^{2}\) .

The solution procedure is exactly the same as in . Figure 22-3 shows the simulation results for a Planck function field of

\(I_{b}(\xi )=1+3\xi ^{2}-4\xi ^{4}\)

for various optical thicknesses, mid using 20 equally-spaced measurement directions. For errorless measurements \(Y_{1}\) the exact result is recovered for all optical thicknesses. Figure 22-3 shows the estimated Planck function field for measurements that have been given a random Gaussian error, with a relative variance of 3%. It is seen that the Planck function field recovery is rather poor for optically thin slabs, getting more and more accurate as the optical thickness increases (up to a point: at very large \(τ_{L}\) the exiting intensities become independent of the internal temperature field and, thus, the temperature field cannot be recovered).
