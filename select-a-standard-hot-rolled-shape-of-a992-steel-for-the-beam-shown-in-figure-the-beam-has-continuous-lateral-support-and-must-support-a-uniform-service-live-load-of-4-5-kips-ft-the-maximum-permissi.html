Guest Question.

Select a standard hot-rolled shape of A992 steel for the beam shown in Figure. The beam has continuous lateral support and must support a uniform service live load of 4.5 kips/ft. The maximum permissible live load deflection is L/240.

Admin
Solution

Ignore the beam weight initially then check for its effect after a selection is made.
\(w_u = 1.2w_D + 1.6w_L = 1.2(0) + 1.6(4.5) = 7.2 kips/ft\)
Required moment strength \(M_u =\frac{1}{8}w_uL^{2} =\frac{1}{8}(7.2)(30)^{2} = 810.0 ft-kips\)
= required \(\phi_{b} M_{n}\)
The maximum permissible live load deflection is
\(\frac{L}{240} =\frac{30(12)}{240} = 1.5 in.\)
From \(\Delta \leq \frac{5}{384}\frac{wL^{4}}{EI}\), the required moment of inertia is
\(I \geq \frac{5}{384}\frac{wL^{4}}{E \Delta} = \frac{5}{384} \frac{(4.5/12)(30 \times 12)^{4}}{29,000(1.5)} = 1890 in.^{4}\)
Assume that the shape will be compact. For a compact shape with full lateral support,
\(M_n = M_p = F_{y}Z_{x}\)
From \(\phi_{b} M_{n} \geq M_{u},\)
\(\phi_{b}F_{y}Z_{x} \geq M_{u}\)
\(Z_{x} \geq \frac{M_{u}}{\phi_{b}F_{y}} =\frac{810.0(12)}{0.90(50)} = 216 in.^{3}\)

The \(Z_x\) table lists hot-rolled shapes normally used as beams in order of decreasing plastic section modulus. Furthermore, they are grouped so that the shape at the top of each group (in bold type) is the lightest one that has enough section modulus to satisfy a required section modulus that falls within the group. In this example, the shape that comes closest to meeting the section modulus requirement is a W21 \(\times \)93, with\( Z_x = 221 in.^{3}\) , but the lightest one is a W24 \(\times\) 84, with \(Z_x = 224 in.^{3}\). Because section modulus is not directly proportional to area, it is possible to have more section modulus with less area, and hence less weight.

Try a W24\( \times\) 84: This shape is compact, as assumed (noncompact shapes are marked as such in the table); therefore \(M_n = M_p\), as assumed. From the Dimensions and Properties Table, the moment of inertia about the axis of bending is
\(I_{x} = 2370 in.^{4} > 1890 in.^{4} (OK)\)
Account for the beam weight:
\(w_u = 1.2w_D + 1.6w_L = 1.2(0.084) + 1.6(4.5) = 7.301 kips/ft\)
Required moment strength \(= M_u = \frac{1}{8}w_uL^{2} =\frac{1}{8} (7.301)(30)^{2} = 821.4 ft-kips\)
The required section modulus is
\(Z_{x} = \frac{M_u}{\phi_{b}F_{y}} = \frac{821.4(12)}{0.90(50)} = 219 in.^{3} < 224 in.^{3} (OK)\)
In lieu of basing the search on the required section modulus, the design strength \(\phi_{b} M_{p}\) could be used, because it is directly proportional to \(Z_x\) and is also tabulated. The required moment of inertia is unchanged, because the deflection is for live loads, which are unchanged. Next, check the shear.
\(V_{u} =\frac{ w_{u}L}{2}= \frac{7.301(30)}{2} = 110 kips\)
From the \(Z_x \)table,
\(\phi_{v}V_{n} = 340 kips > 110 kips (OK)\)
Use a W24 \(\times \)84.
Ignore the beam weight initially, then check for its effect after a selection is made.
\(w_a = w_D + w_L = 0 + 4.5 = 4.5 kips/ft\)
Required moment strength \(= M_a = \frac{1}{8}w_{u}L^{2} =\frac{1}{8}(4.5)(30)^{2} = 506.3 ft-kips\)
= required \(\frac{M_{n}}{ \Omega _{b}}\)

The maximum permissible live load deflection is
\(\frac{L}{240} = \frac{30(12)}{240} = 1.5 in.\)

 

From \(\Delta \leq \frac{5}{384}\frac{wL^{4}}{EI}\), the required moment of inertia is

 

\(I \geq \frac{5}{384}\frac{wL^{4}}{E \Delta} = \frac{5}{384} \frac{(4.5/12)(30 \times 12)^{4}}{29,000(1.5)} = 1890 in.^{4}\)
Assume that the shape will be compact. For a compact shape with full lateral support,
\(M_n = M_p = F_{y}Z_{x}\)

From\(\frac{M_{p}}{\Omega _{b}} \geq M_{a},\)

\(\frac{F_{y}Z_{x}}{\Omega _{b}}\geq M_{a}\) \(Z_{x} \geq \frac{\Omega _{b}M_{a}}{F_{y}}= \frac{1.67(506.3 \times 12)}{50} = 203 in.^{3}\)

The \(Z_{x}\) table lists hot-rolled shapes normally used as beams in order of decreasing plastic section modulus. They are arranged in groups, with the lightest shape in each group at the top of that group. For the current case, the shape with a section modulus closest to 203 \(in.^{3} \)is a W18 \(\times\) 97, but the lightest shape with sufficient section modulus is a W24 \(\times\) 84, with \(Z_{x} = 224 in.^{3}\) Try a W24\( \times\) 84: This shape is compact, as assumed (if it were noncompact, there would be a footnote in the \(Z_x\) table). Therefore, \(M_{n} = M_{p}\) as assumed. From the Dimensions and Properties Table, the moment of inertia about the axis of bending is
\(I_x = 2370 in.^{4} > 1890 in.^{4} (OK)\)
Account for the beam weight:
\(w_a = w_D + w_L = 0.084 + 4.5 = 4.584 kips/ft\)
\(M_a = \frac{1}{8} w_{a}L^{2} = \frac{1}{8} (4.584)(30)^{2} = 515.7 ft/kips\)
The required plastic section modulus is
\(Z_{x} = \frac{\Omega _{b}M_{a}}{F_{y}}= \frac{1.67(515.7 \times 12)}{50} = 207 in.^{3} < 224 in.^{3} (OK)\)
The required moment of inertia is unchanged, because the deflection is for live loads, which are unchanged. Instead of searching for the required section modulus, the search could be based on the required value of \(M_{p}/\Omega _{b}\), which is also tabulated.
Because \(M_{p}/\Omega _{b}\) is proportional to \(Z_{x}\), the results will be the same.
Another approach is to use the allowable stress for compact laterally supported shapes. From Section 5.5 of this book, with the flexural stress based on the
plastic section modulus,
\(F_b = 0.6F_y = 0.6(50) = 30.0 ksi\)
and the required section modulus (before the beam weight is included) is
\(Z_x = \frac{M_{a}}{F_{b}}=\frac{506.3 \times 12}{30} = 203 in.^{3}\)
Next, check the shear. The required shear strength is
\(V_{a} = \frac{w_{a}L^{2}}{2} = \frac{4.584(30)}{2} = 68.8 kips \)
From the\( Z_{x}\) table, the available shear strength is
\(\frac{V_{n}}{\Omega _{v}}= 227 kips > 68.8 kips (OK) \)
Use a W24 \(\times\) 84.
