Guest Question.

The circular fuselage frame shown in Fig. 5.16(a) supports a load, P, which is reacted to by a shear flow, q (i.e., a shear force per unit length, see Chapter 16), distributed around the circumference of the frame from the fuselage skin. If the bending stiffness of the frame is EI, calculate the distribution of bending moment round the frame.

Admin
Solution

The value and direction of this shear flow are quoted here but are derived from theory established in Section 16.3. From our previous remarks on the effect of symmetry, we observe that there is no shear force atthe section A on the vertical plane of symmetry. The unknowns are therefore the bending moment \(M_{ A }\) and normal force \(N_{ A }\). We proceed, as in the previous example, by writing down the total complementary energy C of the system. Then, neglecting shear strains,

 

\(C=\int_{\text {ring }} \int_{0}^{M} d \theta d M-P \Delta\) (i)

 

in which \(\Delta\) is the deflection of the point of application of P relative to the top of the frame. Note that \(M_{ A }\) and \(N_{ A }\) do not contribute to the complement of the potential energy of the system, since, by symmetry, the rotation and horizontal displacements at A are zero. From the principle of the stationary value of the total complementary energy,

 

\(\frac{\partial C}{\partial M_{ A }}=\int_{\text {ring }} d \theta \frac{\partial M}{\partial M_{ A }}=0\) (ii)

 

and

 

\(\frac{\partial C}{\partial N_{ A }}=\int_{ ring } d \theta \frac{\partial M}{\partial N_{ A }}=0\) (iii)

 

The bending moment at a radial section inclined at an angle \(\theta\) to the vertical diameter is, from Fig. 5.16(c),

 

\(M=M_{ A }+N_{ A } R(1-\cos \theta)+\int_{0}^{\theta} q BD R d \alpha\)

 

or

 

\(M=M_{ A }+N_{ A } R(1-\cos \theta)+\int_{0}^{\theta} \frac{P}{\pi R} \sin \alpha[R-R \cos (\theta-\alpha)] R d \alpha\)

 

which gives

 

\(M=M_{ A }+N_{ A } R(1-\cos \theta)+\frac{P R}{\pi}\left(1-\cos \theta-\frac{1}{2} \theta \sin \theta\right)\) (iv)

 

Hence,

 

\(\frac{\partial M}{\partial M_{ A }}=1, \quad \frac{\partial M}{\partial N_{ A }}=R(1-\cos \theta)\) (v)

 

Assuming that the fuselage frame is linearly elastic, we have, from Eqs. (ii) and (iii),

 

\(2 \int_{0}^{\pi} \frac{M}{E I} \frac{\partial M}{\partial M_{ A }} R d \theta=2 \int_{0}^{\pi} \frac{M}{E I} \frac{\partial M}{\partial N_{ A }} R d \theta=0\) (vi)

 

Substituting from Eqs. (iv) and (v) into Eq. (vi) gives two simultaneous equations:

 

\(-\frac{P R}{2 \pi}=M_{ A }+N_{ A } R\) (vii)

 

\(-\frac{7 P R}{8 \pi}=M_{ A }+\frac{3}{2} N_{ A } R\) (viii)

 

These equations may be written in matrix form as follows:

 

\(\frac{P R}{\pi}\left\{\begin{array}{l}-1 / 2 \\-7 / 8\end{array}\right\}=\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & R \\1 & 3 R / 2\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}M_{ A } \\N_{ A }\end{array}\right\}\) (ix)

 

so that

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{c}M_{ A } \\N_{ A }\end{array}\right\}=\frac{P R}{\pi}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & R \\1 & 3 R / 2\end{array}\right]^{-1}\left\{\begin{array}{l}-1 / 2 \\-7 / 8\end{array}\right\}\)

 

or

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{c} M_{ A } \\ N_{ A } \end{array}\right\}=\frac{P R}{\pi}\left[\begin{array}{cc} 3 & -2 \\ -2 / R & 2 / R \end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l} -1 / 2 \\ -7 / 8\end{array}\right\}\)

 

which gives

 

\(M_{ A }=\frac{P R}{4 \pi}, \quad N_{ A }=\frac{-3 P}{4 \pi}\)

 

The bending moment distribution follows from Eq. (iv) and is

 

\(M=\frac{P R}{4 \pi}(2-\cos \theta-2 \theta \sin \theta)\) (x)

 

The solution of Eq. (ix) involves the inversion of the matrix

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & R \\ 1 & 3 R / 2 \end{array}\right]\)

 

which may be carried out using any of the standard methods detailed in texts on matrix analysis. In this example, Eqs. (vii) and (viii) are clearly most easily solved directly; however, the matrix approach illustrates the technique and serves as a useful introduction to the more detailed discussion in Chapter 6.
