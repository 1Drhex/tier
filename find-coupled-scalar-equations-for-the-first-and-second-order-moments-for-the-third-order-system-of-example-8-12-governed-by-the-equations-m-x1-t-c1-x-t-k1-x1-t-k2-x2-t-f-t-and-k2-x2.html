Guest Question.

Find coupled scalar equations for the first- and second-order moments for the third-order system of governed by the equations

\( m\ddot{X}_{1}(t)+c_{1}\dot{X}_{1}(t)+k_{1}X_{1}(t)+k_{2}X_{2}(t)=F(t) \)

and

\( k_{2}X_{2}(t)=c_{2}[\dot{X}_{1}(t)-\dot{X}_{2}(t)] \)
Admin
Solution

In we showed that this system is governed by Eq. 9.5

\( A\dot{\vec{Y}} (t)+B\vec{Y}(t)=\vec{Q} (t)\)

with

\( \ddot{Y}(t)=\left(\begin{matrix} X_{1}(t) \\ X_{2}(t) \\ \dot{X}_{1}(t) \end{matrix} \right) , A=\left(\begin{matrix} -k_{1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -k_{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & m \end{matrix} \right) , B=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & k_{1} \\ 0 & -k^{2}_{2}/c_{2} & k_{2} \\ k_{1} & k_{2} & c_{1} \end{matrix} \right) , \bar{Q}(t)=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ F(t) \end{matrix} \right) \)

Using Eq. 9.6

\( A\dot{\vec{\mu}} _{Y}(t)+B\vec{\mu}_{Y}=\vec{\mu}_{Q}(t)\)

for the equations for the first-order moments gives three first-order differential equations as

\( -k_{1}\dot{\mu }_{Y_{1}}(t)+k_{1}\mu _{Y_{3}}(t)=0 \) \( -k_{2}\dot{\mu }_{Y_{2}}(t)-(k^{2}_{2}/c_{2})\mu _{Y_{2}}(t)-k_{2}\mu _{Y_{3}}(t)=0 \)

And

\( m\dot{\mu }_{Y_{3}}(t)+k_{1}\mu _{Y_{1}}(t)+k_{2}\mu _{Y_{2}}(t)+c_{1}\mu _{Y_{3}}(t)=\mu _{F}(t) \)

Alternatively, one can simply take the expected values of the original coupled equations of motion, giving two equations, with one of them involving a second derivative:

\( m\ddot{\mu }_{X_{1}}(t)+c_{1}\dot{\mu} _{X_{1}}(t)+k_{1}\mu _{X_{1}}(t)+k_{2}\mu _{X_{2}}(t)=\mu _{F}(t) \)

And

\( k_{2}\mu _{X_{2}}(t)=c_{2}[\dot{\mu }_{X_{1}}(t)-\dot{\mu }_{X_{2}}(t)] \)

Given that \( n_{Y}=3 \) , we see that it is possible to use symmetry to reduce the problem to six simultaneous scalar equations governing six second-moment terms. One can use either the upper triangular portion or the lower triangular portion of Eq. 9.8

\(\frac{d}{dt} \Phi _{YY}(t,t)+(A^{-1}B)\Phi _{YY}(t,t)+\Phi _{YY}(t,t)(A^{-1}B)^{T}=A^{-1}\Phi _{QY}(t,t)+\Phi _{YQ}(t,t)(A^{-1})^{T} \)

to give these six equations. Evaluating \( A^{-1}B \) as

\( A^{-1}B =\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & k_{2}/c_{2} & -1 \\ k_{1}/m & k_{2}/m & c_{1}/m \end{matrix} \right) \)

and proceeding through the lower triangular components of Eq. 9.8 in the order (1,1), (2,1), (2,2), (3,1), (3,2), (3,3) gives the equations as

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y^{2}_{1}(t)]-2E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)]=0 \)

 

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{2}(t)]+\frac{k_{2}}{c_{2}}E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{2}(t)]-E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)] -E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)]=0 \)

 

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y^{2}_{2}(t)]+2\frac{k_{2}}{c_{2}} E[Y^{2}_{2}(t)]-2E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)]=0 \)

 

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)]+\frac{k_{1}}{m}E[Y^{2}_{1}(t)]+\frac{k_{2}}{m}E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{2}(t)]+\frac{c_{1}}{m}E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)]-E[Y^{2}_{3}(t)]=\frac{1}{m}E[F(t)Y_{1}(t)] \)

 

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)]+\frac{k_{1}}{m}E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{2(t)}] +\frac{k_{2}}{m}E[Y^{2}_{2}(t)]+\left(\frac{k_{2}}{c_{2}}+\frac{c_{1}}{m} \right) E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)]-E[Y^{2}_{3}(t)]=\frac{1}{m}E[F(t)Y_{2}(t)] \)

and

\( \frac{d}{dt} E[Y^{2}_{3}(t)]+2\frac{k_{1}}{m}E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)]+2\frac{k_{2}}{m}E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)]+2\frac{c_{1}}{m}E[Y^{2}_{3}(t)]=\frac{2}{m}E[F(t)Y_{3}(t)] \)

Note that it is also possible to write these relationships as one matrix equation of the form

\( \dot{\vec{V}}(t)+G \vec{V} (t)=\psi (t) \)

with a vector of unknowns of

\( \vec{V} (t)=\left(E[Y^{2}_{1}(t)],E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{2}(t)],E[Y^{2}_{2}(t)],E[Y_{1}(t)Y_{3}(t)],E[Y_{2}(t)Y_{3}(t)],E[Y^{2}_{3}(t)]\right) ^{T} \)

and a \( 6\times 6 \) matrix of coefficients. Clearly, this version of the second-moment equations has the same form as the vector equation of motion in Eq. 9.5, or the vector first-moment equation in Eq. 9.6. Alternative ways of finding the solutions of Equation 9.8 will be discussed in Section 9.5.
