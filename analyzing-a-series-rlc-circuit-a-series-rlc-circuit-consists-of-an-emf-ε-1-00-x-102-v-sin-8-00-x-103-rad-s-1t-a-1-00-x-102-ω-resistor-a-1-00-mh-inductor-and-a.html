Analyzing a Series RLC Circuit

A series RLC circuit consists of an emf \(\varepsilon=\left(1.00 \times 10^{2} V \right) \sin \left(\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right) t\right), \text { a } 1.00 \times 10^{2} \Omega\) resistor, a 1.00 mH inductor, and a 1.00 μF capacitor. Determine

(a) the maximum current flow

(b) the phase shift

(c) the maximum voltages across the three components

Admin
Solution

(a) We will first determine the impedance using Equation 26-33:

 

\(Z=\sqrt{R^{2}+\left(X_{L}-X_{C}\right)^{2}}\) {26-33}

 

\(\begin{aligned}Z &=\sqrt{R^{2}+\left(X_{ L }-X_{ C }\right)^{2}} \\&=\sqrt{\left(1.00 \times 10^{2} \Omega\right)^{2}+\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1} \cdot 1.00 mH -\frac{1}{\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right) \cdot 1.00 \mu F }\right)^{2}} \\&=1.54 \times 10^{2} \Omega\end{aligned}\)

 

Using this result, we can determine \(i_{\max }\):

 

\(i_{\max }=\frac{\varepsilon_{\max }}{Z}=\frac{1.00 \times 10^{2} V }{1.54 \times 10^{2} \Omega}=0.650 A\)

 

(b) The phase shift can be determined using Equation 26-34:

 

\(\tan \phi=\frac{i_{\max }\left(X_{ L }-X_{ C }\right)}{i_{\max } R}=\frac{X_{ L }-X_{ C }}{R}\) {26-34}

 

\(\begin{aligned}\tan \phi &=\frac{X_{ L }-X_{ C }}{R} \\&=\frac{\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 mH )-\frac{1}{\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 \mu F )}}{1.00 \times 10^{2} \Omega}=-1.17\end{aligned}\)

 

Therefore, \(\phi=\tan ^{-1}(-1.17)=-49.5^{\circ}\).

 

(c) We find the maximum voltages across the resistor, the inductor, and the capacitor from Equations 26-14, 26-18, 26-24, respectively:

 

\(\frac{d i}{d t}=\frac{\varepsilon(t)}{L}=\frac{\varepsilon_{\max }}{L} \sin (\omega t)\) {26-14}

 

\(V_{ L , \max }=i_{\max } X_{ L }\) {26-18}

 

\(V_{ C , \max }=i_{\max } X_{ C }\) {26-24}

 

\(v_{ R , \max }=i_{\max } R=(0.650 A )\left(1.00 \times 10^{2} \Omega\right)=65.0 V\)

 

\(v_{ L , \max }=i_{\max } X_{ L }=(0.650 A )\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 mH )=5.20 V\)

 

\(v_{ C , \max }=i_{\max } X_{ C }=(0.649 A ) \frac{1}{\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 \mu F )}=81.2 V\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

We can get a sense of what is going on in the circuit from the reactances:

 

\(X_{ L }=\omega L=\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 mH )=8.00 \Omega\)

 

\(X_{ C }=\frac{1}{\omega C}=\frac{1}{\left(8.00 \times 10^{3} rad \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(1.00 \mu F )}=1.25 \times 10^{2} \Omega\)

 

The capacitive reactance is much larger than the inductive reactance but comparable to the resistance. As such, we would expect the circuit to behave somewhere in between a purely resistive circuit and a purely capacitive circuit. In a purely resistive circuit, the current is in phase with the applied emf. In a purely capacitive circuit, the current leads the applied emf. For this particular circuit, we found a phase angle of -49.5°. The minus sign indicates that the current is leading the emf, and the 49.5° angle indicates that the circuit is somewhere between a purely resistive circuit \(( \phi =0)\) and a purely capacitive circuit \(\left(\phi=-90^{\circ}\right),\) as we predicted from the reactances.
