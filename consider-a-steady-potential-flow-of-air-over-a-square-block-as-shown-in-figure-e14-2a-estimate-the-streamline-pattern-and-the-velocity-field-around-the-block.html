Guest Question.

Consider a steady potential flow of air over a square block, as shown in Figure E14.2a. Estimate the streamline pattern and the velocity field around the block.

Admin
Solution

The stream function is described by Eq. 14.5.1. Since the flow is symmetrical we will consider only one quarter of the domain, as shown in Figure E14.2b. This domain extends, far upstream of the block where the flow is uniform and the free stream velocity \(U_{\infty}=10 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\), and far above the block where the flow is expected to be uniform as well. The exact distances where the left and top boundaries need to be located depend on the block dimensions and fluid velocity. For the purpose of demonstrating the method of using finite differences for solving two-dimensional, steady potential flows we will select a domain with a reasonable number of grid points with \(\Delta x=\Delta y\), as shown in Figure \(\mathrm{E} 14.2 \mathrm{c}\). For this domain, we will specify \(\psi=0\) on the lower boundary; \(\psi=U_{\infty} H\) on the top boundary; \(\psi=U_{\infty} y\) on the left boundary, since the flow is assumed to be uniform; and \(\partial \psi / \partial x=0\), due to symmetry, on the right boundary. The problem is non-dimensionalized by defining the following dimensionless variables:

\(\frac{\partial^{2} \psi}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} \psi}{\partial y^{2}}=0 (14.5.1)\)

\(Y=\frac{y}{H}, \quad X=\frac{x}{H}, \quad \Psi=\frac{\psi}{U_{\infty} H}\)

The dimensionless form of Eq. 14.5.1 is \(\partial^{2} \Psi / \partial X^{2}+\partial^{2} \Psi / \partial Y^{2}=0\) with the boundary conditions shown in Figure E14.2.c. Similarly, Eq. \(14.5 .3\) is rewritten, with \(\Delta x=\Delta y\), using the dimensionless stream function \(\Psi\) as

\(2 \psi_{i, j}\left(\frac{1}{\Delta x^{2}}+\frac{1}{\Delta y^{2}}\right)=\frac{\psi_{i+1, j}+\psi_{i-1, j}}{\Delta x^{2}}+\frac{\psi_{i, j+1}+\psi_{i, j-1}}{\Delta y^{2}} (14.5.3)\)

\(\Psi_{i, j}=\frac{1}{4}\left[\Psi_{i-1, j}+\Psi_{i, j-1}+\Psi_{i+1, j}+\Psi_{i, j+1}\right]\)

The grid shown in Figure E14.2c has 29 grid points. In order to demonstrate the method, the difference equation will be written for nodes \(1,9,11\), and 22 only. Similarly, difference equations can be written for the other grid points. Hence,

\(\begin{array}{l}\text { Node } 1: \Psi_{2}+\Psi_{5}+\Delta Y-4 \Psi_{1}=0 \\\text { Node } 9: \Psi_{5}+\Psi_{10}+\Psi_{16}+3 \Delta Y-4 \Psi_{9}=0 \\\text { Node } 11: \Psi_{7}+\Psi_{10}+\Psi_{12}+\Psi_{18}-4 \Psi_{11}=0 \\\text { Node } 22: \Psi_{15}+2 \Psi_{21}+\Psi_{29}-4 \Psi_{22}=0\end{array}\)

where we have used central differences to represent the right boundary condition as

\(\frac{\partial \Psi_{i}}{\partial X}=\frac{\Psi_{i+1}-\Psi_{i-1}}{2 \Delta X}=0 \quad \therefore \Psi_{i+1}=\Psi_{i-1}\)

After writing the difference equations at all 29 grid points, a system of 29 linear algebraic equations results. The system can be represented in matrix form as

\(\mathbf{A} \Psi=\mathbf{b}\)

\(=\left[\begin{array}{ccccccccc}-4 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 1 & -4 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ a_{N 1} & a_{N 2} & a_{N 3} & a_{N 4} & a_{N 5} & a_{N 6} & a_{N 7} & \ldots & -4\end{array}\right] \quad \Psi=\left[\begin{array}{c}\Psi_{1} \\ \Psi_{2} \\ \vdots \\ \Psi_{N}\end{array}\right] \mathbf{b}=\left[\begin{array}{c}b_{1} \\ b_{2} \\ \vdots \\ b_{N}\end{array}\right]\)

For the given example the values of the \(b_{i} \cdot \mathrm{s}\) are as follows:

\(b_{1}=-\Delta Y, b_{5}=-2 \Delta Y, b_{9}=-3 \Delta Y, b_{16}=-4 \Delta Y, b_{23}=-5 \Delta Y-1, b_{24} \ldots b_{29}=-1\) \(b_{2}=b_{3}=b_{4}=b_{6}=b_{7}=b_{8}=b_{10} \ldots b_{15}=b_{17} \ldots b_{22}=0\)

Starting with an initial guess of \(\Psi_{i}=0\) at all grid points and using the Gauss-Seidel iterative method, the following solution results. The grid point numbers are listed in the shaded columns, and the values of \(\Psi\) at the corresponding grid points are listed in the adjacent columns.

23 0.825 24 0.815 25 0.803 26 0.788 27 0.774 28 0.765 29 0.762
16 0.651 17 0.632 18 0.607 19 0.576 20 0.542 21 0.524 22 0.519
9 0.48 10 0.455 11 0.419 12 0.365 13 0.295 14 0.271 15 0.265
5 0.315 6 0.29 7 0.249 8 0.171
1 0.155 2 0.14 3 0.115 4 0.072

The solution is also shown in Fig. E14.2-1 as a plot of the \(\Psi\) contours in the domain selected. The plot shows the variation in the values of \(\Psi\) as a function of \(X\) and \(Y\).
