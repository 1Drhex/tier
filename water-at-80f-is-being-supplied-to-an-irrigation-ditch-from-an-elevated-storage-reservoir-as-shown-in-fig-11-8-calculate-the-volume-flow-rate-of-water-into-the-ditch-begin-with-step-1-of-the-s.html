Guest Question.

Water at 80°F is being supplied to an irrigation ditch from an elevated storage reservoir as shown in Fig. 11.8. Calculate the volume flow rate of water into the ditch.

Begin with Step 1 of the solution procedure by writing the energy equation. Use A and B as the reference points and simplify the equation as much as possible.

Admin
Solution

Compare this with your solution:

\( \frac{p_A}{\gamma} +z_A + \frac{v^2_A}{2g}-h_L = \frac{p_B}{\gamma} +z_B + \frac{v^2_B}{2g}\)

Because \( p_A = p_B = 0,\) and \( v_A \) is approximately zero, then

\( z_A -h_L = z_B+ \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \)

\( z_A -z_B = \left(v^2_B/2g\right) +h_L\) (11–3)

Notice that the stream of water at point B has the same velocity as that inside the pipe.

The elevation difference, \( z_A – z_B,\) is known to be 40 ft. However, the energy losses that make up \( h_L \) all depend on the unknown velocity, \( v_B \). Thus, iteration is required. Do Step 3 of the solution procedure now.

There are four components of the total energy loss \( h_L \) :

\( h_L = h_1+h_2+h_3+h_4\)

where

\(h_1 = 1.0\left(v^2_B/2g\right) \) (entrance loss)

\( h_2 = f\left(L/D\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \) (pipe friction loss)

\(= f \left(330/0.3355\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right)\)

\(= 985f \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \)

\(h_3 =f_T\left(L_e/D\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \) (long-radius elbow)

\(= 20f_T \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \)

\(h_4 = f_T\left(L_e/D\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \) (half-open gate valve)

\(= 160f_T \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \)

From Table 10.5, we find \(f_T = 0.016\) for a 4-in steel pipe. Then we have

\(h_L = \left(1.0+985f+20f_T+160f_T\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \)

\(= \left(3.88+985f\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right) \) (11–5)

Now substitute this expression for\( h_L\) into Eq. (11–4) and solve for \( v_B\) in terms of f.

You should have

\( v_B = \sqrt{2580/\left(4.88+985f\right) } \)

Now,

\( z_A-z_B = \left(v^2_B/2g\right) +h_L\)

\( 40ft = \left(v^2_B/2g\right) +\left(3.88+985f\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right)\)

\( =\left(4.88+985f\right) \left(v^2_B/2g\right)\)

Solving for \( v_B\), we get

\( v_B = \sqrt{\frac{2g\left(40\right) }{4.88+985f} } =\sqrt{\frac{2580}{4.88+985f} } \) (11–6)

Equation (11–6) represents the completion of Step 4 of the procedure. Now do Steps 5 and 6.

\( N_R = \frac{v_B D}{\nu} =\frac{v_B\left(0.3355\right) }{9.15 \times 10^{-6}} = \left(0.366 \times 10^5\right) v_B\) (11–7)

\( D/\epsilon = \left(0.3355/1.5 \times 10^{-4}\right)=2235 \)

Step 7 is the start of the iteration process. What is the possible range of values for the friction factor for this system?

Because \( D/\epsilon =2235 \), the lowest possible value of f is 0.0155 for very high Reynolds numbers and the highest possible value is 0.039 for a Reynolds number of 4000. The initial trial value of f must be in this range. Use f = 0.020 and complete Steps 8 and 9.

We find the values for velocity and the Reynolds number by using Eqs. (11–6) and (11–7):

\( v_B= \sqrt{\frac{2580}{4.88+\left(985\right)\left(0.02\right) } } =\sqrt{105.0} =10.25ft/s\)

\( N_R = \left(0.366 \times 10^5\right) \left(10.25\right) = 3.75 \times 10^5\)

Now do Step 10.

You should have f = 0.0175. Because this is different from the initial trial value of f, Steps 8–11 must be repeated now.

Using f = 0.0175, we get

\( v_B= \sqrt{\frac{2580}{4.88+\left(985\right)\left(0.0175\right) } }=\sqrt{116.6} = 10.8ft/s\)

\( N_R = \left(0.366 \times 10^5\right) \left(10.8\right) = 3.94 \times 10^5\)

The new value of f is 0.0175, which is unchanged, and the computed value for \( v_B \) is correct. Therefore, we have

\( v_B = 108ft/s \)

\( Q = A_B v_B = \left(0.0884ft^2\right) \left(10.8ft/s\right) =0.955 ft^3/s\)

This programmed example problem is concluded.
