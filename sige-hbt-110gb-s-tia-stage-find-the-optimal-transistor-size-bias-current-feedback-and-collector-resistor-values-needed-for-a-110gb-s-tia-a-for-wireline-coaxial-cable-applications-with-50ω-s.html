Guest Question.

SiGe HBT 110Gb/s TIA stage

Find the optimal transistor size, bias current, feedback, and collector resistor values needed for a 110Gb/s TIA:

(a) for wireline coaxial cable applications with 50Ω signal source impedance and 20fF pad capacitance;

(b) for a fiber-optic receiver with 15fF diode capacitance and 20fF of pad and device capacitance at the input.

The following technology data are available. 280GHz SiGe HBT parameters for emitter width of \(0.1 \mu \mathrm{m}\) . Technology data are provided per emitter length \(l_{E}\) or per emitter area \(w_{E} ì_{E}\):

\(J_{O P T}=10\mathrm{~mA} / \mu\mathrm{m}^{2}\) ; \(w_{E}=0.1 \mu \mathrm{m}\) ;\( \beta=500\) ; \(V_{C E S A T}=0.3\mathrm{~V}\),\( V_{B E}=0.9 \mathrm{~V}\), peak \(f_{T}=280 \mathrm{GHz}\). \(\tau_{F}=0.25 \mathrm{ps}\) ;\( C_{j e}=17 \mathrm{fF} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\) ;\( C_{b c}^{\prime}=20 \mathrm{fF} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\) ;\( C_{c s}=1.2 \mathrm{fF} / \mu \mathrm{m}\)

\(R_{b}^{\prime} = 75 \Omega \times \mu \mathrm{m}\), \(r_{E}^{\prime}=2 \Omega \times \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\),\( R_{c}^{\prime}=20 \Omega \times \mu \mathrm{m}\)

At \(88 \mathrm{GHz}\):\(\sqrt{\frac{1}{\frac{G_{H B T}}{R_{H B T}}+G_{C, H B T}^{2}+B_{H B T}^{2}}}=5.98 \cdot 10^{13} \mu \mathrm{m}\).

Admin
Solution

Step 1 \(J_{O P T}\) has been set at \(10 \mathrm{~m} \mathrm{~A} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\) for both cases.

(a) \(50 \Omega\) signal source.

Step 2 We choose \(\Delta V=400 \mathrm{mV}\) which give \(S A_{V 0}=-\Delta V /\left(4 V_{T}\right)=-4\). We can quickly verify that \(f_{2}>\sqrt{2} \cdot B W_{3 \mathrm{~dB}}=1.414 \cdot 88 \cdot 10^{9}=124.4 \mathrm{GHz}\).

We are now in a position to calculate the frequency of the output pole from (8.44)

\(f_2\approx \frac{1}{2\pi R_C[C_{bc}+C_{cs}]} = \frac{1}{2\pi R_C[C^\prime _{bc}\cdot w_E+C^\prime _{cs}]l_{EOPT}}\). (8.44)

\(f_{2}=\frac{J_{O P T} w_{E}}{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 2 \cdot V_{T} \cdot\left|A_{V 0}\right|\left(C_{b c}^{\prime} w_{E}+C_{c s}^{\prime}\right)}=\frac{10 \frac{\mathrm{mA}}{\mu \mathrm{m}^{2}} \cdot 0.1 \mu m}{6.28 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.02587 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \frac{\mathrm{fF}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=240.4 \mathrm{GHz}\)

This pole is much higher than 124.4GHz which means that in theory we could afford higher gain. However, we have ignored any load capacitance!

Step 3 From (8.34), we get an initial value for the optimal emitter width by ignoring the terms in\(R_{F}\). We obtain \(l_{E O P T}=2.166 \mu \mathrm{m}\). By assuming a \(25 \%\) larger value in anticipation of the contribution of the terms in \(R_{F,} l_{E O P T}=2.7 \mu \mathrm{m}\) . As a result, \(I_{C}=2.7 \mathrm{~mA}\) . \(R_{C}=\Delta V /\left(2 I_{C}\right)=74 \Omega\) ; \(g_{m}=\frac{2.7 \mathrm{~mA}}{25.867 \mathrm{mV}}=104.4 \mathrm{mS}\) ;\( r_{E}=7.4 \Omega\), \(g_{\text {meff }}=58.9 \mathrm{mS}\),\( C_{\text {bediff }}=14.725 \mathrm{fF}\),\( C_{\text {jbeeff }}=2.59 \mathrm{fF}\), \(C_{\text {beff }}=C_{\text {bediff }}+C_{\text {jbeeff }}=17.31 \mathrm{fF}\), \(C_{b c}=5.4 \mathrm{fF}\) and \(C_{T}=C_{B P}+C_{\text {in }}=20 \mathrm{fF}+17.31 \mathrm{fF}+27 \mathrm{fF}=64.31 \mathrm{fF}\)

\(l_{EOPT}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{1}{Z_0} +\frac{1}{R_E} \frac{1}{1+\omega ^2_f} \right)^2 +\left(\frac{1}{R_E} \frac{\omega _f}{1+\omega ^2_f}\right)^2 } \sqrt{\frac{1}{\frac{G^\prime _u}{R_{HBT}} +G^{\prime 2}_C+B^{\prime 2}_C} } \\ = \frac{1}{\omega } \left(\frac{1}{Z_0} +\frac{1}{R_E} \frac{1}{1+\omega ^2_f} \right)\sqrt{\frac{1}{\frac{G_{HBT}}{R_{HBT}} +G^{2}_{C,HBT} +B^{2}_{C,HBT}}}\) (8.34)

We can now calculate the feedback resistance

\(R_{F}=\frac{\sqrt{2} \cdot\left|A_{V 0}\right|}{\omega C_{T}}=\frac{\sqrt{2} \cdot 4}{6.28 \cdot 88 \cdot 10^{9} \cdot 64 \cdot 3 \cdot 10^{-15}}=159.2 \Omega\)

Step 4 Let us recalculate \(l_{EOPT}\) from (8.34) now that we have a value for \(R_{F}\) . For now, we will ignore \(L_{F}\) and, therefore, \(ω_{F}\) = 0. We obtain \(l_{EOPT}\) = 2.84μm, which is less than 5% larger than the initial estimate, so we will not bother changing it.

(b) In this case, Step 2 is identical to case (a).

Step 3 Again, we can get an initial estimate of \(l_{EOPT}\) from (8.38) ignoring the terms in \(R_{F}\) which will be much smaller this time because they appear as squared. We obtain

\(l_{\mathrm{EOPT}}=\frac{1}{\omega _{3dB}}\sqrt{\frac{1}{R^2_F \left(1+\omega ^2_f\right)^2}+\left(\omega _{3dB}C_D-\frac{\omega _f}{R_F(1+\omega_f^2)} \right)^2 } \sqrt{\frac{1}{\frac{G_{\mathrm{HBT}}}{R_{\mathrm{HBT}}}+G_{C, \mathrm{HBT}}^{2}+B_{\mathrm{HBT}}^{2}}}\) (8.38)

\(l_{\mathrm{EOPT}}=\left(C_{D}+C_{B P}\right) \sqrt{\frac{1}{\frac{G_{\mathrm{HBT}}}{R_{\mathrm{HBT}}}+G_{C, \mathrm{HBT}}^{2}+B_{\mathrm{HBT}}^{2}}}=35 \mathrm{fF} \cdot 5.98 \times 10^{13}=2.1 \mu \mathrm{m}\) .

In reality, if we account for the feedback inductance \(L_{F}\), an even smaller transistor size and input capacitance is possible. As a result \(I_{C}=2.1 \mathrm{~mA}\) . \(R_{C}=\Delta V /\left(2 I_{C}\right)=92.2 \Omega\) \(g_{m}=\frac{2.1 \mathrm{~mA}}{25.867 \mathrm{mV}}=81.17 \mathrm{mS}\) ; \( r_{E}=9.5 \Omega\) , \( g_{meff}=45.82 \mathrm{mS}\) , \(C_{beff}=C_{bediff}+C_{bejeff }=13.46 \mathrm{fF}\) ,\( C_{b c}=4.2 \mathrm{fF}\) and \(C_{T}=C_{D}+C_{B P}+C_{i n}=15 \mathrm{fF}+20 \mathrm{fF}+13.46 \mathrm{fF}+21 \mathrm{fF}=69.46\textrm{fF} \) . We can now calculate the feedback resistance \( R _ { F } = \frac { \sqrt { 2 } \cdot ( A _ { v _ { 0 } } ) } { \omega C _ {T} } =\frac{\sqrt{2} \cdot 4}{6.28 \cdot 88 \cdot 10^{9} \cdot 69.46 \cdot 10^{-13}}=147.36 \Omega\) and the frequency of the input pole
\(f_{1}=\frac{1}{2 \pi \cdot R_{F} \cdot C_{T}}=\frac{1}{6.28 \times 147.36 \times 69.46 \times 10^{-15}}=15.55 \mathrm{GHz}\)

Step 4 Let us recalculate \(l_{EOPT}\) from (8.38) now that we have a value for \(R_{f}\) . For now, we will ignore \(L_{F}\) and, therefore, \(ω_{F}\) = 0. We obtain \(l_{EOPT}\) = 2.22μm, which, again, is less than 5% larger than the initial estimate, so we will not bother changing it.

We are now in a position to calculate the frequency of the output pole from (8.44)

\(f_{2}=\frac{J_{O P T} w_{E}}{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 2 \cdot V_{T} \cdot\left|A_{V 0}\right|\left(C_{b c}^{\prime} w_{E}+C_{c s}^{\prime}\right)}=\frac{10 \frac{\mathrm{mA}}{\mu \mathrm{m}^{2}} \cdot 0.1 \mu \mathrm{m}}{6.28 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.02587 \cdot 4 \cdot(2+1.2) \frac{\mathrm{fF}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=240.4 \mathrm{GHz}\)

This pole is much higher than \(f _1\) but we have ignored any load capacitance. What if the TIA has a fanout of 1, that is \(C_{L}= C_{IN} = 34.46fF\)? In that case, \(f_{2}\) becomes

\(f_{2}=\frac{J_{O P T} w_{E}}{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 2 \cdot V_{T} \cdot\left|A_{V 0}\right|\left(6 \cdot C_{b c}^{\prime} w_{E}+C_{c s}^{\prime}+C_{b e f f}^{\prime} W_{E}\right)}\\ =\frac{10 \frac{\mathrm{mA}}{\mu \mathrm{m}^{2}} \cdot 0.1 \mu \mathrm{m}}{6.28 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.02587 \cdot 4 \cdot(12+1.2+6.4) \frac{\mathrm{fF}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=39.3 \mathrm{GHz}\)

This is definitely so low, that even with inductive peaking we cannot push \(f_{2}\) high enough for a Butterworth response which requires that \( f_{2}>2 \cdot \left|A_{V}\right| \cdot f_{1}=124.4 \mathrm{GHz}\). It is obvious that only an emitter-follower stage can be used as load to minimize the load capacitance. Assuming an equal size emitter-follower as load (similar to the topology in Figure 8.9), theoutput pole frequency becomes

\(f_{2}=\frac{J_{O P T} w_{E}}{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 2 \cdot V_{T} \cdot A_{V 0}\left(2 \cdot C_{b c}^{\prime} w_{E}+C_{c s}^{\prime}+C_{b e f f}^{\prime} W_{E}\right)}\\ =\frac{10 \frac{\mathrm{mA}}{\mu \mathrm{m}^{2}} \cdot 0.1 \mu \mathrm{m}}{6.28 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.02587 \cdot 4 \cdot(4+1.2+6.4) \frac{\mathrm{fF}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=66.32 \mathrm{GHz}\)

This falls short of the 124.4GHz target, so:

Step 5 If we apply inductive peaking at the output node, then we can push \(f_{2}\) to at least 106GHz. We need the feedback inductor as well to further push the bandwidth. Alternatively, we may have to accept a 70% bandwidth rather than 80% bandwidth with respect to data rate, which will result in some ISI and larger rise and fall times.

Note from (a) and (b) that, as mentioned, the output pole does not depend on the size of the transistor, only on the chosen topology, its gain, and its fanout. Therefore, we should check the output pole first to determine what the maximum possible gain should be
