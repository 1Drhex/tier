Guest Question.

Negotiating a Curb

(A) Estimate the magnitude of the force \(\vec{F}\) a person must apply to a wheelchair’s main wheel to roll up over a sidewalk curb (Fig. 12.10a). This main wheel that comes in contact with the curb has a radius r, and the height of the curb is h.

(B) Determine the magnitude and direction of \(\vec{R}\).

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize Think about wheelchair access to buildings. Generally, there are ramps built for individuals in wheelchairs. Steplike structures such as curbs are serious barriers to a wheelchair.

Categorize Imagine the person exerts enough force so that the bottom of the main wheel just loses contact with the lower surface and hovers at rest. We model the wheel in this situation as a rigid object in equilibrium.

Analyze Usually, the person’s hands supply the required force to a slightly smaller wheel that is concentric with the main wheel. For simplicity, let’s assume the radius of this second wheel is the same as the radius of the main wheel. Let’s estimate a combined gravitational force of magnitude \(m g=1400 \mathrm{~N}\) for the person and the wheelchair, acting along a line of action passing through the axle of the main wheel, and choose a wheel radius of \(r=\) \(30 \mathrm{~cm} .\) We also pick a curb height of \(h=10 \mathrm{~cm}\). Let’s also assume the wheelchair and occupant are symmetric and each wheel supports a weight of \(700 \mathrm{~N}\). We then proceed to analyze only one of the main wheels. Figure \(12.10 \mathrm{~b}\) shows the geometry for a single wheel.

When the wheel is just about to be raised from the street, the normal force exerted by the ground on the wheel at point \(B\) goes to zero. Hence, at this time only three forces act on the wheel as shown in the force diagram in Figure \(12.10 \mathrm{c}\). The force \(\overrightarrow{\mathbf{R}}\), which is the force exerted by the curb on the wheel, acts at point \(A\), so if we choose to have our axis of rotation be perpendicular to the page and pass through point \(A\), we do not need to include \(\overrightarrow{\mathbf{R}}\) in our torque equation. The moment arm of \(\overrightarrow{\mathbf{F}}\) relative to an axis through \(A\) is given by \(2 r-h\) (see Fig. 12.10c).

Use the triangle \(O A C\) in Figure \(12.10b\) to find the moment arm \(d\) of the gravitational force \(m \overrightarrow{\mathrm{g}}\) acting on the wheel relative to an axis through point \(A\) :

\(\text { (1) } d=\sqrt{r^{2}-(r-h)^{2}}=\sqrt{2 r h-h^{2}}\)

Apply the second condition for equilibrium to the wheel, taking torques about an axis through \(A\) :

\(\text { (2) } \sum \tau_{A}=m g d-F(2 r-h)=0\)

Substitute for \(d\) from Equation (1):

\(m g \sqrt{2 r h-h^{2}}-F(2 r-h)=0\)

 

Solve for \(F\) :

\(\text { (3) } F=\frac{m g \sqrt{2 r h-h^{2}}}{2 r-h}\)

Simplify:

\(F=m g \frac{\sqrt{h} \sqrt{2 r-h}}{2 r-h}=m g \sqrt{\frac{h}{2 r-h}}\)

Substitute the known values:

\(\begin{aligned}F &=(700 \mathrm{~N}) \sqrt{\frac{0.1 \mathrm{~m}}{2(0.3 \mathrm{~m})-0.1 \mathrm{~m}}} \\&=3 \times 10^{2} \mathrm{~N}\end{aligned}\)

Apply the first condition for equilibrium to the \(x\) and \(y\) components of the forces on the wheel:

\(\text { (4) } \sum F_{x}=F-R \cos \theta=0\)

\(\text { (5) } \sum F_{y}=R \sin \theta-m g=0\)

Divide Equation (5) by Equation (4):

\(\frac{R \sin \theta}{R \cos \theta}=\tan \theta=\frac{m g}{F}\)

Solve for the angle \(\theta\) :

\(\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{m g}{F}\right)=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{700 \mathrm{~N}}{300 \mathrm{~N}}\right)=70^{\circ}\)

Solve Equation (5) for \(R\) and substitute numerical values:

\(R=\frac{m g}{\sin \theta}=\frac{700 \mathrm{~N}}{\sin 70^{\circ}}=8 \times 10^{2} \mathrm{~N}\)

Finalize Notice that we have kept only one digit as significant. (We have written the angle as \(70^{\circ}\) because \(7 \times 10^{1\circ}\) is awkward!) The results indicate that the force that must be applied to each wheel is substantial. You may want to estimate the force required to roll a wheelchair up a typical sidewalk accessibility ramp for comparison.
