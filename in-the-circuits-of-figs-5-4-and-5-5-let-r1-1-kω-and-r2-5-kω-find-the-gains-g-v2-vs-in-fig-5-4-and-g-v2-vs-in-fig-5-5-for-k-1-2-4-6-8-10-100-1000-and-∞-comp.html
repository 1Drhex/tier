Guest Question.

In the circuits of Figs. 5-4 and 5-5 let \(R_{1}=1 k \Omega \text { and } R_{2}=5 k \Omega\). Find the gains \(G^{+}=v_{2} / v_{s}\) in Fig. 5-4 and \(G^{-}=v_{2} / v_{s}\) in Fig. 5-5 for k = 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 100, 1000, and ∞. Compare the results.

Admin
Solution

From (5) in Example 5.3, at R1 = 1 kΩ and R2 = 5 kΩ we have

\(\frac{v_2}{v_s}=\frac{R_2k}{R_2+R_1-R_1k} =(1-b)\frac{k}{1-bk} \) where \(b=\frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2} \) (5)

\(G^{+}=\frac{v_{2}}{v_{s}}=\frac{5 k}{6-k}\) (31)

In Example 5.4 we found

\(G^{-}=\frac{v_{2}}{v_{s}}=-\frac{5 k}{6+k}\) (32)

The gains G– and G+ are calculated for nine values of k in Table 5-4. As k becomes very large, G+ and G– approach the limit gain of -5, which is the negative of the ratio R2/R1 and is independent of k. The circuit of Fig. 5-5 (with negative feedback) is always stable and its gain monotonically approaches the limit gain. However, the circuit of Fig. 5-4 (with positive feedback) is unstable. The gain G+ becomes very large as k approaches six. At k = 6, G+ = ∞.
