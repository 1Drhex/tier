Guest Question.

Water flows uniformly in a rectangular channel of width b and depth y.

FIND Determine the aspect ratio, b/y, for the best hydraulic cross section.

Admin
Solution

The uniform flow is given by \(Q=\frac{\kappa}{n} AR^{{2}/{3}}_{h}S^{{1}/{2}}_{0}\) as

\(Q=\frac{\kappa}{n} AR^{{2}/{3}}_{h}S^{{1}/{2}}_{0}\) (1)

where A = by and P = b + 2y, so that \(R_{h}={A}/{P}={by}/{(b+2y)}\). We rewrite the hydraulic radius in terms of A as

\(R_{h}=\frac{A}{(2y+b)}=\frac{A}{(2y+{A}/{y})} =\frac{Ay}{(2y^{2}+A)} \)

so that Eq. 1 becomes

\(Q=\frac{\kappa }{n}A (\frac{Ay}{2y^{2}+A} )^{{2}/{3}}S_{0}^{{1}/{2}}\)

This can be rearranged to give

\(A^{{5}/{2}}y=K(2y^{2}+A)\) (2)

where \(K=({nQ}/{\kappa S_{0}^{{1}/{2}}})^{{3}/{2}}\) is a constant. The best hydraulic section is the one that gives the minimum A for all y. That is, \({dA}/{dy}=0\).

By differentiating Eq. 2 with respect to y, we obtain

\(\frac{5}{2}A^{{3}/{2}}\frac{dA}{dy} y+A^{{5}/{2}}=K(4y+\frac{dA}{dy} )\)

which, with \({dA}/{dy}=0\), reduces to

\(A^{{5}/{2}}=4Ky\) (3)

With \(K={A^{{5}/{2}}y}/{(2y^{2}+A)}\) from Eq. 2, Eq. 3 can be written in the form

\(A^{{5}/{2}}=\frac{4A^{{5}/{2}}y^{2}}{(2y^{2}+A)} \)

which simplifies to \(y=({A}/{2})^{{1}/{2}}\). Thus, because A = by, the best hydraulic cross section for a rectangular shape has a width b and a depth

\(y=(\frac{A}{2} )^{{1}/{2}}=(\frac{by}{2} )^{{1}/{2}}\)

or

\(2y^{2}=by\)

That is, the rectangle with the best hydraulic cross section is twice as wide as it is deep, or

b/y = 2

COMMENTS A rectangular channel with b/y = 2 will give the smallest area (and smallest wetted perimeter) for a given flowrate. Conversely, for a given area, the largest flowrate in a rectangular channel will occur when b/y = 2. For A = by = constant, if \(y\rightarrow 0 then b\rightarrow \infty\), and the flowrate is small because of the large wetted perimeter \(P=b+2y\rightarrow \infty \). The maximum Q occurs when y = b/2. However, as seen in Fig. E10.5a, the maximum represented by this optimal configuration is a rather weak one. For example, for aspect ratios between 1 and 4, the flowrate is within 96% of the maximum flowrate obtained with the same area and b/y = 2.

An alternate but equivalent method to obtain the aforementioned answer is to use the fact that \({dR_{h}}/{dy}=0\), which follows from Eq. 1 using \({dQ}/{dy}=0\) ( constant flowrate) and \({dA}/{dy}=0\) ( best hydraulic cross section has minimum area). Differentiation of \(R_{h}={Ay}/{(2y^{2}+A)}\) with constant A gives \({b}/{y}=2\) when \({dR_{h}}/{dy}=0\).

The best hydraulic cross section can be calculated for other shapes in a similar fashion. The results (given here without proof) for circular, rectangular, trapezoidal (with 60° sides), and triangular shapes are shown in Fig. E10.5b.
