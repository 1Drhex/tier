Guest Question.

Dimensioning a Footing Under a Column with Eccentric Loading

Given: A 12 in. \(\times\) 12 in. column supporting the following loads: \(P_{D}=120\) kip, \(P_{l}=80\) kip, \(M_{D}=60\) kip ft, and \(M_{L}=40\) kip ft. The effective soil pressure is \(q_{e}=4.5\; Kip/ft^{2}\).

\(P=P_{D}+P_{L}=120+80=200\) Kip

\(M=M_{D}+M_{L}=60+40=100\) Kip ft

Determine: Dimension square/rectangular footings for the following cases.

Case (a): the center line of the column coincides with the centroid of the footing. M is counterclockwise (Fig. E7.2a).

Case (b): the center line of the column is 3 ft from the property line. M is clockwise (Fig. E7.2b).

Case (c): the center line of the column is 3 ft from the property line. M is zero (Fig. E7.2c).

Case (d): the center line of the column is 3 ft from the property line. M is counterclockwise (Fig. E7.2d).

Admin
Solution

Case (a)

 

Square footing (L = B): We use (7.6) and set \(q_{1}=q_{e}\)

(7.6)= \(q_{1}=\frac{P}{BL}+\frac{6P_{e}}{BL^{2}} \) , \(q_{2}=\frac{P}{BL}-\frac{6P_{e}}{BL^{2}} \)

 

\(\frac{200}{L^{2}}+\frac{6(100)}{L_{3}}=4.5\Rightarrow L=7.9\) ft

 

For \(L = B = 8\) ft \(\Rightarrow \left.\begin{matrix}q_{1},q_{2}=3.125\pm 1.17=\end{matrix}\right|_{1.95\;Kip/ft^{2} }^{4.3\; Kip/ft^{2}}\)

 

Rectangular footing: We take B = 6 ft. The pressure equation has the form

 

\(\frac{P}{6L}+\frac{6M}{6L^{2}}=q_{e}\)

 

\(\Downarrow\)

 

\(\frac{200}{6L}+\frac{6(100)}{6L^{2}}=4.5\Rightarrow 9.7\) ft

 

For L = 10 ft and B = 6 ft \(\Rightarrow \left.\begin{matrix}q_{1},q_{2}=3.33\pm 1.0=\end{matrix}\right|_{2.33\: kip/ft^{2}}^{4.33\: kip/ft^{2}}\)

 

Case (b) We position the centroid of the footing so that it is on the line of action of the resultant force. The location of the resultant is

 

\(M_{total}=M-P_{e}=100-200(e)=0\Rightarrow e=0.5\) ft

 

Then

 

\(3+e=\frac{L}{2}\Rightarrow L=2(3+e)=7\) ft

 

\(q_{1}=q_{2}=\frac{P}{LB}\leq q_{e}\rightarrow B\geq \frac{200}{7(4.5)}=6.35\)

 

For L = 7 ft and B = 6.5 ft \(\Rightarrow q_{1}=q_{2}=q=\frac{P}{LB}=\frac{200}{7(6.5)}=4.39\: kip/ft^{2}\)

 

Case (c) For this case, we locate the centroid on the center of the column. Then e = 0 and L = 6.0 ft. The area is determined with

 

\(q=q_{1}=q_{2}=\frac{P}{LB}\leq q_{e}\rightarrow B\geq \frac{200}{6(4.5)}=7.4\)

 

Use L = 6 ft and B = 7.5 ft

 

\(q=\frac{P}{LB}=\frac{200}{7.5(6)}=4.44\: kip/ft^{2}\)

 

Case (d) We decide to locate the centroid on the column line. Then e = 0.5 ft. This leads to the trapezoidal pressure distribution shown below. Taking L = 6 ft, and noting (7.3),

(7.3)= \(q_{1}=\left\{\frac{P}{A}+\frac{(Pe)L_{1}}{I_{y}} \right\} \)

\(q_{2}=\left\{\frac{P}{A}-\frac{(Pe)L_{1}}{I_{y}} \right\} \)

 

\(q_{1}=\frac{200}{6B}+\frac{6(100)}{B(6)^{2}}\leq 4.5\Rightarrow B\geq 11.1\)

 

For L = 6 ft and B = 11.25 ft \(\rightarrow \left.\begin{matrix}q_{1},q_{2}=2.96\pm 1.48=\end{matrix}\right|_{1.48\: kip/ft^{2}}^{4.44\: kip/ft^{2}}\)

 

Another option is to take the centroid on the line of action of the resultant force. Then, e = 0 ft, L = 5 ft, and (7.6) yields

 

\(\frac{200}{5B}=4.5\rightarrow B=8.9\)

 

For L = 5 ft and B = 9 ft \(q=q_{1}=q_{2}=\frac{200}{5(9)}=4.44\: kip/ft^{2}\)
