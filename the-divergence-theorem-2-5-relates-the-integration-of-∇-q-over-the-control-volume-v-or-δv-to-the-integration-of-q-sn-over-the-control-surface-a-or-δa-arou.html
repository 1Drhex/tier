Guest Question.

The divergence theorem \(\int_{V}{(\triangledown .q)}dV=\int_{A}{(q.s_{n})}dA \) relates the integration of \(\triangledown \)· q over the control volume V (or \(\Delta\)V) to the integration of (q · \(s_{n}\)) over the control surface A (or \(\Delta\)A) around that control volume. Use the Cartesian coordinates and a volume with finite lengths \(L_{y} \) and \(Lz\) in the y and z directions, and differential length \(\Delta\)x in the x direction, as shown in Figure.
(a) Then, following the steps given by (B.15) to (B.17), prove this theorem for \(q_{x}\) as the only component of q (i.e., \(q_{y} = q_{z} = 0\)) and x as the only differential spatial variable.
(b) Extend the results to three dimensions, by referring to the results of part (a), and arrive at \(\triangledown .q\equiv \lim _{\Delta V \longrightarrow 0} \frac{\int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})}dA }{\Delta V}=\lim _{\Delta V \longrightarrow 0} \frac{Q\mid _{\Delta A}}{\Delta V} \)

Admin
Solution

(a) The mathematical statement of the divergence theorem is given by \(\int_{V}{(\triangledown .q)}dV=\int_{A}{(q.s_{n})}dA \) or

\( \lim _{\Delta V \xrightarrow[]{} 0 } \int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})}dA=Q\mid _{\Delta A} = \int_{\Delta V}{(\triangledown .q)}dV\)

Since \(\Delta V\) is very small, we assume that \(\triangledown .q\) does not change within \(\Delta V\). Then on the right-hand side we can move \(\triangledown .q\) out of the integral and we have

\( \lim _{\Delta V \xrightarrow[]{} 0 } \frac{ \int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})}dA}{\Delta V}=\frac{ Q\mid _{\Delta A}}{\Delta V} =\triangledown .q \).

Using the steps described in Appendix B, i.e., (B.15) to (B.17), we begin with the left-hand side term and write this for the x-direction component of the heat flux, i.e., \(q_{x}\), and assume that \(q_{x}\) has a different magnitude, but is uniform, over areas \((\Delta A_{x})_{x}\) and \((\Delta A_{x})_{x+\Delta x}\) (the uniformity assumption is justifiable when \(\Delta A_{x}\) is very small). Then we have

\( \frac{\int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})} \Delta A}{\Delta V}=\frac{[(q.s_{n})\Delta A]_{x}+[(q.s_{n})\Delta A]_{x+\Delta x}}{L_{y}L_{z}\Delta x}=\frac{-(q_{x}\Delta A_{x})_{x}+(q_{x}\Delta A_{x})_{x+\Delta x}}{L_{y}L_{z}\Delta x} =\frac{[(q_{x})_{x+\Delta x}-(q_{x})_{x}]\Delta A_{x}}{L_{y}L_{z}\Delta x} =\frac{(q_{x})_{x+\Delta x }-(q_{x})_{x}}{\Delta x}\frac{L_{y}L_{z}}{L_{y}L_{z}} \)

Note that we have defined \( q_{x}\) to be pointing along the positive x axis, for both
surfaces.
We have used \(\Delta V =L_{y}L_{z}\Delta x \) and\( (\Delta A_{x})_{x}=(\Delta A_{x})_{x+\Delta x}=L_{y}L_{z}\) , as shown in Figure. Now taking the limit of \(\Delta x \xrightarrow[]{} 0 \), we have \( \Delta V=L_{y}L_{z}\Delta x \xrightarrow[]{} 0 \)

and using the definition of the differentiation as given by (B.16), we have

\( \lim _{\Delta V \xrightarrow[]{} 0 }\frac{\int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})dA} }{\Delta V}=\lim _{\Delta x \xrightarrow[]{} 0 }\frac{(q_{x})_{x+\Delta x}-(q_{x})_{x}}{\Delta x}\equiv \frac{\delta q_{x}}{\delta x} \)

(b) Now, from the definition (in Cartesian coordinates), we have

\( \triangledown .q\equiv (\frac{∂}{∂ x}s_{x}+\frac{∂ }{∂ y}s_{y}+\frac{∂ }{∂ z}s_{z} ).(q_{x}+q_{y}+q_{z})=\frac{∂ q_{x}}{∂ x}+\frac{∂ q_{y}}{∂ y}+\frac{∂ q_{z}}{∂ z} \)

and since we have assumed that \(q_{y}\) and \(q_{z}\) are zero, we have the final result as

\( \lim _{\Delta V \xrightarrow[]{} 0 }\frac{\int_{\Delta A}{(q.s_{n})dA} }{\Delta V}=\frac{Q\mid _{\Delta A}}{\Delta V \xrightarrow[]{} 0 }=\triangledown .q \)
