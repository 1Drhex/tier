Guest Question.

A sample of soil compacted according to the standard Proctor test has a density of 2.06 \(g / cm ^{3}\) at 100% compaction and at an optimum water content of 14%. What is the dry unit weight? What is the dry unit weight at zero air-voids? If the voids become filled with water what would be the saturated unit weight? Assume \(G_{s}=2.67\).

Admin
Solution

Refer to Fig. Ex. 21.6. Assume V= total volume = 1 \(cm ^{3}\). Since water content is 14% we may write,

 

\(\frac{M_{w}}{M_{s}}=0.14 \text { or } M_{w}=0.14 M_{s}\)

 

and since, \(M_{w}+M_{s}=2.06 g\)

 

\(0.14 M_{s}+M_{s}=1.14 M_{s}=2.06\)

 

or \(M_{s}=\frac{2.06}{1.14}=1.807 g\)

 

\(M_{w}=0.14 \times 1.807=0.253 g\).

 

By definition, \(\rho_{d}=\frac{M_{s}}{V}=\frac{1.807}{1}=1.807 g / cm ^{3} \text { or } \gamma_{d}=1.807 \times 9.81=17.73 kN / m ^{3}\)

 

The volume of solids (Fig. Ex. 21.6) is

 

\(V_{s}=\frac{1.807}{2.67}=0.68 g / cm ^{3}\)

 

The volume of voids \(=1-0.68=0.32 cm ^{3}\)

The volume of water \(=0.253 cm ^{3}\)

The volume of air \(=0.320-0.253=0.067 cm ^{3}\)

If all the air is squeezed out of the samples the dry density at zero air voids would be, by definition,

 

\(\rho_{d}=\frac{1.807}{0.68+0.253}=1.94 g / cm ^{3} \text { or } \gamma_{d}=1.94 \times 9.81=19.03 kN / m ^{3}\)

 

on the other hand, if the air voids also were filled with water,

The mass of water would be = 0.32 x 1 = 0.32 g

The saturated density is

 

\(\rho_{\text {sat }}=\frac{1.807+0.32}{1}=2.13 g / cm ^{3} \text { or } \gamma_{\text {sat }}=2.13 \times 9.81=20.90 kN / m ^{3}\)
