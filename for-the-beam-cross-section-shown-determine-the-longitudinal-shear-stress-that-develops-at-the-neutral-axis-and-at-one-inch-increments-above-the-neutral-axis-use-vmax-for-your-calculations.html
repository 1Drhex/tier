Guest Question.

For the beam cross-section shown, determine the longitudinal shear stress that develops at the neutral axis and at one inch increments above the neutral axis. Use \(V_{\max }\) for your calculations.

Admin
Solution

For the general shear stress equation, a determination must be made for the \(V_{\max }\) and the cross-sectional properties I, b, and Q. The \(V_{\max }\) value is most conveniently obtained directly from the shear diagram.

Because the moment of inertia I is constant for a given cross-section, it may be calculated as

\( I_{x}=\frac{b h^{3}}{12}=\frac{\left(4^{\prime \prime}\right)\left(8^{\prime \prime}\right)^{3}}{12}=171 in .^{4} \)

The width of the cross-section (shear plane) is also constant; therefore, b = 4″.

The values of Q=A\(\bar{y}\) for each of the four shear planes, including the neutral axis at which the horizontal (longitudinal) shear is desired, are shown in Figure 8.23(a) through 8.23(d) and are tabulated as follows:

\( I_{x}=171 \text { in. }^{4} \) , b = 4″

Q=A\(\bar{y}\) =(16 in.²)(2″) = 32 in.³

\(f_{ v }=\frac{V Q}{I b}=\frac{(5,800 \#)\left(32 in .^{3}\right)}{171 in .^{4}(4 in .)}=271 psi \)

(N.A.)

\( I_{x}=171 \text { in. }^{4} \) , b = 4″

Q=A\(\bar{y}\) =(4″)(3″)(2.5″)= 30 in.³

\( f_{ v }=\frac{V Q}{I b}=\frac{(5,800 \#)\left(30 in .^{3}\right)}{171 in .^{4}(4 in .)}=254 psi \)

(1″ above N.A.)

\( I_{x}=171 \text { in. }^{4} \) , b = 4″

Q=A\(\bar{y}\) =(8 in.²)(3″) = 24 in.³

\( f_{ v }=\frac{V Q}{I b}=\frac{(5,800 \#)\left(24 in .^{3}\right)}{171 in .^{4}(4 in .)}=204 psi \)

(2″ above N.A.)

\( I_{x}=171 \text { in. }^{4} \) , b = 4″

Q=A\(\bar{y}\) =(4 in.²)(3.5″) = 14 in.³

\( f_{ v }=\frac{V Q}{I b}=\frac{(5,800 \#)\left(14 in .^{3}\right)}{171 in .{ }^{4}(4 in .)}=119 psi \)

(3″ above N.A.)

Plotting the shear stress values on a graph adjacent to the beam cross-section, we obtain a parabolic curve as shown in Figure 8.24. Had the values of shearing stress been obtained for the corresponding points below the neutral axis, we should have found corresponding magnitudes. By completing the curve, it will be noted that the maximum value of horizontal shearing stress occurs at the neutral plane (surface), where Ay is a maximum and the bending stresses are equal to zero.

Because of its frequent use in design, an expression for the maximum horizontal shearing stress occurring in solid rectangular beams (primarily timber beams) may be derived (Figure 8.25).

Shear plane is maximum at the neutral axis, as found in Figure 8.24.

\( f_{ v }=\frac{V Q}{I b} ; \quad I_{x}=\frac{b h^{3}}{12} ; \quad A=b \times \frac{h}{2}=\frac{b h}{2} \)

b = b; \( \bar{y}=\frac{h}{4} \)

Q=A\(\bar{y}\)

Therefore,

\( f_{ v }=\frac{V\left(\frac{b h}{2} \times \frac{h}{4}\right)}{\left(\frac{b h^{3}}{12}\right)(b)}=\frac{12 V b h^{2}}{8 b^{2} h^{3}}=\frac{3 V}{2 b h} \)

However, bh = area of the entire beam cross-section.
Simplifying,

\(f_{ \underset{(N.A.)}{v _{ max }} }=\frac{3 V}{2 A}=\frac{1.5 V }{A} \) for solid rectangular cross-sections

where

\(f_{ \underset{(N.A.)}{v _{ max }} }\) = maximum shearing stress at the N.A.

V =maximum shear on the loaded beam

A = cross-sectional area of the beam

From the equation just developed, we find that the maximum (design) shear stress for a rectangular beam is 50% larger than the average shear value (Figure 8.26).
