Guest Question.

OXYGENATION OF WATER. The mass transfer coefficient estimated for the oxygenation of water in assumed the resistance to mass transfer was only on the liquid side. Given the sum-of-resistances model for the interphase mass transfer coefficient, is this reasonable?

Admin
Solution

The following table shows some physical property data we can consider in the analysis:

Water Air Property at 25 ◦C
\(10^{−6}\) \(\sim 10^{−5}\) \(v(m^{2}/s)\)
\(1.4 × 10^{−7}\) \(2.2 × 10^{−5}\) \(\alpha _{2}(m^{2}/s)\)
\(1.8 × 10^{−9}\) \(1.76 × 10^{−5}\) \(D_{O2} (m^{2}/s)\)

Thus the binary diffusivity of oxygen is significantly lower in water than in air. Consequently,
if the boundary layers are similar, the dominant resistance will clearly be in the liquid phase. To show this, we estimate the typical film thickness for mass transfer into water is often of the order of ∼0.1 mm or \(δ_{m} = 10^{−4} m.\) For the gas inside the air, the film thickness is expected to be comparable. Therefore we can use the measured diffusivity of oxygen in water from the preceding table to estimate the mass transfer coefficient in both phases as

\(K_{m}^{I}\approx \frac{D_{O2/Water}}{\delta _{m,Water}}=\frac{1.8 × 10^{−9} m^{2}/s}{10^{−4} m}\)\(=1.8 × 10^{−5} m/s,\)

\(K_{m}^{II}\approx \frac{D_{O2/air}}{\delta _{m,air}}=\frac{1.76 × 10^{−5} m^{2}/s}{10^{−4} m}\)=0.18 m/s.

The overall mass transfer coefficient can be calculated from Eq. (6.4.18) given the value of the partition coefficient as

\(\frac{1}{K_{m}}=(\frac{1}{K_{m}^{I}}+\frac{M}{K_{m}^{II}} )\) (6.4.18)

The value of the partition coefficient is calculated from the known Henry’s “Law” coefficient written in terms of the liquid side mole fraction of oxygen:

\(P_{O2}^{II}=HY_{O2}^{I}\)

\(H = 4.4 × 10^{4} bars.\)

To put this into the form

\(C_{O2}^{I}=MC_{O2}^{II}\)

the following manipulation is performed:

\(C_{O2}^{II}=\frac{P_{O2}^{II}}{RT}\)\(=\frac{H}{RTC^{I}}C^{I}Y_{O2}^{II}=M^{-1}C_{O2}^{I}\)

\(M=\frac{RTC^{II}}{H}\)\(=\frac{(8.314 Nm/mol K)(298 K)(55.6 × 10^{3} mol/m^{3})}{4.4 × 10^{4} × 10^{5} N/m^{2}}\)

Then

\(K_{m}^{I}=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{K_{m}^{I}}+\frac{M}{K_{m}^{II}} }\)\(=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{1.8 × 10^{−5} m/s}+\frac{0.031}{0.18 m/s} }\)\(=1.8 × 10^{−6} m/s.\)

Clearly, the resistance to mass transfer is entirely on the liquid side
