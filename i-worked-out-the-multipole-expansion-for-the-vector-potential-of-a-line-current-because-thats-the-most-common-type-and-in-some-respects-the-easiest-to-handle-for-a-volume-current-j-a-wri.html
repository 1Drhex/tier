Guest Question.

I worked out the multipole expansion for the vector potential of a line current because that’s the most common type, and in some respects the easiest to handle. For a volume current J:

(a) Write down the multipole expansion, analogous to Eq. 5.80.

\(A ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi} \oint \frac{1}{ᴫ} d l ^{\prime}=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^{n+1}} \oint\left(r^{\prime}\right)^{n} P_{n}(\cos \alpha) d I ^{\prime}\) (5.80)

(b) Write down the monopole potential, and prove that it vanishes.

(c) Using Eqs. 1.107 and 5.86, show that the dipole moment can be written

\(m =\frac{1}{2} \int( r \times J ) d \tau\) (5.90)

\(a =\frac{1}{2} \oint r \times d l\) (1.107)

\(m \equiv I \int d a =I a\) (5.86)

Admin
Solution

(a) \(I d l \rightarrow J d \tau\) , so \(A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^{n+1}} \int\left(r^{\prime}\right)^{n} P_{n}(\cos \theta) J d \tau\) .

(b) \(A _{ mon }=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi r} \int J d \tau=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi r} \frac{d p }{d t}\) (), where p is the total electric dipole moment. In magnetostatics, p is constant, so dp/dt = 0, and hence \(A _{\operatorname{mon}}= 0\) . qed

(c) \(m =I a =\frac{1}{2} I \oint( r \times d l ) \rightarrow m =\frac{1}{2} \int( r \times J ) d \tau\) . qed
