Guest Question.

Find the time-varying values of the \( \omega _{c1}(t) \) and \( \omega _{c2}(t) \) characteristic frequency parameters and the \( \alpha _{1}(t) \) and \( \alpha _{2}(t) \) bandwidth parameters for the response \( \left\{X(t)\right\} \) of an SDF oscillator excited by uniformly modulated white noise: \( F(t)=W(t)U(t) \) , in which \( \left\{W(t)\right\} \) is stationary white noise.

Admin
Solution

Using Eqs. 7.15, 7.16, and 7.19, the even-order spectral moments are given as response variances: \( \lambda _{0}(t)=\left[\sigma _{X}(t)\right] ^{2}, \lambda _{2}(t)=\left[\sigma _{\dot{X}}(t)\right] ^{2} \) , and \( \lambda _{4}(t)=\left[\sigma _{\ddot{X}}(t)\right] ^{2} \) . The values of the variances have already been investigated in Section 5.6. One of the simplest observations is that \( \lambda _{4}(t)=\infty \) for all values of \( t \) whereas \( \lambda _{0}(t) \) and \( \lambda _{2}(t) \) are finite. Thus, \( \alpha _{2}(t)\equiv \lambda _{2}(t)/\left[\lambda _{0}(t)\lambda _{4}(t)\right] ^{1/2}=0 \) for all time.

Clearly, the \( \alpha _{2}(t) \) bandwidth parameter is of no more use for this modulated response than it was for the stationary response in .

To evaluate the \( \alpha _{1}(t) \) parameter, it is necessary to obtain \( \lambda _{1}(t) \) by integrating \( S_{X\dot{X}}(t,\omega ) \) . This evolutionary cross-spectral density can be written from Eqs. 6.46, 6.48, and 6.49 as

\( S_{X\dot{X}}(t,\omega )=S_{0}H_{xS}(t,\omega )H_{\dot{x}S}(t,-\omega ) \) \( =S_{0}\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right| ^{2}\left(1-e^{-i\omega t}\left[g_{x}(t)+i\omega mh_{x}(t)\right] \right) \times \left(-i\omega -e^{i\omega t}\left[g^{'}_{x}(t)-i\omega mh^{'}_{x}(t)\right] \right) \)

Thus,

\( \lambda _{1}(t)=iS_{0}\int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right|^{2} } sgn(\omega )\times \left(1-e^{-i\omega t}\left[g_{x}(t)+i\omega mh_{x}(t)\right] \right) \times \left(-i\omega -e^{i\omega t}\left[g^{'}_{x}(t)-i\omega mh^{'}_{x}(t)\right] \right) d\omega \)

Expanding the multiplication, substituting \( e^{\pm i\omega t}=\cos (\omega t)\pm i\sin (\omega t) \) , using symmetry and antisymmetry, and simplifying gives

\( \lambda _{1}(t)=2S_{0}\left(\left[1+e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t}\right]\int_{0}^{\infty }{\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right|^{2}\omega d\omega +\omega ^{-1}_{d}e^{-\omega _{0}t}\sin (\omega _{d}t) }\times \left[\omega ^{2}_{0}\int_{0}^{\infty }{\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right|^{2} }\sin (\omega t)d\omega +\int_{0}^{\infty }{\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right|^{2}\omega ^{2}\sin (\omega t)d\omega } \right]-2e^{-\zeta \omega _{0}t}\cos (\omega _{d}t)\int_{0}^{\infty }{\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right|^{2} }\omega \cos (\omega t)d\omega \right) \)

Note that the first integral in this expression is the one evaluated in feq for the stationary response.

It is also possible to evaluate the other three integrals analytically, but the results are rather cumbersome. The accompanying sketch shows numerical values for \( m^{2}(\omega _{0})^{2}\lambda _{1}(t)/S_{0} \) plotted versus \( \omega _{0}t \) for \( \zeta =0.1 \) .

The \( \lambda _{0}(t) \) and \( \lambda _{2}(t) \) spectral moments can be taken directly from Eqs. 5.50 and 5.54 as

\( \lambda _{0}(t)=\sigma ^{2}_{X}(t)=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{2m^{2}\zeta \omega ^{3}_{0}}\left(1-e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t}\left[\frac{\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}+\frac{\zeta \omega _{0}}{\omega _{d}}\sin (2\omega _{d}t)-\frac{\zeta ^{2}\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}\cos (2\omega _{d}t) \right] \right) \)

And

\( \lambda _{2}(t)=\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}(t)=\frac{ G_{0}}{4m^{2}\zeta \omega _{0}}\left(1-e^{-2\zeta \omega _{0}t}\left[\frac{\omega _{0}^{2}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}-\frac{\zeta \omega _{0}}{\omega _{d}}\sin (2\omega _{d}t)-\frac{\zeta ^{2}\omega ^{2}_{0}}{\omega ^{2}_{d}}\cos (2\omega _{d}t) \right] \right) \)

The two characteristic frequency parameters are now evaluated as \( \omega _{c1}(t)=\lambda _{1}(t)/\lambda _{0}(t) \) and \( \omega _{c2}(t)=[\lambda _{2}(t)/\lambda _{0}(t)]^{1/2} \) . The accompanying sketches show numerical values if these quantities and of the bandwidth parameter \( \alpha _{1}(t)\equiv \lambda _{1}(t)/[\lambda _{0}(t)\lambda _{2}(t)]^{1/2} \) for \( \zeta =0.1 \) .

One can observe that \( \omega _{c1}(t) \) and \( \omega _{c2}(t) \) behave in a similar manner, with both beginning at infinity, then converging to \( \omega _{0} \) as time becomes large. The \( \alpha _{1}(t) \) bandwidth parameter begins quite small, indicating that the response is initially quite broadband, then it converges to the stationary value of 0.94 as time grows. For these results with \( \zeta =0.10 \) , the convergence to stationarity is reasonably close within one period of dynamic response \( \omega _{0}t=2\pi \) .

Note that Eq. 6.47

\(S_{XX}(t,\omega )\equiv S_{FF}(\omega ) \left|H_{xS}(t,\omega )\right| ^{2}\) \(=S_{FF}(\omega )\left|H_{x}(\omega )\right| ^{2}\left(1-2g_{x}(t)\cos (\omega t) – 2\omega mh_{x}(t)\sin (\omega t)+g_{x}^{2}(t)+\omega ^{2}m^{2}h_{x}^{2}(t)\right) \)

gives the time-varying \( S_{XX}(t,\omega ) \) for the response of this oscillator and shows that this spectral density decays like \( \omega ^{-2} \) as \( \left|\omega \right| \) tends to infinity. Thus, the alternate time-varying spectral moments, \( \hat{\lambda} _{1}(t) \) and \( \hat{\lambda} _{2}(t) \) , from Eqs. 7.17 and 7.18 do not exist, as noted by Corotis et al. (1972).
