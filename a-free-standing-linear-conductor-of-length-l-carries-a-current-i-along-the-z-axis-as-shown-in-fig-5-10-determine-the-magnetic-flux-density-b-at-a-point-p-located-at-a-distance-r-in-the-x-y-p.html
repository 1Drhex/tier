Guest Question.

A free-standing linear conductor of length l carries a current I along the z axis as shown in Fig. 5-10. Determine the magnetic flux density B at a point P located at a distance r in the x–y plane.

Admin
Solution

From Fig. 5-10, the differential length vector\(dl = \hat{z}dz. \). Hence, \(dl × \hat{R}= dz (\hat{z} ×\hat{R}) =\hat{\phi } sin\hat{\theta } dz, \) where \(\hat{\phi }\) is the azimuth direction and \(\theta \) is the angle between dl and \(\hat{R}\).

Application of Eq. (5.22) gives

\(H =\frac{I}{4π} \int_{z=−l/2}^{z=l/2}{\frac{dl × \hat{R}}{R^2} }=\hat{\phi } \frac{I}{4π} \int_{−l/2}^{l/2}{\frac{ sin \theta }{R^2} dz} (5.25)\)

Both R and θ are dependent on the integration variable z, but the radial distance r is not. For convenience, we convert the integration variable from z to θ by using the transformations

\(R = r csc θ, (5.26a)\)

 

\(z = −r cot θ, (5.26b)\)

 

\(dz = r csc^2 θ dθ. (5.26c)\)

Upon inserting Eqs. (5.26a) and (5.26c) into Eq. (5.25), we have

\(H= \hat{\phi }\frac{I}{4π}\int{\theta _1}^{\theta _2}{\frac{ sin \theta csc^2 θ dθ }{r^2 csc^2 θ}}\)

 

\(= \hat{\phi }\frac{I}{4π}\int{\theta _1}^{\theta _2}{ sin \theta d\theta }\)

 

\(= \hat{\phi }\frac{I}{4π}(cos θ_1 − cos θ_2), (5.27)\)

where \(θ_1 andθ_2\) are the limiting angles at \(z = −l/2 and z = l/2,\) respectively. From the right triangle in Fig. 5-10(b), it follows that

\(cos θ_1 =\frac{l/2}{\sqrt{r^2 + (l/2)^2} } (5.28a)\) \(cos θ_2 = − cos θ_1 =\frac{-l/2}{\sqrt{r^2 + (l/2)^2} } (5.28b)\)

Hence,

\(B = μ_0H =\hat{\phi }\frac{μ_0Il}{2πr\sqrt{ 4r^2 + l^2}} (T). (5.29) \)

For an infinitely long wire with \( l \gg r\), Eq. (5.29) reduces to

\(B =\hat{\phi }\frac{μ_0I}{2πr} (infinitely long wire). (5.30) \)
