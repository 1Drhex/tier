Motion in a Plane

A particle moves in the xy plane, starting from the origin at t = 0 with an initial velocity having an x component of 20 m/s and a y component of -15 m/s. The particle experiences an acceleration in the x direction, given by \(a_{x} = 4.0 m/s^2\).

(A) Determine the total velocity vector at any time.

(B) Calculate the velocity and speed of the particle at t = 5.0 s and the angle the velocity vector makes with the x axis.

(C) Determine the x and y coordinates of the particle at any time t and its position vector at this time.

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize The components of the initial velocity tell us that the particle starts by moving toward the right and downward. The x component of velocity starts at 20 m/s and increases by 4.0 m/s every second. The y component of velocity never changes from its initial value of -15 m/s. We sketch a motion diagram of the situation in Figure 4.6. Because the particle is accelerating in the +x direction, its velocity component in this direction increases and the path curves as shown in the diagram. Notice that the spacing between successive images increases as time goes on because the speed is increasing. The placement of the acceleration and velocity vectors in Figure 4.6 helps us further conceptualize the situation.

Categorize Because the initial velocity has components in both the x and y directions, we categorize this problem as one involving a particle moving in two dimensions. Because the particle only has an x component of acceleration, we model it as a particle under constant acceleration in the x direction and a particle under constant velocity in the y direction.

Analyze To begin the mathematical analysis, we set

\(v_{x i}=20 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}, v_{y i}=-15 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}, a_{x}=4.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}, \text { and } a_{y}=0\)

Use Equation 4.8 for the velocity vector:

\(\overrightarrow{v}_{f}=\overrightarrow{v}_{i}+\overrightarrow{a} t\) (4.8)

\(\overrightarrow{v}_{f}=\overrightarrow{v}_{i}+\overrightarrow{a} t=\left(v_{x i}+a_{x} t\right) \hat{i}+\left(v_{y i}+a_{y} t\right) \hat{j}\)

Substitute numerical values with the velocity in meters per second and the time in seconds:

\(\begin{aligned}\overrightarrow{v}_{f}&=[20+(4.0) t] \hat{i}+[-15+(0) t] \hat{j} \\\text { (1) } \overrightarrow{v}_{f}&=[(20+4.0 t) \hat{i}-15 \hat{j}]\end{aligned}\)

Finalize Notice that the x component of velocity increases in time while the y component remains constant; this result is consistent with our prediction.

Evaluate the result from Equation (1) at t = 5.0 s:

\(\overrightarrow{v}_{f}=[(20+4.0(5.0)) \hat{i}-15 \hat{j}]=(40 \hat{i}-15 \hat{j}) \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}\)

Determine the angle θ that \(\overrightarrow{v}_{f}\) makes with the x axis at t = 5.0 s:

\(\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{v_{y f}}{v_{x f}}\right)=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{-15 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{40 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}\right)=-21^{\circ}\)

Evaluate the speed of the particle as the magnitude of \(\overrightarrow{v}_{f}\) :

\(v_{f}=\left|\overrightarrow{v}_{f}\right|=\sqrt{v_{x f}^{2}+v_{y f}^{2}}=\sqrt{(40)^{2}+(-15)^{2}} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}=43 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

Finalize The negative sign for the angle θ indicates that the velocity vector is directed at an angle of 21° below the positive x axis. Notice that if we calculate \(v_{i}\) from the x and y components of \(\overrightarrow{v}_{i}\), we find that \(v_{f}\) > \(v_{i}\). Is that consistent with our prediction?

Analyze

Use the components of Equation 4.9 with \(x_{i} = y_{i} = 0\) at t = 0 and with x and y in meters and t in seconds:

\(\overrightarrow{r}_{f} = \overrightarrow{r}_{i} + \overrightarrow{v}_{i} t + \frac{1}{2} \overrightarrow{a} t^2\) (4.9)

\(\begin{aligned}x_{f}&=v_{x i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2}=20 t+2.0 t^{2} \\y_{f}&=v_{y i} t=-15 t\end{aligned}\)

Express the position vector of the particle at any time t:

\(\overrightarrow{r}_{f}=x_{f} \hat{i}+y_{f} \hat{j}=\left(20 t+2.0 t^{2}\right) \hat{i}-15 t \hat{j}\)

Finalize Let us now consider a limiting case for very large values of t.
