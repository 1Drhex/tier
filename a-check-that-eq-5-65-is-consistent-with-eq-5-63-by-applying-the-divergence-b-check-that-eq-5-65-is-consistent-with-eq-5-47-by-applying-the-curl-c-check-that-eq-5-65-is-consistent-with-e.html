Guest Question.

(a) Check that Eq. 5.65 is consistent with Eq. 5.63, by applying the divergence.

\(A ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( ᴫ ^{\prime}\right)}{ᴫ} d \tau^{\prime}\) (5.65)

∇ · A = 0 (5.63)

(b) Check that Eq. 5.65 is consistent with Eq. 5.47, by applying the curl.

\(B ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( r ^{\prime}\right) \times \hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}} d \tau^{\prime}\) (5.47)

(c) Check that Eq. 5.65 is consistent with Eq. 5.64, by applying the Laplacian.

\(\nabla^{2} A =-\mu_{0} J\) (5.64)

Admin
Solution

\(\text { (a) } \nabla \cdot A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right) d \tau^{\prime} ; \quad \nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}( \nabla \cdot J )+ J \cdot \nabla \left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\) . But the first term is zero, because \( J \left(ᴫ ^{\prime}\right)\) is a function of the source coordinates, not the field coordinates . And since \(ᴫ = r – r ^{\prime}, \nabla \left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)=- \nabla ^{\prime}\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\) .

So \(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=- J \cdot \nabla ^{\prime}\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\) . But \(\nabla ^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left( \nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J \right)+ J \cdot \nabla ^{\prime}\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\) , and \(\nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J =0\) in magnetostatics (Eq.5.33). So \(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=-\nabla^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)\) , and hence, by the divergence theorem, \(\nabla \cdot A =-\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \nabla ^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right) d \tau^{\prime}=-\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \oint \frac{ J }{ᴫ} \cdot d a ^{\prime}\) , where the integral is now over the surface surrounding all the currents. But J = 0 on this surface, so ∇ · A = 0.

∇ · J = 0. (5.33)

\(\text { (b) } \nabla \times A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \nabla \times\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right) d \tau^{\prime}=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int\left[\frac{1}{ᴫ}( \nabla \times J )- J \times \nabla \left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\right] d \tau^{\prime}\) . But ∇ × J = 0 (since J is not a function of r), and \(\nabla\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)=-\frac{\hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}\) (Eq. 1.101), so \(\nabla \times A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \times \hat{ ᴫ }}{ ᴫ^{2}} d \tau^{\prime}= B\) .

\(\nabla\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)=-\frac{\hat{ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}}\) (1.101)

\(\text { (c) } \nabla^{2} A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \nabla^{2}\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right) d \tau^{\prime}\) . But \(\nabla^{2}\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)= J \nabla^{2}\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\) (once again, J is a constant, as far as differentiation with respect to r is concerned), and \(\nabla^{2}\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)=-4 \pi \delta^{3}( ᴫ )\) (Eq. 1.102). So \(\nabla^{2} A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int J \left( r ^{\prime}\right)\left[-4 \pi \delta^{3}( ᴫ)\right] d \tau^{\prime}=-\mu_{0} J ( r )\) .

\(\nabla^{2} \frac{1}{ᴫ}=-4 \pi \delta^{3}(ᴫ )\) (1.102)
