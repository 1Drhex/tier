Guest Question.

Determine the mean beam lengths of for an optically thin gas layer.

Admin
Solution

From the last example we have

\(\frac{q_{\eta }}{E_{b\eta }} = 1-e^{\kappa _{\eta }L_{c}} =1-2E_{3}(\kappa _{\eta }L),\)

which, for \(\kappa _{\eta }L\ll 1\) , becomes

\(\frac{q_{\eta }}{E_{b\eta }} =1-1+ \kappa _{\eta }L_{0} =1-2 (\frac{1}{2}-\kappa _{\eta }L )=2\kappa _{\eta }L ,\)

or

\(L_{0}=2L.\)

The mean beam length for the entire surface is, of course, again the same. This could also have been found immediately from equation (19.8) \(L_{0}=4\frac{V}{A}.\) as

\(L_{0}=4\frac{V}{A} = 4\frac{A_{plate}\times L}{2A_{plate}}=2L.\)

Obviously, equation (19.8) \(L_{0}=4\frac{V}{A}.\) is trivial to evaluate for any geometry, but even equations (19.7) \(L_{0}=\frac{1}{\pi A}\int_{A}^{}{}\int_{2\pi }^{}{} S_{max} (r_{w},\widehat{s} )\cos \theta d\Omega dA\) (mean beam length to a part of the bounding surface) and (19.5) \(L_{0}=\frac{1}{\pi }\int_{V}^{}{}\frac{\cos \theta dV}{S^{2}}\) (mean beam length to a point on the bounding surface) are readily integrated for many configurations.
