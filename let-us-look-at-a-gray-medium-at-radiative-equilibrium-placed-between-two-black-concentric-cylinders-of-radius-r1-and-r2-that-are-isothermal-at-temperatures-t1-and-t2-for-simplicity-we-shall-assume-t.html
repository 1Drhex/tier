Guest Question.

Let us look at a gray medium at radiative equilibrium placed between two black concentric cylinders of radius \(R_{1}\) and \(R_{2}\) that are isothermal at temperatures \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\). For simplicity, we shall assume that the medium does not scatter (\(σ_{s}\) = 0), and that its absorption coefficient κ, is constant.
We desire to find the heat flux from inner to outer cylinder as a function of the ratio \(R_{1}/R_{2}\) and the optical thickness of the medium, \(\tau_{12} = \tau _{2} - \tau _{1} = \kappa R_{2} - R_{1}\) ).

Admin
Solution

For one-dimensional radiative equilibrium problems such as this, it is usually advantageous to apply equations (15.34) \(\triangledown _{\tau } \cdot q =(1-w)(4\pi I_{b}-G )\) and (15.36) \(\triangledown _{\tau }G=-(3-A_{1}w )q\) independently (rather than eliminating radiative heat flux). Then, from equation (15.34) we have, in cylindrical coordinates ( with w=0 and \(\tau = κr\)),

\(\frac{1}{\tau }\frac{d}{d\tau } (\tau q)= 4n^{2} \sigma T^{4}-G=0.\)

If we multiply by τ and integrate, we find

\(\tau q=C_{1} or q=\frac{C_{1}}{\tau }\)

Substituting this expression into equation (15.36) gives

\(\frac{d G}{d\tau }=-3q =- \frac{3C_{1} }{\tau } ,\)

or

\(G=-3C_{1}\ln \tau +C_{2}\).

The boundary conditions are , from equation (15.46) \(2q\cdot \overset{\wedge }{n}=4J_{w}-G=\frac{\epsilon }{2-\epsilon }(4\pi I_{bw}-G )\) with ε = 1 ,

\(\tau =\tau _{1} : 2q \cdot \overset{\wedge }{n} = 2q = 4n^{2} \sigma T^{4}_{1}-G\),
\(\tau =\tau _{2} : 2q \cdot \overset{\wedge }{n} = -2q = 4n^{2} \sigma T^{4}_{2}-G\),

from which \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\) may be determined as

\(C_{1}= \frac{4n^{2} \sigma (T^{4}_{1}-T^{4}_{2})}{\frac{2}{\tau _{1} } + \frac{2}{\tau _{2} } + 3 \ln \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} } , C_{2}= 4n^{2} \sigma T^{4}_{2} + C_{1}\left(\frac{2}{\tau _{2} }+3\ln \tau _{2} \right)\)

Heat flux and temperature then follow as

\(\Psi = \frac{q}{n^{2}\sigma (T^{4}_{1}-T^{4}_{2}) } = \frac{2}{1+ \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} + \frac{3}{2} \tau _{2} \ln \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} } \left(\frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau }\right) ,\)
\(\Phi = \frac{T^{4}-T^{4}_{2} }{T^{4}_{1}-T^{4}_{2} } = \frac{1+\frac{3}{2} \tau _{2} \ln \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} }{1+ \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} + \frac{3}{2} \tau _{2} \ln \frac{\tau _{2}}{\tau _{1}} } .\)

As expected, the \(P_{1}\)-approximation does well for optically thick media. For the optically thin case, however, as κ →0 the heat flux goes to

\(\Psi _{1} \rightarrow \frac{2}{1+R_{2}/R_{1} }\frac{R_{2} }{R_{1} } = 2 /\left(1+ \frac{R_{1} }{R_{2} } \right) ,\)

while the correct answer should be \(\Psi _{1} → 1\), as we know from Chapter 5, equation (5.35) \(q_{1}= \frac{ \sigma (T^{4}_{1}-T^{4}_{2})}{\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1} } + \frac{A_{1} }{A_{2}} \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2} } -1\right) }\) . Therefore, for \(R_{2}/R_{1} \rightarrow 1\) the correct optically thin limit is obtained (and the gap between such cylinders becomes a plane-parallel slab), while for small inner cylinders, \(R_{2}/R_{1} << 1\) , the error becomes larger and may be as large as 100%!
