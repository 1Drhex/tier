Guest Question.

Derive the equations of motion of the double compound pendulum shown in Figure 5.13 using the principle of virtual work in conjunction with the inertial forces of d’Alembert.

Admin
Solution

Since the two links of the pendulum are assumed to be rigid, two coordinates are sufficient to describe the motion of the system: Φ for the upper link of mass \(m_{1}\) and \(\psi\) for the lower link of mass \(m_{2}\). We sketch the free-body diagrams for each link, as shown in Figure 5.14, to help us calculate the virtual work. The unlabeled arrows at the top of each body are reaction forces that are not calculated. Note that inertia terms are included with each free-body diagram. They are included to emphasize that they are part of the force balance as interpreted by d’Alembert. Let \(I_{1}\)

and \(I_{2}\) be the mass moments of inertia of the respective
bodies about their respective center of mass, \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\).

We derive the virtual work expression for each coordinate and set it equal to zero to find the respective equations of motion. First, we consider the virtual work done by forces and moments corresponding to \(\psi\) when it undergoes a small virtual rotation \(\delta \psi\), while Φ is held constant and thus \(\delta \phi=0\). This is permissible since the two generalized coordinates are independent.

We consider one of the components of the virtual work to show how the formulation proceeds. For the lower link, the force equals the product of mass \(m_{2}\) and the rectilinear acceleration at mass center \(C_{2}\), \(c \psi\). The virtual
displacement is \(c \delta \psi\). Therefore, this component of the virtual work is the product − \(c \psi\) \(c \delta \psi\), where the minus sign indicates a negative rotation according to the right-hand rule.
In the next equation, the forces and moments are shown in square brackets. The complete virtual work is then

\(\begin{aligned}\delta W_{\psi}=&-\left[m_{2} c \ddot{\psi}\right] c \delta \psi-\left[m_{2} b \ddot{\phi}\right] c \cos (\phi-\psi) \delta \psi \\&-\left[m_{2} g\right] c \sin \psi \delta \psi-\left[I_{2} \ddot{\psi}\right] \delta \psi \\&+\left[m_{2} b \dot{\phi}^{2}\right] c \sin (\phi-\psi) \delta \psi \\=&\left[-\left(m_{2} c^{2}+I_{2}\right) \ddot{\psi}-m_{2} b c(\ddot{\phi} \cos (\phi-\psi)\right.\\&\left.\left.-\dot{\phi}^{2} \sin (\phi-\psi)\right)-m_{2} g c \sin \psi\right] \delta \psi,\end{aligned}\)

where \(m_{2} c \dot{\psi}^{2}\), which is perpendicular to the virtual rotation \(\delta \psi\), and the torque \(I_{1} \ddot{\phi}\) do no work. Similarly, for the virtual rotation \(\delta \phi\), with \(\psi\) held constant
and thus \(\delta \psi\) = 0, he virtual work in the Φ coordinate is

\(\begin{aligned}\delta W_{\phi}=&\left[-m_{1} a^{2} \ddot{\phi}-m_{1} g a \sin \phi-I_{1} \ddot{\phi}\right.\\&-m_{2} b c \psi \cos (\phi-\psi)-m_{2} b^{2} \ddot{\phi} \\&\left.-m_{2} g b \sin \phi-m_{2} b c \dot{\psi}^{2} \sin (\phi-\psi)\right] \delta \phi,\end{aligned}\)

where \(m_{1} a \dot{\phi}^{2}\), \(m_{2} a \dot{\phi}^{2}\), and \(I_{2} \ddot{\psi}\) do no work and have been removed from the expression. Since the virtual work done by all forces, including inertia forces, equals zero by d’Alembert’s principle, we can write down the equation of motion for each coordinate system by setting the generalized forces, that is the
expression in each of the above square brackets, equal to zero.

If we assume that only small oscillations take place, meaning that Φ and \(\psi\) are small and higher order terms such as \(\dot{\phi}^{2}\) can be neglected, the linearized equations of motion are

\(\begin{array}{r}\left(m_{2} c^{2}+I_{2}\right) \ddot{\psi}+m_{2} b c \ddot{\phi}+m_{2} c g \psi=0 \\m_{2} b c \psi+\left(m_{1} a^{2}+I_{1}+m_{2} b^{2}\right) \ddot{\phi}+\left(m_{1} a+m_{2} b\right) g \phi=0\end{array}\)

These two coupled equations can be expressed in matrix form,

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\begin{array}{cc}m_{2} c^{2}+I_{2} & m_{2} b c \\m_{2} b c & m_{1} a^{2}+I_{1}+m_{2} b^{2}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\ddot{\psi} \\\ddot{\phi}\end{array}\right\}} \\&+\left[\begin{array}{cc}m_{2} c g & 0 \\0 & \left(m_{1} a+m_{2} b\right) g\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\psi \\\phi\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0\end{array}\right\},\end{aligned}\)

where the coupling is seen in the off-diagonal terms in the inertia matrix. The zero vector on the right-hand side of the equal sign indicates that there is no forcing function; with no external forcing the pendulum oscillations are in free vibration. Matrix methods are
introduced to solve such systems in Chapter 6.
