Guest Question.

(a) Using binomial series, find all the Laurent series of

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z\left(z^{2}-1\right)\left(z^{2}+4\right)}\) (3.22)

with center at the origin of the z plane. (b) Identify which Laurent series of X(z) is a z transform.

Admin
Solution

The zero-pole plot of X(z) depicted in Fig. 3.3a has three distinct annuli of convergence, namely, \(A_{ I }, A_{ II }, \text { and } A_{ III }\) as illustrated in Fig. 3.3d. The radius of the inner circle of annulus \(A_{ I }\) can be reduced to zero and that of the outer circle of annulus \(A_{ III }\) can be increased to infinity. Thus three Laurent series can be obtained for this function, one for each annulus.

 

Annulus \(A_{ I }\): To obtain the Laurent series for the innermost annulus of convergence in Fig. 3.3d, that is, \(A_{ I }, X(z)\) must be expressed in terms of binomial series that converge for values of z in the annulus \(R_{0}<|z|<1 \text { where } R_{0} \rightarrow 0\). Equation (3.22) can be

 

expressed as

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z\left(z^{2}-1\right)\left(z^{2}+4\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{-4 z\left(1-z^{2}\right)\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}}{-4 z}\) (3.23)

 

From Eqs. (3.21b) and (3.21a), we have

 

\((1+b)^{-1}=1+(-b)+b^{2}+(-b)^{3}+\cdots\) (3.21a)

 

\((1-b)^{-1}=[1+(-b)]^{-1}=1+b+b^{2}+b^{3}+\cdots\) (3.21b)

 

\(\left(1-z^{2}\right)^{-1}=1+z^{2}+\left(z^{2}\right)^{2}+\cdots+\left(z^{2}\right)^{n}+\cdots\) (3.24a)

 

and

 

\(\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}=\left[1-\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)\right]^{-1}\)

 

\(=1+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)^{2}+\cdots+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)^{k}+\cdots\) (3.24b)

 

respectively. Since both of the above series converge and \(\left(z^{2}-2\right) /(-4 z)\) is finite for \(0<|z|<1\), the substitution of Eqs. (3.24a) and (3.24b) into Eq. (3.23) will yield a series representation for X(z) that converges in annulus \(A_{ I }\). We can write

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}}{-4 z}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{-4 z}\left[1+z^{2}+\cdots+\left(z^{2}\right)^{n}+\cdots\right] \cdot\left[1+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)+\cdots+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)^{k}+\cdots\right]\)

 

\(=\frac{z^{2}-4}{-4 z} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left(z^{2}\right)^{n}\left(-\frac{z^{2}}{4}\right)^{k}\)

 

and after some routine algebraic manipulation, the series obtained can be expressed as

 

\(X(z)=z^{-1}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} z^{2 n-1}\) (3.25)

 

where

 

\(C_{n}=1+2 \sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{k}\)

 

The sum in the formula for \(<br /> C_{n}\) is a geometric series with a common ratio of −1/4 and hence it can be readily evaluated as

 

\(\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{k}=-\frac{1}{5}\left[1-\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{n}\right]\)

 

(see Eq. (A.46b)). Thus

 

\(S=\frac{1-z^{(N+1)}}{1-z}\) (A.46b)

 

\(C_{n}=\frac{1}{5}\left[3+2\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{n}\right]\)

 

and on calculating the coefficients, the series in Eq. (3.25) assumes the form

 

\(X(z)=\cdots+\frac{19}{32} z^{5}+\frac{5}{8} z^{3}+\frac{1}{2} z+z^{-1}\) (3.26)

 

Annulus \(A_{I I}\) (see Fig. 3.3d): A series that converges in annulus \(A_{I I}\), that is, \(1<|z|<2\), can be obtained in the same way. Equation (3.22) can be expressed as

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z\left(z^{2}-1\right)\left(z^{2}+4\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{4 z^{3}\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}}{4 z^{3}}\) (3.27)

 

where

 

\(\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}=1+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)^{2}+\cdots+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)^{n}+\cdots\) (3.28)

 

and \(\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}\) can be expressed in terms of the binomials series in Eq. (3.24b). The series in Eqs. (3.24b) and (3.28) converge in the region \(1<|z|<2\), as can be easily shown by using the ratio test, and since \(\left(z^{2}-4\right) / 4 z^{3}\) is finite for \(|z|>0\), and from Eq. (3.27) a series representation for X(z) for annulus \(A_{I I}\) can be obtained as

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+z^{2} / 4\right)^{-1}}{4 z^{3}}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{4 z^{3}}\left[1+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)+\cdots+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)^{n}+\cdots\right]\)

 

\(\cdot\left[1+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)+\cdots+\left(-z^{2} / 4\right)^{k}+\cdots\right]\)

 

\(=\frac{z^{2}-4}{4 z^{3}} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{z^{2}}\right)^{n}\left(-\frac{z^{2}}{4}\right)^{k}\)

 

After some manipulation and some patience, the series obtained can be simplified to

 

\(X(z)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(E_{n} z^{2 n-3}-\frac{3}{5} z^{-(2 n+1)}\right)\) (3.29)

 

where

 

\(E_{n}=\frac{2}{5}\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{n-1}\)

 

If we calculate the numerical values of the coefficients in Eq. (3.29), we get

 

\(X(z)=\cdots+\frac{1}{40} z^{3}-\frac{1}{10} z+\frac{2}{5} z^{-1}-\frac{3}{5} z^{-3}-\frac{3}{5} z^{-5}-\cdots\) (3.30)

 

Annulus \(A_{I I I}\) (see Fig. 3.3d): A series that converges in annulus \(A_{I I I}\), that is, \(2<|z|<R_{\infty}\), can be obtained by expressing X(z) in Eq. (3.22) as

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z\left(z^{2}-1\right)\left(z^{2}+4\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z^{5}\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)\left(1+4 / z^{2}\right)}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+4 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}}{z^{5}}\) (3.31)

 

where

 

\(\left(1+4 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}=\left[1-\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)\right]^{-1}\)

 

\(=1+\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)+\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)^{2}+\cdots+\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)^{k}+\cdots\) (3.32)

 

and \(\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}\) can be represented by the binomial series in Eq. (3.28). The series in Eqs. (3.28) and (3.32) converge in the region \(2<|z|<\infty \text { and since }\left(z^{2}-4\right) / z^{5}\) is finite for \(|z|<\infty\), a series representation for X(z) for annulus \(A_{I I I}\) can be obtained from Eq. (3.31) as

 

\(X(z)=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)\left(1-1 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}\left(1+4 / z^{2}\right)^{-1}}{z^{5}}\)

 

\(=\frac{\left(z^{2}-4\right)}{z^{5}}\left[1+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)+\cdots+\left(1 / z^{2}\right)^{n}+\cdots\right]\)

 

\(\cdot\left[1+\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)+\cdots+\left(-4 / z^{2}\right)^{k}+\cdots\right]\)

 

\(=\frac{z^{2}-4}{z^{5}} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{z^{2}}\right)^{n}\left(-\frac{4}{z^{2}}\right)^{k}\)

 

After quite a bit of algebra, one can show that

 

\(X(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} G_{n} z^{-2 n-3}\) (3.33)

 

where

 

\(G_{n}=F_{n}-4 F_{n-1}\)

 

with

 

\(F_{n}=\sum_{k=0}^{n}(-4)^{k}=\frac{1}{5}\left[1-(-4)^{n+1}\right]\)

 

Hence

 

\(G_{n}=\frac{1}{5}\left[-3+8(-4)^{n}\right]\)

 

and on evaluating the coefficients in Eq. (3.33), we get

 

\(X(z)=z^{-3}-7 z^{-6}+25 z^{-7}-103 z^{-9}+\cdots\) (3.34)

 

(b) A comparison of Eqs. (3.26), (3.30), and (3.34) shows that the three Laurent series obtained for X(z) are all linear combinations of positive and/or negative powers of z and are, in fact, quite similar to each other. Yet only the last one is a z transform that satisfies the absolute-convergence theorem (Theorem 3.1) since this is the only Laurent series that converges in the outermost annulus.
