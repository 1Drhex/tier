Guest Question.

Van der Waals interaction. Consider two atoms a distance R apart.

Because they are electrically neutral you might suppose there would be no force between them, but if they are polarizable there is in fact a weak attraction. To model this system, picture each atom as an electron (mass m, charge -e attached by a spring (spring constant to the nucleus (charge +e ), as in Figure 7.13. Weâ€™ll assume the nuclei are heavy, and essentially motionless. The Hamiltonian for the unperturbed system is

\( H^{0}=\frac{1}{2 m} p_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k x_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2 m} p_{2}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k x_{2}^{2} \) (7.103).

The Coulomb interaction between the atoms is

\( H^{\prime}=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{R}-\frac{e^{2}}{R-x_{1}}-\frac{e^{2}}{R+x_{2}}+\frac{e^{2}}{R-x_{1}+x_{2}}\right) \) (7.104).

(a) Explain Equation 7.104. Assuming that \( \left|x_{1}\right| \text { and }\left|x_{2}\right| \) are both much less than R, show that

\( H^{\prime} \cong-\frac{e^{2} x_{1} x_{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}} \) (7.105).

(b) Show that the total Hamiltonian (\( H^{0} \) plus Equation 7.105) separates into
two harmonic oscillator Hamiltonians:

\( H=\left[\frac{1}{2 m} p_{+}^{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left(k-\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right) x_{+}^{2}\right]+\left[\frac{1}{2 m} p_{-}^{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left(k+\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right) x_{-}^{2}\right] \) (7.106).

under the change variables

\( x_{\pm} \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(x_{1} \pm x_{2}\right), \quad \text { which entails } \quad p_{\pm}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(p_{1} \pm p_{2}\right) \). (7.107).

(c) The ground state energy for this Hamiltonian is evidently

\( E=\frac{1}{2} \hbar\left(\omega_{+}+\omega_{-}\right), \quad \text { where } \quad \omega_{\pm}=\sqrt{\frac{k \mp\left(e^{2} / 2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}\right)}{m}} \). (7.108).

Without the Coulomb interaction it would have been \( E_{0}=\hbar \omega_{0} \), where \( \omega_{0}=\sqrt{k / m} . \text { Assuming that } k \gg\left(e^{2} / 2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}\right) \) , show that

\( \Delta V \equiv E-E_{0} \cong-\frac{\hbar}{8 m^{2} \omega_{0}^{3}}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\right)^{2} \frac{1}{R^{6}} \) . (7.109).

Conclusion: There is an attractive potential between the atoms, proportional to the inverse sixth power of their separation. This is the van der Waals interaction between two neutral atoms.

(d) Now do the same calculation using second-order perturbation theory.
Hint: The unperturbed states are of the form \( \psi_{n_{1}}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{n_{2}}\left(x_{2}\right) \), where \( \psi_{n}(x) \) is a one-particle oscillator wave function with mass m and spring constant \( k ; \Delta V \) is the second-order correction to the ground state energy, for the perturbation in Equation 7.105 (notice that the first-order correction is zero).

Solution

(a) The first term is the nucleus/nucleus interaction, the second is the interaction between the nucleus of atom 2 and the electron in atom 1, the third is between nucleus 1 and electron 2, and the last term is the interaction between the electrons.

\( \frac{1}{R-x}=\frac{1}{R}\left(1-\frac{x}{R}\right)^{-1}=\frac{1}{R}\left[1+\left(\frac{x}{R}\right)+\left(\frac{x}{R}\right)^{2}+\ldots\right] \).

so

\( \approx \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{e^{2}}{R}\left(-\frac{2 x_{1} x 2}{R^{2}}\right)=-\frac{e^{2} x_{1} x_{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}} \).

(b) Expanding Eq. 7.106:

\( H=\left[\frac{1}{2 m} p_{+}^{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left(k-\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right) x_{+}^{2}\right]+\left[\frac{1}{2 m} p_{-}^{2}+\frac{1}{2}\left(k+\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right) x_{-}^{2}\right] \) (7.106).

\( H=\frac{1}{2 m}\left(p_{+}^{2}+p_{-}^{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2} k\left(x_{+}^{2}+x_{-}^{2}\right)-\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\left(x_{+}^{2}-x_{-}^{2}\right) \).

\( =\frac{1}{2 m}\left(p_{1}^{2}+p_{2}^{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2} k\left(x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}\right)-\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\left(2 x_{1} x_{2}\right)=H^{0}+H^{\prime} \), (Eqs. 7.103 and 7.105).

\( H^{0}=\frac{1}{2 m} p_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k x_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2 m} p_{2}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k x_{2}^{2} \) (7.103).

\( H^{\prime} \cong-\frac{e^{2} x_{1} x_{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}} \) (7.105).

(c)

\( \omega_{\pm}=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\left(1 \mp \frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} k}\right)^{1 / 2} \cong \omega_{0}\left[1 \mp \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)-\frac{1}{8}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)^{2}+\ldots\right] \) \( \Delta V \cong \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_{0}\left[1-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)-\frac{1}{8}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)^{2}+\right. \)

\( \left.1+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)-\frac{1}{8}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)^{2}\right]-\hbar \omega_{0} \).

\( =\frac{\hbar \omega_{0}}{2}\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3} m \omega_{0}^{2}}\right)^{2}=-\frac{1}{8} \frac{\hbar}{m^{2} \omega_{0}^{3}}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\right)^{2} \frac{1}{R^{6}} \).

(d) In first order:

\( E_{0}^{1}=\left\langle 0\left|H^{\prime}\right| 0\right\rangle=-\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\left\langle\psi_{0}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{0}\left(x_{2}\right)\left|x_{1} x_{2}\right| \psi_{0}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{0}\left(x_{2}\right)\right\rangle=-\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\langle x\rangle_{0}\langle x\rangle_{0}=0 \).

In second order:

\( E_{0}^{2}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\left|\left\langle\psi_{n}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle\right|^{2}}{E_{0}-E_{n}} . \text { Here } \quad\left|\psi_{0}\right\rangle=|0\rangle|0\rangle, \quad\left|\psi_{n}\right\rangle=\left|n_{1}\right\rangle\left|n_{2}\right\rangle \), so

\( =\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right)^{2} \sum_{n_{1}=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_{2}=1}^{\infty} \frac{\left|\left\langle n_{1}\left|x_{1}\right| 0\right\rangle\right|^{2}\left|\left\langle n_{2}\left|x_{2}\right| 0\right\rangle\right|^{2}}{E_{0,0}-E_{n_{1}, n_{2}}} \). [use ]

\( =\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right)^{2} \frac{|\langle 1|x| 0\rangle|^{2}|\langle 1|x| 0\rangle|^{2}}{\left(\frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_{0}+\frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_{0}\right)-\left(\frac{3}{2} \hbar \omega_{0}+\frac{3}{2} \hbar \omega_{0}\right)} \quad\left[\text { zero unless } n_{1}=n_{2}=1\right] \),

\( =\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} R^{3}}\right)^{2}\left(-\frac{1}{2 \hbar \omega_{0}}\right)\left(\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_{0}}\right)^{2}=-\frac{\hbar}{8 m^{2} \omega_{0}^{3}}\left(\frac{e^{2}}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\right)^{2} \frac{1}{R^{6}} \).

[There is an interesting fraud in this well-known problem. If you expand \( H^{\prime} \text { to order } 1 / R^{5} \), the extra term has a nonzero expectation value in the ground state of \( H^{0} \), so there is a non-zero first-order perturbation, and the dominant contribution goes like \( 1 / R^{5}, \text { not } 1 / R^{6} \) (as desired). The model gets the power \right” in three dimensions (where the expectation value is zero), but not in one. See A. C. Ipsen and K. Splittorff, Am. J. Phys. 83, 150 (2015).].
