Guest Question.

Power Plant’s Emissions

A coal-fired power plant produces a net electricity output of 1 GW. A key problem faced by the power plant is the emission of sulfur as a combustion byproduct. The coal contains 1% sulfur, and it is known to react with rainwater in the atmosphere to produce sulfuric acid—the main ingredient of acid rain. The power plant is equipped with a scrubber system that removes 96% of the sulfur from the exhaust gas. See Figure 7.24. (a) By using the plant’s real efficiency of 32%, calculate the plant’s daily fuel consumption. (b) How much sulfur escapes the scrubber and is released into the environment each day?

We will consider a 1 s time interval during which the power plant draws heat, produces work, and exhausts some sulfur into the atmosphere. The plant’s real efficiency relates “what we get” relative to “what we paid,” and by using Equation (7.15), the heat supplied to the plant by the fuel can be determined. Table 7.3 lists the heating value of coal as 30 MJ/kg. The given information for the sulfur percentage and the scrubber’s efficiency can be used to determine the amount of sulfur released each day.

 

\(\eta=\frac{W_{ t }-W_{ p }}{Q_{ sg }} \approx \frac{W_{ t }}{Q_{ sg }}\) (Equation 7.15)

Solution

(a) By using Equation (7.5), the plant produces

 

\(P_{ avg }=\frac{W}{\Delta t}\) (equation 7.5)

 

\(\begin{aligned}W_{t} &=(1 GW )(1 s ) \quad \leftarrow\left[P_{\text {avg }}=\frac{W}{\Delta t}\right] \\&=1 GW \cdot s \\&=1\left(\frac{ GJ }{ s }\right)( s ) \\&=1 GJ\end{aligned}\)

 

of electrical energy each second. The amount of heat that must be supplied to the steam generator by burning coal is

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q_{ sg } &=\frac{1 GJ }{0.32} \leftarrow\left[\eta=\frac{W_{ t }}{Q_{ sg }}\right] \\&=3.125 GJ\end{aligned}\)

 

where we have neglected the small amount of pump work, \(W_{ p }\) , in Equation (7.15). By using Equation (7.6), the mass of fuel consumed each second is

 

\(\eta=\frac{W_{ t }-W_{ p }}{Q_{ sg }} \approx \frac{W_{ t }}{Q_{ sg }}\) (Equation 7.15)

 

\(Q=m H\) (Equation 7.6)

 

\(\begin{aligned}m_{\text {coal }} &=\frac{3125 MJ }{30 MJ / kg } \leftarrow[Q=m H] \\&=104.2( MJ ) \frac{ kg }{ MJ } \\&=104.2 kg\end{aligned}\)

 

where we have converted the SI prefix on the numerical value for \(Q_{ sg }\) so that the numerator and denominator are dimensionally consistent. Over the course of a day, the plant must therefore use

 

\(\begin{aligned}m_{\text {coal }} &=\left(104.2 \frac{ kg }{ s }\right)\left(60 \frac{ s }{ min }\right)\left(60 \frac{ min }{ h }\right)\left(24 \frac{ h }{ day }\right) \\&=9.000 \times 10^{6}\left(\frac{ kg }{ s }\right)\left(\frac{ s }{ min }\right)\left(\frac{ min }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ h }{ day }\right) \\&=9000 \times 10^{6} \frac{ kg }{ day }\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) With the coal containing 1% sulfur and a scrubber efficiency of 96%,

 

\(\begin{aligned}m_{\text {sulfur }} &=(0.01)(1-0.96)\left(9.000 \times 10^{6} \frac{ kg }{ day }\right) \\&=3.600 \times 10^{3} \frac{ kg }{ day }\end{aligned}\)

 

is the daily release of sulfur.
