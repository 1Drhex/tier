Guest Question.

Standard test signals such as square wave, triangular wave, sinusoidal wave, etc. are routinely used in electronics, communication and allied areas for a variety of purposes. It is rather easy to generate high quality square waves in electronic circuits. It is not so easy to make pure sinusoids. One of the commonly used methods to generate low power sinusoidal signals of high waveform quality is to generate a good square wave and pass it through a narrow band-pass filter with a large \(Q\) factor. The centre frequency of filter is adjusted to be equal to the frequency of square wave. This example deals with applying a series \(RLC\) circuit for this purpose. Consider the circuit in Fig. 12.10-12 .
This circuit is used to filter the square wave shown and deliver a sine wave to the \(20 Ω\) load resistance. We will accept the fact that the square wave shown can be expressed in the form of a series as follows.
\(v_{S}(t)=\sum_{n=1 \atop n \text { odd }}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \sin 2 \pi \times 10^{3} t\)

(a) Find and plot the steady-state output waveform expected from the circuit.
(b) If the quality factor of the inductor used was measured to be \(50\) at \(1 kHz\) and the test square wave was obtained from a function generator which has an output resistance of \(50 Ω\), find and plot the steady-state output voltage waveform that will be observed.

Admin
Solution

Solution (a)

The circuit is the familiar series \(RLC\) circuit and the frequency-response function for resistor voltage in such a circuit has been shown to be

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{V_{R}(j \omega)}{V_{S}(j \omega)} &=\frac{j 2 \xi \omega \omega_{n}}{\left(\omega_{n}^{2}-\omega^{2}\right)+j 2 \xi \omega_{n} \omega} \\ &=\frac{2 \xi \omega \omega_{n}}{\sqrt{\left(\omega_{n}^{2} \omega^{2}\right)^{2}+4 \xi^{2} \omega_{n}^{2} \omega^{2}}} \angle \phi_{R} \text { where } \phi_{R}=\frac{\pi}{2}-\tan ^{-1} \frac{2 \xi \omega_{n} \omega}{\omega_{n}^{2}-\omega^{2}} \mathrm{rad} \end{aligned}\)

 

We define \(x =\omega/\omega_{n}\) and express the above function as

\(\frac{V_{R}(j \omega)}{V_{S}(j \omega)}=\frac{j 2 \xi x}{\left(1-x^{2}\right)+j 2 \xi x}\)

 

The input signal is a sum of sinusoids with odd multiples of \(1 kHz\) as their frequencies. Thus, the separation between frequencies is large and it is quite possible that \(x\) may turn out to be quite large compared to \(1\) for many of the sinusoidal components. We calculate \(\omega_{n}\) to verify this.

\(f_{n}=1 / 2 \pi \sqrt{L C}=\frac{1}{2 \pi \times \sqrt{25.3 \times 10^{-3} \times 1 \times 10^{-6}}}=1 \mathrm{kHz}\)

 

Critical resistance \(=2\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}=318 2\Omega \)

\(\therefore\) Damping factor \(\xi=\frac{20}{318 2}=0.063\) and Quality factor \(Q =\frac{1}{2\xi}=7 95\)

Therefore, the values of \(x\) of interest to us are the odd integers. For \(x ≥ 3\), we can approximate the gain and phase expressions as shown in the following.

\(\begin{array}{l} \frac{V_{R}(j \omega)}{V_{S}(j \omega)}=\frac{j 2 \xi x}{\left(1-x^{2}\right)+j 2 \xi x} \\ \left|\frac{V_{R}(j \omega)}{V_{S}(j \omega)}\right|=\frac{2 \xi x}{\sqrt{\left(1-x^{2}\right)^{2}+4 \xi^{2} x^{2}}} \approx \frac{2 \xi}{x} \text { for } x>1 \\ \text { Phase }=\frac{\pi}{2}-\left(\pi-\tan ^{-1} \frac{2 \xi x}{x^{2}-1}\right) \text { for } x>1 \\ \approx-\frac{\pi}{2}+\tan ^{-1} \frac{2 \xi}{x}=-\frac{\pi}{2}+\frac{2 \xi}{x} \text { for } x>1 \end{array}\)

 

The following table shows gain and phase of output for \(x\) values from \(1\) to \(11\).

x 1 3 5 7 9 11
Gain 1 0.042 0.025 0.018 0.014 0.012
Phase(rad) 0 -1.53 -1.545 -1.553 -1.557 -1.56

 

Now the first six terms of output may be constructed using the amplitude information.

\(\begin{aligned} v_{o}(t)=& 1 \sin 2 \pi \times 10^{3} t+0.014 \sin \left(6 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.53\right)+0.005 \sin \left(10 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.545\right) \\ &+0.0026 \sin \left(14 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.553\right)+0.0013 \sin \left(18 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.557\right) \\ &+0.0011 \sin \left(22 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.56\right) \end{aligned}\)

 

Obviously, the first three terms are the ones which are significant. All the others can easily be ignored (including the higher frequency components that we did not evaluate).

\(\therefore v_{o}(t) \approx 1 \sin 2 \pi \times 10^{3} t+0.014 \sin \left(6 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.53\right)+0.005 \sin \left(10 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.545\right) \mathrm{V}\)

 

This waveform has only about \(1.5\)% of other frequency content. It is almost pure sinusoid at \(1 kHz,\) The \(25.3 mH\) inductor has a reactance of \(2π × 10^{3} × 25.3 × 10^{-3} = 159 Ω\) at \(1 kHz\) and since its \(Q\) at that frequency is given as \(50\), it has a series resistance of \(159/50 = 3.18 Ω\). The function generator that provides the square wave contributes \(50 Ω\). Thus, the total series resistance in the series \(RLC\) circuit is \(73.18\) \(Ω\). The value of \(f_{n}\) of the circuit remains unchanged at \(1 kHz\). But its \(\xi\) factor becomes \(0.23\) now. Also, the total voltage that develops across \(73.18 Ω\) has to be multiplied by \(20/73.18 = 0.273\) to calculate the voltage across the load resistance. Thus, the frequency-response expression now is

\(\frac{V_{R}(j \omega)}{V_{S}(j \omega)}=\frac{j 0.547 \xi x}{\left(1-x^{2}\right)+j 2 \xi x}\)

 

We continue to use the same approximations for \(x >> 1.\)

The following table shows gain and phase of output for \(x\) values from \(1\) to \(11.\)

x 1 3 5 7 9 11
Gain 0.273 0.042 0.025 0.018 0.014 0.011
Phase(rad) 0 -1.418 -1.479 -1.505 -1.52 -1.53

 

Now the first six terms of output may be constructed using the amplitude information.

\(\begin{aligned} v_{o}(t)=& 0.273 \sin 2 \pi \times 10^{3} t+0.014 \sin \left(6 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.418\right)+0.005 \sin \left(10 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.479\right) \\ &+0.0026 \sin \left(14 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.505\right)+0.0015 \sin \left(18 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.52\right) \\ &+0.001 \sin \left(22 \pi \times 10^{3} t-1.53\right) \end{aligned}\)

 

Higher frequency terms are neglected. The output waveforms for the two cases are plotted in Fig. 12.10-13.

The output in case (b) shows distortion clearly.

This example illustrates the importance of high quality factor in filtering a square wave to a high \(Q\) sine wave. It also tells us that an otherwise high \(Q\) factor circuit may appear to be a low \(Q\) circuit if we forget about the output resistance of the signal generator that we use to test the circuit. The test signal should be passed on to the test circuit through a unity gain buffer amplifier with negligible output resistance.
