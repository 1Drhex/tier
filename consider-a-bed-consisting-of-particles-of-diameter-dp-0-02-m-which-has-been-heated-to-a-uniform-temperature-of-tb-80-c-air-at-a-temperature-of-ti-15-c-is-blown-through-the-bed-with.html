Guest Question.

Consider a bed consisting of particles of diameter \(D_{p}=0.02\) which has been heated to a uniform temperature of \(T_{b}=80^{\circ} C\). Air at a temperature of \(T_{i}=15^{\circ} C\) is blown through the bed with a mass velocity of \(G=0.5 kg / s m ^{2}\). Immediately afterwards, it is seen that the air exiting the bed is at \(T_{e}=65^{\circ} C\). Estimate the bed height using the correlations proposed by (a) Whitaker and (b) Lof and Hawley. Porosity of the bed is known to be \(\epsilon=0.5\).

Admin
Solution

Step 1 Air properties are taken at the mean temperature of \(T_{m}=\frac{T_{i}+T_{o}}{2}=\frac{15+65}{2}=40^{\circ} C\).

Density of air: \(\rho=1.117 kg / m ^{3}\)

Specific heat of air: \(C_{p}=1005 J / kg ^{\circ} C\)

Kinematic viscosity of air: \(\nu=17.1 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s\)

Thermal conductivity of air: \(k=0.0274 W / m ^{\circ} C\)

Prandtl number of air: \(\operatorname{Pr}=0.699\)

Step 2 Superficial velocity of air is calculated as (based on unit bed cross section area) \(U_{s}=\frac{G}{\rho}=\frac{0.5}{1.092}=0.448 m / s\). Hence, the modified Reynolds number is given by

\(R e_{m}=\frac{U_{s} D_{p}}{\nu(1-\epsilon)}=\frac{0.448 \times 0.02}{17.1 \times 10^{-6}(1-0.5)}=1047.1\)

The volumetric heat transfer coefficient may then be calculated based on Whitaker correlation (17.122) \(h_{v(a)}\) as

\(h_{v}=\frac{6(1-\epsilon)^{2} k}{\epsilon D_{p}^{2}}\left(0.5 \operatorname{R} e_{m}^{\frac{1}{2}}+0.2 \operatorname{Re}_{m}^{\frac{2}{3}}\right) \operatorname{Pr}^{\frac{1}{3}}\) (17.122)

\(h_{v(a)}=\frac{6(1-0.5)^{2} \times 0.0274}{0.5 \times 0.02^{2}}\left(0.5 \times 1047.1^{\frac{1}{2}}+0.2 \times 1047.1^{\frac{2}{3}}\right) 0.0 .699^{\frac{1}{3}}\) \(=6711.9 W / m ^{3}{ }^{\circ} C\)

Step 3 Modeling heat transfer between bed and air: Consider a bed of height H and unit cross section area as shown in Fig. 17.28. The axis of the bed is vertical and oriented parallel to the z axis. Consider a bed element of thickness \(\Delta z\) as shown. Heat balance requires that heat gained by air as it moves up through the element be equal to the heat lost by the bed particles. Thus

\(G C_{p} T(z+\Delta z)-G C_{p} T(z)=h_{v}\left(T_{b}-T\right) \Delta z\)

Using Taylor expansion and retaining two terms and in the limit \(\Delta z \rightarrow 0\), the above simplifies to

\(G C_{p} \frac{ d T}{ d z}=h_{v}\left(T_{b}-T\right)\)

This equation is in variable separable form and is easily integrated once with respect to z to get \(-\ln \left(T_{b}-T\right)=\frac{h_{v} z}{G C_{p}}+C\) where C is a constant of integration. Using \(T=T_{i}\) at z = 0 we have \(-\ln \left(T_{b}-T_{i}\right)=C\) . Hence the air temperature at any z is given by

\(\ln \left(\frac{T_{b}-T_{i}}{T_{b}-T}\right)=\frac{h_{v} z}{G C_{p}}\)

We are given that \(T=T_{e}\) at z = H. Hence, we have

\(H=\frac{G C_{p}}{h_{v}} \ln \left(\frac{T_{b}-T_{i}}{T_{b}-T_{e}}\right)\)

Step 4 Based on above analysis the bed height may be calculated.

Whitaker correlation: \(H_{(a)}=\frac{0.5 \times 1005}{6711.9} \ln \left(\frac{80-15}{80-65}\right)=0.110 m\)

Lof and Hawley correlation: \(H_{(b)}=\frac{0.5 \times 1005}{6186.88} \ln \left(\frac{80-15}{80-65}\right)=0.119 m\)

From a design point of view the bigger answer may be chosen.
