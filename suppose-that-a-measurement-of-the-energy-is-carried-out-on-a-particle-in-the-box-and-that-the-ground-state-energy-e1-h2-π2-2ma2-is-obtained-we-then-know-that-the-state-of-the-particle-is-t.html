Guest Question.

Suppose that a measurement of the energy is carried out on a particle in the box and that the ground-state energy \(E_{1}=\hslash ^{2}\pi ^{2}/2ma^{2}\) is obtained. We then know that the state of the particle is the ground state, with energy eigenfunction \(\left\langle x|E_{1}=\hslash ^{2}\pi ^{2}/2m a^{2} \right\rangle =\psi _{1} (x)\). What if we now change the potential energy well that is confining the particle and pull the walls of the well out rapidly so they are positioned at x =±a instead of x = ±a/2?

In fact, we imagine pulling the walls out so rapidly that instantaneously the state of the particle doesn’t change. As can be readily seen by comparing the wave function of the particle in this state with the energy eigenfunctions of the new, larger potential well (see Fig. 6.11), the particle is no longer in an energy eigenstate. Thus we can ask, for example, what the probability is that a subsequent measurement of the energy of the particle will yield a particular energy eigenvalue such as the ground-state energy of the new well.\( ^{19}\)

Solution

we call the initial state |i〉 and the final state |f〉, the amplitude to find a particle in the state |i〉 in the state |f〉 is 〈f|i〉. Since we have already calculated the position-space wave functions, it is convenient, as noted in , to calculate the amplitude 〈f|i〉 by inserting a complete set of position states between the bra and the ket:

\(\left\langle f|i\right\rangle=\int{dx}\left\langle f|x\right\rangle\left\langle x|i\right\rangle\)

 

The amplitude 〈x |i〉, where the initial state is the ground state of the well of width a \((|i〉=|E_{1} ^{width a)}〉 )\), is given by

\(\left\langle x|E_{1} ^{width a} \right\rangle=\psi _{1} ^{width a}(x)= \begin{cases} \sqrt{\frac{2}{a} }\cos\frac{\pi }{a} & \left|x\right|\lt a/2 \\ 0 & \left|x\right|\gt a/2 \end{cases} \)

 

while the amplitude 〈f|x〉, where the final state is the ground state of the well with width 2a \((|f〉=|E_{1} ^{width 2a}〉)\) , is given by

\(\left\langle E_{1} ^{width 2a}|x \right\rangle=\begin{cases} \sqrt{\frac{1}{a} }\cos\frac{\pi x}{2a} & \left|x\right|\lt a \\ 0 & \left|x\right|\gt a \end{cases}\)

 

Thus

\(\left\langle E_{1} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle=\int{dx\left\langle E_{1} ^{width 2a}|x\right\rangle }\left\langle x|E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle\)

 

\( =\int_{-a/2}^{a/2}{dx\sqrt{\frac{1}{a} }\cos\frac{\pi x}{2a}\sqrt{\frac{2}{a} }cos\frac{\pi x}{a} }=\frac{8}{3\pi }\)

 

where the integrand is nonzero only for |x| < a/2 because \(\left\langle E_{1} ^{width a}|x\right\rangle \) is nonzero only in this region. Thus the probability of finding the particle in the ground state of the bigger well is

\(|\left\langle E_{1} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle |^{2}=\frac{64}{9\pi ^{2} }=0.72\)

 

In this way we could go on to calculate the probability of finding the particle in the other energy eigenstates of the bigger well. The form of the energy eigenfunction for the n =3 state of the bigger well, as shown in Fig. 6.11, suggests that there is a significant overlap of the wave functions \(\psi _{1} ^{width a}\) and \(\psi _{3} ^{width 2a}\),and thus there should be a significant probability of finding the particle in this n = 3 state as well (see Problem 6.11). On the other hand, we can also quickly see that there is zero amplitude of finding the particle in the even n states. Since \(\left\langle x|E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle \) is an even function of \(x|\psi _{n}(-x)=\psi _{n}(x)\) for n odd] while \(\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}|x\right\rangle \) for n even is an odd function of \(x|\psi _{n}(-x)=-\psi _{n}(x)\) for n even], the product of an even and an odd function is of course an odd function, which vanishes when integrated from -a/2 to a/2. The evenness or oddness of the energy eigenfunctions, often referred to as their parity, turns out to be a general characteristic of the eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian when the potential energy is even, that is, V (-x) = V (x). We will discuss the reason for this more fully in Chapter 7.

Finally, we can ask how the system evolves in time after the walls of the potential energy well have been pulled out. Since the system is no longer in an energy eigenstate, it is no longer a stationary state, and thus can exhibit interesting time dependence. Since the initial state at t = 0 can be written as a superposition of the energy eigenstates:

\(|\psi (0)〉=|E_{1} ^{width a}〉=\sum{| E_{n} ^{width 2a}〉\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}| E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle }\)

 

then

\( |\psi (t)〉=e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar }\sum{|E_{n} ^{width 2a}〉\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a}\right\rangle }\)

 

\(=\sum\limits_{n}{e ^{-iE_{n} width 2at/\hbar }|E_{n} ^{width 2a}〉 \left\langle E_{n} ^{ width 2a }|E_{1} ^{width a} \right\rangle }\)

 

and therefore

\(\left\langle x|\psi (t)\right\rangle=\sum\limits_{n}{e^{-i\hbar n^{2}\pi ^{2}t/8ma^{2} }\left\langle x|E_{n} ^{width 2a} \right\rangle\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a} \right\rangle }\)

 

\( =\sum\limits_{n}{e^{-i\hbar n^{2}\pi ^{2}t/8ma^{2} }\psi _{n} ^{width 2a} (x)\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a} \right\rangle }\)

 

Once the amplitudes \(\left\langle E_{n} ^{width 2a}|E_{1} ^{width a} \right\rangle \)have been calculated, it is probably best to carry out the sum numerically to see how the wave function evolves in time.
