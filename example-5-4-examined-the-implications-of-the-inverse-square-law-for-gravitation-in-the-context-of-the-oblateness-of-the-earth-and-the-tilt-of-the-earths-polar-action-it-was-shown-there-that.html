Guest Question.

examined the implications of the inverse square law for gravitation in the context of the oblateness of the Earth and the tilt of the Earth’s polar action. It was shown there that the Moon and the Sun each exert a force-couple system acting at the Earth’s center of mass. Show that the primary consequence of these couples is a nearly steady precession of the Earth’s polar axis about an axis that is normal to the orbital plane, accompanied by a very minor wobble. Determine the precession rate associated with each attracting body.

Admin
Solution

Although the role of gravitational moments exerted on the Earth is not like the effect of gravity on a spinning top, the implications of the similarities of a top and the Earth’s rotation is quite interesting. It was shown in that the moment exerted by the Moon is more than twice as large as the Sun’s effect, so we start by considering the Moon’s effect. The previous analysis found that the Moon’s gravitational attraction can be resolved into a force and couple acting at the Earth’s center (of mass) given by

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{F}=\frac{G M_{\text {moon }} M_{e}}{R_{\text {moon }}^{2}} \bar{e}_{R}, \\\\\bar{M}=\frac{3 G M_{\text {moon }}\left(I-I^{\prime}\right)}{R_{\text {moon }}^{3}}\left(\bar{e}_{R} \cdot \bar{k}\right)\left(\bar{e}_{R} \times \bar{k}\right),\end{array} (1) \)

where \(\bar{k}\) is the unit vector from the South Pole to the North Pole, \(\bar{e}_{R}\) is the unit vector from the center of the Earth to the center of the Moon, \(R\) is the distance between centers, and \(I\) and \(I^{\prime}\) are the centroidal moments of inertia, with \(I\) being about the polar axis. To describe the unit vectors and angular motion of the Earth, we let \(x y z\) be the body-fixed coordinate system, and let \(X Y Z\) be a translating reference frame whose origin is always situated at the Earth’s center with the \(X Y\) plane coincident with the orbital plane of the Moon. An auxiliary coordinate system \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) is defined such that it precesses about the \(Z\) axis by \(\psi\) and nutates about the \(y^{\prime}\) axis by angle \(\theta\). The \(y^{\prime}\) axis, which is the line of nodes, lies in the orbital plane. The spin rate of \(x y z\) relative to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) is \(\dot{\phi}\), which is the spin rate \(\omega_{e}\) of the Earth. As shown in the sketch, the radial line to the Moon coincides with the Moon’s orbital plane, and this line rotates at the orbital speed \(\Omega\) of the Moon, which we consider to be constant. (By taking \(R_{\text {moon }}\) and \(\Omega\) to be constant, we are ignoring the eccentricity of the orbit.)

Thus the angle between the \(X\) axis and \(\bar{e}_{R}\) is \(\Omega t\). (The definition of the instant when \(t=0\) will not be relevant to the analysis.)

Because of the axisymmetry of the Earth about its polar axis, we can use \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) as the global coordinate system for the equations of motion. The transformation from \(X Y Z\) to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) components is the standard one for Eulerian angles:

\(\begin{array}{l}{\left[\begin{array}{lll}x^{\prime} & y^{\prime} & z^{\prime}\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}=[R]\left[\begin{array}{lll}X & Y & Z\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}}, \\\\{[R]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \theta & 0 & -\sin \theta \\\\0 & 1 & 0 \\\\\sin \theta & 0 & \cos \theta\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \psi & \sin \psi & 0 \\\\-\sin \psi & \cos \psi & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]}. (2)\end{array}\)

The components of \(\bar{e}_{R}\) relative to \(X Y Z\) are found from the sketch to be

\(\bar{e}_{R}=\cos (\Omega t) \bar{I}+\sin (\Omega t) \bar{J}\)

Using this representation in conjunction with \([R]\) gives the \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) components of \(\bar{e}_{R} .\) Trigonometric identities for the sine and cosine of the difference of two angles simplify the result to

\(\left\{e_{R}\right\}_{x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=[R]\left\{\begin{array}{c}\cos (\Omega t) \\\\\sin (\Omega t) \\\\0\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}\cos \theta \cos (\Omega t-\psi) \\\\\sin (\Omega t-\psi) \\\\\sin \theta \cos (\Omega t-\psi)\end{array}\right\}. (3) \)

This expression enables us to compute the dot and cross products of \(\bar{e}_{R}\) and \(\bar{k}^{\prime} \equiv \bar{k}\) :

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{e}_{R} \cdot \bar{k}=\sin \theta \cos (\Omega t-\psi), \\\\\bar{e}_{R} \times \bar{k}=\sin (\Omega t-\psi) \bar{i}-\cos \theta \cos (\Omega t-\psi) \bar{j}.\end{array}\)

Substituting these terms into \(\bar{M}\), as described in Eqs. (1), leads to

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{M}=\Gamma \sin \theta \sin (2 \Omega t-2 \psi) \bar{i}-G \sin \theta \cos \theta[1+\cos (2 \Omega t-2 \psi)] \bar{j}, \\\\\Gamma=\frac{3 G M_{\text {moon }}\left(I-I^{\prime}\right)}{2 R_{\mathrm{moon}}^{3}}.\end{array} (4) \)

We are now ready to address the equations of motion. The angular velocity of the Earth is

\(\bar{\omega}=\psi \bar{K}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}^{\prime}+\dot{\phi} \bar{k}^{\prime}=-\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \bar{i}^{\prime}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}^{\prime}+(\dot{\phi}+\psi \cos \theta) \bar{k}^{\prime}. (5)\)

The translational and rotational portions of the motion are uncoupled, so the rotational kinetic energy is the same as in Eq. (10.2.2) for a spinning top. Rather than using potential energy to describe the role of gravity, it is easier to evaluate the virtual work done by the gravitational moment. The Eulerian angles are the generalized coordinates. The virtual rotation is found by analogy to the angular velocity to be

\(T=\frac{1}{2}\left[I^{\prime} \dot{\psi}^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}+I^{\prime} \dot{\theta}^{2}+I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi})^{2}\right] (10.2.2)\)

 

\(\overline{\delta \Theta}=-\delta \psi \sin \theta \bar{i}^{\prime}+\delta \theta \bar{j}^{\prime}+(\delta \phi+\delta \psi \cos \theta) \bar{k}^{\prime}.\)

The virtual work is \(\bar{M} \cdot \overline{\delta \Theta}\), which leads to the generalized forces being

\(Q_{\psi}=\Gamma(\sin \theta)^{2} \sin (2 \Omega t-2 \psi), \quad Q_{\theta}=-\Gamma \sin \theta \cos \theta[1+\cos (2 \Omega t-2 \psi)].\)

The spin angle does not appear explicitly in \(T\) and \(Q_{\phi}=0\), so the associated generalized momentum is conserved. Thus the Lagrange equation for the spin angle is the same as for a spinning top:

\(p_{\phi}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\phi}}=I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}) \equiv I^{\prime} \beta_{\phi}. (6)\)

Unlike the precession angle of a spinning top, here the precession angle is not ignorable because the generalized forces depend on this variable. The Lagrange equation for the precession is

\(\begin{array}{l}{\left[I^{\prime}(\sin \theta)^{2}+I(\cos \theta)^{2}\right] \ddot{\psi}+I \ddot{\phi} \cos \theta+2\left(I-I^{\prime}\right) \dot{\psi} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta \cos \theta} \\\\-I \dot{\theta} \dot{\phi} \sin \theta=\Gamma(\sin \theta)^{2} \sin (2 \Omega t-2 \psi).\end{array} (7) \)

The equation of motion for the nutation is the same as that for the spinning top, except that the potential-energy term is replaced with the generalized force \(Q_{\theta}\). Substitution of Eq. (6) into that equation gives

\(I^{\prime} \ddot{\theta}-I^{\prime}(\dot{\psi} \sin \theta)\left(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta-\beta_{\phi}\right)=-\Gamma \sin \theta \cos \theta[1+\cos (2 \Omega t-2 \psi)]. (8)\)

We know from astronomical observation that the nutation angle is nearly constant. Furthermore, if the time-dependent term were not present in Eq. (6), a constant value of \(\theta\) would lead to a constant value of \(\dot{\psi}\). Thus we let

\(\theta=\theta_{0}+\varepsilon \theta_{1}(t), \quad \dot{\psi}=\dot{\psi}_{0}+\varepsilon \dot{\psi}_{1}(t). (9)\)

where \(\varepsilon\) is a small parameter and the quantities with subscript one are not large. When the leading-order terms that arise in Eq. (8) are matched to the constant portion of the right side, the result is

\(I^{\prime}\left(\dot{\psi}_{0} \sin \theta_{0}\right)\left(\dot{\psi}_{0} \cos \theta_{0}-\beta_{\phi}\right)=\Gamma \sin \theta_{0} \cos \theta_{0}.\)

The nutation angle is not zero, so \(\sin \theta_{0}\) can be factored out. The precession rate obtained from this quadratic equation is much less than the Earth’s spin rate, so we may simplify the preceding relation by ignoring the contribution of \(\dot{\psi}\) to Eq. (6), which gives \(I^{\prime} \beta_{\phi} \approx I \dot{\phi}_{0}\). Correspondingly, we find†

\(\dot{\psi}_{0} \approx-\frac{\Gamma \cos \theta_{0}}{I \dot{\phi}_{0}}=\frac{3}{2} \frac{G M_{\text {moon }}}{R_{\text {moon }}^{3} \dot{\phi}_{0}}\left(\frac{I^{\prime}-I}{I}\right) \cos \theta_{0}. (10)\)

The spin rate of the Earth is \(\dot{\phi}_{0}=7.292\left(10^{-5}\right) ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\). lists \(M_{\text {moon }}=\) \(7.348\left(10^{22}\right) ~\mathrm{kg}\) and \(R_{\text {moon }}=3.844\left(10^{8}\right) ~\mathrm{m}\). The quantity \(\left(I^{\prime}-I\right) / I\) is called the dynamic elipticity. The accepted value for this parameter is \(1 / 305\), which leads to

\(\left(\dot{\psi}_{0}\right)_{\text {moon }}=5.356\left(10^{-12}\right) ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

The corresponding period is \(2 \pi /\left(\dot{\psi}_{0}\right)_{\text {moon }}\), which is 37180 years. This value is \(50 \%\) greater than the observed period of 26000 years. One source for the discrepancy is that we have not accounted for the effect of the Sun. Changing the parameters in Eq. (10) to those for the Sun gives an expression for the precession rate if the Sun is the sole attracting body. The significant aspect is that the orbital plane of the Moon is approximately \(5^{\circ}\) different from the ecliptic plane, which is the term used to refer to the plane of the Earth’s orbit. Thus it is a reasonable approximation to use the same precession axis to analyze the effects of the Sun and the Moon. Correspondingly, we may add the associated gravitational moment components associated with each attracting body. The oscillatory frequency \(2 \Omega\) is much lower for the Sun than for the Moon, but the mean values, which are the nonoscillatory parts of the moments, add. The parameters for the Sun are \(M_{s}=1.98892\left(10^{30}\right) ~\mathrm{kg}\) and \(R_{s}=1.4960\left(10^{11}\right)\) \(\mathrm{m}\), which leads to

\(\left(\dot{\psi}_{0}\right)_{\mathrm{sun}}=2.452\left(10^{-12}\right) ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

The period obtained from adding to the precession rate the contributions of the Moon and the Sun is

\(T=\frac{2 \pi}{\left(\dot{\psi}_{0}\right)_{\text {moon }}+\left(\dot{\psi}_{0}\right)_{\text {sun }}}=25500 \text { years. }\)

This result is very close to the measured period of 26000 years. The agreement is even more remarkable when one considers that the analysis treated the Earth as though it were a rigid body, which ignores such effects as tidal motions of the oceans. The average precessional motion is referred to in astronomy as the precession of the equinoxes. (At an equinox the polar axis is perpendicular to \(\bar{e}_{R}\), corresponding to \(\Omega t\) in the figure for this problem being an odd multiple of \(\pi / 2 .\) ) The consequence of the precession of the equinoxes is that 13 000 years from now the seasons will be reversed, so that the first day of summer in the Northern Hemisphere will come in December. Another consequence is that Polaris, which is popularly known as the North Star because it is almost concurrent with the Earth’s polar axis, in the future will no longer be aligned in that manner.

One could proceed to analyze the second-order approximation associated with Eqs. (9). Because the first-order terms describe the effect of the mean value of the gravitational moment, the higher-order terms are driven by the oscillatory portion of the moment. The contributions of the Moon and the Sun add, but, as was noted previously, they have vastly different frequencies. In any event, the nutation and precession rates must exhibit minor fluctuations. The more noticeable one is the nutation, which represents a minor wobble. Earth-based astronomers must compensate for this wobble, as well as the precession, when they aim a telescope.

† Goldstein (1980) in Section 11-3 derived this expression from Lagrange’s equations by evaluating the gravitational potential energy of a nonspherical axisymmetric body whose axis is tilted relative to the ecliptic plane. The derivation of the gravitational force-couple system in is more general because it addresses an arbitrary body.
