Guest Question.

Two spaceships A and B are moving in opposite directions, as shown in Figure 39.16. An observer on the Earth measures the speed of ship A to be 0.750c and the speed of ship B to be 0.850c. Find the velocity of ship B as observed by the crew on ship A.

Admin
Solution

Solution We can solve this problem by taking the \(S^{\prime}\) frame as being attached to ship A, so that v=0.750 c relative to the Earth (the S frame). We can consider ship \(\mathrm{B}\) as moving with a velocity \(u_{x}=-0.850 c\) relative to the Earth. Hence, we can obtain the velocity of ship \(\mathrm{B}\) relative to ship A by using Equation 39.16

\(u_{x}^{\prime}=\frac{u_{x}-v}{1-\frac{u_{x} v}{c^{2}}}=\frac{-0.850 c-0.750 c}{1-\frac{(-0.850 c)(0.750 c)}{c^{2}}}=-0.977 c\)

The negative sign indicates that ship \(\mathrm{B}\) is moving in the negative x direction as observed by the crew on ship A. Note that the speed is less than c. That is, a body whose speed is less than c in one frame of reference must have a speed less than c in any other frame. (If the Galilean velocity transformation equation were used in this example, we would find that \(u_{x}^{i}=u_{x}-v=-0.850 c-0.750 c=-1.60 c, \) which is impossi-ble. The Galilean transformation equation does not work in relativistic situations.)
