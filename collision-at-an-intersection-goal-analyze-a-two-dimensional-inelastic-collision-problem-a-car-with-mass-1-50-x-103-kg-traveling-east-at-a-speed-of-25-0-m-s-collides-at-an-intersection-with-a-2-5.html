Collision at an Intersection

GOAL Analyze a two-dimensional inelastic collision.

PROBLEM A car with mass \(1.50 \times 10^{3} kg\) traveling east at a speed of 25.0 m/s collides at an intersection with a \(2.50 \times 10^{3} -kg\) van traveling north at a speed of 20.0 m/s, as shown in Figure 6.16. Find the magnitude and direction of the velocity of the wreckage after the collision, assuming that the vehicles undergo a perfectly inelastic collision (that is, they stick together) and assuming that friction between the vehicles and the road can be neglected.

STRATEGY Use conservation of momentum in two dimensions. (Kinetic energy is not conserved.) Choose coordinates as in Figure 6.16. Before the collision, the only object having momentum in the x-direction is the car, while the van carries all the momentum in the y-direction. After the totally inelastic collision, both vehicles move together at some common speed \(v_{f}\) and angle \(\theta\). Solve for these two unknowns, using the two components of the conservation of momentum equation.

Admin
Solution

It’s also possible to first find the x- and y-componentsFind the x-components of the initial and final total momenta: \(\begin{aligned}\sum p_{x i} &=m_{ car } v_{ car }=\left(1.50 \times 10^{3} kg \right)(25.0 m / s ) \\&=3.75 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s \\\sum p_{x f} &=\left(m_{ car }+m_{ van }\right) v_{f} \cos \theta=\left(4.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right) v_{f} \cos \theta\end{aligned}\)

 

Set the initial x-momentum equal to the final x-momentum: \(\text { (1) } 3.75 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s =\left(4.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right) v_{f} \cos \theta\)

 

Find the y-components of the initial and final total momenta:

\(\begin{aligned}\sum p_{i y} &=m_{ van } v_{ van }=\left(2.50 \times 10^{3} kg \right)(20.0 m / s ) \\&=5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s \\\sum p_{f y} &=\left(m_{ car }+m_{ van }\right) v_{f} \sin \theta=\left(4.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right) v_{f} \sin \theta\end{aligned}\)

 

Set the initial y-momentum equal to the final y-momentum: \(\text { (2) } 5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s =\left(4.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right) v_{f} \sin \theta\)

 

Divide Equation (2) by Equation (1) and solve for \(\theta\):

\(\begin{aligned}\tan \theta &=\frac{5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s }{3.75 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m }=1.33 \\\theta &=53.1^{\circ}\end{aligned}\)

 

Substitute this angle back into Equation (2) to find \(v_{f}\) : \(v_{f}=\frac{5.00 \times 10^{4} kg \cdot m / s }{\left(4.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right) \sin 53.1^{\circ}}=15.6 m / s\)

 

REMARKS It’s also possible to first find the x- and y components \(v_{f x} \text { and } v_{f y}\) of the resultant velocity. The magnitude and direction of the resultant velocity can then be found with the Pythagorean theorem, \(v_{f}=\sqrt{v_{f x}^{2}+v_{f y}^{2}}\) , and the inverse tangent function \(\theta=\tan ^{-1}\left(v_{f y} / v_{f x}\right)\). Setting up this alternate approach is a simple matter of substituting \(v_{f x}=v_{f} \cos \theta\) and \(v_{f y}=v_{f} \sin \theta\) into Equations (1) and (2).
