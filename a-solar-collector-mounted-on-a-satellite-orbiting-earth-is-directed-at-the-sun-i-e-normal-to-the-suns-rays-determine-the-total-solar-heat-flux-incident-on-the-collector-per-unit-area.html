Guest Question.

A solar collector mounted on a satellite orbiting Earth is directed at the sun (i.e., normal to the sun’s rays). Determine the total solar heat flux incident on the collector per unit area.

Admin
Solution

The total heat rate leaving the sun is \(\dot{Q} _{S} =4\pi R^{2}_{S} E_{b} (T_{S} )\) , where \(R_{S} \simeq 6.96 \times 10^{8} m\) is the radius of the sun. Placing an imaginary spherical shell around the sun of radius \(S_{Es} =1.496 \times 10^{11} m\) , where \(S_{Es}\) is the distance between the sun and Earth, we find the heat flux going through that imaginary sphere (which includes the solar collector) as

\(q_{sol} =\frac{4\pi R^{2}_{S} E_{b} (T_{S} )}{4\pi S^{2}_{ES} } =I_{b} (T_{S} )\frac{\pi R^{2}_{S} }{S^{2}_{ES}} =I_{b} (T_{S} ) \Omega _{S}\) ,

where we have replaced the sun’s emissive power by intensity, \(E_{b} =\pi I_{b}\) , and \(\Omega _{S}=6.80 \times 10^{-5} sr\) is the solid angle with which the sun is seen from Earth, as determined in Therefore, with \(I_{b} (T_{S} )=\sigma T^{4}_{S} /\pi\) and \(T_{S}=5777 K\) ,

\(q_{in} =-(\sigma T^{4}_{S}/\pi )(\Omega _{S})=-\frac{1}{\pi } 5.670\times 10^{-8} \times 5777^{4} \times 6.80 \times 10^{-5} W/m^{2} = -1367 W/m^{2}\),

where we have added a minus sign to emphasize that the heat flux is going into the collector. The total incoming heat flux may, of course, also be determined from equation

\((q_{\lambda } )_{in} = \int_{\cos \theta _{i}\lt 0 }^{}{I_{\lambda } (\widehat{S_{i} } )\cos \theta _{i} d\Omega _{i}}\) .

as

\(q_{in} = \int_{\cos \theta _{i}\lt 0 }^{}{I (\widehat{S_{i} } )\cos \theta _{i} d\Omega _{i}}\) .

Since light from the sun arrives only from the extremely small solid angle of \(\Omega _{S}\) (over which \(\cos \theta _{i}\simeq -1\) ), the integral may be written as

\(q_{in}=I_{i}\times (-1)\times \Omega _{S}\) .

Comparing this with the previous expression we see that the incoming solar intensity at the collector, 7;,
is identical to the intensity leaving from the sun’s surface, \(I_{b}(T_{s})\). This invariability property of radiative
intensity as it travels through vacuum will be discussed in more detail in Chapter 9.

The absolute value of the heat flux determined in the previous example is known as the solar constant. The most accurate measurements and spectral integration of extraterrestrial solar irradiation data have been made during the past twenty years by Earth-orbiting satellites [2,10,11], and are qualitatively indicated in Fig. 1-3, resulting in

\(q_{sol}=(1367±2)W/m^{2}\) (1.40)
