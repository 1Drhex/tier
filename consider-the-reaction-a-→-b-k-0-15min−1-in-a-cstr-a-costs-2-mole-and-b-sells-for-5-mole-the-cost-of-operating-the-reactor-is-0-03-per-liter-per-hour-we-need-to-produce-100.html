Guest Question.

Consider the reaction:

\(A \rightarrow B , \quad k=0.15 min ^{-1}\)

in a CSTR. A costs $2/mole, and B sells for $5/mole. The cost of operating the reactor is $0.03 per liter per hour. We need to produce 100 moles of B/hour using \(C_{ A 0}\) = 2 moles/L. Assume no value or cost of disposal of unreacted A (i.e., separation or recovery cost to A is identical to the fresh A cost).

a. Perform a mole balance on the reactor to relate the conversion with reactor size.

b. What is the optimum conversion and reactor size?

c. What is the cash flow (or profit per mole of A feed to the reactor) from the process?

d. At what operating cost do we break even?

Admin
Solution

A sketch of the reactor system is shown in Fig. E5.4.

a. It is understood that the CSTR is operating under constant temperature (isothermal) and steady state conditions, that is, no accumulation of any sort of materials inside the reactor. Mole balance around the reactor for B yields

In – Out + Generation = Accumulation

\(0-F_{B}+r_{B} V=0\) (E5.4.1)

The rate law gives

\(r_{B}=v_{B} r=k C_{A}\) (E5.4.2)

It is understood that the reaction mixture density is not changing, that is, the volumetric flow rates at the reactor inlet and the outlet are nearly unchanged. We have

\(C_{A}=\frac{F_{A}}{V}=\frac{F_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}{Q}=C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)\) (E5.4.3)

Substituting Eqs. (E5.4.2) \(r_{B}=v_{B} r=k C_{A}\) and (E5.4.3) \(C_{A}=\frac{F_{A}}{V}=\frac{F_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}{Q}=C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)\) into Eq. (E5.4.1) \(0-F_{B}+r_{B} V=0\) , we obtain

\(V=\frac{F_{B}}{r_{B}}=\frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\) (E5.4.4)

b. Based on the economic information at hand, we can estimate the gross profit for the reaction process as

GP$ = Value from B – Cost of A – Operating Cost

\(GP \$=\$_{ B } F_{ B }-\$_{ A } F_{ A 0}-\$_{ V } V\) (E5.4.5)

where \(\$_{B}\) , \(\$_{A}\) , and \(\$_{V}\) are the molar value of product B, molar cost of reactant A, and the unit operating cost of reactor based on the reactor volume and time, respectively.

Based on the stoichiometry, we have

\(F_{B}=F_{A 0}-F_{A}=F_{A 0} f_{A}\) (E5.4.6)

which leads to

\(F_{A 0}=\frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}}\) (E5.4.7)

Substituting Eqs. (E5.4.7) \(F_{A 0}=\frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}}\) and (E5.4.4) \(V=\frac{F_{B}}{r_{B}}=\frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\) into Eq. (E5.4.5) \(GP \$=\$_{ B } F_{ B }-\$_{ A } F_{ A 0}-\$_{ V } V\) , we obtain

\(G P \$=\$_{B} F_{B}-\$_{A} \frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}}-\$_{V} \frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\) (E5.4.8)

To find the optimum conversion, we maximize the gross profit. That is starting by setting

\(\frac{d G P \$}{d f_{A}}=0\) (E5.4.9)

Since the flow rate of product B is fixed, not a function of the conversion \(f_{ A }\) , we have,

\(0=\frac{d G P \$}{d f_{A}}=0+\$_{A} \frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}^{2}}-\$_{V} \frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)^{2}}\) (E5.4.10)

which leads to

\(\frac{f_{A}^{2}}{\left(1-f_{A}\right)^{2}}=\frac{\$_{A} k C_{A 0}}{\$_{V}}=\frac{2 \$ / mol \times 0.15 min ^{-1} \times 2 mol / L }{0.03 \$ /( l \cdot h )}=1200\) (E5.4.11)

Taking square root on both sides of Eq. (E5.4.11) \(\frac{f_{A}^{2}}{\left(1-f_{A}\right)^{2}}=\frac{\$_{A} k C_{A 0}}{\$_{V}}=\frac{2 \$ / mol \times 0.15 min ^{-1} \times 2 mol / L }{0.03 \$ /( l \cdot h )}=1200\) yields

\(\frac{f_{A}}{1-f_{A}}=\pm 20 \sqrt{3}\) (E5.4.12)

Thus,

\(f_{A}=\frac{\pm 20 \sqrt{3}}{1 \pm 20 \sqrt{3}}=20 \sqrt{3} \frac{20 \sqrt{3} \pm 1}{1199}\) (E5.4.13)

Since the conversion of A must be between 0 (no conversion) and 1 (100% conversion) in order for it to be physically possible, we discard the value that is greater than unit and obtain

\(f_{A}=20 \sqrt{3} \frac{20 \sqrt{3}-1}{1199}=0.97194\) (E5.4.14)

Next we check if indeed this conversion gives the maximum gross profit. We can either plot out the variation of GP$ versus \(f_{ A }\) to see if we indeed having a maximum GP$ at this \(f_{ A }\) value or checking the second derivative of GP$,

\(\frac{d^{2} G P \$}{d f_{A}^{2}}=-2 \$_{A} \frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}^{3}}-2 \$_{V} \frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)^{3}}\) (E5.4.15)

Clearly

\(\frac{d^{2} G P \$}{d f_{A}^{2}}<0\)

A negative second order derivative means that the value of GP$ is lower for either higher or lower \(f_{ A }\). Thus, we indeed have obtained a maximum value of the gross profit at the conversion of \(f_{ A }\) = 0.97194. This confirms that the optimum conversion is \(f_{ A }\) = 0.97194. The reactor size can be computed from Eq. (E5.4.4) \(V=\frac{F_{B}}{r_{B}}=\frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\)

 

\(V=\frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}=\frac{100}{0.15 \times 60 \times 2 \times(1-0.97194)} l =197.99 L\)

c. The cash flow based on the optimum conversion:

\(\frac{G P \$}{F_{A 0}}=\$_{B} \frac{F_{B}}{F_{A 0}}-\$_{A} \frac{F_{B}}{f_{A} F_{A 0}}-\$_{V} \frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right) F_{A 0}}=\$_{B} f_{A}-\$_{A}-\$_{V} \frac{f_{A}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\)

 

\(=5 \times 0.97194-2-\frac{0.03 \times 0.97194}{0.15 \times 60 \times 2 \times(1-0.97194)} \$ / mol – A\)

 

\(=\$ 2.802 / mol – A\)

d. At break-even point, the net profit (or in this case the gross profit) is zero. Therefore, at the break-even point, operating cost and the value of product are equal.

The value of product is,

\(\$_{B} F_{B}=5 \times 100 \$ / h =500 \$ / h\)

which is also the operating cost at break-even.

The operating conditions for the break-even point can be obtained by setting the gross profit to zero. This was not asked here.

\(0=G P \$=\$_{B} F_{B}-\$_{A} \frac{F_{B}}{f_{A}}-\$_{V} \frac{F_{B}}{k C_{A 0}\left(1-f_{A}\right)}\) (E5.4.16)

which can be rearranged to

\(f_{A}^{2}-\left\lgroup 1+\frac{\$_{A}}{\$_{B}}-\frac{\$_{V}}{k C_{A 0} \$_{B}} \right\rgroup f_{A}+\frac{\$_{A}}{\$_{B}}\) (E5.4.17)

or

\(f_{A}^{2}-\frac{4199}{3000} f_{A}+\frac{2}{5}\) (E5.4.18)

Solving Eq. (E5.4.18 ) \(f_{A}^{2}-\frac{4199}{3000} f_{A}+\frac{2}{5}\), we obtain

\(f_{A}=0.99944424 \text { or } 0.400222\)

This concludes the solution to Example 5.4.
