Guest Question.

Determine both \(i_{1} \text { and } i_{L}\) in the circuit shown in Fig. 8.20a for t > 0.

Admin
Solution

After t = 0, when the voltage source is disconnected as shown in Fig. 8.20b, we easily calculate an equivalent inductance,

 

\(L_{ eq }=\frac{2 \times 3}{2+3}+1=2.2\) mH

 

an equivalent resistance, in series with the equivalent inductance,

 

\(R_{ eq }=\frac{90(60+120)}{90+180}+50=110 \Omega\)

 

and the time constant,

 

\(\tau=\frac{L_{ eq }}{R_{ eq }}=\frac{2.2 \times 10^{-3}}{110}=20 \mu s\)

 

Thus, the form of the natural response is \(K e^{-50,000 t}\), where K is an unknown constant. Considering the circuit just prior to the switch opening \(\left(t=0^{-}\right), i_{L}=18 / 50 A . \text { Since } i_{L}\left(0^{+}\right)=i_{L}\left(0^{-}\right)\), we know that \(i_{L}=18 / 50 A \text { or } 360 mA \text { at } t=0^{+}\) and so

 

\(i_{L}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}360 mA & t<0 \\360 e^{-50,000 t} mA & t \geq 0\end{array}\right.\)

 

There is no restriction on \(i_{1}\) changing instantaneously at t = 0, so its value at \(t=0^{-}(18 / 90 A \text { or } 200 mA )\) is not relevant to finding \(i_{1}\) for t > 0. Instead, we must find \(i_{1}\left(0^{+}\right)\) through our knowledge of \(i_{L}\left(0^{+}\right)\). Using current division,

 

\(i_{1}\left(0^{+}\right)=-i_{L}\left(0^{+}\right) \frac{120+60}{120+60+90}=-240\) mA

 

Hence,

 

\(i_{1}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}200 mA & t<0 \\-240 e^{-50,000 t} mA & t \geq 0\end{array}\right.\)
