Guest Question.

Air at 1 atm and 300 K enters an exchanger like that shown in Figure with a velocity of 15 m/s. Calculate the heat-transfer coefficient.

Admin
Solution

We obtain the air properties from Table as

 

\(\rho= 1.1774 kg/m^{3} c_{p} = 1.0057 kJ/kg · ^{\circ}C\)

 

\(\mu = 1.983 × 10^{−5} kg/m ·s Pr = 0.708\)

 

From Figure we have

 

\(\sigma = \frac{A_{c}}{A} = 0.697 D_{h} = 0.0118 ft = 3.597 mm\)

 

The mass velocity is thus

 

\(G = \frac{\dot{m}}{A_{c}}=\frac{ \rho u_{∞}A}{A_{c}}=\frac{ (1.1774)(15)}{0.697} = 38.18 kg/m^{2} ·s\)

 

and the Reynolds number is

 

\( Re = \frac{D_{h}G}{\mu} = \frac{(3.597 × 10^{−3})(38.18)}{1.983 × 10^{−5}} = 6.926 × 10^{3} \)

 

From Figure we can read

 

\(St Pr^{2/3} = 0.0036 = \frac{h}{Gc_{p}}Pr^{2/3}\)

 

and the heat-transfer coefficient is

 

\(h = (0.0036)(38.18)(1005.7)(0.708)^{−2/3}\) \(= 174 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C [30.64 Btu/h · ft^{2} · ^{\circ}F]\)

 

The values of \(\mu, k, c_{p}\), and Pr are not strongly pressure-dependent and may be used over a fairly wide range of pressures
