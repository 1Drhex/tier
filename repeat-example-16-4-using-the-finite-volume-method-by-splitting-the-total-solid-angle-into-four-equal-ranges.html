Guest Question.

Repeat using the finite volume method, by splitting the total solid angle into four equal ranges.

Admin
Solution

As in we will put the z-axis perpendicular to the paper in Fig. 16-4, from which the polar angle 6 is measured. Because of the axial symmetry, it is best to assign each H, the entire range of polar angles, and a quarter of the azimuthal range. Thus, breaking up by quadrant we choose

\(\Omega _{1} : 0\leq \psi \lt \frac{\pi }{2} , 0\leq \theta \leq \pi ,\)
\(\Omega _{2}: \frac{\pi }{2} \leq \psi \lt \pi , 0\leq \theta \leq \pi ,\)
\(\Omega _{3} : \pi \leq \psi \lt \frac{3\pi }{2} , 0\leq \theta \leq \pi ,\)
\(\Omega _{4} : \frac{3\pi }{2} \leq \psi \lt 2\pi , 0\leq \theta \leq \pi\).

The solid angle vectors \(s_{i}\), are obtained with \(\widehat{s}=\sin \theta \cos \psi \widehat{i}+\sin \theta \sin \psi \widehat{J} +\cos \theta \widehat{k}\) as

\(s_{i}=\int_{\triangle \psi _{i}}^{}{}\int_{0}^{\pi }{}\widehat{s}\sin \theta d\theta d\psi = \int_{\triangle \psi _{i}}^{}{}\left[\frac{\pi }{2}\cos \psi \widehat{i} + \frac{\pi }{2}\sin \psi \widehat{J}+0\widehat{k} \right] d\psi\)
\(=\left(\frac{\pi }{2}\sin \psi \widehat{i} -\frac{\pi }{2}\cos \psi \widehat{J}\right) |_{\triangle \psi _{i}},\)

or

\(s_{1}=\frac{\pi }{2}(\widehat{i}+\widehat{J} ) , s_{2}=-\frac{\pi }{2}(\widehat{i}-\widehat{J} ), s_{3}=-\frac{\pi }{2}(\widehat{i}+\widehat{J} ) s_{4}=\frac{\pi }{2}(\widehat{i}-\widehat{J} ),\)

which are identical to the directions in Example 16.4, except for the factor \(\pi\) (which gives the total solid angle of the \(\Omega _{i}\)), and the fact that the \(\widehat{s}_{i }\) in were projections in thex-y-plane, while the \({s}_{i }\) in this example actually lie in the x-y-planc. Now, with \(A_{k}=\frac{1}{2}L,\beta V=\kappa (\frac{1}{2}L)^{2}=\frac{1}{4}L and \Omega _{i} =\pi\), we obtain from equation (16.60)

\(\Omega _{i}(i=1): I_{p1}=\frac{\frac{1}{4} L\pi S_{p1}+I_{W1}\left|s_{1}\cdot (-\widehat{i} )\right|\frac{1}{2}L + I_{s1}\left|s_{1}\cdot (-\widehat{J} )\right| \frac{1}{2}L }{\frac{1}{4}L\pi + s_{1}\cdot \widehat{i} \frac{1}{2}L + s_{1}\cdot \widehat{J}\frac{1}{2}L } , p=1,2,3,4,\)

and similarly for the other three directions. Evaluating the dot products and simplifying this may be written in general form for all nodes and ail directions as

\(I_{pi}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p}+ I_{px_{i},i}+ I_{py_{i},i} ), p,i=1,2,3,4\)

where \(I_{px_{i},i}\) is the intensity entering volume p across the x = const face in the \(s_{i}\) direction, and similarly for\(I_{py_{i},i}\). Thus

\(I_{p1,1}= \frac{1}{3}(S_{p1}+ I_{W1,1}+I_{S1,1})=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p1}+0+I_{bw}),\)
\(I_{p2,1}= \frac{1}{3}(S_{p2}+ I_{W2,1}+I_{S2,1})=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p2}+I_{p1,1}+I_{bw}),\)
…,

which is exactly the same as the \(S_{2}\)-approximation together with the step scheme. This is to be expected since (i) the finite volume method—as applied here—^uses the step scheme, and (ii) the \(S_{2}\)-approximation satisfies all half-moments for this very simple case. The strength of the finite volume method lies in the fact that it is easily applied to irregular geometries, and that it conserves radiative energy.
