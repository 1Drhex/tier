Guest Question.

To illustrate the importance of selection of the correct area(s) to use in design calculations, consider a small double-pipe heat exchanger like that shown in the photographs of Figure 10-3e and f . The cross section through the double-pipe arrangement is shown in Figure Example 12-5, with the following dimensions given:

\(d_{1i} = 3.0 mm\)
\(d_{1o} = 3.2 mm\)
\(d_{2} = 6.0 mm\)

Length of tube section perpendicular to page = L = 1.5 m

The hot fluid is water that flows inside the inner tube. The cold fluid is ethylene glycol,
which flows in the annular region between the two tubes. The mean flow velocity for the water is 2.3 m/s and the mean velocity for the glycol is 2.5 m/s. The mean temperature for the water is \(38^{\circ}C\), while the mean temperature of the glycol is \(20^{\circ}C\). Determine the overall heat-transfer coefficient of the exchanger, assuming the tubes are constructed of stainless steel with \(k = 16 W/m · ^{\circ}C\).

Admin
Solution

There are several areas that need to be calculated:

Flow area for water = \(\frac{πd^{2}_{1i}}{4} = \frac{π(0.003)^{2}}{4} = 7.07 × 10^{−6} m^{2}\)

Flow area for glycol =\(π \frac{\left(d^{2}_{2} − d^{2}_{1o}\right)}{4} = 2.023 × 10^{−5} m^{2}\)

Heat-transfer surface area for water =\( πd_{1i}L = 0.01414 m^{2}\)

Heat-transfer surface area for glycol =\( πd_{1o}L = 0.01508 m^{2}\)

Note that only the outside surface of the inner tube transfers heat to the glycol. The overall annular dimensions, however, influence the hydraulic diameter, which must be used to calculate the Reynolds number, and subsequently the heat-transfer coefficient for the glycol.

The hydraulic diameter for the glycol flowing in the annular section is calculated using Equation (6-14):

\(D_{H} = \frac{4A_{c}}{P} = d_{2} − d_{1o} = 0.0028 m\)

The fluid properties are obtained from Appendix A, giving
Water at \(38^{\circ}C\):

\(c_{p} = 4174 J/kg ·^{\circ}C\)
\(\rho = 993 kg/m^{3}\)

 

\(\mu = 6.82 × 10^{−4} kg/m ·s\)
\(k = 0.63 W/m · ^{\circ}C\)
Pr = 4.53

Glycol at \(20^{\circ}C\):

\(c_{p} = 2382 J/kg · ^{\circ}C\)
\(\rho = 1117 kg/m^{3}\)
\(\mu = 0.0214 kg/m ·s\)
\(k = 0.249 W/m · ^{\circ}C\)
Pr = 204

The Reynolds numbers are calculated as

Water Re = \(\frac{\rho d_{1i}u}{\mu} = \frac{(993)(0.003)(2.3)}{6.82 × 10^{−4 }}= 10,046\)

Glycol Re = \(\frac{\rho D_{H} u}{\mu} = \frac{(1117)(0.0028)(2.5)}{0.0214} = 365\)

We thus have turbulent flow for the water flowing in the inner tube and laminar flow for the glycol flowing in the outer annulus. In the absence of more specific information regarding the inlet temperatures, we may use Figures 12-4 and 12-5 to estimate the convection heat-transfer coefficients. From Figure 12-4 at Re = 10,000, we have

\(\frac{Nu}{Pr^{0.4}} = 36\)

so that

\(h_{i} =\frac{ (36)(4.53)^{0.4}(0.63)}{0.003} = 13,800 W/m^{2} ·^{\circ} C\)

For laminar flow of the glycol, we need to calculate the Graetz number:

\(Gz = RePr \left(\frac{d}{ L} \right)= (365)(204) \left(\frac{0.0028}{ 1.5} \right)= 139\)

and from Figure 12-5 we read the value of the Nusselt number as

Nu = 9.5

so that the value of the convection coefficient is calculated as

\(h_{o} = \frac{(9.5)(0.249)}{0.0028} = 845 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C\)

The respective thermal resistances are now determined as

Water side resistance =\( \frac{1}{h_{i}A_{i}}=\frac{ 1}{(13,800)(0.01414)}= 0.0051\)

Glycol side resistance =\( \frac{1}{h_{o}A_{o}}=\frac{ 1}{(845)(0.01508)}= 0.078\)

Conduction resistance = \(\frac{ln(d_{1o}/d_{1i})}{2πkL }=\frac{ln(3.2/3.0)}{2π(16)(1.5)}= 0.00043\)

The product of overall heat transfer coefficient and area is then

\(UA = \frac{1}{(0.0051 + 0.078 + 0.00043)}= 11.9 W/^{\circ}C\)

If the value of U is based on the inner (water side) tube area,
\(U_{i} = 845 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C\)
and if it is based on the outer (glycol side) tube area
\(U_{o} = 767 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C\)

The glycol side value of h is clearly the controlling coefficient, and the conduction resistance is negligible within the accuracy of the estimates obtained from the respective figures.
