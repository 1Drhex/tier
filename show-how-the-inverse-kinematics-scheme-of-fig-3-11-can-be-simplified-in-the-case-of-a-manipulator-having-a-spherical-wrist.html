Guest Question.

Show how the inverse kinematics scheme of Fig. 3.11 can be simplified in the case of a manipulator having a spherical wrist.

Admin
Solution

With reference to a 6-DOFs manipulator having a spherical wrist, let \( q_{p} \) = \(\left[ q_{1} q_{2} q_{3} \right]^{T}\) denote the vector of joint variables determining arm position and \( q_{O} \) = \(\left[ \vartheta_{4} \vartheta_{5} \vartheta_{6} \right]^{T}\) the vector of joint variables determining wrist orientation. For such manipulator, it is possible to solve inverse kinematics in two stages; namely, first solve for \( q_{P} \) and then solve for \(q_{O} \) Let \( p_{d} \) (t) and \(R_{d} \left(t\right) \) = \( \left[n_{d} \left(t\right) n_{d} \left(t\right) a_{d} \left(t\right) \right] \) be the desired time history of end-effector position and orientation. From (2.93), it is possible to compute the desired time behaviour of wrist position as

\( p_{W}\) = \( p_{e} – d_{6} a_{e}\) (2.93)

\( p_{Wd}\) = \( p_{d} – d_{6} a_{d}\)

Taking time derivative of both sides gives

\(\dot{p}_{Wd} \) = \(\dot{p}_{d} – d_{6} \omega _{d} \times a_{d}\)

where the time derivative of the unit vector \( a_{d}\) (constant norm) has been expressed as \(\dot{a}_{d} \) = \(\omega _{d} \times a_{d}\) By using the block-partitioned form of the Jacobian in (3.42) with p = \( p_{W}\), the differential kinematics equation for arm linear velocity is

\(\dot{p}_{W} \) = \( J_{PP} \left(q_{P}\right) \dot{q}_{P}\)

where \( J_{PP}\) formally coincides with \( J_{11} \) evaluated for p = \( p_{W} \). In view of this, the joint velocity solution of kind (3.70) can be written as

\(\dot{q}_{P} \) = \(J^{-1}_{PP} \left(q_{P}\right) \left(\dot{p}_{Wd} + k_{P} e_{PW} \right) \)

where \( e_{PW} \) = \(P_{Wd} − P_{W} \) and \(K_{p}\) is a positive definite matrix. The resulting block scheme is shown in Fig. S3.1.

Once the time behaviour of \( q_{P} \) and \(\dot{q}_{P} \) has been computed in the first stage, the differential kinematics equation for wrist angular velocity is

\(\omega \) = \( J_{OP} \left(q_{P}\right) \dot{q}_{p} + J_{OO} \left(q_{P}, q_{O}\right) \dot{q}_{O}\)

where \( J_{OP} \) and \(J_{OO}\) respectively denote the Jacobians \(J_{21}\) and \(J_{22}\) in (3.42) evaluated for p = \(p_{W}\). In view of this, the joint velocity solution of kind (3.70) can be written as

\( \dot{q}_{O} \) = \(J^{-1}_{OO} \left(q_{P},q_{O}\right) \left(\omega _{d} – J_{OP} \left(q_{P}\right) \dot{q}_{P} + K_{O} e_{O} \right) \)

where \(e_{O}\) is the orientation error in (3.85) and \(K_{O} \) is a positive definite matrix. The resulting block scheme for the second stage is shown in Fig. S3.2.

It is anticipated that the two-stage algorithm leads to a computational savings with respect to the algorithm based on the inverse of the full Jacobian.

\( J \) = \(\left[\begin{matrix} J_{11} & J_{12} \\ J_{21} & J_{22} \end{matrix} \right] \) (3.42)

\(\dot{q} \) = \(J^{-1}_{A}\nabla a \left(q\right) \left(\dot{x}_{d} + K_{e} \right)\) (3.70)

\( e_{O} \) = \(\frac{1}{2} \left( n_{e} \left(q\right) \times n_{d} + s_{e} \left(q\right) \times s_{d} + a_{e}\left(q\right) \times a_{d} \right) \) (3.85)
