Guest Question.

Consider an isothermal slab at temperature T = 1000 K, and a total pressure of p = I atm. The slab consists of a mixture of 70% \(N_{2}\), 20% \(H_{2}O\) and 10% \(CO_{2}\) (by volume), and is bordered by cold, black walls. Determine the heat loss from this slab as a function of slab thickness L, using the WSGG method with Truelove’s parameters. In addition, determine the heat lost from the layer if the gas is mixed with soot (\(\rho _{p}=2,000 kg/m^{3}, f_{0}=5\times 10^{-6}\)).

Admin
Solution

If no soot is present, the summation over / in equation (19.104) \(\epsilon (T,s)=\alpha (T,s)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{K}{}\sum\limits_{l=1}^{L}{}\alpha _{kl}(T)\left(1-e^{-[\kappa _{gk}(\rho _{H_{2}O}+\rho _{CO_{2}})+\kappa _{pl}\rho _{p}.f_{0}]s}\right)\) can be carried out immediately, and with \(a _{0}=(0.588-0.2401)+(-0.165+0.2834)=0.466\) , etc., and also using \(\kappa _{k}=\kappa _{g,k}(0.2+0.1)\) atm, we obtain

\(a _{0}=0.466 , a _{1}=0.337 , a _{2}=0.159, a _{3}=0.038;\)
\kappa _{0}=0, \kappa _{1}=0.267 m^{-1} , \kappa _{2}=4.65 m^{-1}, \kappa _{3}= 71.7 m^{-1}.[/latex]

If we use the exact solution to the (spectral) equation of transfer, the answer was found in Example (13.2) for a gray medium as

\(q=\sigma T^{4}[1-2E_{3}(\kappa L)]\) (19.105)

(where we have set \(T_{w}=0 , \epsilon _{w}= 1 , and \tau _{L}=\kappa L\) ). Therefore, if we use the WSGG method, this leads to (in nondimensional form)

\(\Psi =\frac{q}{\sigma T^{4}}=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{3}{}\alpha _{\kappa } [1-2E_{3}(\kappa _{k}L)]\) ( 19.106)

A plot of this result is given in Fig. 19-9, together with line-by-line (LBL) results obtained from the HITEMP database [3]. The WSGG method is seen to give results of very respectable accuracy, except for extremely long path lengths, for which the method overpredicts the gas emissivity somewhat.

If soot is added to the gas mixture, the number of terms is doubled. With \(\rho _{p}f_{0}=10^{-2} kg/m^{3}\) , we obtain

\(a _{01}=0.348 , a _{02}=0.118; a _{11}= 0.246, a _{12}=0.091;\)
\(a _{21}=0.143 , a _{22}=0.016; a _{31}=0.034, a _{32}=0.004.\)

Similarly, with \(\kappa _{p1}\rho _{p}f_{0}= 541\times 10^{-2}=5.41 m^{-1}\) and \(\kappa _{p2}\rho _{p}f_{0}=27.49 m^{-1}\), the corresponding gray-gas absorption coefficients become

\(\kappa _{01}=5.41 m^{-1}, \kappa _{02}=27.49 m^{-1} ; \kappa _{11}=5.68 m^{-1}, \kappa _{12}= 27.76 m^{-1};\)

\(\kappa _{21}=10.05 m^{-1}, \kappa _{22}=32.14 m^{-1} ; \kappa _{31}=77.11 m^{-1}, \kappa _{32}= 99.19 m^{-1};\)

and the nondimensional heat flux is evaluated from

\(\Psi =\frac{q}{\sigma T^{4}}=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{3}{} \sum\limits_{l=1}^{2}{} a _{kl} [1-2E_{3}(\kappa _{kl}L)]\) (19.107)

which is also plotted in Fig. 19-9 together with LBL results for a soot absorption coefficient that varies linearly with wavenumber, and that has \(C_{0}\) = 5.71 [obtained from equation (11.124), using the Chang and Charalampopoulos refi-active index of m = 1.89 – 0.69i of Example 11.4]. For such soot the WSGG model overpredicts emission by about 20% for small slab thicknesses, and the error gradually diminishes with increasing L . Choosing \(C_{0}\) = 7, or m = 1.38 – 0.69i, would result in almost perfect agreement.

Also, equation (19.104) \(\epsilon (T,s)=a (T,s)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{K}{}\sum\limits_{l=1}^{L}{}\alpha _{kl}(T)\left(1-e^{-[\kappa _{gk}(\rho _{H_{2}O}+\rho _{CO_{2}})+\kappa_{pl}\rho _{p}.f_{0} ]s}\right)\) requires the density of the soot, while equation (11.124) does not. There is additional uncertainty attached to the soot’s density, which was here arbitrarily set to \(\rho _{p}= 2,000 kg/m^{3}\) (approximating the density of pyrolytic graphite) and, finally, there are great variations possible in the index of refraction, as shown in Fig. 11-25. Therefore, considering the substantial uncertainties surrounding soot generation and properties, the agreement may be considered good; differences can be attributed to the soot model rather than the WSGG approximation.
