Guest Question.

A photon of radiation of wavelength \(305\, nm\) ejects an electron with a kinetic energy of \(1.77\, eV\) from a metal. Calculate the longest wavelength of radiation capable of ejecting an electron from the metal.

 

Admin
Solution

Collect your thoughts You can use eqn 7A.10,

\(hν = E_k + Φ\) or \(E_k = hν − Φ\) Photoelectric effect (7A.10)

rearranged into \(Φ = hν − E_k\), to compute the work function because you know the frequency of the photon from \(ν = c/λ\). The threshold for photoejection is the lowest frequency at which electron ejection occurs without there being any excess energy; that is, the kinetic energy of the ejected electron is zero. Setting \(E_k = 0\) in \(E_k = hν − Φ\) gives the minimum photon frequency as \(ν_{min} = Φ/h\). Use this value of the frequency to calculate the corresponding wavelength, \(λ_{max}\).

The minimum frequency for photoejection is

\(ν_{min}=\frac{\Phi}{h}=\frac{hν-E_k}{h}\overset{\overset{ν=c/\lambda}{\downarrow } }{=} \frac{c}{\lambda}-\frac{E_k}{h}\)

The longest wavelength that can cause photoejection is therefore

\(\lambda_{max}=\frac{c}{ν_{min}}=\frac{c}{c/\lambda -E_k/h}=\frac{1}{1/\lambda -E_k/hc}\)

Now substitute the data. The kinetic energy of the electron is

\(E_k=1.77\, eV×(1.602×10^{-19}\, JeV^{-1})=2.83…×10^{-19}J\)
so

\(\frac{E_k}{hc}=\frac{2.83…×10^{-19}J}{(6.626\times 10^{-34}\, Js)\times (2.998\times 10^8 ms^{-1})}=1.42…\times 10^6\, m^{-1}\)

Therefore, with \(1/\lambda=1/305\, nm=3.27…×10^ 6\,m^{−1}\),

\(\lambda_{max}=\frac{1}{(3.27…×10^6\, m^{−1})-(1.42…\times 10^6\, m^{-1})}=5.40\times 10^{-7}\, m\) or \(540 nm\).
