Guest Question.

The catalytic decomposition of reactant \((\mathrm{A} \rightarrow \mathrm{R})\) is studied in a packed bed reactor filled with \(2.4-\mathrm{mm}\) pellets and using a very high recycle rate of product gases (assume mixed flow). The results of a long-time run and additional data are given below.

t, hr 0 2 4 6
X_A 0.75 0.64 0.52 0.39

\(\mathscr{D}_{e}=5 \times 10^{-10} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{m}\) cat \(\cdot \mathrm{s}\)

 

\(\rho_{s}=1500 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3} \mathrm{cat}\)

 

\(\tau^{\prime}=4000 \mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{s} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\)

Find the kinetics of reaction and deactivation, both in the diffusion-free and in the strong pore diffusion resistance regime.

Admin
Solution

First of all, note that deactivation occurs during the run, so guess or try to fit the data with the simplest rate form for such situations, or

\(\left.\begin{array}{l}-r_{\mathrm{A}}^{\prime}=k^{\prime} C_{\mathrm{A}} \mathbf{a} \\-\frac{d \mathbf{a}}{d t}=k_{d} \mathbf{a}\end{array}\right\}\)

The performance equations for this rate form and mixed flow are analogous to those for plug flow, shown in Table 21.1 ,

\(\begin{array}{c}\text { In diffusion-free } \\ \text { regime }\end{array} \quad \cdots\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}}-1\right)=k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime} \mathbf{a}=k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime} e^{-k_{d} t}\) …(i)

In strong diffusion regime…\(\left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}}-1\right)=k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime} \mathbf{a} \mathscr{E}=\frac{k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}}{M_{T d}} \mathbf{a}=\frac{k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}}{M_{T}}: \underline{e}-k_{d} t / 2\) …(ii)

If this rate form is correct then for both strong or no diffusion resistance effects a plot of \(\ln \left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}}-1\right)\) vs \(\mathrm{t}\) should give a straight line. Making this tabulation and plot, as shown in Fig. E21.1 shows that the above rate form fits the data.

Let us next see whether the reactor is operating in the diffusion-free or strong diffusion regime.

Guess No Intrusion of Diffusional Resistances. Then from Eq. (i) we have

\(\ln \left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}}-1\right)=\ln \left(k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}\right)-k_{d} t^{t}\) From Fig. E21.1

\(\left.\begin{array}{l}\text { the intercept }=1.1 \\\text { the slope }=-0.26\end{array}\right\} \text { thus }\left\{\begin{array}{l}k^{\prime}=7.5 \times 10^{-4} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{s}} \\k_{d}=0.26 \mathrm{hr}^{-1}\end{array}\right.\)

Now check the Thiele modulus to verify that we really are in the diffusion resistance free regime. Thus at \(t=0\)

\(M_{T}=L \sqrt{\frac{k^{\prime \prime \prime}}{\mathscr{D}_{e}}}=\frac{2.4 \times 10^{-3}}{6} \sqrt{\frac{\left(7.5 \times 10^{-4}\right)(1500)}{5 \times 10^{-10}}}=18.9 ! ! !\)

Unfortunately, this value indicates strong resistance to pore diffusion. This contradicts our starting assumption, so our original guess was wrong.

Guess That the Runs Were Made in the Regime of Strong Resistance to Pore Diffusion. Then Eq. (ii) becomes

\(\ln \left(\frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}}-1\right)=\ln \left(\frac{k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}}{M_{T}}\right)-\frac{k_{d}}{2} t\)

From Fig. E21.1 we have

\(\left.\begin{array}{l}\text { the intercept }=1.1 \\\text { the slope }=-0.26\end{array}\right\} \text { thus }\left\{\begin{array}{l}\ln \left(\frac{k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}}{M_{T}}\right)=1.1 \\k_{d}=0.52 \mathrm{hr}^{-1}\end{array}\right.\)

From the value for the intercept

\(\frac{k^{\prime} \tau^{\prime}}{M_{T}}=3.0\)

We find

\(k^{\prime}=9 \frac{L^{2} \rho_{s}}{\left(\tau^{\prime}\right)^{2} \mathscr{D}_{e}}=(9) \cdot \frac{\left(2.4 \times 10^{-3} / 6\right)^{2}(1500)}{(4000)^{2}\left(5 \times 10^{-10}\right)}=0.27 \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{s}}\)

Check the Thiele modulus at \(\mathrm{t}=0\)

\(M_{T}=L \sqrt{\frac{k^{\prime} \rho_{s}}{\mathscr{D}_{e}}}=\frac{2.4 \times 10^{-3}}{6} \sqrt{\frac{(0.27)(1500)}{5 \times 10^{-10}}}=360\)

This value of Thiele modulus represents strong pore diffusional resistance. This is consistent with our original guess. Hence the final rate equations:

(a) In the diffusion-free regime (for very small \(d_{p}\) ) with deactivation

\(-r_{\mathrm{A}}^{\prime}=0.27 C_{\mathrm{A}} \mathbf{a}, \frac{\mathrm{mol}}{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{s}} \cdot \cdots \text { with } \cdot \cdots-\frac{d \mathbf{a}}{d t}=0.52 \mathbf{a}, \mathrm{hr}^{-1}\)

(b) In the strong pore diffusion resistance regime (for large \(d_{p}\) ) with deactivation

– \(-r_{\mathrm{A}}^{\prime}=0.27 C_{\mathrm{A}} \mathbf{a} \mathscr{E}\)

with \(\ldots\) \(-\frac{d \mathbf{a}}{d t}=0.52 \mathbf{a}\) }

\(\mathscr{E}=\frac{1}{M_{T D}}=\frac{1}{L} \sqrt{\frac{5 \times 10^{-10}}{0.27(1500) \mathbf{a}}}=\frac{1.11 \times 10^{-6}}{L \mathbf{a}^{1 / 2}}\)

combining all gives

\(-r_{\mathrm{A}}^{\prime}=\frac{3 \times 10^{-7}}{L} C_{\mathrm{A}} \mathbf{a}^{1 / 2}, \quad \frac{\mathrm{mol}}{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{s}} \cdot \cdots \text { with } \cdot \cdots-\frac{d \mathbf{a}}{d t}=0.52 \mathbf{a}, \mathrm{hr}^{-1}\)

Note: In the strong pore diffusion regime the rate is lower but the catalyst deactivates more slowly. Actually, for the catalyst used here if we could have been free of diffusional resistances reaction rates would have been 360 times as fast as those measured.
