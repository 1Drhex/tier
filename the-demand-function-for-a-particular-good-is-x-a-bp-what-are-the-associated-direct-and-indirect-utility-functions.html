Guest Question.

The demand function for a particular good is X = a + bp. What are the associated direct and indirect utility functions?

Admin
Solution

Note that p is the relative price of good x with respect to the other good which we’ll call z. Also, let m be income measured in units of z. Thus the consumer’s budget constraint is px + z = m. How do you know that there must be another good around?

From

\( \frac{dμ(p; q,m)}{dp} = a + bp \) .

we find

\( μ(p; q,m) = a p + \frac{bp^2}{2} +C \) .

Here C is a constant of integration. Since μ(q; q,m) =m, note that

\( C =m−a q − \frac{bq^2}{2} \).

therefore,

\( μ(p; q,m) = a p +\frac{bp^2}{2} + m− aq − \frac{bq^2}{2}\).

A money metric utility function behaves like an indirect utility function with respect to q and m when holding p fixed. Therefore, an indirect utility function consistent with the demand function given above is

\( υ (q,m) =m−aq −\frac{bq^2}{2}\).

We can drop the terms in p since they are just constants. (Use Roy’s identity to check that this indirect utility is indeed consistent with our original demand function.)

To get the direct utility function we must solve

\( u(x, z) = \min _q \left\{\upsilon (q,m) :qx+z =m\right\} = \min _q \left\{m-aq – \frac{bq^2}{2} :qx+z = m\right\} \).

use the budget constraint to eliminate m from the objective function and get the optimal value of q

\( q^* = \frac{x-a}{b} \).

Thus

\( u(x, z) = z + \frac{(x-a)^2}{26} \).

This is, of course, a quasilinear utility function.

On the back of an envelope, solve \( max_{x,z} =\left\{u(x,z) :px+z = m\right\} \) and check that you get precisely the original demand function for x. What’s the demand for z?
