Guest Question.

Consider a homogeneous cork floating in a container fully filled with liquid. The cork is tied to the bottom of the container by a rope (Fig. 10.4). The container moves with respect to the ground at constant acceleration α. The liquid and the floater are assumed to be at rest with respect to the container. The mass density \(\acute{m}\) of the cork is smaller than the mass density m of the water, i.e. \(\acute{m} < m\).

a) Determine the expression of Archimedes’ force \(F_A\), which is the net force exerted by the fluid on the surface S of the cork,

\(F_A =- \int_{S}{p d S} \).

b) Determine the angle α between the rope and the vertical axis when the cork is at rest with respect to the container.

Admin
Solution

a) In the absence of viscous friction, the only external force density exerted on a small
volume of liquid is its weight density, i.e.

\(f^{ext} = mg\).

Since there is no shear, no vorticity and no friction, i.e. \(τ^{fr} = 0\), according to equation (10.81) the divergence of the stress tensor reduces to the opposite of the pressure gradient,

\(\tau = \Bigl(\tau ^{fr}-p\Bigr) 1\).

∇· τ = −∇ p

Thus, Newton’s 2nd law (10.35) can be recast as,

\(m \dot{\nu } =f^{ext} +\nabla .\tau\).

−∇ p = −m g + m α

The integral over the volume of the previous expression can be written as,

\(– \int_{V}{\nabla p dV } = -m (g-\alpha ) \int_{V}{dV} =-mV(g-\alpha )\).

Archimedes’ force \(F_A\) exerted by the fluid on the surface S of the cork is obtained by applying the divergence theorem,

\(F_A=- \int_{S}pdS=- \int_{V}{\nabla p dV } =−m V(g-a)\)

In the particular case where there is no acceleration, i.e. α = 0, we recover Archimedes’ principle,

\(F_A = −m V g\) (Archimedes’ principle)

b) The cork of mass \(\acute{M}\) is subjected to three external forces, its weight \(\acute{M} g\), Archimedes’ force \(F_A\) and the tension T in the rope. Thus, Newton’s 2nd applied on the cork reads,

\(\acute{M} g + F_A+ T = \acute{M} α\).

The mass of the homogeneous cork can be written as \(\acute{M} = \acute{m} V \) where \(\acute{m}\) is its mass density. Thus, the tension is given by,

\(T = −\acute{m} V (g − α) − F_A = (m − \acute{m}) V (g − α)\).

These vectorial quantities are expressed as,

\(T = T_x \hat{x} +T_y \hat{y}\). \(α = α \hat{x}\) \(g = – g \hat{y}\).

where \(\hat{x}\) is the unit vector along the horizontal axis defined positively towards the right (Fig. 10.4) and \(\hat{y}\) is the unit vector along the vertical axis defined positively upwards.
The angle α is the angle between the rope and the vertical axis, and the tension is collinear to the rope. Thus,

\(\tan \alpha = \frac{T_x}{T_y} =\frac{\alpha }{g}\) thus \(\alpha = \arctan \Bigl(\frac{\alpha }{g}\Bigr)\).

which means that the cork leans forwards if α > 0 and backwards if α < 0.
