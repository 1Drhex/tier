Guest Question.

Determine the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors of \(\mathrm{A}=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3\end{array}\right)\). Also sketch the effect of multiplying the eigenvectors by matrix \(\mathbf{A}\).

Admin
Solution

What do we find first, the eigenvalues or eigenvectors?

Eigenvalues, because they produce eigenvectors.

We carry out the above procedure:

Step 1 .

We need to find the values of \(\lambda\) which satisfy \(\operatorname{det}(A-\lambda I)=0\). First we obtain \(\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I}\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I} &=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 0 \\1 & 3\end{array}\right)-\lambda\left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\0 & 1\end{array}\right) \\&=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 0 \\1 & 3\end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{ll}\lambda & 0 \\0 & \lambda\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{cc}2-\lambda & 0 \\1 & 3-\lambda\end{array}\right)\end{aligned}\)

Substituting this into \(\operatorname{det}(\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I})\) gives

\(\operatorname{det}(\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I})=\operatorname{det}\left(\begin{array}{cc}2-\lambda & 0 \\1 & 3-\lambda\end{array}\right)\)

To find the determinant, we use formula (6.1), \(\operatorname{det}\left(\begin{array}{cc}a & b \\ c & d\end{array}\right)=a d-b c\), thus:

\(\operatorname{det}(\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I})=\operatorname{det}\left(\begin{array}{cc} 2-\lambda & 0 \\1 & 3-\lambda\end{array}\right)=(2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-0\)

For eigenvalues we equate this determinant to zero:

\((2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)=0 \quad \text { implies } \lambda_{1}=2 \text { or } \lambda_{2}=3\)

Step 2 .

For each eigenvalue, \(\lambda\) determine the corresponding eigenvector \(\mathbf{u}\) by solving the system \((\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I}) \mathrm{u}=\mathrm{O}\). Let \(\mathbf{u}\) be the eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_{1}=2\). Substituting \(\mathrm{A}=\left(\begin{array}{ll}2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3\end{array}\right)\) and \(\lambda_{1}=\lambda=2\) into \((\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I}) \mathbf{u}=\mathrm{O}\) gives

\( \begin{aligned} (\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I}) \mathbf{u}=\left[\left(\begin{array}{ll} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{ll} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{array}\right)\right] \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{O} \\ \left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array}\right) \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{O} \end{aligned} \)

Remember, \(\mathbf{O}=\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\) and let \(\mathbf{u}=\left(\begin{array}{l}x \\ y\end{array}\right)\), so we have

\( \left(\begin{array}{ll} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right) \)

Multiplying out gives

\( \begin{aligned} &0+0=0 \\ &x+y=0 \end{aligned} \)

Remember, the eigenvector cannot be the zero vector, therefore at least one of the values, \(x\) or \(y\), must be non-zero. From the bottom equation we have \(x=-y\).

 

The simplest solution is \(x=1, y=-1\) but we could have \(\left(\begin{array}{r}2 \\ -2\end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{r}-3 \\ 3\end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{r}\pi \\ -\pi\end{array}\right),\left(\begin{array}{r}5 \\ -5\end{array}\right), \ldots\) Hence we have an infinite number of eigenvectors belonging to \(\lambda=2\). We can write down the general eigenvector \(\mathbf{u}\).

How?

Let \(x=s\) then \(y=-s\) where \(s \neq 0\) and is a real number. Thus the eigenvectors belonging to \(\lambda=2\) are

\( \mathbf{u}=\left(\begin{array}{r} s \\ -s \end{array}\right)=s\left(\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -1 \end{array}\right) \text { where } s \neq 0 \quad\left[\left(\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -1 \end{array}\right) \text { is one of the simplest eigenvectors }\right] \)

Similarly, we find the general eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) belonging to the other eigenvalue \(\lambda_{2}=3\). Putting \(\lambda_{2}=\lambda=3\) into \([\mathrm{A}-\lambda \mathrm{I}] \mathrm{v}=\mathrm{O}\) gives

\( (\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I}) \mathbf{v}=\left[\left(\begin{array}{ll} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{ll} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{array}\right)\right] \mathbf{v}=\mathbf{O} \text { simplifies to }\left(\begin{array}{rr} -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array}\right) \mathbf{v}=\mathbf{O} \)

By writing \(\mathbf{v}=\left(\begin{array}{l}x \\ y\end{array}\right)\) [different \(x\) and \(y\) from those above] and \(\mathbf{O}=\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\) we obtain

\( \left(\begin{array}{rr} -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right) \)

Multiplying out:

\(-x+0=0, \quad x+0=0\)

From these equations we must have \(x=0\).

What is \(y\) equal to?

We can choose \(y\) to be any real number apart from zero because the eigenvector cannot be zero. Thus

\(y=s \text { where } s \neq 0\)

The general eigenvector belonging to \(\lambda_{2}=3\) is

\(\mathbf{v}=\left(\begin{array}{l}x \\ y\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ s\end{array}\right)=s\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) where \(s \neq 0 \quad\left[\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\right.\) is one of the simplest eigenvectors \(]\)

Summarizing the above we have:

Eigenvector \(\mathbf{u}=s\left(\begin{array}{r}1 \\ -1\end{array}\right)\) belonging to \(\lambda_{1}=2\) and eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}=s\left(\begin{array}{l}0 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) belonging to \(\lambda_{2}=3\) What does all this mean?

The given matrix A scalar multiplies the eigenvector \(\mathbf{u}\) by 2 and \(\mathbf{v}\) by 3 because

\(\mathrm{Au}=2 \mathbf{u} \text { and } \mathrm{Av}=3 \mathbf{v}\)

Plotting these eigenvectors, the effect of multiplying by the matrix A is shown in Fig. 7.3.

Matrix A doubles \(\left(\lambda_{1}=2\right)\) the eigenvector \(\mathbf{u}\) and triples \(\left(\lambda_{2}=3\right)\) the eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) as you can see in Fig. 7.3. Matrix A does not change the direction of the eigenvectors.
