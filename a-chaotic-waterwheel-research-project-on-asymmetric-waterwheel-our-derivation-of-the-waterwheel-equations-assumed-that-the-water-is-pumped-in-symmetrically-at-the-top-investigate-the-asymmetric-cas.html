Guest Question.

A Chaotic Waterwheel

(Research project on asymmetric waterwheel) Our derivation of the waterwheel equations assumed that the water is pumped in symmetrically at the top. Investigate the asymmetric case. Modify \(Q(\theta)\) in (9.1.5) appropriately. Show that a closed set of three equations is still obtained, but that (9.1.9) includes a new term. Redo as much of the analysis in this chapter as possible. You should be able to solve for the fixed points and show that the pitchfork bifurcation is replaced by an imperfect bifurcation (Section 3.6). After that, you’re on your own! This problem has not yet been addressed in the literature.

\( Q(\theta )=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }{q_n\cos n\theta .} \) (9.1.5)

\(\dot{a}_1=\omega b_1-Ka_1 \\ \dot{b}_1=-\omega a_1-Kb_1 +q_1 \\ \dot{\omega } =(-v\omega +\pi gra_1)/I. \) (9.1.9)

Admin
Solution

We start off the problem with the same equations, but now \(Q(\theta)\) has \(p_{n} \sin (n \theta)\) terms in the Fourier series. Previously we used only the \(q_{n} \cos (n \theta)\) because we assumed \(Q(\theta)\) was an even function.

\(\begin{aligned}&\frac{\partial m}{\partial t}=Q-K m-\omega \frac{\partial m}{\partial \theta} \\&m(\theta, t)=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[a_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+b_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right] \\&Q(\theta)=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[p_{n} \sin (n \theta)+q_{n} \cos (n \theta)\right]\end{aligned}\)

If we go back through the derivation

\(\begin{gathered}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[a_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+b_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right]\right]=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[p_{n} \sin (n \theta)+q_{n} \cos (n \theta)\right] \\\quad-K \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[a_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+b_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right]-\omega \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[a_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+b_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right] \\\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[\dot{a}_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+\dot{b}_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right]=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[p_{n} \sin (n \theta)+q_{n} \cos (n \theta)\right] \\\quad-K \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\left[a_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)+b_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)\right]-\omega \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty} n\left[a_{n}(t) \cos (n \theta)-b_{n}(t) \sin (n \theta)\right]\end{gathered}\)

The \(\dot{\omega}\) equation stays the same because \(Q(\theta)\) wasn’t involved in the derivation.

\(\dot{\omega}=\frac{-\nu \omega+\pi g r a_{1}}{I}\)

Equating terms gives

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{a}_{n}=n \omega b_{n}-K a_{n}+p_{n} \\&\dot{b}_{n}=-n \omega a_{n}-K b_{n}+q_{n}\end{aligned}\)

And all equations for \(n=1\) are

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{a}_{1}=\omega b_{1}-K a_{1}+p_{1} \\&\dot{b}_{1}=-\omega a_{1}-K b_{1}+q_{1} \\&\dot{\omega}=\frac{-\nu \omega+\pi g r a_{1}}{I}\end{aligned}\)

We have a new term \(p_{1}\) in the \(\dot{a}_{1}\) equation.

Now solving for the fixed points,

\(\begin{aligned}&0=\omega b_{1}-K a_{1}+p_{1} \\&0=-\omega a_{1}-K b_{1}+q_{1} \\&0=\frac{-\nu \omega+\pi g r a_{1}}{I} \Rightarrow 0=-\nu \omega+\pi g r a_{1}\end{aligned}\)

Next we eliminate \(b_{1}\) from the first two equations, solve for \(a_{1}\), then substitute into the third equation.

\(\begin{aligned}0 &=K \omega b_{1}-K^{2} a_{1}+K p_{1} \\0 &=-\omega^{2} a_{1}-K \omega b_{1}+\omega q_{1} \\0 &=\left(K \omega b_{1}-K^{2} a_{1}+K p_{1}\right)+\left(-\omega^{2} a_{1}-K \omega b_{1}+\omega q_{1}\right) \\&\left.=-K^{2} a_{1}+K p_{1}-\omega^{2} a_{1}+\omega q_{1}\right) \\a_{1} &=\frac{K p_{1}+\omega q_{1}}{K^{2}+\omega^{2}} \\0 &=-\nu \omega+\pi g r a_{1}=-\nu \omega+\pi g r \frac{K p_{1}+\omega q_{1}}{K^{2}+\omega^{2}} \\&=-\nu \omega^{3}-K^{2} \nu \omega+\pi g r K p_{1}+\pi g r q_{1} \omega \\0 &=\nu \omega^{3}+\left(\nu K^{2}-\pi g r q_{1}\right) \omega-\pi g r K p_{1} \\&=\omega\left(\nu \omega^{2}+\nu K^{2}-\pi g r q_{1}\right)-\pi g r K p_{1}\end{aligned}\)

Looking at this, we can see the same solutions as before.

\(p_{1}=0 \Rightarrow 0=\omega\left(\nu \omega^{2}+\nu K^{2}-\pi g r q_{1}\right) \Rightarrow \omega=0, \pm \sqrt{\frac{\pi g r q_{1}}{\nu}-K^{2}}\)

This creates a pitchfork bifurcation. However, for \(p_{1} \neq 0\) along with proper values for the other coefficients, the pitchfork bifurcation is ruined and replaced with an imperfect bifurcation similar to that in Figure 3.6.3 in the text.
