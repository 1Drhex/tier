Guest Question.

An incident normal shock wave travels in quiescent air (at 100 kPa and 300 K) in a direction perpendicular to a plane wall. The incident wave causes a pressure ratio of 2.60. Find the temperature, pressure, velocity, and Mach number in state \(c\) behind the reflected shock wave.

Admin
Solution

Referring to Fig. 11.3a, one finds the same results for this moving shock problem for the following quantities. Note that for quiescent air ahead of the shock, \(V_{a} = 0\), \(W_{1s} = V_{s}\), and \(M_{1s} = M_{a}\).

For \(p_{2}/p_{1} = 2.6\)

\(M_{si} = M_{1s} = M_{a} = 1.54\) (The subscript \(si\) denotes the incident wave.)

\(V_{si}=W_{1s}=M_{si}\sqrt{\gamma RT_{1}} =1.54\sqrt{\left(1.4\right) \left(288J/kg\cdot K\right) \left(300 K\right) } =535.6 m/s\)

Therefore,

\(M_{2} = 0.687\)

\(T_{2}/T_{1} = 1.347\); \(T_{2} = 404.1K\)

\(p_{2}/p_{1} = p_{b}/p_{a} = 2.60\) as given.

\(W_{2s}=\left(V_{s} – V_{b}\right) =M_{2}\sqrt{\gamma RT_{2}} =\left(0.687\right) \sqrt{\left(1.4\right) \left(288J/kg\cdot K\right) \left(404.1 K\right) } =277.3 m/s\)

 

\(V_{b} = 258.3 m/s\)

For the reflected shock, the wall condition requires that \(V_{c} = 0\). Therefore,

\(V_{2} = V_{sr} + V_{b}\) (The subscript \(sr\) denotes the reflected wave.)

\(V_{3} = V_{sr}\)

The continuity equation, Eqn. (11.3) \(ρ_{1}(V_{s} – V_{a})= ρ_{2}(V_{s} – V_{b})\), for the reflected wave becomes

\(ρ_{2}(V_{sr} + V_{b}) = ρ_{3}V_{sr}\)

This expression is rearranged to give

\(\frac{\rho _{3}}{\rho _{2}} =\frac{V_{sr}+V_{b}}{V_{sr}} \)

Note that

\(M_{sr}=\frac{V_{sr}+V_{b}}{\sqrt{\gamma RT_{2}} } \)

Equation (4.18d) \(\beta \equiv \frac{p_{2}-p_{1}}{p_{1}} =\frac{2\gamma }{\gamma +1} \left(M^{2}_{1}-1\right)\) is used to relate \(ρ_{3}/ρ_{2}\) to \(M_{sr}\):

\(\frac{\rho _{3}}{\rho _{2}} =\frac{\left(\gamma +1\right) M^{2}_{sr}}{\left(\gamma -1\right) M^{2}_{sr} +2}=\frac{\left(\gamma +1\right)\frac{\left(V_{sr}+V_{b}\right) ^{2}}{\gamma RT_{2}} }{\left(\gamma -1\right) \frac{\left(V_{sr}+V_{b}\right) ^{2}}{\gamma RT_{2}} +2} =\frac{V_{sr}+V_{b}}{V_{sr}} \)

Rearranging the above expression yields

\((γ + 1) (V_{sr} + V_{b})^{2}V_{sr}= (γ – 1) (V_{sr} + V_{b})^{2}V_{sr}+(γ – 1) (V_{sr} + V_{b})^{2} V_{b}+2(γRT_{2}) V_{sr} + 2(γRT_{2}) V_{b}\)

With the values of \(V_{b}\) and \(T_{2}\), the above equation becomes

\(2V^{3}_{sr} + 930V^{2}_{sr} – 245,713V_{sr} – 91,042,585 = 0\)

 

\(V_{sr} = 329.0 m/s\)

\(M_{2}=M_{sr}=\frac{V_{sr}+V_{b}}{\sqrt{\gamma RT_{2}} } =\frac{329.0 m/s + 258.3 m/s}{\sqrt{\left(1.4\right) \left(288 J/kg\cdot K\right) \left(404.1 K\right) } } \approx 1.455\) and \(M_{3} ≈ 0.72\)

It is noted that \((V_{sr} + V_{b}) =V_{2}\) and

\(V_{2}=M_{2}\sqrt{\gamma RT_{2}} =1.455\times \sqrt{1.4\times 287\times 404} \approx 587.0 m/s\) same as \((V_{sr} + V_{b})\).

\(p_{3}/p_{2} = p_{c}/p_{b} ≈ 2.30\); \(p_{3} = p_{c} = 2.30 \times 2.6 \times 100 kPa = 598.0 kPa\)

\(T_{3}/T_{2} = T_{c}/T_{b} ≈ 1.29\); \(T_{3} = T_{c} = 1.29 \times 1.347 \times 300 K = 521.3 K\)

\(V_{3}=M_{3}\sqrt{\gamma RT_{3}} =0.72\times \sqrt{1.4\times 287\times 521.3} =329.0 m/s\)

This value of \(V_{3}\) is the same as \((V_{sr} – V_{c})\) , where \(V_{c} = 0\) as required by the boundary condition due to reflection.
