Guest Question.

A machine of mass 150 kg is attached to the end of the cantilever beam of Figure 10.18. The machine operates at 2000 rpm and has a rotating unbalance of 0.965 kg · m. What is the steady-state amplitude of vibration of the end of the beam?

Admin
Solution

The nondimensional formulation of the governing mathematical problem is

\( \frac{\partial^{4}w}{\partial x^{4}}+\frac{\partial^{2}w}{\partial t^{2}} =0\) (a)

subject to

\(\begin{matrix}w(0,t)=0 && \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}(0,t)=0 && \frac{\partial^{2}w}{\partial x^{2}}(1,t)=0 \end{matrix} \) (b)

\( \frac{\partial^{3}w}{\partial x^{3}}(1,t)=\beta \frac{\partial^{2} w}{\partial t^{2}}(1,t)+\alpha \sin\tilde{\omega }t \) (c)

where

\(\tilde{\omega }=\omega \sqrt{\frac{\rho AL^{4}}{EI} } \)

 

\(=209.4\frac{rad}{s}\sqrt{\frac{(280kg)(3m)^{3}}{(210\times10^{9}N/m^{2})(1.2\times10^{-4}m^{4})} } \)

\(=3.63 \) (d)

\(\beta =\frac{m}{\rho AL}=\frac{150kg}{280kg}=0.536 \) (e)

and

\(\alpha =\frac{m_{0}e\omega ^{2}L^{2}}{EI} \)

\(=\frac{(0.965 kg\cdot m)(209.4 rad/s)^{2}(3m)^{2}}{(210\times10^{9}N/m^{2})(1.2\times10^{-4}m^{4})} \) (f)

\(=0.010 \)

The boundary condition Equation (c) is developed by applying Newton’s law to a FBD of the machine as shown in Figure 10.18.(b) The problem is nonhomogeneous due to this boundary condition. From Table 10.4 te characteristic equation for the homogeneous problem of a beam with a concentrated mass at its end is

\(\lambda ^{1/4}(1+\cos\lambda ^{1/4}-\cosh\lambda ^{1/4})+\beta (\cos\lambda ^{1/4}\sinh\lambda ^{1/4}-\cosh\lambda ^{1/4}\sin\lambda ^{1/4})=0 \) (g)

The corresponding mode shapes for the homogeneous problem are

\(X_{k}(x)=C_{k}\left[\cos\lambda ^{1/4}x-\cosh\lambda ^{1/4}x++\frac{\cos\lambda ^{1/4}+\cosh\lambda ^{1/4}}{\sin\lambda ^{1/4}+\sinh\lambda ^{1/4}}(\sinh\lambda ^{1/4}x-\sin\lambda ^{1/4}x)\right] \) (h)

where \(C_k\) is chosen to normalize the mode shape with respect to the scalar product defined by

\((X_{j}(x),X_{k}(x))=\int_{0}^{1}{X_{j}(x),X_{k}(x)dx}+\beta X_{j}(1)X_{k}(1) \) (i)

The first six nondimensional natural frequencies and normalization constants are given in Table 10.5. The expansion theorem implies that the solution of the nonhomogeneous problem can be expanded in a series of normalized mode shapes. To this end,

\(w(x,t)=\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{p_{k}(t)X_{k}(x)} \) (j)

Substituting for w(x, t) into the governing partial differential equation, multiplying by \(X_j(x)\)
for an arbitrary j, and integrating from 0 to 1 leads to

\( \begin{matrix} \sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{(\ddot{p}_{k}+\lambda p_{k})} & \int_{0}^{1}{X_{j}(x)X_{k}(X)dx}=0 \end{matrix} \) (k)

The mutual orthonormality of the mode shapes implies

\(\int_{0}^{1}{X_{j}(x)X_{k}(x)dx}=\delta _{jk}-\beta X_{j}(1)X_{k}(1) \) (l)

Use of this orthogonality condition leads to

\(\ddot{p}_{j}+\lambda _{j}p_{j}=\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{(\ddot{p}_{k}+\lambda _{k}p_{k})}\beta X_{j}(1)X_{k}(1) \) (m)

Substituting for w(x, t) from the expansion theorem in the nonhomogeneous boundary condition leads to

\(\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{\frac{d^{3}X_{k}}{dx^{3}} }(1)p_{k}(t)=\alpha \sin\tilde{\omega}t +\beta \sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{X_{k}(1)\ddot{ p}_{k}(t)} \) (n)

The mode shapes satisfy the boundary conditions for the nonhomogeneous problem. Thus

\( \frac{d^{3}X}{dx^{3}}(1)=-\lambda \beta X_{k}(1) \) (o)

which when used in the preceding equation gives

\(\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{(\ddot{p}_{k}+\lambda _{k}p_{k})X_{k}(1)}=\alpha \sin\tilde{\omega }t \) (p)

and which when substituted into the previously derived differential equations for the principal coordinates uncouples these equations and gives

\( \begin{matrix} \ddot{p}_{j}+\lambda _{j}p_{j}=\alpha X_{j}(1)\sin\tilde{\omega }t&&&j=1,2,... \end{matrix} \) (q)

The steady-state solution for each of the principal coordinates is now easily obtained and the expansion theorem is used to write the steady-state solution as

\( w(x,t)=\left[\sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{\frac{\alpha X_{k}(1)}{\lambda _{k}-\tilde{\omega }^{2} } }X_{k }(x)\right]\sin \tilde{\omega } t \) (r)

The nondimensional steady-state amplitude of the end of the beam is

\(\alpha \sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{\frac{X_{k}^{2}(1)}{\lambda _{k}-\tilde{\omega }^{2} } }1.67\times10^{-4} \) (s)

The dimensional amplitude is obtained using Equation (10.29) as \(1.67\times10^{-4}(3m)=4.0 mm.\)

\(\begin{matrix} x^{*}=\frac{x}{L} && t^{*}=t\sqrt{\frac{EI}{\rho AL^{4}} } && w^{*}=\frac{w}{L}&& f^{*}=\frac{f}{f_{m}} \end{matrix}\) (10.29)
