Guest Question.

Show that the linearized CSTR model of can be written in the state-space form of Equations
\( \dot{x} = Ax+Bu+Ed \) (1)
\( y=Cx\) (2)
Derive state-space models for two cases:
(a) Both \(c_{A} \) and T are measured.
(b) Only T is measured.

Admin
Solution

The linearized CSTR model in Equations
\( \frac{dc^{\prime }_{A}}{dt} = a_{11}c^{\prime }_{A}+a_{12}T^{\prime } \)
\( \frac{dT^{\prime }}{dt} = a_{21}c^{\prime }_{A}+a_{22}T^{\prime }+b_{2}T^{\prime }_{c} \)
can be written in vector-matrix form using deviation variables:
\( [\begin{matrix} \frac{dc_{A}^{\prime }}{dt} \\ \frac{dT^{\prime }}{dt} \end{matrix}] = [\begin{matrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{matrix} ][\begin{matrix} c_{A}^{\prime } \\ T^{\prime } \end{matrix} ] + [\begin{matrix} 0 \\ b_{2} \end{matrix} ] T^{\prime }_{c} \) (3)

Let \( x_{1} \triangleq c_{A}^{\prime } \space and \space x_{2} \triangleq T^{\prime} \), and denote their time derivatives by \( \dot{x}_{1} \space and \space \dot{x}_{2} \).
In Equation (3) the coolant temperature \( T_{c} \) is considered to be a manipulated variable. For this example, there is a single control variable, \( u≜ T^{\prime }_{c} \), and no disturbance variable. Substituting these definitions into Equation (3) gives

\( [\begin{matrix} \dot{x} _{1} \\ \dot{x} _{2} \end{matrix} ] = \underbrace{[\begin{matrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{matrix} ]}_{A} [\begin{matrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \end{matrix} ] + \underbrace{[\begin{matrix} 0 \\ b_{2} \end{matrix} ]}_{B}u \) (4)

which is in the form of Equation (1) with \( x = col[x_{1},x_{2}]. \) (“col” denotes a column vector.)

(a) If both T and \( c_{A} \) are measured, then y=x and C = I in Equation (2), where I denotes the 2×2 identity matrix. A and B are defined in Equation (4).

(b) When only T is measured, output vector y is a scalar, \( y = T^{\prime} \) , and C is a row vector, C=[0, 1].

Note that the state-space model for Example 6.5 has d=0, because disturbance variables were not included in Equation (3). By contrast, suppose that the feed composition and feed temperature are considered to be disturbance variables in the original nonlinear CSTR model in Equations
\( V\frac{dc_{A}}{dt} = q(c_{Ai}-c_{A}) – Vkc_{A} \)
and
\( V\rho C\frac{dT}{dt} = wC(T_{i}-T)+(-\Delta H_{R})Vkc_{A}+UA(T_{c}-T) \).
Then the linearized model would include two additional deviation variables \( c^{\prime }_{Ai} \space and \space T^{\prime }_{i} \) , which would also be included in Equation (3). As a result, Equation (4) would be modified to include two disturbance variables, \( d_{1} \triangleq c^{\prime }_{Ai} \space and \space d_{2} \triangleq T^{\prime }_{i} \).

The state-space model in Equation (1) contains both dependent variables, the elements of x, and independent variables, the elements of u and d. But why is x referred to as the “state vector” ? This term is used because x(t) uniquely determines the state of the system at any time, t. Suppose that at time t, the initial value x(0) is specified and u(t) and d(t) are known over the time period [0, t]. Then x(t) is unique and can be determined from the analytical solution or by numerical integration. Analytical techniques are described in control engineering textbooks (e.g., Franklin et al., 2005; Ogata, 2008), while numerical and analytical solutions can be readily obtained using software packages such as MATLAB or Mathematica.
