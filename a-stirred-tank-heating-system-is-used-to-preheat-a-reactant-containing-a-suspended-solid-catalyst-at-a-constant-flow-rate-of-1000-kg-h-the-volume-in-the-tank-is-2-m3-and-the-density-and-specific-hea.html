Guest Question.

A stirred-tank heating system is used to preheat a reactant containing a suspended solid catalyst at a constant flow rate of 1000 kg/h. The volume in the tank is \(2 m^3 \) and the density and specific heat of the suspended mixture are, respectively, \(900 kg/m^3 \), and \(1 cal/g \space ^{∘}C \). The process initially is operating with inlet and outlet temperatures of \(100 \space and \space 130 \space ^{∘} C\), respectively. The steady-state energy input is \(3 × 10^{7} cal/h\). The transfer function is
\(T^{\prime}(s)=\frac{{1}/{wC}}{\frac{m}{w}s+1 }Q^{\prime}(s)+\frac{1}{\frac{m}{w}s+1 }T^{\prime}_{i}(s) \)
The following questions concerning process operations are posed:
(a) What are the values of K and \(\tau \)?
(b) If the heater input is suddenly increased by +30%, how long will it take for the tank temperature to achieve 99% of the final temperature change?
(c) If the inlet temperature is increased suddenly from \( 100 \ to \ 120 \space ^{∘} C\), how long will it take before the outlet temperature changes from \(130 \ to \ 135 \space ^{∘} C\)?

Solution

(a) Using Equation
\(T^{\prime}(s)=\frac{{1}/{wC}}{\frac{m}{w}s+1 }Q^{\prime}(s)+\frac{1}{\frac{m}{w}s+1 }T^{\prime}_{i}(s) \),
the gain and time constants can be determined
\(K=\frac{1}{wC}=\frac{1}{(10^{6}\frac{g}{h} )(\frac{1cal}{g \space ^{\circ}C} )} =10^{-6} \frac{^{\circ}C}{cal/h} \)

\(\tau = \frac{V\rho }{w}=\frac{(2m^{3})(9×10^{5}\frac{g}{m^{3}} )}{10^{6}\frac{g}{h}} =1.8h \)

Note that the calculated outlet temperature change as a result of the input change; hence, the outlet temperature at the final steady state is \(130 \space ^{\circ }C +9 \space ^{\circ }C=139\space ^{\circ }C\).
However, use of the Final Value Theorem is an unnecessary formality when a transfer function is written in the standard form with gain and time constant. The input change need only be multiplied by the steady-state gain to obtain the ultimate change in the process output, assuming that the final value does in fact exist and is finite. In this case
\(T^{\prime}(t\rightarrow \infty )=K\Delta Q=(10^{-6} \space ^{\circ }C/cal \cdot h)(9×10^{6}cal/h)=9 \space ^{\circ }C\).

(c) Because the gain of the disturbance transfer function (that relates \(T^{\prime} \ to \ T^{\prime}_{i}\)) is one, an input temperature change of \(20 \space ^{\circ}C\) causes an outlet temperature of \(20 \space ^{\circ}C\). The time required for the output to change by \(5 \space ^{\circ}C\) , or 25% of the ultimate steady-state change, can be calculated from Fig. 5.3 as \( t/\tau = 0.3\) or t = 0.54 h. Equation
\(y(t) =KM(1-e^{-t/\tau })\)
furnishes a more accurate way to calculate this value:
\(\frac{y(t)}{KM}= 1-e^{-t/\tau }\)
\(\frac{5 \space ^{\circ }C}{(1)(20\space ^{\circ }C)}= 1-e^{-t/\tau }\)
\(e^{-t/\tau }=0.75\)
\(-\frac{t}{\tau}=\ln 0.75=-0.288 \)
t = 0.52 h
