Guest Question.

Adiabatic Flame Temperature in Steady Combustion

Liquid octane (\(C_{8}H_{18}\)) enters the combustion chamber of a gas turbine steadily at 1 atm and 25°C, and it is burned with air that enters the combustion chamber at the same state, as shown in Fig. 15–26. Determine the adiabatic flame temperature for (a) complete combustion with 100 percent theoretical air, (b) complete combustion with 400 percent theoretical air, and (c) incomplete combustion (some CO in the products) with 90 percent theoretical air.

Admin
Solution

Liquid octane is burned steadily. The adiabatic flame temperature is to be determined for different cases.

Assumptions 1 This is a steady-flow combustion process. 2 The combustion chamber is adiabatic. 3 There are no work interactions. 4 Air and the combustion gases are ideal gases. 5 Changes in kinetic and potential energies are negligible.

Analysis (a) The balanced equation for the combustion process with the theoretical amount of air is

\(\mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}(l)+12.5\left(\mathrm{O}_{2}+3.76 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\right) \rightarrow 8 \mathrm{CO}_{2}+9 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+47 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\)

The adiabatic flame temperature relation \(H_{\text {prod }}=H_{\text {react }}\) in this case reduces to

\(\sum N_{p}\left(\bar{h}_{f}^{\circ}+\bar{h}-\bar{h}^{\circ}\right)_{p}=\sum N_{r} \bar{h}_{f, r}^{\circ}=\left(N \bar{h}_{f}^{\circ}\right)_{\mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}}\)

since all the reactants are at the standard reference state and \(\bar{h}_{f}^{\circ}=0\) for \(\mathrm{O}_{2}\) and \(\mathrm{N}_{2}\). The \(\bar{h}_{f}^{\circ}\) and \(\bar{h}\) values of various components at \(298 \mathrm{~K}\) are

Substance \(\bar{h}_{f}^{\circ}\)
kJ/kmol		 \(\bar{h}_{298 \mathrm{~K}}\)
kJ/kmol
\(C_{3}\mathrm{H}_{18}(l)\) –249,950 –
\(\mathrm{O}_{2}\) 0 8682
\(\mathrm{~N}_{2}\) 0 8669
\(\mathrm{H}_{2}O(g)\) –241,820 9904
\(\mathrm{CO}_{2}\) –393,520 9364

Substituting, we have

\(\begin{array}{l}\left(8 \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{CO}_{2}\right)\left[\left(-393,520+\bar{h}_{\mathrm{CO}_{2}}-9364\right) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{CO}_{2}\right] \\+\left(9 \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right)\left[\left(-241,820+\bar{h}_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}-9904\right) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right] \\+\left(47 \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{N}_{2}\right)\left[\left(0+\bar{h}_{\mathrm{N}_{2}}-8669\right) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{N}_{2}\right] \\=\left(1 \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}\right)\left(-249,950 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}\right)\end{array}\)

which yields

\(8 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{CO}_{2}}+9 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}+47 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{N}_{2}}=5,646,081 \mathrm{~kJ}\)

It appears that we have one equation with three unknowns. Actually we have only one unknown-the temperature of the products \(T_{\text {prod }}\) since \(h=h(T)\) for ideal gases. Therefore, we have to use an equation solver or a trial-and-error approach to determine the temperature of the products.

A first guess is obtained by dividing the right-hand side of the equation by the total number of moles, which yields \(5,646,081 /(8+9+47)=88,220 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol}\). This enthalpy value corresponds to about \(2650 \mathrm{~K}\) for \(\mathrm{N}_{2}, 2100 \mathrm{~K}\) for \(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\), and \(1800 \mathrm{~K}\) for \(\mathrm{CO}_{2}\). Noting that the majority of the moles are \(\mathrm{N}_{2}\), we see that \(T_{\text {prod }}\) should be close to \(2650 \mathrm{~K}\), but somewhat under it. Therefore, a good first guess is \(2400 \mathrm{~K}\). At this temperature,

\(\begin{aligned}8 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{CO}_{2}}+9 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}+47 \bar{h}_{\mathrm{N}_{2}} &=8 \times 125,152+9 \times 103,508+47 \times 79,320 \\&=5,660,828 \mathrm{~kJ}\end{aligned}\)

This value is higher than \(5,646,081 \mathrm{~kJ}\). Therefore, the actual temperature is slightly under \(2400 \mathrm{~K}\). Next we choose \(2350 \mathrm{~K}\). It yields

\(8 \times 122,091+9 \times 100,846+47 \times 77,496=5,526,654\)

which is lower than \(5,646,081 \mathrm{~kJ}\). Therefore, the actual temperature of the products is between 2350 and \(2400 \mathrm{~K}\). By interpolation, it is found to be \(T_{\text {prod }}=2395 \mathrm{~K}\).

(b) The balanced equation for the complete combustion process with 400 percent theoretical air is

\(\mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}(l)+50\left(\mathrm{O}_{2}+3.76 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\right) \rightarrow 8 \mathrm{CO}_{2}+9 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+37.5 \mathrm{O}_{2}+188 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\)

By following the procedure used in \((a)\), the adiabatic flame temperature in this case is determined to be \(T_{\text {prod }}=962 \mathbf{K}\)

Notice that the temperature of the products decreases significantly as a result of using excess air.

(c) The balanced equation for the incomplete combustion process with 90 percent theoretical air is

\(\mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{H}_{18}(l)+11.25\left(\mathrm{O}_{2}+3.76 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\right) \rightarrow 5.5 \mathrm{CO}_{2}+2.5 \mathrm{CO}+9 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+42.3 \mathrm{~N}_{2}\)

Following the procedure used in \((a)\), we find the adiabatic flame temperature in this case to be \(T_{\text {prod }}=2236 \mathbf{K}\).

Discussion Notice that the adiabatic flame temperature reaches its maximum value when complete combustion occurs with the theoretical amount of air (part \(a\) ). It decreases as a result of incomplete combustion (part \(c\) ) or using excess air (part \(b\) ).
