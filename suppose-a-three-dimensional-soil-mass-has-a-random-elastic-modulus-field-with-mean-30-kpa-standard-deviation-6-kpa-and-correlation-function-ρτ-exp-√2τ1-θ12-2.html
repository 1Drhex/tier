Guest Question.

Suppose a three-dimensional soil mass has a random elastic modulus field with mean 30 kPa, standard deviation 6 kPa, and correlation function

\(\rho(\tau)=\exp \left\{-\sqrt{\left(\frac{2 \tau_{1}}{\theta_{1}}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{2 \tau_{2}}{\theta_{2}}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{2 \tau_{3}}{\theta_{3}}\right)^{2}}\right\}\) (3.154)

where \(\theta_{1}=\theta_{2}=4 \text { and } \theta_{3}=1\) (assume that \(\theta_{3}\) is the correlation length in the vertical direction). Suppose further that settlement of a foundation on this soil has been found to depend on the average elastic modulus over a volume of size \(V=T_{1} \times T_{2} \times T_{3}=2 \times 3 \times 8\). What is the mean and standard deviation of this average?

Admin
Solution

Suppose that the elastic modulus field is denoted by \(X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right), \text { where }\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)\) is the spatial position with \(t_{3}\) measured vertically. Let our average elastic modulus be \(X_{V}\), defined by

 

\(X_{V}=\frac{1}{2(3)(8)} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right) d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3}\) (3.155)

 

where we have placed our origin at one corner of the averaging domain, with \(t_{3}\) positive downward. (Since the field is stationary, we can place the origin wherever we want—stationarity is suggested by the fact that the mean and variance are not dependent on position, and Eq. 3.154 is a function of τ rather than position.)

The mean of \(X_{V}\) is obtained by taking expectations:

 

\(\begin{aligned}E \left[X_{V}\right] &= E \left[\frac{1}{2(3)(8)} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right) d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3}\right] \\&=\frac{1}{2(3)(8)} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} E \left[X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)\right] d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} \\&=\frac{1}{2(3)(8)} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} 30 d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} \\&=30\end{aligned}\)

 

so we see that the mean is preserved by averaging (as expected). That is, \(\mu_{X_{V}}=\mu_{X}=30\).

To obtain the variance we write, for V = 2(3)(8),

 

\(\operatorname{Var}\left[X_{V}\right]= E \left[\left(X_{V}-\mu_{X_{V}}\right)^{2}\right]\)

 

\(= E \left[\left(\frac{1}{V} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2}\left(X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)-\mu_{X}\right) d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3}\right)^{2}\right]\)

 

\(= E \left[\frac{1}{V^{2}} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2}\left(X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)-\mu_{X}\right)\right.\)

 

\(\left.\times\left(X\left(s_{1}, s_{2}, s_{3}\right)-\mu_{X}\right) d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} d s_{1} d s_{2} d s_{3}\right]\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}=& \frac{1}{V^{2}} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} E \left[\left(X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)-\mu_{X}\right)\right.\\&\left.\times\left(X\left(s_{1}, s_{2}, s_{3}\right)-\mu_{X}\right)\right] d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} d s_{1} d s_{2} d s_{3} \\=& \frac{1}{V^{2}} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \operatorname{Cov}\left[X\left(t_{1}, t_{2}, t_{3}\right)\right.\\&\left.X\left(s_{1}, s_{2}, s_{3}\right)\right] d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} d s_{1} d s_{2} d s_{3} \\=& \frac{\sigma_{X}^{2}}{V^{2}} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{8} \int_{0}^{3} \int_{0}^{2} \rho\left(t_{1}-s_{1}, t_{2}-s_{2}, t_{3}-s_{3}\right) \\& \times d t_{1} d t_{2} d t_{3} d s_{1} d s_{2} d s_{3} \\=& \sigma_{X}^{2} \gamma(2,3,8)\end{aligned}\)

 

(Aside: The last three expressions would also have been obtained if we had first assumed \(\mu_{X_{V}}=\mu_{X}=0\), which would have made the earlier expressions somewhat simpler; however, this is only a trick for computing variance and must be used with care, i.e., the mean is not actually zero, but it may be set to zero for the purposes of this calculation.)

The variance function \(\gamma\left(T_{1}, T_{2}, T_{3}\right)\) is nominally defined, as above, by a sixfold integration. Since the correlation function \(\rho\left(t_{1}-s_{1}, t_{2}-s_{2}, t_{3}-s_{3}\right)\) is constant along diagonal lines where \(t_{1}-s_{1}, t_{2}-s_{2}, \text { and } t_{3}-s_{3}\) are constants, the sixfold integration can be reduced to a threefold integration (see, e.g., Eq. 3.112):

 

\(\begin{aligned}\gamma\left(T_{1}, T_{2}\right)=& \frac{1}{T_{1}^{2} T_{2}^{2}} \int_{-T_{1}}^{T_{1}} \int_{-T_{2}}^{T_{2}}\left(\left|T_{1}\right|-\left|\tau_{1}\right|\right)\left(\left|T_{2}\right|-\left|\tau_{2}\right|\right) \\& \times \rho\left(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}\right) d \tau_{2} d \tau_{1}\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\gamma\left(T_{1}, T_{2}, T_{3}\right)\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{\left[T_{1} T_{2} T_{3}\right]^{2}} \int_{-T_{3}}^{T_{3}} \int_{-T_{2}}^{T_{2}} \int_{-T_{1}}^{T_{1}}\left(\left|T_{1}\right|-\left|\tau_{1}\right|\right)\left(\left|T_{2}\right|-\left|\tau_{2}\right|\right)\)

 

\(\times\left(\left|T_{3}\right|-\left|\tau_{3}\right|\right) \rho\left(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}, \tau_{3}\right) d \tau_{1} d \tau_{2} d \tau_{3}\) (3.156)

 

Since the given correlation function, Eq. 3.154, is quadrant symmetric, that is, since \(\rho\left(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}, \tau_{3}\right)=\rho\left(-\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}, \tau_{3}\right)=\rho\left(\tau_{1},-\tau_{2}, \tau_{3}\right)=\cdots=\rho\left(-\tau_{1},-\tau_{2},-\tau_{3}\right)\), the variance function can be further simplified to

 

\(\gamma\left(T_{1}, T_{2}, T_{3}\right)\)

 

\(=\frac{8}{\left[T_{1} T_{2} T_{3}\right]^{2}} \int_{0}^{T_{3}} \int_{0}^{T_{2}} \int_{0}^{T_{1}}\left(\left|T_{1}\right|-\tau_{1}\right)\left(\left|T_{2}\right|-\tau_{2}\right)\)

 

\(\times\left(\left|T_{3}\right|-\tau_{3}\right) \rho\left(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}, \tau_{3}\right) d \tau_{1} d \tau_{2} d \tau_{3}\) (3.157)

 

Thus, to find the variance of \(X_{V}\), we must evaluate Eq. 3.157 for \(T_{1}=2, T_{2}=3, \text { and } T_{3}=8\). For this, we will use Gaussian quadrature [see Griffiths and Smith (2006) or Press et al. (1997) and Appendices B and C],

 

\(\gamma\left(T_{1}, T_{2}, T_{3}\right) \simeq \frac{1}{T_{1} T_{2} T_{3}}\)

 

\(\times \sum_{k=1}^{n_{g}} w_{k}\left[\sum_{j=1}^{n_{g}} w_{j}\left\{\sum_{i=1}^{n_{g}} w_{i} f\left(\tau_{1 i}, \tau_{2 j}, \tau_{3 k}\right)\right\}\right]\) (3.158)

 

where

 

\(\begin{aligned}f\left(\tau_{1 i}, \tau_{2 j}, \tau_{3 k}\right)=&\left(\left|T_{1}\right|-\tau_{1 i}\right)\left(\left|T_{2}\right|-\tau_{2 j}\right)\left(\left|T_{3}\right|-\tau_{3 k}\right) \\& \times \rho\left(\tau_{1 i}, \tau_{2 j}, \tau_{3 k}\right) \\\tau_{1 i}=& \frac{T_{1}}{2}\left(1+z_{i}\right) \\\tau_{2 j}=& \frac{T_{2}}{2}\left(1+z_{j}\right) \\\tau_{3 k}=& \frac{T_{3}}{2}\left(1+z_{k}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

and where \(w_{i} \text { and } z_{i}\) are the weights and evaluation points of Gaussian quadrature and \(n_{g}\) is the number of evaluation points to use. The accuracy of Gaussian quadrature is about the same as obtained by fitting a (2n)th-order polynomial to the integrand. The weights and evaluation points are provided in Appendix B for a variety of \(n_{g}\) values. Using \(n_{g}=20\), we get

 

\(\gamma(2,3,8) \simeq 0.878\)

 

Note that when \(n_{g}=5\) the Gaussian quadrature approximation gives \(\gamma(2,3,8) \simeq 0.911\), a 4% relative error. The variance of the 2 × 3 × 8 average is thus

 

\(\operatorname{Var}\left[X_{V}\right]=\sigma_{X}^{2} \gamma(2,3,8) \simeq(6)^{2}(0.878)=31.6\)

 

so that \(\sigma_{X_{V}}=\sqrt{31.6}=5.6\).
