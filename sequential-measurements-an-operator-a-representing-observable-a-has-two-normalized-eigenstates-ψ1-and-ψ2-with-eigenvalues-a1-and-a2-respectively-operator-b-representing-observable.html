Guest Question.

Sequential measurements. An operator \( \hat{A} \) , representing observable A, has two (normalized) eigenstates \( ψ_1 \) and \( ψ_2 \) , with eigenvalues \( a_1 \) and \( a_2 \) , respectively. Operator \( \hat{B} \) , representing observable B, has two (normalized) eigenstates \( Φ_1 \) and \( Φ_2 \) , with eigenvalues \( b_1 \) and \( b_2 \) . The eigenstates are related by

\( \psi_{1}=\left(3 \phi_{1}+4 \phi_{2}\right) / 5, \quad \psi_{2}=\left(4 \phi_{1}-3 \phi_{2}\right) / 5 \).

(a) Observable A is measured, and the value \( a_1 \) is obtained. What is the state of the system (immediately) after this measurement?

(b) If B is now measured, what are the possible results, and what are their probabilities?

(c) Right after the measurement of B, A is measured again. What is the probability of getting \( a_1 \) ? (Note that the answer would be quite different if I had told you the outcome of the B measurement.)

Admin
Solution

(a) \( \psi_{1} \).

(b) \( \left.\left.b_{1} \text { (with probability } 9 / 25\right) \text { or } b_{2} \text { (with probability } 16 / 25\right) \).

(c) Right after the measurement of B:

With probability 9/25 the particle is in state \( \phi_{1}=\left(3 \psi_{1}+4 \psi_{2}\right) / 5 \); in that case the probability of getting \( a_1 \) is 9/25.

With probability 16/25 the particle is in state \( \phi_{2}=\left(4 \psi_{1}-3 \psi_{2}\right) / 5 \) in that case the probability of getting \( a_1 \) is 16/25.

So the total probability of getting \( a_1 \) is \( \frac{9}{25} \cdot \frac{9}{25}+\frac{16}{25} \cdot \frac{16}{25}=\frac{337}{625}=0.5392 \).

[Note: The measurment of B (even if we don’t know the outcome of that measurement) collapses the wave function, and thereby alters the probabilities for the second measurment of A. If the graduate student inadvertantly neglected to measure B, the second measurement of A would be certain to reproduce the result \( a_1 \) .]
