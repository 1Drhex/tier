Guest Question.

Let the sequences \(x[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},2,3 \right\}\) and \(h[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},2 \right\}\) .

a. Compute the circular convolution of the sequences.

b. Compute the linear convolution of the sequences using the circular convolution.
c. Compute the linear convolution of the sequences using the DFT.

Admin
Solution

a. The circular convolution of x[n] and h[n] is:

\(f[n]=x[n]\otimes h[n]=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{2}{x([n-k]mod 3)h[k]}\) (1)

We will implement relation (1) by placing the samples of the sequences h[k] ={1, 2, 0} and x[–k] = {1, 3, 2} in two concentric circles, as shown in the figures below.

It is: \(f[0] = (1, 2, 0) × (1, 3, 2)^{T}\) = 1 ∙ 1 + 2 ∙ 3 = 7

It is: \(f[1] = (1, 2, 0) × (2, 1, 3)^{T}\)= 1 ∙ 2 + 2 ∙ 1 = 4

It is: \(f[2] = (1, 2, 0) × (3, 2, 1)^{T}\) = 1 ∙ 3 + 2 ∙ 2 = 7

Consequently, the circular convolution of x[n] and h[n] is \(f[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{7},4,7 \right\}\) (2)

b. The length of the sequence \(x[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},2,3 \right\}\) is \(N_{1} = 3,\) whereas the length of the sequence h[n] = {1, 2} is \(N_{2} =2.\) The linear convolution of x[n] and h[n] creates a sequence of length \(N_{1} + N_{2} – 1 \)= 3 + 2 – 1 = 4. Consequently, we must add one and two zeros to x[n] and h[n] sequences, respectively, i.e., \(x[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},2,3,0 \right\}\) and \(h[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},2,0,0 \right\}\)

The circular convolution, x[n] ⊗ h[n], will be:

\(y[n]=x[n]\otimes h[n]=\sum\limits_{k=0}^{3}{h[k]x[(n-k)mod 4]}\) (3)

Thus,

\(y[0]=(1,2,0,0)\times (1,0,3,2)^{T}\)=1∙1=1
\(y[1]=(1,2,0,0)\times (2,1,0,3)^{T}\)=1∙2+2∙1=4 (4)
\(y[2]=(1,2,0,0)\times (3,2,1,0)^{T}\)=1∙3+2∙2=7
\(y[3]=(1,2,0,0)\times (0,3,2,1)^{T}\)=2∙3=6

Using the circular convolution, the linear convolution of the sequences is \(y[n]=\left\{\underset{\uparrow }{1},4,7,6 \right\}.\) The result is the same with that of the linear convolution of the sequences x[n] and h[n].
c. In order to calculate the linear convolution of x[n] and h[n], first we will add the appropriate number of zeros to the sequences so that their length is \(N = N_{1}+N_{2} – 1\) = 4, that is x[n] = {1, 2, 3, 0} and y[n] = {1, 2, 0, 0}.

The DFT of x[n] is: X[k]=DFT[x[n]]=\(\sum\limits_{n=0}^{3}{x[n]W_{4}^{kn}}\) (5)

Consequently, the DFT matrix for x[n] will be:

\(\begin{bmatrix} X[0] \\ X[1] \\ X[2] \\ X[3] \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} W_{4}^{0} & W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{0} \\ W_{4}^{0} & W_{4}^{1}&W_{4}^{2}&W_{4}^{3}\\W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{2}&W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{2}\\W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{3}&W_{4}^{2}&W_{4}^{1} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x[0] \\ x[1] \\ x[2] \\ x[3] \end{bmatrix}\) (6)

where:

\(W_{4}^{0}=(e^{-j\frac{2\pi }{4} })^{0}=1\)
\(W_{4}^{1}=e^{-j\frac{2\pi }{4} }=e^{-j\frac{\pi }{2} }=\cos \frac{\pi }{2}-j\sin \frac{\pi }{2}=-j\)
\(W_{4}^{2}=e^{-j\frac{2\pi }{4}2 }=e^{-j\pi }=\cos \pi -j\sin \pi =-1\)
\(\eta ' W_{4}^{2}=(W_{4}^{1})^{2}=(-j)^{2}=j^{2}=-1\)
\(W_{4}^{3}=W_{4}^{2}.W_{4}=(-1)(-j)=j\)
\(W_{4}^{4}=(W_{4}^{2})^{2}=(-1)^{2}=1=W_{4}^{0}\)

Thus,

\(\begin{bmatrix} X[0] \\ X[1] \\ X[2] \\ X[3] \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1&1&1\\ 1 & -J&-1&J\\1&-1&1&-1\\1&J&-1&-J\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -2-2j \\ 2 \\ -2+2j \end{bmatrix}\) (7)

That is, X[k] = DFT[x[n]] = {6,−2 − 2 j,−2 + 2 j} (8)
Likewise, for H[k]:

\(\begin{bmatrix} H[0] \\ H[1] \\ H[2] \\ H[3] \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1&1&1\\ 1 & -j&-1&j\\1&-1&1&-1\\1&j&-1&-j\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1-2J \\ -1 \\ 1+2j \end{bmatrix}\) (9)

That is, H[k] = DFT[h[n]] = {3, 1 − 2 j, − 1, 1 + 2 j} (10)
It is true that:

Y[k]=DFT[y[n]]\(=X[k]H[k]\Rightarrow\)
\(Y(k)=\left\{6,-2-2j,2,-2+2j\right\}\times \left\{3,1-2j,-1,1+2j\right\} \Rightarrow\)
\(Y[k]=\left\{18,-6+2j,-2,-6-2j\right\}\)(11)
But y[n]=IDFT[Y[k]]=\(\frac{1}{4}\sum\limits_{k=0}^{3}{Y[k]W_{4}^{-kn}} ,0\leq n\leq 3\) (12)

So, the IDFT matrix for y[n] will be:

\(\begin{bmatrix} y[0] \\y[1] \\ y[2] \\ y[3] \end{bmatrix}=\frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix} W_{4}^{0} & W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{0} \\ W_{4}^{0} & W_{4}^{-1}&W_{4}^{-2}&W_{4}^{-3}\\W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{-2}&W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{-2}\\W_{4}^{0}&W_{4}^{-3}&W_{4}^{-2}&W_{4}^{-1} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} Y[0] \\Y[1] \\ Y[2] \\ Y[3] \end{bmatrix}\) (13)

where

\(W_{4}^{0}=1\)
\(W_{4}^{-1}=(W_{4}^{1})^{-1}=(-j)^{-1}=\frac{1}{-j} =\frac{j}{-j^{2}}=j\) (14)
\(W_{4}^{-2}=(W_{4}^{2})^{-1}=\frac{1}{-1}=-1 \)
\(W_{4}^{-3}=(W_{4}^{3})^{-1}=\frac{1}{j}=\frac{j}{j^{2}}=-j \)
\((13),(14)\Rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} y[0] \\y[1] \\ y[2] \\ y[3] \end{bmatrix}=\frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix}1 &1&1&1 \\ 1&j&-1&-j\\1&-1&1&-1\\1&-j&-1&j \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 18 \\-6+2j \\ -2 \\ -6-2j \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\4 \\ 7 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}\)

Thus, y[n] = {1, 4, 7, 6}. The result is the same with that of the linear convolution of x[n] and h[n].
