Guest Question.

Consider the fatigue life of a structural joint for which the S/N curve has been found to be \( N_{f}=K(S_{r})^{-4} \) . In actual use, the joint will be subjected to a narrowband stochastic stress \( \left\{X(t)\right\} \) that is mean-zero and has standard deviation \( \sigma _{X} \) . This narrowband stress can be written as \( X(t)=A(t)\cos [\omega _{c}t+\theta (t)] \) , with \( A(t) \) and \( \theta (t) \) being independent and \( \theta (t) \) uniformly distributed on the set of possible values. Rather than being Rayleigh distributed, it is believed that \( A(t) \) has the one-sided Gaussian distribution

\( p_{A(t)}(u)=\frac{1}{\pi^{1/2}\sigma _{X}}\exp \left(\frac{-u^{2}}{4\sigma ^{2}_{X}} \right) U(u) \)

Compare the fatigue life predicted by the Rayleigh approximation, the use of \( S_{r}=2A(t) \) and \( p_{A(t)}(u) \) in Eq. 11.49 \(\frac{1}{E(N(T))} \equiv E(\Delta D)=K^{-1}E(S_{r}^{m}) \), and the use of Eq. 11.66

\(GR=\left(1+\frac{m(m-1)(kurtosis-3)}{24} \right) ^{-1} \) for \( kurtosis \gt 3\)

\(GR=1+\frac{m(m-1)(3- kurtosis)}{24} ^{-1} \) for \( kurtosis \lt 3\)

Admin
Solution

Before starting on the fatigue computation, it is appropriate to check for consistency of the model. In particular, we find that

\( E[A^{2}(t)]=\int_{0}^{\infty }{u^{2}} p_{A(t)}(u)du=2\sigma ^{2}_{X} \)

so the narrowband process gives \( E[X^{2}(t)]=E[A^{2}(t)]/2=\sigma ^{2}_{X} \) (see ). Thus, the model is consistent with \( \sigma _{X} \) being the standard deviation of the stress. Now we note that the Rayleigh approximation from Eqs. 11.50 \(E(\Delta D)=K^{-1}(2)^{3m/2}\lambda _{0}^{m/2}\Gamma \left(1+\frac{m}{2} \right) \) and 11.51 \( E(T)= \frac{2\pi \sigma _{X}}{E(\Delta D)\sigma _{X}} =\frac{2\pi }{E(\Delta D)}\left(\frac{\lambda _{0}}{\lambda _{2}} \right) ^{1/2}\) is \( E(T_{Ray})=\pi K/[64(\lambda _{0})^{3/2}(\lambda _{2})^{1/2}] \) , as in . For this narrowband process we can say that \( \lambda _{2}=\omega ^{2}_{c}\lambda _{0} \), and we also know that \( \lambda _{0}=\sigma ^{2}_{X} \) . This allows the Rayleigh approximation to be rewritten as

\( E(T_{Ray})=\pi K/(64\omega _{c}\sigma ^{4}_{X}) \)

Using \( S_{r}=2A(t) \) in Eq. 11.49 \(\frac{1}{E(N(T))} \equiv E(\Delta D)=K^{-1}E(S_{r}^{m}) \) gives

\( E(\Delta D)=K^{-1}E(S^{4}_{r})=2^{4}K^{-1}\int_{0}^{\infty }{u^{4}} p_{A(t)}(u)du=192K^{-1}\sigma ^{4}_{X} \)

and Eq. 11.51 then gives the expected fatigue life as \( E(T)=\pi K/[96\omega _{c}(\sigma _{X})^{4}] \) . Thus, this gives a prediction of \( E(T) \) that is 33% smaller than the Rayleigh approximation.

In order to use Eq. 11.66

\(GR=\left(1+\frac{m(m-1)(kurtosis-3)}{24} \right) ^{-1} \) for \( kurtosis \gt 3\)

\(GR=1+\frac{m(m-1)(3- kurtosis)}{24} ^{-1} \) for \( kurtosis \lt 3\)

we must find the kurtosis of the stress process. To do this, we first integrate over the possible values for \( \theta (t) \) to find that

\( E[X^{4}(t)]=E[A^{4}(t)]\int_{0}^{2\pi }{\frac{\cos ^{4}(\psi )}{2\pi} } d\psi =\frac{3}{8} E[A^{4}(t)] \)

From the integral used in the previous step, we find that \( E[A^{4}(t)]=12\sigma ^{4}_{X} \) so \( E[X^{4}(t)]=4.5\sigma ^{4}_{X} \) . Thus this process has \( kurtosis=4.5 \) , and Eq. 11.66 then gives \( GR=24/[24+12(1.5)]\approx 0.571 \) , which predicts a 43% reduction in fatigue life as compared with the Rayleigh approximation (which would be appropriate for a Gaussian process). This value is relatively consistent with the 33% reduction calculated in the previous step. To neglect the non-Gaussian effect would be a more serious error, because it would result in a fatigue life prediction that was significantly too large.
