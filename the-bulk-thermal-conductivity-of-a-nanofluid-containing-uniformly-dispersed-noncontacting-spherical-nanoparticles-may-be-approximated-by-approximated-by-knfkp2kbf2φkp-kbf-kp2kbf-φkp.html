Guest Question.

The bulk thermal conductivity of a nanofluid containing uniformly dispersed, noncontacting spherical nanoparticles may be approximated by

 

\(k_{nf}=\left [ \frac{k_{p}+2k_{bf}+2\varphi (k_{p}-k_{bf})}{k_{p}+2k_{bf}-\varphi (k_{p}-k_{bf})} \right ]k_{bf}\)

 

where φ is the volume fraction of the nanoparticles, and \(k_{bf}, k_{p}\), and \(k_{nf}\) are the thermal conductivities of the base fluid, particle, and nanofluid, respectively. Likewise, the dynamic viscosity may be approximated as [20]

 

\(\mu _{nf}=\mu _{bf}(1+2.5\varphi )\)

 

Determine the values of \(k_{nf}, \rho _{nf}, c_{p,nf}, \mu _{nf}\), and \(\alpha _{nf}\) for a mixture of water and \(A1_{2}O_{3}\) nanoparticles at a temperature of T = 300 K and a particle volume fraction of φ = 0.05. The thermophysical properties of the particles are \(k_{p}=36.0 W/m\cdot K,\rho _{p}=3970 kg/m^{3}\), and \(c_{p,p}=0.765 kJ/kg\cdot K\).

Admin
Solution

Known: Expressions for the bulk thermal conductivity and viscosity of a nanofluid with spherical nanoparticles. Nanoparticle properties.

 

Find: Values of the nanofluid thermal conductivity, density, specific heat, dynamic viscosity, and thermal diffusivity.

 

Assumptions:
1. Constant properties.
2. Density and specific heat are not affected by nanoscale phenomena.

 

Properties: Table A.6 (T = 300 K): Water; \(k_{bf}=0.613 W/m\cdot K,\rho _{bf}=997 kg/m^{3}, c_{p,bf}=4.179 kJ/kg\cdot K,\mu _{bf}=855\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2} .\)

 

Analysis: From the problem statement,

 

\(k_{nf}=\left [ \frac{k_{p}+2k_{bf}+2\varphi (k_{p}-k_{bf})}{k_{p}+2k_{bf}-\varphi (k_{p}-k_{bf})} \right ]k_{bf}\)

 

\(=\left [ \frac{36.0 W/m\cdot K+2\times 0.613 W/m\cdot K+2\times 0.05(36.0-0.613)W/m\cdot K}{36.0W/m\cdot K+2\times 0.613W/m\cdot K-0.05(36.0-0.613)W/m\cdot K} \right ]\)

 

× 0.613 W/m ⋅ K

 

= 0.705 W/m ⋅ K

 

Consider the control volume shown in the schematic to be of total volume V. Then the average density of the nanofluid, \(ρ_{nf}\), is equal to the total mass within the volume divided by the volume:

 

\(\rho _{nf}=\left [ \rho _{bf}V(1-\varphi ) +\rho _{p}V\varphi \right ]/V\)

 

or,

 

\(\rho_{nf}=997kg/m^{3}\times (1-0.05)+3970kg/m^{3}\times 0.05=1146kg/m^{3}\)

 

Similarly, the average specific heat can be defined as the total energy stored per unit temperature difference divided by the mass:

 

\(c_{p,nf}=\left [ \rho _{bf}V(1-\varphi ) c_{p,bf}+\rho _{p}V\varphi c_{p,p}\right ]/(\rho _{nf}V)\)

 

which yields

 

\(c_{p,nf}=\frac{997kg/m^{3}\times 4.179 kJ/kg\cdot K(1-0.05)+3970 kg/m^{3}\times 0.765 kJ/kg\cdot K\times (0.05)}{1146 kg/m^{3}}\)

 

= 3.587 kJ/kg ⋅ K

 

From the problem statement, the dynamic viscosity of the nanofluid is

 

\(\mu _{nf}=855\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}\times (1+2.5\times 0.05)=962\times 10^{-6}N\cdot s/m^{2}\)

 

The nanofluid’s thermal diffusivity is

 

\(\alpha _{nf}=\frac{k_{nf}}{\rho _{nf}c_{p,nf}}=\frac{0.705W/m\cdot K}{1146 kg/m^{3}\times 3587 J/kg\cdot K}=171\times 10^{-9}m^{2}/s\)

 

Comments:
1. The ratio of the nanofluid thermal conductivity to the base fluid (water) thermal conductivity is

 

\(\frac{k_{nf}}{k_{bf}}=\frac{0.705W/m\cdot K}{0.613W/m\cdot K}=1.150\)

 

Similarly, \(\rho _{nf}/\rho _{bf}=1.149,c_{p,nf}/c_{p,bf}=0.858,\mu _{nf}/\mu _{bf}=1.130\), and \(\alpha _{nf}/\alpha _{bf}= 1.166\). The relatively large thermal conductivity and thermal diffusivity of the nanofluid enhance heat transfer rates in some applications. However, all of the thermophysical properties are affected by the addition of the nanoparticles, and, as will become evident in Chapters 6 through 9, properties such as the viscosity and specific heat are adversely affected. This condition can degrade thermal performance when the use of nanofluids involves convection heat transfer.

2. The expression for the nanofluid’s thermal conductivity (and viscosity) is limited to dilute mixtures of noncontacting, spherical particles. In some cases, the particles do not remain separated but can agglomerate into long chains, providing effective paths for heat conduction through the fluid and larger bulk thermal conductivities. Hence, the expression for the thermal conductivity represents the minimum possible enhancement of the thermal conductivity by spherical nanoparticles. An expression for the maximum possible isotropic thermal conductivity of a nanofluid, corresponding to agglomeration of the spherical particles, is available [21], as are expressions for dilute suspensions of nonspherical particles [22]. Note that these expressions can also be applied to nanostructured composite materials consisting of a particulate phase interspersed within a host binding medium, as will be discussed in more detail in Chapter 3.

3. The nanofluid’s density and specific heat are averaged values. As such, these properties do not depend on the manner in which the nanoparticles are dispersed within the base liquid.
