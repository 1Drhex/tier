Guest Question.

A rubber membrane, 0.5 mm thick, separates hydrogen and oxygen gases. The hydrogen is at 25 C and 2 atm pressure and the oxygen is at 25 C and 0.9 atm pressure. What are the initial rates of mass flux of the hydrogen and oxygen through the membrane?

Admin
Solution

The molar flow through the membrane is given by Eq. (11.43):

For flat walls : \(\dot{n} _{A} =\frac{D_{AB} A}{L}\left(C_{A_{1} } -C_{A_{2} }\right)\) (11.43)

 

\(\dot{n} _{A} =\frac{D_{AB} A}{L}\left(C_{A_{1} } -C_{A_{2} }\right)\) (11.49)

 

The diffusion coefficients are in Table 11.5 .

Table 11.5 Binary diffusion coefficient DAB in solids.

\(H_{2}\) \(Si O_{2}\) 500 1.3\(\times 10^{-12} \) N Fe 1100 4\(\times 10^{-11} \)

For the \(H_{2}- rubber system\) , we have \(D_{AB}=10.2\times 10^{-10} m^{2}/s\) , and for the \(O{2}- rubber system\) , we have \(D_{AB}=1.58\times 10^{-10} m^{2}/s\) .

The concentrations of the gases in the membrane at the left and right boundaries can be determined from the solubilities of the gases and the pressure of the gases using Eq. (11.46) :

\(C_{A,solid- side} =SP_{A,gas -side}\) (11.46)

 

\(C_{A,solid- side} =SP_{A,gas -side}\) (11.50)

 

For the hydrogen on the left side of the membrane (location 1) : \(S=2.1\times 10^{-3} kmol/m^{3}bar\) from Table 11.6

Table 11.6 Solubility of gasesolid combinations.
Gas Solid T(K) S \((kmol/m^{3}bar )\)
\(O_{2}\) Rubber 298 3.12\(\times 10^{-3}\)
\(N_{2}\) Rubber 298 1.56\(\times 10^{-3}\)
\(H_{2}\) Rubber 298 2.1\(\times 10^{-3}\)
\(CO_{2}\) Rubber 298 4.015\(\times 10^{-2}\)
He \(SiO_{2}\) 293 4.5\(\times 10^{-4}\)
\(H_{2}\) Ni 358 9.01\(\times 10^{-3}\)

 

and the partial pressure of the hydrogen is \(2 atm\times \left(101.325kPa/atm\right) \times \left(1 bar/100kPa\right) =2.0265 bar\) . Hence, from Eq. (11.50), \(C_{H_{2} }=0.00426kmol/m^{3}\) at location 1. On the right side of the membrane (location 2), \(C_{H_{2} }=0\) .

For the oxygen on the right side of the membrane (location 2): \(S=3.12\times 10^{-3} kmol/m^{3}bar\) from Table 11.6 and the partial pressure of the oxygen is \(0.9atm\times \left(101.325kPa/atm\right) \times \left(1 bar/100kPa\right) =0.9119 bar\) . Hence, from Eq. (11.50), \(C_{O_{2} }=0.00285kmol/m^{3}\) . On the left side of the membrane (location 1), \(C_{O_{2} }=0\) .

The molar fluxes of the gases through the membrane are, from Eq. (11.49),

\(\frac{\dot{n} _{H_{2} } }{A} =\frac{D_{AB} }{L}\left(C_{H_{2}\mid _{1} } -C_{H_{2}\mid _{2} } \right) =\frac{10.2\times 10^{-10} }{0.0005} \left(0.00426-0\right) =8.69\times 10^{-9} kmol/sm^{2}\)

 

\(\frac{\dot{n} _{O_{2} } }{A} =\frac{D_{AB} }{L}\left(C_{O_{2}\mid _{1} } -C_{O_{2}\mid _{2} } \right) =\frac{1.58\times 10^{-10} }{0.0005} \left(0-0.00285\right) =-9.01\times 10^{-10} kmol/sm^{2}\)

 

Multiplying these results by the respective molecular weights of the hydrogen and oxygen, we get the mass fluxes of the gases :

\(\frac{\dot{m} _{H_{2} } }{A} =\frac{\dot{n} _{H_{2} } }{A}M_{H_{2} }=\left(8.69\times 10^{-9}kmol/sm^{2} \right) \left(2kgH_{2}/kmolH_{2} \right) =1.74\times 10^{-8} kg/sm^{2}\)

 

\(\frac{\dot{m} _{O_{2} } }{A} =\frac{\dot{n} _{O_{2} } }{A}M_{O_{2} }=\left(-9.01\times 10^{-10}kmol/sm^{2} \right) \left(32kgO_{2}/kmolO_{2} \right) =-2.88\times 10^{-8} kg/sm^{2}\)

 

The initial hydrogen mass flux through the membrane is to the right at \(1.74\times 10^{-8} kg/s\) per \(m^{2}\) of membrane.

The initial oxygen mass flux through the membrane is to the left at \(2.88\times 10^{-8} kg/s\) per \(m^{2}\) of membrane.
