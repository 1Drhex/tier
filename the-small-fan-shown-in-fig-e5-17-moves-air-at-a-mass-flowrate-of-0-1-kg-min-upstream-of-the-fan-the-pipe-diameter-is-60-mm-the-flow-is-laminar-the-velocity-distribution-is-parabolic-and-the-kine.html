Guest Question.

The small fan shown in Fig. E5.17 moves air at a mass flowrate of 0.1 kg/min. Upstream of the fan, the pipe diameter is 60 mm, the flow is laminar, the velocity distribution is parabolic, and the kinetic energy coefficient, \(\alpha _{1}\), is equal to 2.0. Downstream of the fan, the pipe diameter is 30 mm, the flow is turbulent, the velocity profile is quite uniform, and the kinetic energy coefficient, \(\alpha _{2}\), is equal to 1.08. The rise in static pressure across the fan is 0.1 kPa, and the fan motor draws 0.14 W.
FIND Compare the value of loss calculated
(a) assuming uniform velocity distributions, and
(b) considering actual velocity distributions.

Admin
Solution

Application of \(\frac{p_{out}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{out}\overline{V}^{2}_{out} }{2}+gz_{out} =\frac{p_{in}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{in}\overline{V}^{2}_{in} }{2}+gz_{in}+w_{shaft net in}-loss\) to the contents of the control volume shown in Fig. E5.17 leads to

\(gz_{2}=gz_{1}=0\)( change in gz is negligible )

\(\frac{p_{2}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{2}\overline{V}^{2}_{2} }{2}+gz_{2} =\frac{p_{1}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{1}\overline{V}^{2}_{1} }{2}+gz_{1}-loss+w_{shaft net in}\) (1)

or solving Eq. 1 for loss we get

\(loss=w_{shaft net in}-(\frac{p_{2}-p_{1}}{\rho } )+\frac{\alpha _{1}\overline{V}^{2}_{1} }{2}-\frac{\alpha _{2}\overline{V}^{2}_{2} }{2} \) (2)

To proceed further, we need values of \(w_{shaft net in}, \overline{V}_{1}\), and \(\overline{V}_{1}\).

These quantities can be obtained as follows. For shaft work

\(w_{shaft net in}=\frac{power to fan motor}{\dot{m} }\)

or

\(w_{shaft net in}=\frac{(0.14W)[1{({N\cdot m}/{s})}/{W}]}{0.1{kg}/{min}} (60{s}/{min})\)

so that

\(w_{shaft net in}=84.0{N\cdot m}/{kg}\) (3)

For the average velocity at section (1), \(\overline{V}_{1}\), from \(\dot{m}=\rho _{1}A_{1}V_{1}=\rho _{2}A_{2}V_{2}\) we obtain

\(\overline{V}_{1} =\frac{\dot{m} }{\rho A_{1}}=\frac{\dot{m}}{\rho ({\pi D^{2}_{1}}/{4})}=\frac{(0.1{kg}/{min})({1min}/{60s})(1000{mm}/{m})^{2}}{(1.23{kg}/{m^{3}})[{\pi (60mm)^{2}}/{4}]} =0.479{m}/{s}\) (4)

For the average velocity at section (2), \(\overline{V}_{2}\),

\(\overline{V}_{2} =\frac{(0.1{kg}/{min})({1min}/{60s})(1000{mm}/{m})^{2}}{(1.23{kg}/{m^{3}})[{\pi (30mm)^{2}}/{4}]} =1.92{m}/{s}\) (5)

(a) For the assumed uniform velocity profiles (\(\alpha _{1}=\alpha _{2}=1.0\)), Eq. 2 yields

\(loss=w_{shaft net in}-(\frac{p_{2}-p_{1}}{\rho } )+\frac{\overline{V}^{2}_{1} }{2}-\frac{\overline{V}^{2}_{2} }{2}\) (6)

Using Eqs. 3, 4, and 5 and the pressure rise given in the problem statement, Eq. 6 gives

\(loss=84.0\frac{N\cdot m}{kg}-\frac{(0.1kPa)(1000{Pa}/{kPa})(1{{N}/{m^{2}}}/{Pa})}{1.23 {kg}/{m^{3}}}+\frac{(0.479{m}/{s})^{2}}{2[{1(kg\cdot m)}/{(N\cdot s^{2})}]} -\frac{(1.92{m}/{s})^{2}}{2[{1(kg\cdot m)}/{(N\cdot s^{2})}]}\)

or

\(loss=84.0{N\cdot m}/{kg}-81.3{N\cdot m}/{kg}+0.115{N\cdot m}/{kg}-1.84{N\cdot m}/{kg}=0.975{N\cdot m}/{kg}\)

(b) For the actual velocity profiles (\(\alpha _{1}=2\),\(\alpha _{2}=1.08\)), Eq. 1 gives

\(loss=w_{shaft net in}-(\frac{p_{2}-p_{1}}{\rho } )+\alpha _{1}\frac{\overline{V}^{2}_{1} }{2}-\alpha _{2}\frac{\overline{V}^{2}_{2} }{2}\) (7)

If we use Eqs. 3, 4, and 5 and the given pressure rise, Eq. 7 yields

\(loss=84{N\cdot m}/{kg}-\frac{(0.1kPa)(1000{Pa}/{kPa})(1{{N}/{m^{2}}}/{Pa})}{1.23 {kg}/{m^{3}}}+\frac{2(0.479{m}/{s})^{2}}{2[{1(kg\cdot m)}/{(N\cdot s^{2})}]} -\frac{1.08(1.92{m}/{s})^{2}}{2[{1(kg\cdot m)}/{(N\cdot s^{2})}]}\)

or

\(loss=84.0{N\cdot m}/{kg}-81.3{N\cdot m}/{kg}+0.230{N\cdot m}/{kg}-1.99{N\cdot m}/{kg}=0.940 {N\cdot m}/{kg}\)

COMMENT The difference in loss calculated assuming uniform velocity profiles and actual velocity profiles is not large compared to \(w_{shaft net in}\) for this fluid flow situation.
