Guest Question.

Motion under a constant force. A particle of mass m is subject to a constant force F. If it starts from rest at the origin, at time t = 0, find its position (x), as a function of time.

Admin
Solution
\(\frac{dp}{dt} =F\Rightarrow p=Ft+constant,\)

but since p = 0 at t = 0, the constant must be zero, and hence

\(p=\frac{mu}{\sqrt{1-u^2/c^2} }=Ft. \)

Solving for u, we obtain

\(u=\frac{(F/m)t}{\sqrt{1-(Ft/mc)^2} }. \) (12.60)

The numerator, of course, is the classical answer—it’s approximately right, if \((F/m)t\ll c.\) But the relativistic denominator ensures that u never exceeds c; in fact, as t → ∞ , u → c.

To complete the problem we must integrate again:

\(x(t)=\frac{F}{m}\int_{0}^{t}{\frac{\acute{t}}{\sqrt{1+(F\acute{t}/mc)^2} } d\acute{t}} \\=\frac{mc^2}{F}\sqrt{1+(F\acute{t}/mc)^2} \mid ^t_0=\frac{mc^2}{F} \left[\sqrt{1+(Ft/mc)^2}-1 \right] . \) (12.61)

In place of the classical parabola, \(x(t) = (F/2m)t^2,\) the graph is a hyperbola (Fig. 12.30); for this reason, motion under a constant force is often called hyperbolic motion. It occurs, for example, when a charged particle is placed in a uniform electric field.
