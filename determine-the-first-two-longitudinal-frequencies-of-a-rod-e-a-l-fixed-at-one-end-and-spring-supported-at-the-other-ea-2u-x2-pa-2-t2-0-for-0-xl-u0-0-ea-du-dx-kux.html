Guest Question.

Determine the first two longitudinal frequencies of a rod (E, A, L) fixed at one end and spring-supported at the other:
\(-EA\frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial x^{2}} +\rho A\frac{\partial^{2}u}{\partial t^{2}}=0 \) for 0 <x<L
u(0) = 0, \(\left(EA\frac{du}{dx}+ku \right)|_{x=L} =0 \)
Use (a) two linear finite elements and (b) one quadratic element.

 

Admin
Solution

Note that the problem at hand is a hyperbolic equation. Hence, the eigenvalue is the square of the natural frequency of axial vibration, \(\omega \).

(a) For the mesh of two linear elements, the assembled equations of the eigenvalue problem are:

\(\left(\frac{EA}{h}\left[\begin{matrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right] -\omega ^{2}\frac{\rho Ah}{6}\left[\begin{matrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right] \right)\left\{\begin{matrix} U_{1} \\ U_{2} \\ U_{3} \end{matrix} \right\} =\left\{\begin{matrix} Q_{1}^{1} \\ Q_{2}^{1}+Q_{1}^{2} \\ Q_{2}^{2} \end{matrix} \right\} \)

where h = L/2. The boundary conditions are: \(U_{1} =0\) and \(Q^{2}_{2} =-kU_{3}\). It is clear that the term \(kU_{3}\) , when taken to the left side, should add to the second diagonal term of the stiffness matrix (because \(kU_{3}\) does not contain \(\lambda \) in order to add it to the mass matrix). The condensed equations are given by

\(\left(\frac{EA}{h}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1+c \end{bmatrix}-\omega ^{2} \frac{\rho Ah}{6} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \right)\left\{\begin{matrix} U_{2} \\ U_{3} \end{matrix} \right\} =\left\{\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right\} \)

where \(c=\frac{kh}{EA} \) . Setting the determinant of the coefficient matrix to zero, we obtain the characteristic polyniomial,

\(7\bar{\lambda }^{2}-(10+4c)\overline{\lambda }+(1+2c) =0 \) where \(\overline{\lambda }=\frac{\rho h^{2}}{6E}\cdot \omega ^{2} \)

This gives two roots, which are the two eigenvalues. The natural frequncies are obtained from

\(\omega _{i}=\frac{1}{h}\sqrt{\frac{6E\overline{\lambda }_{i} }{\rho } } \) , i = 1, 2

(b) For the mesh of one quadratic element, the equations of the eigenvalue problem are:

\(\left(\frac{EA}{3h}\left[\begin{matrix}7 & -8 & 1 \\ -8 & 16 & -8 \\ 1 & -8 & 7 \end{matrix} \right]-\omega ^{2} \frac{\rho Ah}{30} \left[\begin{matrix} 4 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 16 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 & 4 \end{matrix} \right] \right)\left\{\begin{matrix} U_{1} \\ U_{2} \\U_{3} \end{matrix} \right\}=\left\{\begin{matrix} Q^{1}_{1} \\ Q^{1}_{2} \\ Q^{1}_{3} \end{matrix} \right\} \)

where h = L. The boundary conditions are: \(U_{1}=0\) and \(Q_{3}^{1}=-kU_{3}\) . The condensed equations are given by

\(\left(\frac{EA}{3h}\begin{bmatrix} 16 & -8 \\ -8 & 7+c \end{bmatrix} -\omega ^{2} \frac{\rho Ah}{30}\begin{bmatrix} 16 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \right)\left\{\begin{matrix} U_{2} \\ U_{3} \end{matrix} \right\}=\left\{\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right\} \)

where \(c=\frac{3kh}{EA}\) . Setting the determinant of the coefficient matrix to zero, we obtain the characteristic polyniomial,

\(15\bar{\lambda }^{2} -(52+4c)\bar{\lambda } +(12+4c)=0\) , where \(\bar{\lambda }=\frac{\rho h^{2}}{10E}\cdot \omega ^{2} \)

This gives two roots, and the natural frequncies are obtained from

\(\omega _{i}=\frac{1}{h} \sqrt{\frac{10E\bar{\lambda }_{i} }{\rho } } \) , i=1,2.
