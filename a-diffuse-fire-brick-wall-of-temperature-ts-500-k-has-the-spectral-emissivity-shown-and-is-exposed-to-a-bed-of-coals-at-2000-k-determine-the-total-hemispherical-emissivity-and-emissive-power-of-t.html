Guest Question.

A diffuse, fire brick wall of temperature \(T_{s}=500 K\) has the spectral emissivity shown and is exposed to a bed of coals at 2000 K. Determine the total, hemispherical emissivity and emissive power of the fire brick wall. What is the total absorptivity of the wall to irradiation resulting from emission by the coals?

Admin
Solution

Known: Brick wall of surface temperature \(T_{s}=500 K\) and prescribed \(\varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda)\) is exposed to coals at \(T_{c}=2000 K \text { . }\)

Find:
1. Total, hemispherical emissivity of the fire brick wall.
2. Total emissive power of the brick wall.
3. Absorptivity of the wall to irradiation from the coals.

Schematic:

Assumptions:
1. Brick wall is opaque and diffuse.
2. Spectral distribution of irradiation at the brick wall approximates that due to emission
from a blackbody at 2000 K.

Analysis:
1. The total, hemispherical emissivity follows from Equation 12.43.

 

\(\varepsilon(T)=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda, T) E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, T) d \lambda}{E_{b}(T)}\) (12.43)

 

\(\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right)=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda) E_{\lambda, b}\left(\lambda, T_{s}\right) d \lambda}{E_{b}\left(T_{s}\right)}\)

 

Breaking the integral into parts,

 

\(\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right)=\varepsilon_{\lambda, 1} \frac{\int_{0}^{\lambda_{1}} E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_{b}}+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 2} \frac{\int_{\lambda_{1}}^{\lambda_{2}} E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_{b}}+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 3} \frac{\int_{\lambda_{2}}^{\infty} E_{\lambda, b} d \lambda}{E_{b}}\)

 

and introducing the blackbody functions, it follows that

 

\(\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right)=\varepsilon_{\lambda, 1} F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 2}\left[F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}\right]+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 3}\left[1-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}\right]\)

\(\lambda_{1} T_{s}=1.5 \mu m \times 500 K =750 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}=0.000\)

 

\(\lambda_{2} T_{s}=10 \mu m \times 500 K =5000 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}=0.634\)

Hence

\(\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right)=0.1 \times 0+0.5 \times 0.634+0.8(1-0.634)=0.610\)

 

2. From Equations 12.32 and 12.36 , the total emissive power is

 

\(E_{b}=\sigma T^{4}\) (12.32)

 

\(\varepsilon(T) \equiv \frac{E(T)}{E_{b}(T)}\) (12.36)

 

\(E\left(T_{s}\right)=\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right) E_{b}\left(T_{s}\right)=\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right) \sigma T_{s}^{4}\)

 

\(E\left(T_{s}\right)=0.61 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} W / m ^{2} \cdot K ^{4}(500 K )^{4}=2162 W / m ^{2}\)

 

3. From Equation 12.52, the total absorptivity of the wall to radiation from the coals is

 

\(\alpha=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \alpha_{\lambda}(\lambda) G_{\lambda}(\lambda) d \lambda}{\int_{0}^{\infty} G_{\lambda}(\lambda) d \lambda}\) (12.52)

 

Since the surface is diffuse, \(\alpha_{\lambda}(\lambda)=\varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda)\). Moreover, since the spectral distribution of the irradiation approximates that due to emission from a blackbody at 2000 K, \(G_{\lambda}(\lambda) \propto E_{\lambda, b}\left(\lambda, T_{c}\right)\). It follows that

 

\(\alpha=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda) E_{\lambda, b}\left(\lambda, T_{c}\right) d \lambda}{\int_{0}^{\infty} E_{\lambda, b}\left(\lambda, T_{c}\right) d \lambda}\)

 

Breaking the integral into parts and introducing the blackbody functions, we then obtain

 

\(\alpha=\varepsilon_{\lambda, 1} F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 2}\left[F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}\right]+\varepsilon_{\lambda, 3}\left[1-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}\right]\)

 

From Table 12.2

 

\(\lambda_{1} T_{c}=1.5 \mu m \times 2000 K =3000 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}=0.273\)

 

\(\lambda_{2} T_{c}=10 \mu m \times 2000 K =20,000 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{2}\right)}=0.986\)

Hence

\(\alpha=0.1 \times 0.273+0.5(0.986-0.273)+0.8(1-0.986)=0.395\)

 

Comments:
1. The emissivity depends on the surface temperature \(T_{s}\), while the absorptivity depends
on the spectral distribution of the irradiation, which depends on the temperature of the source \(T_{c}\).

2. The surface is not gray, \(\alpha \neq \varepsilon\). This result is to be expected. Since emission is associated with \(T_{s}=500 K\), its spectral maximum occurs at \(\lambda_{\max } \approx 6 \mu m\). In contrast, since irradiation is associated with emission from a source at \(T_{c}=2000 K\), its spectral maximum occurs at \(\lambda_{\max } \approx 1.5 \mu m\). Even though \(\varepsilon_{\lambda} \text { and } \alpha_{\lambda}\) are equal, because they are not constant over the spectral ranges of both emission and irradiation, \(\alpha \neq \varepsilon\). For the prescribed spectral distribution of \(\varepsilon_{\lambda}=\alpha_{\lambda}, \varepsilon \text { and } \alpha\) decrease with increasing \(T_{s} \text { and } T_{c}\), respectively, and it is only for \(T_{s}=T_{c} \text { that } \varepsilon=\alpha\).
