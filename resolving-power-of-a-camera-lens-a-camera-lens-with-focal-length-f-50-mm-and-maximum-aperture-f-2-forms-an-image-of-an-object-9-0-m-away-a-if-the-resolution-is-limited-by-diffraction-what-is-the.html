Guest Question.

RESOLVING POWER OF A CAMERA LENS

A camera lens with focal length \(f\) = 50 mm and maximum aperture \(f\)/2 forms an image of an object 9.0 m away. (a) If the resolution is limited by diffraction, what is the minimum distance between two points on the object that are barely resolved? What is the corresponding distance between image points? (b) How does the situation change if the lens is “stopped down” to \(f\)/16? Use λ = 500 nm in both cases.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

This example uses the ideas about resolving power, image formation by a lens (Section 34.4), and \(f\)-number (Section 34.5). From Eq. (34.20), the \(f\)-number of a lens is its focal length \(f\) divided by the aperture diameter \(D\). We use this equation to determine \(D\) and then use Eq. (36.17) (the Rayleigh criterion) to find the angular separation θ between two barely resolved points on the object. We then use the geometry of image formation by a lens to determine the distance \(y\) between those points and the distance \(y^{\prime}\) between the corresponding image points.

\(f \text {-number of a lens }=\frac{f}{D}\) (34.2)

\(\sin \theta_{1}=1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}\) (36.17)

EXECUTE:

(a) The aperture diameter is \(D = f/(f-number)\) = (50 mm)/2 = 25 mm = 25 × \(10^{-3}\)m. From Eq. (36.17) the angular separation θ of two object points that are barely resolved is

\(\theta \approx \sin \theta=1.22 \frac{\lambda}{D}=1.22 \frac{500 \times 10^{-9} \mathrm{~m}}{25 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~m}}=2.4 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{rad}\)

We know from our thin-lens analysis in Section 34.4 that, apart from sign, \(y / s=y^{\prime} / s^{\prime}\), [see Eq. (34.14)]. Thus the angular separations of the object points and the corresponding image points are both equal to θ. Because the object distance \(s\) is much greater than the focal length \(f\) = 50 mm, the image distance \(s^{\prime}\) is approximately equal to \(f\). Thus

\(\begin{array}{rlrl}\frac{y}{9.0 \mathrm{~m}} & =2.4 \times 10^{-5} & y & =2.2 \times 10^{-4}\mathrm{~m}=0.22 \mathrm{~mm} \\\frac{y^{\prime}}{50 \mathrm{~mm}} & =2.4 \times 10^{-5} &y^{\prime}& =1.2 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~mm} \\& =0.0012 \mathrm{~mm} & \approx \frac{1}{800} \mathrm{~mm}\end{array}\)

(b) The aperture diameter is now (50 mm)/16, or one-eighth as large as before. The angular separation between barely resolved points is eight times as great, and the values of \(y\) and \(y^{\prime}\) are also eight times as great as before:

\(y=1.8 \mathrm{~mm} \quad y^{\prime}=0.0096 \mathrm{~mm}=\frac{1}{100} \mathrm{~mm}\)

Only the best camera lenses can approach this resolving power.

 

EVALUATE: Many photographers use the smallest possible aperture for maximum sharpness, since lens aberrations cause light rays that are far from the optic axis to converge to a different image point than do rays near the axis. But as this example shows, diffraction effects become more significant at small apertures. One cause of fuzzy images has to be balanced against another.
