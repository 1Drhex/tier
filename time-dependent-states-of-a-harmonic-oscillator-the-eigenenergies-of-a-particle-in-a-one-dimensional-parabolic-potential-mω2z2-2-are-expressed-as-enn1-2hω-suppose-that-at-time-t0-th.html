Guest Question.

Time-dependent states of a harmonic oscillator The eigenenergies of a particle in a one-dimensional parabolic potential \(m^{*}\omega ^{2}z^{2}/2\) are expressed as \(E_{n}=\left ( n+\frac{1}{2} \right )h\omega\) Suppose that at time t = 0, the particle has equal probability of being in the ith and jth eigenstate and zero probability of being in any other state. Derive an expression for the time dependence of the velocity v(t).

Admin
Solution

At any time, the wave function of the electron is a linear superposition of all eigenstates and hence can be written as
\(\psi \left ( z,t \right )=\sum_{n}C_{n}\left ( t \right )\phi _{n} \left ( z \right )\) .

The probability of finding the electron in the nth eigenstate at time t is \(|C_{n}\left ( t \right )|^{2}\). Obviously, \(\sum _{n}|C_{n}\left ( t \right )|^{2}=1\), at all t, for the sake of normalization (the electron has unit probability of being in a mixture of one or more allowed states at all times).
The time-dependent Schrodinger equation is
\(ih\frac{\partial \psi \left ( z,t \right )}{\partial t}=H\psi \left ( z,t \right )\),

where
\(H=-\frac{h^{2}}{2m^{*}}\frac{d^{2}}{dz^{2}}+\frac{1}{2}m^{*}\omega ^{2}z^{2}\).

we get
\(ih\sum_{n}\frac{\partial C_{n}\left ( t \right )}{\partial }\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )=\sum_{n}C_{n}\left ( t \right )H\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )=\sum_{n}C_{n}\left ( t \right )E_{n}\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )\),

where we have used the fact that H is time independent, and we have also made use of the time-independent Schrodinger equation \(H\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )=E_{n}\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )\).

Multiplying the above equation throughout by \(\phi _{n}^{*}\left ( z \right )\) and integrating over all z, we get \(i \hbar \sum_{n} \frac{\partial C_{n}(t)}{\partial t} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \phi_{m}^{*}(z) \phi_{n}(z) \mathrm{d} z=\sum_{n} C_{n}(t) E_{n} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \phi_{m}^{*}(z) \phi_{n}(z)\)

Since the \(\phi \)are eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian, which is always a Hermitian operator, they are orthonormal, meaning \( \int_{-\infty }^{+\infty }\phi _{m}^{*}\left ( z \right )\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )dz=\delta _{m,n}\), where the delta is the Kronecker delta, i.e., it is 1 when m = n and 0 otherwise. Therefore, the last equation reduces to
\(ih\sum_{n}\frac{\partial C_{n}\left ( t \right )}{\partial t}\delta _{m,n}=\sum_{n}C_{n}\left ( t \right )E_{n}\delta _{m,n}\).

The Kronecker delta removes the summation since only one term (corresponding to m = n) will survive in the sum and all the other terms will vanish. This means,
\(ih\frac{\partial C_{m}\left ( t \right )}{\partial t}=C_{m}\left ( t \right )E_{m}\).

The solution of the last equation is
\(C_{m}\left ( t \right )=C_{m}\left ( 0 \right )e^{-\frac{1E_{m}t}{h}}\).

we obtain
\(\psi \left ( z,t \right )=\sum_{n}e^{-iE_{n}t/h}C_{n}\left ( 0 \right )\phi _{n}\left ( z \right )\).

The problem states that at time t = 0, there was equal probability of being in the ith and jth state and zero probability of being in any other state. Hence \(C_{i}\left ( o \right )=C_{j}\left ( o \right )=1/\sqrt{2}\) and all the other C(0)s are zero. Substituting these results
we get ,
\(\psi \left ( z,t \right )=e^{-\frac{1E_{i}}{h}}C_{i}\left ( 0 \right )\phi _{i}\left ( z \right )+e^{-\frac{1E_{j}}{h}}C_{j}\left ( 0 \right )\phi _{j}\left ( z \right ) =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}[e^{-\frac{1E_{i}}{h}}\phi _{i}\left ( z \right )+e^{-\frac{1E_{j}}{h}}\phi _{j}\left ( z \right )]\).

Hence,
\(\langle z\rangle(t)= \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \psi^{*}(z, t) z \psi(z, t) \mathrm{d} z \)

\(= \frac{1}{2} \int _{\infty}^{+\infty} \left[e^{\frac {i E_{i} t}{\bar{h}}} \phi _{i}^{*}(z) + e^{\frac{i E_{j}t}{\bar{h}}} \phi _{j}^{*}(z)\right] z \left[\mathrm{e}^{-\frac{i E_{i} t}{\bar{h}}} \phi _{i}(z)+\mathrm{e}^{-\frac{i E_{j} t}{\bar{h}}} \phi _{j}(z)\right] \mathrm{d} z \)

\(= \frac{1}{2}\left[\int _{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi _{i}^{*}(z) z \phi _{i}(z) dz+\int _{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi _{j}^{*}(z) z \phi _{j}(z) dz\right]\).

\(+e^{i\left(E_{i}-E_{j}\right) t / \bar{h}} \int _{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi _{i}^{*}(z) z \phi _{j}(z) dz +e^{i\left(E_{j}-E_{i}\right) t\bar{h}} \int _{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi _{j}^{*}(z) z \phi _{i}(z)dz ]\)

\(=\int _{-\infty }^{+\infty }\psi ^{*}\left ( z,t \right )z\psi \left ( z,t \right )dz[\frac{e^{i\left ( E_{i}-E_{j} \right )t/h}+e^{-i\left ( E_{i}-E_{j} \right )t/h}}{2}]\),

where we have used the fact that in any eigenstate , \(\left \langle z \right \rangle= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi_{i}^{*}(z) z \phi_{i}(z) \mathrm{d} z=0\) and \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi_{i}^{*}(z) z \phi_{j}(z) \mathrm{d} z= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi_{j}^{*}(z) z \phi_{i}(z) \mathrm{d}z\) since the operator z is Hermitian .

Therefore ,
\(\left \langle z \right \rangle\left ( t \right )=\int_{-\infty }^{+\infty }dz\phi _{i}^{*}\left ( z \right )z\phi _{j}\left ( z \right )\cos [\left ( i-j \right )\omega t]\),
since \(E_{n}=\left ( n+\frac{1}{2} \right )h\omega\).
If i=j+1, then
\(\int_{-\infty }^{+\infty }dz\phi _{i}^{*}\left ( z \right )z\phi _{j}\left ( z,t \right )=\sqrt{\frac{i}{2}\frac{h}{m^{*}\omega }}\),

and
\(\left \langle z \right \rangle\left ( t \right )=\sqrt{\frac{i}{2}\frac{h}{m^{*}\omega }}\cos \left ( \omega t \right )\).

If i = j − 1, then
\(\int_{-\infty }^{+\infty }dz\phi _{i}^{*}\left ( z \right )z\phi _{j}\left ( z \right )=\sqrt{\frac{j}{2}\frac{h}{m^{*}\omega }}\),

and
\(\left \langle z \right \rangle\left ( t \right )=\sqrt{\frac{j}{2}\frac{h}{m^{*}\omega }}\cos \left ( \omega t \right )\).
In all the other cases, z (t) = 0.
Therefore,

\( \upsilon \left ( t \right )=\frac{d\left \langle z \right \rangle \left ( t \right )}{dt} =\left\{\begin{matrix} -\sqrt{\frac{ih\omega }{2m^{\ast }}} & for i = j\pm 1 \\ 0 & otherwise \end{matrix}\right .\)

for \(i=j\pm 1 0\) otherwise .

The physical interpretation of this result is that classical simple harmonic motion, where the velocity of the oscillator varies sinusoidally with time, can be initiated by placing the quantum mechanical oscillator in a mixture of two adjacent eigenstates.
