Guest Question.

Subband population in a cylindrical quantum wire.

Consider a cylindrical InSb nanowire (or quantum wire) of diameter 50 nm. The effective mass of electrons in InSb \((m^{\ast}) \) is 0.0145 times the free electron mass of 9.1 × 10−31 kg. Find the energies of the three lowest eigenstates (also known as subbands) in the conduction band of the nanowire. What fraction of the electrons in the wire occupies the lowest subband at room temperature if the Fermi level is 100 \(\mu \) eV above the lowest subband?

Admin
Solution

We have to first solve the time-independent Schrodinger equation in cylindrical coordinates:

\(– \frac{h^{2}}{2m^{\ast}} \left [\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial }{\partial r} \left (r \frac{\partial \psi (r, \theta , z)}{ \partial r } \right )+ \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial ^{2} \psi (r, \theta , z)}{\partial \theta ^{2}}+ \frac{\partial ^{2} \psi (r, \theta , z)}{ \partial z^{2}} \right ]\)

 

\(+ V (r , \theta ,z ) \psi (r , \theta ,z) = E \psi (r , \theta ,z) \)

where \(\psi (r , \theta ,z) \) is the wave function, r is the radial coordinate, \(\theta\) is the polar coordinate, and z is the axial coordinate (along the nanowire axis).

The solution of this equation subject to the condition \(V (r , \theta ,z ) = 0 \) for r ≤ D/2 and \(V (r , \theta ,z ) = \infty\) for r > D/2 (D is the wire diameter) is [12]

\(\psi _{m,n,k_{z}} (r , \theta ,z) = NJ_{m} ( k_{m, n} r) e ^{im \theta} e^{i k_{z}z} \)

where \(J_{m} \) is the Bessel function of the mth order, N is a normalization constant, and \(k_{z} \) is the wave vector along the nanowire axis. The energy eigenstates are given by

\(E_{m,n} =\frac{h^{2}}{2m^{\ast}} ( k_{m, n}^{2} + k_{z}^{2}) \) (3.184)

The boundary condition dictates that the wave function vanishes at r = D/2. This means that we will have to set \(k_{0,n}D/2 \) equal to the zeros of the Bessel function for m = 0 to find the allowed values of \(k_{0,n}. \) For D = 50 nm, we get

\(k_{0,1} = 9.6 × 10^{7} m ^{-1} \)
\(k_{0,2} = 2.2 × 10^{8} m ^{-1} \)
and
\(k_{0,3} = 3.5 × 10^{8} m ^{-1} \)

The subband energy bottoms are found by setting \(k_{z} \) = 0 in Equation (3.184). This yields the energies of the three lowest subbands as
\(E_{0،1} =\frac{h^{2} k_{0,1}^{2} }{2m^{\ast}} = 0.9 k_{B} T \)

\(E_{0،2} =\frac{h^{2} k_{0,2}^{2} }{2m^{\ast}}= 4.8 k_{B} T \)

and
\(E_{0،3} =\frac{h^{2} k_{0,3}^{2} }{2m^{\ast}} = 12.1 k_{B} T \)

where \(k_{B} T \)is the thermal energy at room temperature, i.e., \(4.186 × 10^{−21} joules\). The probability of an electron occupying any energy state (at equilibrium) is given by the Fermi-Dirac occupation probability \(1/[e^{(E−E_{F})/k_{B} T} + 1] \), where \(E_{F} \) is

the Fermi energy, which is 100 \(\mu \) eV or \(3.8 × 10^{-3} k_{B} T\) above the lowest subband energy.

The probabilities of finding electrons in the first three energy levels are deter- mined by the Fermi level placement, which yields

\(E_{0،1} – E_{F}= −3.8 × 10^{−3} k_{B} T\)

 

\(E_{0،2} – E_{F} = 3.8962 k_{B} T \)

and

\(E_{0،3} – E_{F} = 11.1962 k_{B} T \)

The corresponding occupation probabilities are given by

\(p(E_{0،1}) = \frac{1}{ e ^{-3.8× 10^{-3}} +1}= 0.5,\)

 

\(p(E_{0،2}) = \frac{1}{ e ^{3.896} +1}= 0.02, \)

and

\(p(E_{0،3}) = \frac{1}{ e ^{11.196} +1}= 1.37 × 10^{-5}. \)

We are interested only in the electrons in the conduction band and seek to find the relative subband populations in the conduction band alone. Therefore, the fraction of electrons in the lowest subband is given by (neglecting the contributions of the fourth and higher subbands):

\(f(E_{0،1}) = \frac{p(E_{0،1})}{ p(E_{0،1})+ p(E_{0،2}) + p(E_{0،3})} =0.96. \)

Therefore, 96% of the electrons in the InSb nanowire occupy the lowest subband at room temperature.
