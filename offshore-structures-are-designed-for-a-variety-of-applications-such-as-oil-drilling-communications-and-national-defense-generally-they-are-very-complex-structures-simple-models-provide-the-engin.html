Guest Question.

Offshore structures are designed for a variety of applications, such as oil drilling, communications, and national
defense. Generally, they are very complex structures. Simple models provide the engineer with an approximation
of how the actual structure will behave. From this basic understanding, the engineer can proceed with more advanced and realistic mathematical models. Formulate a two degree-of-freedom model of an offshore structure and use the stiffness-flexibility approach to derive the equations of motion.

Admin
Solution

Solution: Consider the two degree-of-freedom model, shown in Figure 6.6, of a tower that oscillates transversely. 7 Coordinate \(x\) is in the direction along the axis of the tower and coordinate \(y\) is in the transverse direction. In this model, half of the mass of the tower is lumped at the top. (The other half of the mass is lumped at the bottom and is incorporated into the foundation.) For the purposes of this example, the tower is fixed at the bottom. The lumped mass at the top of the tower can translate and rotate as the oscillation progresses. We neglect the axial extension of the tower and assume that the lumped mass remains essentially at height \(L\) from the base. In reality, the lumped mass decreases in height as θ increases. However, for linear oscillation, θ is small and the position of \(m\) remains along the horizontal line. If the tower oscillates in a fluid such as water, then the added mass of the water that oscillates with the structure is similarly lumped at the ends. (The concept of added mass was discussed in Chapter 2.) Two coordinates are needed to delineate this oscillation, a translation \(y(t)\) and a rotation \(θ(t)\). From the free-body diagram for the lumped mass in Figure 6.6, the forces acting on the

mass can be summed to obtain the rectilinear equation of motion using Newton’s second law of motion,

 

\(m \ddot{y}+c_{y} \dot{y}+Q(t)=P(t)\)

 

and the moments acting on the mass about the base can be summed to obtain the rotational equation of motion,

 

\(J \ddot{\theta}+c_{\theta} \dot{\theta}+M(t)=0\)

 

where \(c_{y}\) and \(c_{θ}\) are the viscous damping coefficients in the two directions, \(J\) is the mass moment of inertia for the lumped mass about the base, \(Q(t)\) is the shear force between the lumped mass and the tower, \(M(t)\) is the internal moment between the lumped mass and the tower, and \(P(t)\) is the externally applied force on the tower. If there is an external moment acting on the tower, it would appear on the right-hand side of the moment equation of motion.

The equations of motion can be written as

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left[\begin{array}{cc}m & 0 \\0 & J\end{array}\right] &\left\{\begin{array}{c}\ddot{y} \\\ddot{\theta}\end{array}\right\}+\left[\begin{array}{cc}c_{y} & 0 \\0 & c_{\theta}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{y} \\\dot{\theta}\end{array}\right\} \\&+\left[\begin{array}{ll}k_{11} & k_{12} \\k_{21} & k_{22}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}y \\\theta\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}P(t) \\0\end{array}\right\} .\end{aligned}\)

 

These equations cannot be solved as we do not know the stiffness coefficients \(k_{i j}\). We next derive the forcedisplacement
expressions relating\(Q\) to \(y\) and \(M\) to θ. Even though (\(y\), θ) and (\(Q\), \(M\)) are time-dependent, we are only looking for the stiffness properties which are static relations. There are two options for deriving these relations. The force-displacement relation can be written in terms of a stiffness matrix,

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{c}Q \\M\end{array}\right\}=\left[\begin{array}{ll}k_{11} & k_{12} \\k_{21} & k_{22}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}y \\\theta\end{array}\right\}\) (6.4)

 

Alternatively, the displacement-force relationship (the inverse of Equation 6.4) can be written in terms of a flexibility matrix,

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}y \\\theta\end{array}\right\}=\left[\begin{array}{ll}f_{11} & f_{12} \\f_{21} & f_{22}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}Q \\M\end{array}\right\}\) (6.5)

 

The relation between the stiffness and flexibility matrices is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left[\begin{array}{ll}k_{11} & k_{12} \\k_{21} & k_{22}\end{array}\right] &=\left[\begin{array}{ll}f_{11} & f_{12} \\f_{21} & f_{22}\end{array}\right]^{-1} \\&=\frac{1}{f_{11} f_{22}-f_{12} f_{21}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}f_{22} & -f_{12} \\-f_{21} & f_{11}\end{array}\right]\end{aligned}\) (6.6)

 

It turns out to be simpler to derive the flexibility matrix and then invert it to obtain the stiffness matrix.8
For the flexibility approach, \(y=f_{11} Q+f_{12} M\), with

\(Q\)=1 and \(M\) = 0. Let \(y\) be the deflection of a cantilever beam due to a unit load at the end, as shown in Figure 6.7. From strength of materials, the deflection at the end of a cantilever beam due to a unit force at the free end is \(L^{3} / 3 E I\). Therefore,

 

\(y=f_{11} Q=f_{11}=\frac{L^{3}}{3 E I}\)

 

where\(I\) is the area moment of inertia about the axis perpendicular to bending.

Similarly, the rotation at the end of a cantilever beam is given by \(\theta=f_{21} Q+f_{22} M\). For\(Q\)=1 at the free end and \(M\) = 0, we have

 

\(\theta=f_{21} Q=f_{21}=\frac{L^{2}}{2 E I}\)

 

To determine the second column of the flexibility matrix, we set \(Q\)=0 and \(M\) = 1, and again use results from
strength of materials. The deflection of the free end of the cantilever beam due to a unit moment applied at the free end is

 

\(y=f_{12} M=f_{12}=\frac{L^{2}}{2 E I}\)

 

Similarly, the rotation of the free end of the cantilever beam due to unit moment applied at the free end is

 

\(\theta=f_{22} M=f_{22}=\frac{L}{E I}\)

 

As predicted by the Maxwell-Betti reciprocity theorem, \(f_{12}=f_{21}\), and the flexibility matrix is

 

\([f]=\frac{1}{E I}\left[\begin{array}{cc}L^{3} / 3 & L^{2} / 2 \\L^{2} / 2 & L\end{array}\right]\)

 

with Equation 6.6 yielding the stiffness matrix,

 

\([K]=\frac{E I}{L}\left[\begin{array}{cc}12 / L^{2} & -6 / L \\-6 / L & 4\end{array}\right]\)

 

The equations of motion are then

 

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\begin{array}{cc}m & 0 \\0 & J\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}\ddot{y} \\\ddot{\theta}\end{array}\right\}+\left[\begin{array}{cc}c_{y} & 0 \\0 & c_{\theta}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{y} \\\dot{\theta}\end{array}\right\}} \\&+\frac{E I}{L}\left[\begin{array}{cc}12 / L^{2} & -6 / L \\-6 / L & 4\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}y \\\theta\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}P(t) \\0\end{array}\right\}\end{aligned}\) (6.7)

 

We will learn how to solve such equations later in this chapter.
