Guest Question.

Consider a bilayer composed of two homogeneous magnetic metallic layers labelled (1) and (2), maintained at a constant temperature T. The sides of this bilayer are covered by two electrodes which are connected to a resistance measurement circuit. A constant electric current is driven through the bilayer. The voltage drop across the bilayer \(\Delta \varphi\) is to be determined for each magnetic configuration in order to determine the effective resistance of the bilayer. The two magnetic layers have the same thickness d and their magnetisation vectors are oriented along the vertical axis in either a parallel (p) or an anti-parallel (a) configuration (Fig. 11.12) [186].
In a magnetic metal, the electric conductivity depends on the spin of the conduction electrons with respect to the magnetisation. The spins are oriented either upwards or downwards. Treat the electrons as a chemical substance that has two isomers labelled U and D (for up and down). Both have the electric charge \(q_e\). Assume that there is no transition (or ‘chemical reaction’) from U to D or from D to U, i.e. we assume that the spin diffusion length λ (c.f. ) is much larger than the thickness d of the sample, \(λ \gg d\).
The electrons with a spin parallel to the magnetisation have a conductivity \(σ_p\), those with a spin anti-parallel to the magnetisation have a conductivity \(σ_a\).

At constant temperature T, the vectorial empirical relations (11.92) applied to electrons U and D in each layer can be written as,

\(\begin{cases}j _s=- \bigl(\frac{\kappa }{T}+\sigma \varepsilon ^2\bigr) \nabla T -\frac{\sigma \varepsilon }{q_e} \nabla \bar{\mu _e} \\j _e = - \frac{\sigma \varepsilon }{q_e}\nabla T - \frac{\sigma }{q^2_e} \nabla \bar{\mu _e}\end{cases} \) (11.92)

\(j _U = - \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U}{q^2_e} \nabla \bar{\mu }_U\) and \(j _D = - \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D}{q^2_e} \nabla \bar{\mu }_D\).

\(j _U = - \frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{q^2_e} \nabla \bar{\mu }_U\) and \(j _D = - \frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{q^2_e} \nabla \bar{\mu }_D\).

where the electric conductivities \(σ^{(1)}_{U}\) and \(σ^{(1)}_{D}\) of layers 1 and the electric conductivities \(σ^{(2)}_{U}\) and \(σ^{(2)}_{D}\) of layers 2 have the values \(σ_p \space or \space σ_a\), depending on the relative orientation of the spin with respect to the magnetisation.

1. Determine the variation of the electrochemical potential across each layer for each spin direction, \(\Delta \bar{\mu } ^{(1)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu } ^{(2)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu } ^{(1)}_D,\Delta \bar{\mu } ^{(2)}_D \) in terms of \(\Delta \varphi\) and the electrochemical potentials at the interface, \(\bar{\mu }_U (0)\) and \(\bar{\mu }_D (0)\). In order to keep the expressions simple, choose the zero of the chemical potential scale to be that of electrons in the electrodes.
2. Using the continuity conditions for the electron current densities \(j_U\) and \(j_D\) at the interface, determine the currents of electrons \(I_U\) and \(I_D\) in terms of \(σ^{(1)}_{U} , σ^{(2)}_{U} , σ^{(1)}_{D}\) and \(σ^{(2)}_{D}\) and\(\Delta \varphi\) .
3. Express Ohm’s law for the bilayer in terms of \(σ^{(1)}_{U} , σ^{(2)}_{U} , σ^{(1)}_{D}\) and \(σ^{(2)}_{D}\) . Find an expression for the effective electric conductivity σ of the bilayer in terms of the electric conductivities of the U and D electrons in layers (1) and (2).

4. Determine the effective electric conductivity \(σ_−\) in terms of \(σ_p\) and \(σ_a\) when the bilayer is in a parallel magnetic configuration. Likewise, find an expression for the effective electric conductivity \(σ_+\) when the bilayer is in the anti-parallel magnetic configuration.
5. Compare the effective resistivities \(ρ_±\) of the bilayer in its two magnetic configurations. Use the following notation for the resistivities \(ρ_p\) and \(ρ_a\) corresponding to the conductivities \(σ_p\) and \(σ_a\),

\(ρ_p = ρ_0 − Δρ\) and \(ρ_a = ρ_0 +Δρ\)

where \(Δρ < ρ_0\).

Solution

1. At the electrodes, i.e. at x = −d and x = d, the chemical potential of the electrons does not depend on the spin. We can choose the zero of the chemical potential to be at the electrodes,

\(μ_U (−d) = μ_D (−d) = μ_U (d) = μ_D (d) = 0\).

At the electrodes, the electrochemical potentials of U and D electrons reduce to the electrostatic potentials of the electrodes (Fig. 11.12),

\(\bar{\mu }_U(-d) = \bar{\mu } _D (-d) =q_e \varphi (-d)= q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} \).

\(\bar{\mu }_U(d) = \bar{\mu } _D (d) =q_e \varphi (d)= – q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} \).

The electrochemical potential variations of U and D electrons across layers (1) and (2) are given by,

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U = \bar{\mu }_U(-d) -\bar{\mu }_U(0)= q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} -\bar{\mu }_U(0)\).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U = \bar{\mu }_U(0) -\bar{\mu }_U(d)= q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} +\bar{\mu }_U(0)\).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D = \bar{\mu }_D(-d) -\bar{\mu }_D(0)= q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} -\bar{\mu }_D(0)\).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D = \bar{\mu }_U(0) -\bar{\mu }_D(d) =q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} +\bar{\mu }_D(0)\).

2. The currents of electrons \(I_U\) and \(I_D\) are independent of the position between the electrodes because we assume that there is no reaction U → D or D → U. Thus the currents of electrons \(I_U\) and \(I_D\) are given by,

\(I_U = \int_{S}{j _U}.dS\) \(I_D = \int_{S}{j _D}.dS\)

where the infinitesimal surface element dS is oriented along the electron current densities \(j_U \space and \space j_D\). The electrochemical potential differences \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D\) and \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D\) across each layer can be written as,

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U = \int_{-d}^{0}{dr .(- \nabla \bar{\mu }_U )}\) and \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U = \int_{0}^{d}{dr .(- \nabla \bar{\mu }_U )}\).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D = \int_{-d}^{0}{dr .(- \nabla \bar{\mu }_D )}\) and \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D = \int_{0}^{d}{dr .(- \nabla \bar{\mu }_D )}\).

where the infinitesimal length element dr is oriented along the electron current densities \(j_U\) and \(j_D\). The thermoelectric materials 1 and 2 have a length d and a cross-section surface area A, which can be written as,

\(d= \int_{-d}^{0}{dr .\hat{x} } =\int_{0}^{d}{dr .\hat{x} }\) and \(A= \int_{S}{dS.\hat{x} }\).

where the unit vector \(d\hat{x}\) is oriented along the electron current densities \(j_U\) and \(j_D\). The integrals of the electron transport equations over the volume are the product of integrals over the cross-section area A times integrals over the length d of the thermoelectric materials,

\(\int_{S}{j _U}.dS \int_{-d}^{0}{dr} .\hat{x} =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U}{q^2_e}\int_{-d}^{0}{dr}.(-\nabla \bar{\mu }_U ) \int_{S}{dS}.\hat{x}\).

\(\int_{S}{j _U}.dS \int_{0}^{d}{dr} .\hat{x} =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{q^2_e}\int_{0}^{d}{dr}.(-\nabla \bar{\mu }_U ) \int_{S}{dS}.\hat{x}\).

\(\int_{S}{j _D}.dS \int_{-d}^{0}{dr} .\hat{x} =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D}{q^2_e}\int_{-d}^{0}{dr}.(-\nabla \bar{\mu }_D ) \int_{S}{dS}.\hat{x}\).

\(\int_{S}{j _D}.dS \int_{0}^{d}{dr} .\hat{x} =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{q^2_e}\int_{0}^{d}{dr}.(-\nabla \bar{\mu }_D ) \int_{S}{dS}.\hat{x}\).

The electron transport equations integrated over the volume reduce to,

\( I_U =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U}{q^2_e} \frac{A}{d} \Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{q^2_e}\frac{A}{d}\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U \).

\( I_D =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D}{q^2_e} \frac{A}{d} \Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{q^2_e}\frac{A}{d}\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D \).

Using the expressions of \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D \) and \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D \) established in section 1, the previous conditions become,

\(\sigma ^{(1)}_U\Bigl(q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} – \bar{\mu }_U(0) \Bigr) = \sigma ^{(2)}_U\Bigl(q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} + \bar{\mu }_U(0) \Bigr)\).

\(\sigma ^{(1)}_D\Bigl(q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} – \bar{\mu }_D(0) \Bigr) = \sigma ^{(2)}_D\Bigl(q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} + \bar{\mu }_D(0) \Bigr)\).

This implies that,

\(\bar{\mu }_U (0) = \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U – \sigma ^{(2)}_U}{\sigma ^{(1)}_U + \sigma ^{(2)}_U} q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2}\).

\(\bar{\mu }_D (0) = \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D – \sigma ^{(2)}_D}{\sigma ^{(1)}_D + \sigma ^{(2)}_D} q_e \frac{\Delta \varphi }{2}\).

Applying the previous relations to the expressions of \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U ,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U ,\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D\) and \(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D\) obtained in section 1, we find

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_U =\biggl(1-\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U -\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{\sigma ^{(1)}_U +\sigma ^{(2)}_U} \biggr)q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{\sigma ^{(1)}_U +\sigma ^{(2)}_U} q_e \Delta \varphi \).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_U =\biggl(1+\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U -\sigma ^{(2)}_U}{\sigma ^{(1)}_U +\sigma ^{(2)}_U} \biggr)q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_U}{\sigma ^{(1)}_U +\sigma ^{(2)}_U} q_e \Delta \varphi \).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(1)}_D =\biggl(1-\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D -\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{\sigma ^{(1)}_D +\sigma ^{(2)}_D} \biggr)q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} =\frac{\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{\sigma ^{(1)}_D +\sigma ^{(2)}_D} q_e \Delta \varphi \).

\(\Delta \bar{\mu }^{(2)}_D =\biggl(1+\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D -\sigma ^{(2)}_D}{\sigma ^{(1)}_D +\sigma ^{(2)}_D} \biggr)q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{2} =\frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_D}{\sigma ^{(1)}_D +\sigma ^{(2)}_D} q_e \Delta \varphi \).

Taking into account the previous expressions, the currents of electrons \(I_U\) and \(I_D\) can be written as,

\(I_U = \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} \sigma ^{(2)}_{U} }{\sigma ^{(1)}_{U}+ \sigma ^{(2)}_{U}} \frac{A}{d} \frac{\Delta \varphi }{q_e} = \biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{U} }\biggr) ^{-1}\frac{A}{d} \frac{\Delta \varphi }{q_e}\).

\(I_D = \frac{\sigma ^{(1)}_{D} \sigma ^{(2)}_{D} }{\sigma ^{(1)}_{D}+ \sigma ^{(2)}_{D}} \frac{A}{d} \frac{\Delta \varphi }{q_e} = \biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{D} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{D} }\biggr) ^{-1}\frac{A}{d} \frac{\Delta \varphi }{q_e}\).

3. Ohm’s law (11.76) applied to the bilayer reads,

\(j_q = −σ (s, n_e, q) ∇\varphi \). (11.76)

\(j_q = −σ ∇ \varphi \).

The electrostatic potential difference \(\Delta \varphi \) is given by,

\(\Delta \varphi = \int_{-d}^{d}{dr} .(-\nabla \varphi )\).

The integrals of Ohm’s law over the volume is the product of the integral over the crosssection area A times the integral over the length 2d of the thermoelectric materials,

\(\int_{S}{j _q} .dS \int_{-d}^{d}{dr} .\hat{x} =\sigma \int_{-d}^{d}{dr}.(-\nabla \varphi ) \int_{S}{dS}.\hat{x}\).

Ohm’s law integrated over the volume reduce to,

\(I= \sigma \frac{A}{2d} \Delta \varphi \).

The total electric current I flowing through the metallic material can be written in terms of the currents of electrons \(I_U \space and\space I_D \) as,

\(I = q_e (I_U + I_D) \).

Taking into account the expressions for \(I_U and I_D\). obtained in section 2, the electric current I is expressed as,

\(I= 2 \Biggl(\biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{U} }\biggr)^{-1}+\biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{D} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{D} }\biggr)^{-1}\Biggr) \frac{A}{2d} \Delta \varphi \).

Comparing these two expressions of Ohm’s law, we deduce an effective electric conductivity σ which can be written in terms of the electric conductivities \(σ^{(1)}_{U} , σ^{(2)}_{U} ,σ^{(1)}_{D} \space and \space σ^{(2)}_{D}\) as,

\(σ= 2 \Biggl(\biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{U} }\biggr)^{-1}+\biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(1)}_{D} }+\frac{1}{\sigma ^{(2)}_{D} }\biggr)^{-1}\Biggr) \).

4. In the parallel magnetic configuration, the electric conductivities of each layer are defined as,

\(\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} = \sigma ^{(2)}_{U} = \sigma _p \) and \(\sigma ^{(1)}_{D} = \sigma ^{(2)}_{D} = \sigma _a \).

Comparing the two expressions of Ohm’s law, we can write the electric conductivity \(σ_− \) between the two electrodes as,

\(σ_− = σ_p + σ_a\).

In the anti-parallel magnetic configuration, with an upward magnetisation in the first layer and a downward magnetisation in the second layer, the electric conductivities of each layer are given by,

\(\sigma ^{(1)}_{U} = \sigma ^{(2)}_{D}=\sigma _p \) and \( \sigma ^{(2)}_{U} = \sigma ^{(1)}_{D} = \sigma _a \).

Comparing the two expressions of Ohm’s law, we can write the electric conductivity \(σ_+\) between the two electrodes as,

\(\sigma _+ =4 \biggl(\frac{1}{\sigma _p}+\frac{1}{\sigma _a } \biggr)^{-1} \).

5. The resistivities are defined as the inverse of the conductivities (11.74),

\(\Delta \varphi = −ρ (s, n_A, q) · j_q \). (11.74)

\(\rho _- =\frac{1}{\sigma _-} \) and \(\rho _p =\frac{1}{\sigma _p} \).

\(\rho _+ =\frac{1}{\sigma _+} \) and \(\rho _a =\frac{1}{\sigma _a} \).

The resistivities \(ρ_− \space and \space ρ_+\) are written in terms of the resistivities \(ρ_0\) and Δρ as,

\(\rho _- = \biggl(\frac{1}{\rho _p}+\frac{1}{\rho _a }\biggr) ^{-1}=\frac{\rho _p \rho _a }{\rho _p+\rho _a } = \frac{(\rho _0-\Delta \rho )(\rho _0+\Delta \rho) }{\rho _0-\Delta \rho +\rho _0+\Delta \rho } =\frac{\rho _0}{2} -\frac{(\Delta \rho )^2}{2\rho _0}\).

\(\rho _+ = \frac{\rho _p+\rho _a }{4} =\frac{\rho _0-\Delta \rho +\rho _0+\Delta \rho }{4} =\frac{\rho _0}{2}\).

Comparing these two expressions, we find the following inequality for the resistivity across the layers,

\(ρ_+ > ρ_−\).

Thus, if the bilayer is in the anti-parallel magnetic configuration, its resistivity is larger than if it is in the parallel magnetic configuration.

For certain magnetic metals, the relative change of resistivity between the two magnetic configurations can become very large. This effect is called giant magnetoresistance. It was discovered in 1988 and gave rise to a Nobel Prize in physics, awarded to Albert Fert and Peter Grünberg.
