Guest Question.

Glycerin at room temperature \( (68^{\circ} F)\) flows at a flow rate of 89cfs in a 5-ft-diameter pipe for a length of 10 ft, as illustrated in Figure EP 1.28. (a) Determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. (b) Graphically illustrate the velocity profile at the pipe cross section. (c) Graphically illustrate the shear stress at the pipe cross section.

Admin
Solution

(a) The fluid properties for glycerin at room temperature \( (68^{\circ} F)\) are read from Table A.4 in Appendix A. In order to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, the Reynolds number, R is computed from Equation 1.48 \(R=\frac{F_{I} }{F_{V} } =\frac{\rho \upsilon ^{2}L^{2} }{\mu \upsilon L} =\frac{\rho \upsilon L}{\mu } \) as follows:

\(slug:1lb\frac{sec^{2} }{ft} \) \(\rho :=2.44\frac{slug}{ft^{3} } \) \(\mu :=31200\times 10^{-6}lb\frac{sec}{ft^{2} } \)
\(Q:=89\frac{ft^{3} }{sec} \) \(D:=5ft\) \(A:=\frac{\pi .D^{2} }{4} =19.635 ft^{2} \) \(v:=\frac{Q}{A} =4.533\frac{ft}{s} \)
\(R:=\frac{\rho .v.D}{\mu } =1.772 \times 10^{3} \)

Thus, because\(R=1.772\times 10^{3} \lt 2000\)the pipe flow is laminar.
(b) Because the pipe flow is laminar, the velocity profile is assumed to be parabolic and is evaluated by applying Equation 1.71 \(\upsilon =\upsilon _{max} [1-(\frac{r}{r_{0} } )^{2} ]\) as follows:

\(\upsilon =\upsilon _{max} [1-(\frac{r}{r_{0} } )^{2} ]\)

where \(\upsilon _{max} \) is evaluated by applying Equation 1.69 \(\upsilon _{max}=\frac{\Delta pr^{2}_{0}}{4\mu L} \) as follows:

\(\upsilon _{max} =\frac{\Delta pr^{2}_{0} }{4\mu L} \)

and where \(\Delta p\) for laminar pipe flow is evaluated by applying Poiseuille’s law (Equation 1.70) \(\Delta p=\frac{128\mu QL}{\pi D^{4} } \) as follows:

\(\Delta \rho =\frac{128\mu QL}{\pi D^{4} } \)
\(L:=10 ft\) \(\Delta p:=\frac{128.\mu.Q.L}{\pi .D^{4}} =1.807psf\) \(r_{0} :=\frac{D}{2} =2.5ft\)
\(v_{max} :=\frac{\Delta p.r^{2}_{0} }{4.\mu .L} =9.065\frac{ft}{s} \) \(v(r):=r_{max} [1-(\frac{r}{r_{0} } )^{2} ]\)

(c) The shear stress is evaluated by applying Newton’s second law of motion for either laminar or turbulent flow (Equation 1.53) \(\tau =\tau _{max} \frac{r}{r_{0} } \) as follows:

\(\tau =\tau _{max} \frac{r}{r_{0} }\)

where \(\tau _{max} \) is evaluated by applying Equation 1.50 \(\tau _{max} =\tau _{w} =\frac{r_{0} }{2} \frac{\Delta p}{l} \) as follows:

\(\tau _{max}=\tau _{w} =\frac{r_{0} }{2}\frac{ \Delta \rho }{L} \)

and where Δp for laminar pipe flow was already evaluated by applying Poiseuille’s law (Equation 1.70) \(\Delta p=\frac{128\mu QL}{\pi D^{4} } \) in (b) above.

\(\tau _{max} =\frac{r_{0} }{2}\frac{ \Delta \rho }{L} =0.226psf\) \(\tau (r):=\tau _{max} \frac{r}{r_{0} } \)

A graphical illustration of the shear stress at the pipe cross section is given as follows:

Note that the shear stress function, τ (r) was divided by psf in order to properly display the correct values for this function. Mathcad plots values in its default (base) units. The base unit for pressure (or shear stress) in the BG system of units is psi, so Mathcad saves them in psi. Thus, in order to properly display the values in psf, one may divide the shear stress by psf, which essentially removes the units and displays only the values. Alternatively, because the pipe flow is laminar, the shear stress may also be evaluated by applying Newton’s law of viscosity for laminar flow, Equation 1.58 \(\tau =\mu \frac{d\upsilon }{dy} \), as follows:

\(\tau =\mu \frac{d\upsilon }{dy} =\mu (-\frac{d\upsilon }{dr})\)
\(v_{max} 9.065 \frac{ft}{sec}\) \(r_{o} =2.5ft\) \(\mu :=31.200\times 10^{-6} lb\frac{s}{ft^{2} } \)
\(v(r):=v_{max} [1-(\frac{r}{r_{o} } )^{2} ]\) \(-\frac{d}{dr} v(r)\longrightarrow \frac{2.901r}{fts} \) \( \tau (r)=\mu \frac{2.901r}{fts} \)
