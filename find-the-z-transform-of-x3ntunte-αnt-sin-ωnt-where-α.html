Guest Question.

Find the z transform of

\( x_{3}(nT)=u(nT)e^{-\alpha T} \sin \omega nT \)

where \( \alpha \lt 0 \)

Admin
Solution

Evidently, we require the z transform of a product of signals and, therefore, this is a clear case for the complex convolution of Theorem 3.10. Let

\( x_{1}(nT)=u(nT)\sin \omega nT \) and \( x_{2}(nT)=u(nT)e^{-\alpha n T} \)

From , parts (g) and (e), we have

\( X_{1}(z)=\frac{z\sin \omega T}{(z-e^{j\omega T} )(z-e^{-j\omega T} )} \) and \( X_{2}(z)=\frac{z}{(z-e^{-\alpha T} )} \)

We note that \( X_{1}(z) \) has a complex-conjugate pair of poles at \( e^{\pm j\omega T} \) whereas \( X_{2}(z) \) has
a real pole at \( z=e^{-\alpha T} \), Since \( \left|e^{\pm j\omega T}\right|=1 \), the radius of convergence of \( X_{1}(z) \) is unity and with α assumed to be negative, the radius of convergence of \( X_{2}(z) \) is less than unity. Thus, according to the evaluation technique described earlier, the correct formula to use is that in Eq. (3.14a) and, by a lucky coincidence, the mappings in Fig. 3.5 apply.

\( X_{3}(z)=Z[x_{1}(nT)x_{2}(nT) ]=\frac{1}{2\pi j}\oint_{\Gamma _{1} }^{}{X_{1}(\upsilon )X_{2}(\frac{z}{\upsilon } )\upsilon ^{-1} d\upsilon } \) (3.14a)

Now

\( X_{1}(\upsilon )=\frac{\upsilon \sin \omega T}{(\upsilon -e^{j\omega T} )(\upsilon -e^{-j\omega T} )} \)

and thus it has poles at \( \upsilon =e^{\pm j\omega T} \). On the other hand,

\( X_{2}(z/\upsilon )=\frac{z}{(z-e^{-\alpha T} )}\mid_{z\rightarrow z/\upsilon } =\frac{z/\upsilon }{z/\upsilon -e^{-\alpha T} }=\frac{-ze^{\alpha T} }{\upsilon -ze^{\alpha T} } \)

and, as a result, it has a pole at \(\upsilon =ze^{\alpha T} \) . Hence the common region of convergence of \( X_{1}(\upsilon ) \) and \( X_{2}(z/\upsilon ) \) is the annulus given by

\(1\lt \left|\upsilon \right|\lt ze^{\alpha T} \)

as depicted in Fig. 3.7. Therefore, the complex convolution assumes the form

\( X_{3}(z)=\frac{1}{2\pi j}\oint_{\Gamma _{1} }^{}{X_{1}(\upsilon )X_{2}(\frac{z}{\upsilon } )\upsilon ^{-1} }d\upsilon \)

 

\( =\frac{1}{2\pi j}\oint_{\Gamma _{1} }^{}{\frac{-ze^{\alpha T}\sin \omega T }{(\upsilon -ze^{\alpha T} )(\upsilon -e^{j\omega T} )(\upsilon -e^{-j\omega T} )} }d\upsilon \)

where \( \Gamma _{1} \) is a contour in the annulus of Fig. 3.7. By evaluating the residues of the
integrand at \( \upsilon =e^{+ j\omega T} \) and \( e^{- j\omega T} \) , we obtain

\( X_{3}(z)={\frac{-ze^{\alpha T}\sin \omega T }{(\upsilon -ze^{\alpha T} )(\upsilon -e^{-j\omega T} )} }\mid _{\upsilon =e^{j\omega T} } \)

 

\( +{\frac{-ze^{\alpha T}\sin \omega T }{(\upsilon -ze^{\alpha T} )(\upsilon -e^{j\omega T} )} }\mid _{\upsilon =e^{-j\omega T} } \)

 

\( =\frac{ze^{-\alpha T}\sin \omega T }{z^{2}-2ze^{- \alpha T}\cos \omega T+e^{-2\alpha T} } \)

The radius of convergence of \( X_{3}(z) \) is equal to the magnitude of the poles, which is given
by the above equation as \( \sqrt{e^{-2\alpha T} }=e^{-\alpha T} \) . Alternatively, the annulus of convergence in Fig. 3.7 exists, if \( \left|ze^{\alpha T} \right|\gt 1 \), that is, \( X_{3}(z) \) converges if \( \left|z \right|\gt e^{-\alpha T} \).

The above approach was used primarily to illustrate the complex-convolution theorem
which happens to be quite important in the design of nonrecursive filters (see Chap. 9).
A simpler approach for the solution of the problem at hand would be to use the complexscale- change theorem (Theorem 3.5), as will now be demonstrated. From, part (g), we have

\( Z[u(nT)\sin \omega nT]=\frac{z\sin \omega T}{z^{2}-2z\cos \omega T+1 } \)

and from the complex-scale-change theorem, we can write

\( Z[w ^{-n}x(nT) ]=X(w z) \)

Hence

\( Z[u(nT)w^{-n}\sin \omega nT ]=\frac{w z\sin \omega T}{(w z)^{2}-2(w z) \cos \omega T+1 } \)

 

\( =\frac{ z w^{-1} \sin \omega T}{ z^{2}-2 z w^{-1} \cos \omega T+w^{-2} } \)

Now with \(w=e^{\alpha T} \) , we deduce

\( Z[u(nT)e^{-\alpha nT}\sin \omega nT ] =\frac{ z e^{-\alpha T} \sin \omega T}{ z^{2}-2 z e^{-\alpha T} \cos \omega T+e^{-2\alpha T} } \)

 

Theorem 3.5 Complex Scale Change For an arbitrary real or complex constant w,

\( Z[w^{-n}x(nT) ]=X(wz) \)

Proof

\( Z[w^{-n}x(nT) ]=\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{[w^{-n}x(nT) ]z^{-n} } \)

 

\( =\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)(wz)^{-n} } \)

 

\(= X(wz) \)

Evidently, multiplying a discrete-time signal by \( w^{-n} \) is equivalent to replacing z by wz in its z transform. If the signal is multiplied by \( \upsilon ^{-n} \), then we can write \( \upsilon ^{n}=(1/\upsilon )^{-n} \) and thus

\( Z[\upsilon ^{n}x(nT) ]= Z[(\frac{1}{\upsilon } )^{-n}x(nT) ]=X(z/\upsilon ) \)
