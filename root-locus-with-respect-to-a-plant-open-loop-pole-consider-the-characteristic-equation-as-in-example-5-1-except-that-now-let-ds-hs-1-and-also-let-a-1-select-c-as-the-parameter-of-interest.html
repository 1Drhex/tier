Guest Question.

Root Locus with Respect to a Plant Open-Loop Pole
Consider the characteristic equation as in , except that now let \( D(s) = H(s) = 1 \) and also let \( A = 1. \) Select \( c \) as the parameter of interest in the equation

\( 1 + G(s) = 1 + \frac{1}{s(s + c)}. \) (5.13)

Find the root locus of the characteristic equation with respect to \( c. \)

Admin
Solution

The corresponding closed-loop characteristic equation in polynomial form is

\( s^2 + cs + 1 = 0. \) (5.14)

The alternatives of Eq. (5.6) with the associated definitions of poles and zeros will apply if we let

\( 1 + KL(s) = 0, \) (5.6)

\( L = \frac{s}{s^2 + 1}, \ \ \ \ \ b(s) = s, \ \ \ \ \ m = 1, \ \ \ \ \ z_i = 0, \\ K = c, \ \ \ \ \ a(s) = s^2 + 1, \ \ \ \ \ n = 2, \ \ \ \ \ p_i = +j, −j. \) (5.15)

Thus, the root-locus form of the characteristic equation is

\( 1 + c \frac{s}{s^2 + 1} = 0.\)

The solutions to Eq. (5.14) are easily computed as

\( r_1,r_2 = −\frac{c}{2} ± \frac{\sqrt{c^2 − 4}}{2} . \) (5.16)

The locus of solutions is shown in Fig. 5.3, with the poles [roots of \( a(s) \) ] again indicated by \( ×\) ’s and the zero [root of \( b(s)\) ] by the circle (\( \circ \) ) symbol. Note that when \( c = 0, \) the roots are at the \( ×\) ’s on the imaginary axis and the corresponding response would be oscillatory. The damping ratio \( ζ \) grows as \( c \) increases from \( 0. \) At \( c = 2,\) there are two roots at \( s = −1, \) and the two locus segments abruptly change direction and move in opposite directions along the real axis; this point of multiple roots where two or more roots come into the real axis is called a break-in point.
