Guest Question.

Water enters a rotating lawn sprinkler through its base at the steady rate of 1000 ml/s as sketched in Fig. E5.10a. The exit area of each of the two nozzles is 30 \(mm^{2}\) and the flow leaving each nozzle is in the tangential direction. The radius from the axis of rotation to the centerline of each nozzle is 200 mm.

FIND (a) Determine the resisting torque required to hold the sprinkler head stationary.
(b) Determine the resisting torque associated with the sprinkler rotating with a constant speed of 500 rev/min.
(c) Determine the speed of the sprinkler if no resisting torque is applied.

Admin
Solution

To solve parts (a), (b), and (c) of this example we can use the same fixed and nondeforming, disk-shaped control volume illustrated in Fig. 5.4. As indicated in Fig. E5.10a, the only axial torque considered is the one resisting motion, \(T_{ shaft }\).

(a) When the sprinkler head is held stationary as specified in part (a) of this example problem, the velocities of the fluid entering and leaving the control volume are shown in Fig. E5.10b. Equation \(-r_{2}V_{\theta 2}\dot{m}=T_{shaft} \) applies to the contents of this control volume. Thus,

\(T_{shaft} =-r_{2}V_{\theta 2}\dot{m} \) (1)

Since the control volume is fixed and nondeforming and the flow exiting from each nozzle is tangential,

\(V_{\theta 2}=V_{2}\) (2)

Equations 1 and 2 give

\( T_{shaft} =-r_{2}V_{2}\dot{m}\) (3)

In , we ascertained that \(V_{2}= 16.7 {m}/{s}\). Thus, from Eq. 3 with

\( \dot{m}=Q\rho=\frac{(1000{ml}/{s})(10^{-3}{m^{3}}/{liter})(999{kg}/{m^{3}})}{(1000{ml}/{liter})} =0.999{kg}/{s}\)

we obtain

\(T_{shaft}=-\frac{(200 mm)(16.7{m}/{s})(0.999{kg}/{s})[1{({N}/{kg})}/{({m}/{s^{2}})}]}{(1000{mm}/{m})} \)

or

\(T_{shaft}=-3.34 N \cdot m\)

(b) When the sprinkler is rotating at a constant speed of 500 rpm, the flow field in the control volume is unsteady but cyclical. Thus, the flow field is steady in the mean. The velocities of the flow entering and leaving the control volume are as indicated in Fig. E5.10c. The absolute velocity of the fluid leaving each nozzle, \( V_{2}\), is from \( V=W+U\),

 

\(V_{2}=W_{2}-U_{2}\) (4)

where

\(W_{2}=16.7{m}/{s}\)

as determined in . The speed of the nozzle, \(U_{2}\) is obtained from

\( U_{2}=r_{2}\omega \) (5)

Application of the axial component of the moment-of-momentum equation (\(-r_{2}V_{\theta 2}\dot{m}=T_{shaft} \)) leads again to Eq. 3. From Eqs. 4 and 5,

\( V_{2}=16.7{m}/{s}-r_{2}\omega =16.7{m}/{s}-\frac{(200 mm)(500{rev}/{min})(2\pi {rad}/{rev})}{(1000{mm}/{m})(60{s}/{min})} \)

or

\(V_{2}=16.7{m}/{s}-10.5{m}/{s}=6.2{m}/{s} \)

Thus, using Eq. 3, with \(\dot{m}=0.999{kg}/{s} \) (as calculated previously), we get

\( T_{shaft}=-\frac{(200 mm)(6.2{m}/{s})0.999{kg}/{s}[1{({N}/{kg})}/{({m}/{s^{2}})}]}{(1000{mm}/{m})} \)

or

\(T_{shaft}=-1.24N\cdot m \)

COMMENT Note that the resisting torque associated with sprinkler head rotation is much less than the resisting torque that is required to hold the sprinkler stationary.
(c) When no resisting torque is applied to the rotating sprinkler head, a maximum constant speed of rotation will occur as demonstrated below. Application of Eqs. 3, 4, and 5 to the contents of the control volume results in

\( T_{shaft}=-r_{2}(W_{2}-r_{2}\omega )\dot{m} \) (6)

For no resisting torque, Eq. 6 yields

\(0=-r_{2}(W_{2}-r_{2}\omega )\dot{m} \)

Thus,

\( \omega =\frac{W_{2}}{r_{2}} \) (7)

In , we learned that the relative velocity of the fluid leaving each nozzle, \( W_{2}\), is the same regardless of the speed of rotation of the sprinkler head, \( \omega\), as long as the mass flowrate of the fluid, \( \dot{m} \), remains constant. Thus, by using Eq. 7 we obtain

\(\omega =\frac{W_{2}}{r_{2}} =\frac{(16.7{m}/{s})(1000{mm}/{m})}{(200mm)}=83.5{rad}/{s}\)

or

\( \omega =\frac{(83.5{rad}/{s})(60{s}/{min})}{(2\pi {rad}/{rev})} =797rpm\)

For this condition (\( T_{shaft}=0\)), the water both enters and leaves the control volume with zero angular momentum.

COMMENT By repeating the calculations for various values of the angular velocity,\( \omega\), the results shown in Fig. E5.10d are obtained. It is seen that the magnitude of the resisting torque associated with rotation is less than the torque required to hold the rotor stationary. Even in the absence of a resisting torque, the rotor maximum speed is finite.
