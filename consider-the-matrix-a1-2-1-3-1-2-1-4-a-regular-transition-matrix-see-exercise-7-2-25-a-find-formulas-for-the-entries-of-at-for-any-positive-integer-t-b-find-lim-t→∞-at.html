Guest Question.

Consider the matrix \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ll} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{4} \end{array}\right]\) , a regular transition matrix (see Exercise 7.2.25).

a. Find formulas for the entries of \(A^t\) , for any positive integer \(t\) .

b. Find \(\lim _{t\rightarrow \infty} A^{t}\). (Hint: Diagonalize \(A\) .)

Admin
Solution

It may be informative to compute \(A^t\) for a few values of \(t\) first, using a calculator or computer. For example:

\(A^{10} \approx\left[\begin{array}{rr} 0.6000003815 & 0.5999994278 \\ 0.399996183 & 0.4000005722 \end{array}\right]\)

Further numerical experimentation suggests that:

\( \lim _{t \rightarrow \infty} A^{t}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 0.6 & 0.6 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{array}\right]\)

We cannot be certain of this result, of course, for (at least) two reasons: We can only check finitely many values of \(t\) , and the results the calculator produces are afflicted with round-off errors.

To prove our conjecture \( \lim _{t \rightarrow \infty} A^{t}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 0.6 & 0.6 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{array}\right]\) , we follow the hint and diagonalize matrix \(A\) :

• Eigenvalues: \(f_{A}(\lambda)=\lambda^{2}-(\operatorname{tr} A) \lambda+(\operatorname{det} A)=\lambda^{2}-\frac{3}{4} \lambda-\frac{1}{4}\)

\(\qquad =(\lambda-1)\left(\lambda+\frac{1}{4}\right)=0\) . The eigenvalues are 1 and \(-\frac{1}{4} \) .

• Eigenvectors: \( E_{1}=\operatorname{ker}\left[\begin{array}{rr} -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{2} & -\frac{3}{4} \end{array}\right]=\operatorname{span}\left[\begin{array}{l} 3 \\ 2 \end{array}\right]\) ,

\(E_{-1 / 4}=\operatorname{ker}\left[\begin{array}{ll} \frac{3}{4} & \frac{3}{4} \\ \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right]=\operatorname{span}\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -1 \end{array}\right]\)

• Thus : \(S^{-1} A S=D=\left[\begin{array}{lr} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & – frac{1}{4} \end{array}\right]\) where \( S=\left[\begin {array} {rr} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{array}\right]\) .

To compute the powers of matrix \(A\), we solve the equation \(S^{-1} A S=D\) for \(A\) and find that \(A = SDS^{-1} \). Now:

\( A^{t}=\left(S D S^{-1}\right)^{t}=\overbrace{\left(S D S^{-1}\right)\left(S D S^{-1}\right) \cdots\left(S D S^{-1}\right)}^{t \text { times }}=S D^{t} S^{-1}\)

note the cancellation of the terms of the form \(S^{-1} S\). (Compare this with of Section 3.4.).
Matrices \(D^t\) and \(S^{-1}\) are easy to compute: To find \(D^t\) , raise the diagonal entries of D to the \(t\) th power. Thus, the problem of finding \(A^t\) is essentially solved.

\(S^{-1}=\frac{1}{5}\left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & -3 \end{array}\right], \quad D^{t}=\left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} \end{array}\right]\)

so that:

\(A^{t}=S D^{t} S^{-1} =\frac{1}{5}\left[\begin{array}{lr} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & -3 \end{array}\right]\)

 

\(\hspace{60 pt} =\frac{1}{5}\left[\begin{array}{ll} 3+2\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} & 3-3\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} \\ 2-2\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} & 2+3\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t} \end{array}\right]\)

Since the term \(\left(-\frac{1}{4}\right)^{t}\) approaches 0 as t goes to infinity, we have:

\(\lim _{t \rightarrow \infty} A^{t}=\frac{1}{5}\left[\begin{array}{ll} 3 & 3 \\ 2 & 2 \end {array} \right]=\left[\begin {array} {ll} 0.6 & 0.6 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{array}\right]\)

confirming our earlier conjecture.
