Guest Question.

Two cars, one with and one without ABS, are stopping from the speed \(v_{0}=100 km/h\) by full breaking. The first without ABS by blocking the wheels, i.e. sliding (’kinetic friction’), the second with ABS with still rolling wheels (ideal ’limiting static friction’ assumed).

Determine for both cars the time \(t_{B}\) and the distance \(s_{B}\) for stopping if the coefficients of static and kinetic friction between pavement and tire are \(\mu _{0}=0.7 \) and \(\mu=0.45 \), respectively.

Admin
Solution

From the equation of motion of the first car (sliding)

\(\longrightarrow : m\dot{v} = m\ddot{s} = -R\) , \(\uparrow : 0= N – mg\)

and the friction law

\(R = \mu N\) ,

it follows

\(\dot{v}=- \mu g \) .

Integration yields with \(v\left(t=0\right) = v_{0} \) and \(s\left(t=0\right) = 0 \)

 

\(v\left(t\right) = v_{0} – \mu gt\) , \(s\left(t\right) = v_{0}t – \frac{1}{2}\mu gt^{2} \) .

The stopping time and distance are calculated from the condition \(v=0\) :

\(t_{B}=\frac{v_{0} }{\mu g} \) , \(s_{B} = s\left(t_{B} \right) = \frac{v^{2}_{0} }{\mu g} \frac{v^{2}_{0} }{2\mu g} = \frac{v^{2}_{0} }{2\mu g} \) .

With the given coefficient of kinetic friction, we obtain

\(\underline{\underline{t_{B} } } = \frac{100}{3.6\cdot 0.45\cdot 9.81} = \underline{\underline{6.3s} } \) , \(\underline{\underline{s_{B} } } = \frac{100^{2} }{3.6^{2}\cdot 2\cdot 0.45\cdot 9.81 } = \underline{\underline{87 m} } \) .

For the second car the wheels are still rolling under the limit condition of static friction (ABS), i.e. the friction force is now given by

\(H = H_{0} = \mu _{0} N\) .

This means that in the calculation above only R must replaced by \(H_{0} \) and \(\mu \) by \(\mu _{0} \) , respectively. Thus, for the car with ABS, we obtain

\(\underline{\underline{t_{B} } } = 6.3\cdot \frac{0.45}{0.7} = \underline{\underline{4.05 s} } \) , \(\underline{\underline{s_{B} } } = 87\cdot \frac{0.45}{0.7} = 56 m \) .

Remark: Note that stopping time and distance are inverse proportional to the friction coefficient. Note also that, because of the neglected reaction time, the numbers for \(t_{B}\) and \(s_{B}\) in reality might be higher!
