Guest Question.

A solid that has a latent heat of fusion Lf melts at a tempera- ture Tm . (a) Calculate the change in entropy of this sub- stance when a mass m of the substance melts

 

Admin
Solution

Let us assume that the melting occurs so slowly that it can be considered a reversible process. In this case the temperature can be regarded as constant and equal to Tm Making use of Equations 22.9 and that for the latent heat of fusion \(Q=m L_{f}\) ( Eq. 20.6), we find that

\(\Delta S=\int \frac{d Q_{r}}{T}=\frac{1}{T_{n}} \int d Q=\frac{Q}{T_{m}}=\frac{m L_{f}}{T_{m}}\)

Note that we are able to remove \(T_{n}\) from the integral because the process is isothermal. Note also that \(\Delta S\) is positive. This means that when a solid melts, its entropy increases because the molecules are much more disordered in the liquid state than they are in the solid state. The positive value for \(\Delta S\) also means that the substance in its liquid state does not spontaneously transfer energy from itself to the surroundings and freeze because to do so would involve a spontaneous decrease in entropy.

(b) Estimate the value of the change in entropy of an ice cube when it melts.

Let us assume an ice tray makes cubes that are about \(3 \mathrm{~cm}\) on a side. The volume per cube is then (very roughly) \(30 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}\). This much liquid water has a mass of \(30 \mathrm{~g}\). From Table 20.2 we find that the latent heat of fusion of ice is \(3.38 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kg}.\) Substituting these values in to our answer for part (a), we find that

\(\Delta S=\frac{m L_{f}}{T_{m}}=\frac{(0.03 \mathrm{~kg})\left(9.93 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kg}\right)}{278 \mathrm{~K}}=4 \times 10^{1} \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{K}\)

We retain only one significant figure, in keeping with the nature of our estimations.
