Guest Question.

A metal hoop with a radius r = 6 in. is released from rest on the 20° incline. If the coefficients of static and kinetic friction are \(\mu_{s}=0.15 \text { and } \mu_{k}=0.12\), determine the angular acceleration α of the hoop and the time t for the hoop to move a distance of 10 ft down the incline.

Admin
Solution

The free-body diagram shows the unspecified weight mg, the normal force N, and the friction force F acting on the hoop at the contact point C with the incline. The kinetic diagram shows the resultant force \(m \bar{a}\) through G in the direction of its acceleration and the couple I. The counterclockwise angular acceleration requires a counterclockwise moment about G, so F must be up the incline.

Assume that the hoop rolls without slipping, so that \(\bar{a}=r \alpha\). Application of the components of Eqs. 6 /1 with x- and y-axes assigned gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Sigma F &=m \overline{ a } \\\Sigma M_{G} &=\bar{I} \alpha\end{aligned}\) (6/1)

 

\(\left[\Sigma F_{x}=m \bar{a}_{x}\right] \quad m g \sin 20^{\circ}-F=m \bar{a}\)

 

\(\left[\Sigma F_{y}=m \bar{a}_{y}=0\right] \quad N-m g \cos 20^{\circ}=0\)

 

1 \(\left[\Sigma M_{G}=\bar{I} \alpha\right] \quad F r=m r^{2} \alpha\)

 

Elimination of F between the first and third equations and substitution of the kinematic assumption \(\bar{a}=r \alpha\) give

 

2 \(\bar{a}=\frac{g}{2} \sin 20^{\circ}=\frac{32.2}{2}(0.342)=5.51 ft / sec ^{2}\)

 

Alternatively, with our assumption of \(\bar{a}=r \alpha\) for pure rolling, a moment sum about C by Eq. 6 /2 gives \(\bar{a}\) directly. Thus,

 

\(\Sigma M_{P}=\bar{I} \alpha+m \bar{a} d\) (6/2)

 

\(\left[\Sigma M_{C}=\bar{I} \alpha+m \bar{a} d\right] \quad m g r \sin 20^{\circ}=m r^{2} \frac{\bar{a}}{r}+m \bar{a} r \quad \bar{a}=\frac{g}{2} \sin 20^{\circ}\)

 

To check our assumption of no slipping, we calculate F and N and compare F with its limiting value. From the above equations,

 

\(\begin{aligned}&F=m g \sin 20^{\circ}-m \frac{g}{2} \sin 20^{\circ}=0.1710 m g \\&N=m g \cos 20^{\circ}=0.940 m g\end{aligned}\)

 

But the maximum possible friction force is

 

\(\left[F_{\max }=\mu_{s} N\right] \quad F_{\max }=0.15(0.940 mg )=0.1410 mg\)

 

Because our calculated value of 0.1710mg exceeds the limiting value of 0.1410mg, we conclude that our assumption of pure rolling was wrong. Therefore, the hoop slips as it rolls and \(\bar{a} \neq r \alpha\). The friction force then becomes the kinetic value

 

\(\left[F=\mu_{k} N\right] \quad F=0.12(0.940 mg )=0.1128 mg\)

 

The motion equations now give

 

\(\left[\Sigma F_{x}=m \bar{a}_{x}\right] \quad m g \sin 20^{\circ}-0.1128 m g=m \bar{a}\)

 

\(\bar{a}=0.229(32.2)=7.38 ft / sec ^{2}\)

 

3 \(\left[\Sigma M_{G}=\bar{I} \alpha\right] \quad 0.1128 m g(r)=m r^{2} \alpha\)

 

\(\left[\Sigma M_{G}=\bar{I} \alpha\right] \quad 0.1128 m g(r)=m r^{2} \alpha\)

 

The time required for the center G of the hoop to move 10 ft from rest with constant acceleration is

 

\(\left[x=\frac{1}{2} a t^{2}\right] \quad t=\sqrt{\frac{2 x}{a}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(10)}{7.38}}=1.646 sec\)

 

Helpful Hints

1 Because all of the mass of a hoop is a distance r from its center G, its moment of inertia about G must be \(m r^{2}\).

2 Note that \(\bar{a}\) is independent of both m and r.

3 Note that α is independent of m but dependent on r.
