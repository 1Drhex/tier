Guest Question.

Let a and b be two constant vectors. Show that

\( \int( a \cdot \hat{r})( b \cdot \hat{r}) \sin \theta d \theta d \phi=\frac{4 \pi}{3}( a \cdot b ) \). (7.99).

(the integration is over the usual range: \( 0<\theta<\pi, 0<\phi<2 \pi \text { ) } \). Use this result to demonstrate that

\( \left\langle\frac{3\left( S _{p} \cdot \hat{r}\right)\left( S _{e} \cdot \hat{r}\right)- S _{p} \cdot S _{e}}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=0 \),

for states with\( \ell=0 \text {. Hint: } \hat{r}=\sin \theta \cos \phi \hat{\imath}+\sin \theta \sin \phi \hat{\jmath}+\cos \theta \hat{k} \). Do the angular integrals first.

Admin
Solution
\( I \equiv \int( a \cdot \hat{r})( b \cdot \hat{r}) \sin \theta d \theta d \phi \)

\( =\int\left(a_{x} \sin \theta \cos \phi+a_{y} \sin \theta \sin \phi+a_{z} \cos \theta\right)\left(b_{x} \sin \theta \cos \phi+b_{y} \sin \theta \sin \phi+b_{z} \cos \theta\right) \sin \theta d \theta d \phi \).

\( \text { But } \int_{0}^{2 \pi} \sin \phi d \phi=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \cos \phi d \phi=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \sin \phi \cos \phi d \phi=0 \), so only three terms survive :

\( I=\int\left(a_{x} b_{x} \sin ^{2} \theta \cos ^{2} \phi+a_{y} b_{y} \sin ^{2} \theta \sin ^{2} \phi+a_{z} b_{z} \cos ^{2} \theta\right) \sin \theta d \theta d \phi \).

\( \text { But } \int_{0}^{2 \pi} \sin ^{2} \phi d \phi=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \cos ^{2} \phi d \phi=\pi, \quad \int_{0}^{2 \pi} d \phi=2 \pi \), so

\( I=\int_{0}^{\pi}\left[\pi\left(a_{x} b_{x}+a_{y} b_{y}\right) \sin ^{2} \theta+2 \pi a_{z} b_{z} \cos ^{2} \theta\right] \sin \theta d \theta . \quad \text { But } \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin ^{3} \theta d \theta=\frac{4}{3}, \int_{0}^{\pi} \cos ^{2} \theta \sin \theta d \theta=\frac{2}{3} \).

so \( I=\pi\left(a_{x} b_{x}+a_{y} b_{y}\right) \frac{4}{3}+2 \pi a_{z} b_{z} \frac{2}{3}=\frac{4 \pi}{3}\left(a_{x} b_{x}+a_{y} b_{y}+a_{z} b_{z}\right)=\frac{4 \pi}{3}( a \cdot b ) \). QED

[Alternatively, noting that I has to be a scalar bilinear in a and b, we know immediately that I = A(a.b), where A is some constant (same for all a and b). To determine A, pick \( a = b =\hat{k} \); then \( I=A=\int \cos ^{2} \theta \sin \theta d \theta d \phi=4 \pi / 3 .]\)

For states with \( \ell=0 \), the wave function is independent of θ and \( \phi\left(Y_{0}^{0}=1 / \sqrt{4 \pi}\right) \), so

\( \left\langle\frac{3\left( S _{p} \cdot \hat{r}\right)\left( S _{e} \cdot \hat{r}\right)- S _{p} \cdot S _{e}}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=\left\{\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{r^{3}}|\psi(r)|^{2} r^{2} d r\right\} \int\left[3\left( S _{p} \cdot \hat{r}\right)\left( S _{e} \cdot \hat{r}\right)- S _{p} \cdot S _{e}\right] \sin \theta d \theta d \phi \).

The first angular integral is \( 3(4 \pi / 3)\left( S _{p} \cdot S _{e}\right)=4 \pi\left( S _{p} \cdot S _{e}\right) \), while the second is \( -\left( S _{p} \cdot S _{e}\right) \int \sin \theta d \theta d \phi= -4 \pi\left( S _{p} \cdot S _{e}\right)\), so he two cancel, and the result is zero. QED [Actually, there is a little sleight-of-hand here,
since for \( \ell=0, \psi \rightarrow \text { constant as } r \rightarrow 0 \). and hence the radial integral diverges logarithmically at the origin.
Technically, the first term in Eq. 7.90 is the field outside an infinitesimal sphere; the delta-function gives the field inside. For this reason it is correct to do the angular integral first (getting zero) and not worry about the radial integral.]

\( B =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi r^{3}}[3( \mu \cdot \hat{r}) \hat{r}- \mu ]+\frac{2 \mu_{0}}{3} \mu \delta^{3}( r ) \) (7.90).
