Guest Question.

Consider the system of with \( m_{1} = m_{2} = 100 kg, k_{1} = k_{2} = 500 N/m \). Find the values of dashpots in the system that will give uncoupled modes with 10% damping in the lower-frequency mode and 1% damping in the higher-frequency mode.

Admin
Solution

Using

\( m =\begin{pmatrix} 100 & 0 \\ 0 & 100 \end{pmatrix} kg \) , \( k =\begin{pmatrix} 200 & -100 \\ -100 & 100 \end{pmatrix} \frac{N}{m} \)

Gives

\( \theta =\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 1.618 & -0.618 \end{pmatrix} \) , \( \lambda =\begin{pmatrix} 0.3820 & 0\\ 0 & 2.618 \end{pmatrix} (rad/s)^{2} \) , \( \hat{m} =\begin{pmatrix} 361.8 & 0\\ 0 & 138.2 \end{pmatrix} kg \)

Setting \( \omega _{j}=(\lambda _{j})^{1/2} \) and \( \beta _{j}=2\zeta _{j}\omega _{j} \) , with \( \zeta _{1}=0.10 \) and \( \zeta _{2}=0.01 \) , gives

\( \beta =\begin{pmatrix} 0.1236 & 0 \\ 0& 0.0324 \end{pmatrix} rad/s \)

Solving Eq. 8.22

\(\hat{c} \equiv \theta ^{T}c\theta =\hat{m} \beta \)

for \( c \) gives \( c=(\theta ^{-1})^{T}\hat{m} \beta \theta ^{-1} \) . Rather than invert a matrix that is not diagonal, we can now solve Eq. 8.18

\( \hat{m} \equiv \theta ^{T} m \theta\)

for the inverse as \( \theta ^{-1}=\hat {m}^{-1}\theta ^{T}m \) and use this to obtain

\( c=m\theta \beta \hat {m}^{-1}\theta ^{T}m=\begin{pmatrix} 5.758 & 4.081 \\4.081 & 9.839 \end{pmatrix} \)

(This technique for finding \( \theta ^{-1} \) is not particularly important for a 2DF but can save significant computational time for large matrices.) Using m, k, and c now gives the scalar equations of motion as

\( 100\ddot{X}_{1}(t)+5.758\dot{X}_{1}(t)+4.081\dot{X}_{2}(t)+200X_{1}(t)-100X_{2}(t)=F_{1}(t) \)

And

\( 100\ddot{X}_{2}(t)+4.081\dot{X}_{1}(t)+9.839\dot{X}_{2}(t)-100X_{1}(t)+100X_{2}(t)=F_{2}(t) \)

The accompanying sketch shows an arrangement of dashpots that will give these equations of motion- Note, in particular, that the value of the dashpot connecting the two masses is negative. Obviously, a negative dashpot is not a physical element, but that is the result obtained any time that an off-diagonal element of \( c \) is found to be positive. Furthermore, it is not unusual for such a procedure of assigning values of modal damping to result in a model that does not correspond to physical dashpots. This anomaly does not cause any difficulty in the mathematical analysis or any pathological behavior in the dynamic response, despite its apparent lack of logical explanation. One should always keep in mind, though, that even when it corresponds to positive dashpots, the linear damping matrix c usually represents no more than a crude approximation of the actual energy losses that limit the dynamic response of a real system.
