Guest Question.

Consider the \(130 \mathrm{~nm}\) BiCMOS CML latch with inductive peaking and \(4 \mathrm{~mA}\) tail current shown in Figure 11.22(a). All MOSFETs have 130nm gate length and the same parameters as in the previous examples. The HBTs have \(170 \mathrm{~nm}\)emitter width, \(J_{p f T}=8 \mathrm{~mA} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}, C_{b e}=23.2 p F \times\) \(I_{C}, C_{c s}^{\prime}=1.1 \mathrm{fF} / \mu \mathrm{m}, C_{b c}^{\prime}=11 \mathrm{f} \mathrm{F} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\), and the gain of the HBT differential pairs is \(A_{V}=2.85\). Find the size of \(\mathrm{M}_{1}, \mathrm{M}_{2}, Q_{1}-Q_{6}, R_{L}\), and \(L_{P}\) when the latch is driving a fanout-of-2 load. Calculate the bandwidth and estimate the data rate for the data path using the small signal equivalent circuit in Figure 11.22(b). Calculate the load capacitance seen by emitter-followers \(Q_{5}\) and \(Q_{6}\) on the clock path. \(Q_{5}\) and \(Q_{6}\)are biased at half the peak \(f_{T}\) current density, \(2 \mathrm{~mA}\) each. The voltage swing on the data path is \(300 \mathrm{mV}\) while that on the clock path is \(600 \mathrm{mV}\).

Admin
Solution

(a) \( W(\mathrm{M} 1, \mathrm{M} 2)=I_{T} /(0.3 \mathrm{~mA} / \mu \mathrm{m})=4 \mathrm{~mA} / 0.3=13 \mu \mathrm{m} \\\)
\( l_{E}\left(Q_{1}-Q_{4}\right)=4 \mathrm{~mA} /\left(0.17 \mu \mathrm{m} \times 12 \mathrm{~mA} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\right)=2 \mu \mathrm{m} \\\)
\( l_{E}\left(Q_{5}-Q_{6}\right)=2 \mathrm{~mA} /\left(0.17 \mu \mathrm{m} \times 4 \mathrm{~mA} / \mu \mathrm{m}^{2}\right)=3 \mu \mathrm{m} \\\)
\( R_{L}=\Delta \mathrm{V} / \mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{T}}=300 \mathrm{mV} / 4 \mathrm{~mA}=75 \Omega\) .
For k=1, the total capacitance seen by \( R_{L}\) , ignoring \( R_{b}\) is
\( C_{T}=C_{c s 1}+C_{b c 1}+C_{b c 3}+C_{c s 3}+C_{b e 4}+\left(1-A_{V}\right) C_{b c 4}+k\left(C_{b e}+\left(1-A_{V}\right) C_{b c}\right)=72 \mathrm{fF} \)
With these values for \( R_{L}\) and \( C_{T}\) , we obtain \( L=130 \mathrm{pH}\) and a bandwidth \( B W_{3 \mathrm{~dB}}=1.6 \times \frac{1}{2 \pi R_{L} \mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{T}}}=47 \mathrm{GHz}\) which is fast enough for operation at \( 62 \mathrm{~Gb} / \mathrm{s}\) .For k=2
\( C_{T}=C_{c s 1}+C_{b c 1}+C_{b c 3}+C_{c s 3}+C_{b e 4}+\left(1-A_{V}\right) C_{b c 4}+k\left(C_{b e}+\left(1-A_{V}\right) C_{b c}\right)=100.5 \mathrm{fF}\)
\( L_{P}=184 \mathrm{pH}\) and \( B W_{3 \mathrm{~dB}}=33.8 \mathrm{GHz}\) which is good enough for operation at \( 45 \mathrm{~Gb} / \mathrm{s}\)

(b) The load capacitance seen by each emitter-follower is \(C_{g s}(\mathrm{M} 1)+C_{g d}(\mathrm{M} 1)=13 \mathrm{fF}+6.5 \mathrm{fF}=19.5 \mathrm{fF}\), much smaller than the load capacitance, and the resistance at the clock input is also much smaller than \(R_{L}\).

Note that the HBT has no capacitance between the emitter and the substrate (or ground). The only other capacitance at that node comes from the padding resistance. The small load capacitance and the high \(f_{T}\) of the EF stage explains why the clock path is sufficiently fast to pass a \(45 \mathrm{GHz}\) clock signal even when the emitter-follower is biased at \(J_{p f T} / 2\), where the HBT \(f_{T}\) is still larger than \(100 \mathrm{GHz}\).
