Friction on a Banked Curve

A car is travelling on a curve of radius r banked at an angle \(\theta\). The coefficient of static friction between the car and the road is \(\mu_{s}\).

(a) Derive an expression for the maximum speed that the car can travel without slipping.

(b) Derive an expression for the normal force.

Admin
Solution

(a) Steps 1 and 2. Identify all the forces acting on the object, and draw the FBD.

The forces acting on the car are the force of gravity,
the normal force from the incline, and the force of friction, with the FBD shown in Figure 5-42. Since the situation involves the maximum speed beyond which the car will slip, the force of friction will point down along the incline to oppose that.

Steps 3 and 4. Establish the directions for the coordinate axes, and define the positive directions.

The car moves in a horizontal circle, and the acceleration points toward the centre of the circular path.

Therefore, we again choose a horizontal (radial) r-axis and a vertical y-axis. We will define the positive directions as upward for the y-axis and to the left for the r-axis.

Step 5. Resolve the forces into components along the coordinate axes.

In Figure 5-43 we show the components of the normal force and the force of friction. For the normal force, the components are \(N_{r}=N \sin \theta \text { and } N_{y}=N \cos \theta\).

The static friction force components are \(f_{r}=f_{ s } \cos \theta\) and \(f_{y}=-f_{ s } \sin \theta .\)

 

Step 6. Write the equations of motion along each axis.

The sum for the forces along the vertical direction gives us

 

\(\sum F_{y}=m a_{y}\)

 

Remembering that we have defined up as the positive direction,

 

\(N \cos \theta-m g-f_{ s } \sin \theta=0\) (1)

 

If the car is travelling at the maximum speed possible without slipping, then it is on the verge of slipping, and the force of static friction is at its maximum value. Therefore, we have

 

\(N \cos \theta-m g-N \mu_{ s } \sin \theta=0\) (2)

 

Next, we sum the forces along the radial direction:

 

\(\sum F_{r}=m a_{r}\)

 

We have defined the radial direction toward the centre of the circle as positive, and that is also the direction of the centripetal acceleration, so we have

 

\(N \sin \theta+N \mu_{ s } \cos \theta=\frac{m v^{2}}{r}\) (3)

 

Step 7. Solve the equations of motion.

Rearranging (2) gives

 

\(N \cos \theta-N \mu_{ s } \sin \theta=m g\) (4)

 

We divide (3) by (4) to eliminate N and m:

 

\(\frac{\sin \theta+\mu_{ s } \cos \theta}{\cos \theta-\mu_{ s } \sin \theta}=\frac{v^{2}}{r g}\)

 

\(v^{2}=\frac{r g\left(\sin \theta+\mu_{ s } \cos \theta\right)}{\cos \theta-\mu_{ s } \sin \theta}\)

 

\(v=\sqrt{\frac{r g\left(\sin \theta+\mu_{ s } \cos \theta\right)}{\cos \theta-\mu_{ s } \sin \theta}}\)

 

(b) We can use (4) to write an expression for the normal force:

 

\(N=\frac{m g}{\cos \theta-\mu_{ s } \sin \theta}\) (5)

 

Making sense of the result

 

When the coefficient of friction is zero, the value of the normal force reduces to \(m g / \cos \theta, \text { and } v^{2}\) reduces to \(r g \tan \theta\) , as we
obtained in Example 5-24.
