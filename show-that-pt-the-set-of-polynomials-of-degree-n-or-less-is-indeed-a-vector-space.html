Guest Question.

Show that \(P(t)\), the set of polynomials of degree \(n\) or less, is indeed a vector space.

Admin
Solution

We need to check all 10 axioms with the elements or vectors (polynomials) of this space which have the general polynomial form:

\(\begin{gathered}\mathbf{p}(t)=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}, \mathbf{q}(t)=d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n} \text { and } \\ \mathbf{r}(t)=e_{0}+e_{1} t+e_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+e_{n} t^{n}\end{gathered}\)
  1. Adding two vectors (two polynomials):
\(\begin{aligned}\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t) &=\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)}+\underbrace{d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)} \\&=\left(c_{0}+d_{0}\right)+\left(c_{1}+d_{1}\right) t+\left(c_{2}+d_{2}\right) t^{2}+\cdots+\left(c_{n}+d_{n}\right) t^{n}\end{aligned}\)

Hence \(\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t)\) is in \(P(t)\) which means that we have closure under addition.

  1. Commutative law:

We are required to show that \(\mathrm{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t)=\mathbf{q}(t)+\mathbf{p}(t)\)

\(\begin{aligned}\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t) &=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}+d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n} \\&=\underbrace{d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)}+\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)} \\&=\mathbf{q}(t)+\mathbf{p}(t)\end{aligned}\)
  1. Associative law:

We need to show that \([\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t)]+\mathbf{r}(t)=\mathbf{p}(t)+[\mathbf{q}(t)+\mathbf{r}(t)]\). Substituting the above polynomials into this yields:

\(\begin{gathered}{[\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t)]+\mathbf{r}(t)=\left[\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)}+\underbrace{d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)}\right] } \\\qquad \begin{array}{c}+\underbrace{e_{0}+e_{1} t+e_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+e_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{r}(t)} \\=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}+d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n} \\+e_{0}+e_{1} t+e_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+e_{n} t^{n}\end{array} \\=\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)} \\+\left((\underbrace{d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)}+\underbrace{e_{0}+e_{1} t+e_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+e_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{r}(t)})\right) \\=\mathbf{p}(t)+[\mathbf{q}(t)+\mathbf{r}(t)]\end{gathered}\)
  1. What is the zero vector in this case?

The zero polynomial \(\mathrm{O}\) is the real number 0 and is in the set \(P(t)\). Clearly

\(\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{O}=\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)}+0=\mathbf{p}(t)\)
  1. Additive inverse. For the polynomial \(\mathbf{p}(t)\) we have the inverse as \(-\mathrm{p}(t)\) and
\(\begin{aligned}\mathbf{p}(t)-\mathbf{p}(t) &=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}-\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}-c_{0}-c_{1} t-c_{2} t^{2}-\cdots-c_{n} t^{n} \\&=\left(c_{0}-c_{0}\right)+\left(c_{1}-c_{1}\right) t+\left(c_{2}-c_{2}\right) t^{2}+\cdots+\left(c_{n}-c_{n}\right) t^{n} \\&=0+0+0+\cdots+0=0=\mathrm{O}\end{aligned}\)
  1. Let \(k\) be a real scalar then
\(\begin{aligned}k \mathbf{p}(t) &=k\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=k c_{0}+k c_{1} t+k c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+k c_{n} t^{n}\end{aligned}\)

Hence \(k \mathbf{p}(t)\) is also in the space \(P(t)\) because it is a polynomial of degree less than or equal to \(n\).

  1. Associative law for scalar multiplication. Let \(k_{1}\) and \(k_{2}\) be real scalars:
\(\begin{aligned}k_{1}\left(k_{2} \mathbf{p}(t)\right) &=k_{1}\left(k_{2}\left[c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right]\right) \\&=k_{1}\left(k_{2} c_{0}+k_{2} c_{1} t+k_{2} c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+k_{2} c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=k_{1} k_{2} c_{0}+k_{1} k_{2} c_{1} t+k_{1} k_{2} c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+k_{1} k_{2} c_{n} t^{n} \\&=k_{1} k_{2} \underbrace{\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right)}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)} \quad[\text { factorizing }] \\&=\left(k_{1} k_{2}\right) \mathbf{p}(t)\end{aligned}\)
  1. Distributive law. Let \(k\) be a real scalar, then we have:
\(\begin{aligned}k[\mathbf{p}(t)+\mathbf{q}(t)] &=k\left[\underbrace{c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)}+\underbrace{d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n} t^{n}}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)}\right] \\&=k c_{0}+k c_{1} t+k c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+k c_{n} t^{n}+k d_{0}+k d_{1} t+k d_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+k d_{n} t^{n} \\& \underbrace{=}_{\text {Factorising }} k\underbrace{\left(c_{0}+c_{1}t+c_{2}t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n}t^{n}\right)}_{=\mathbf{p}(t)}+k \underbrace{\left(d_{0}+d_{1} t+d_{2}t^{2}+\cdots+d_{n}t^{n}\right)}_{=\mathbf{q}(t)} \\&=k \mathbf{p}(t)+k \mathbf{q}(t)\end{aligned}\)
  1. Distributive law for scalars. Let \(k_{1}\) and \(k_{2}\) be real scalars:
\(\begin{aligned}\left(k_{1}+k_{2}\right) \mathbf{p}(t) &=\left(k_{1}+k_{2}\right)\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2}t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=k_{1}\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right)+k_{2}\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=k_{1} \mathbf{p}(t)+k_{2} \mathbf{p}(t)\end{aligned}\)
  1. The identity element is the real number 1 . We have
\(\begin{aligned}1 \mathbf{p}(t) &=1\left(c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=\left(1 \times c_{0}\right)+\left(1 \times c_{1} t\right)+\left(1 \times c_{2} t^{2}\right)+\cdots+\left(1\times c_{n} t^{n}\right) \\&=c_{0}+c_{1} t+c_{2} t^{2}+\cdots+c_{n} t^{n}=\mathrm{p}(t)\end{aligned}\)

All 10 axioms are satisfied, therefore the set \(P(t)\) of polynomials of degree \(n\) or less is a vector space.
