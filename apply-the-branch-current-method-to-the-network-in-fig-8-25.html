Guest Question.

Apply the branch-current method to the network in Fig. 8.25.

Admin
Solution

Solution 1:

Step 1: Since there are three distinct branches (cda, cba, ca), three currents of arbitrary directions \((I_1, I_2, I_3)\) are chosen, as indicated in Fig. 8.25. The current directions for \(I_1 and I_2\) were chosen to match the “pressure” applied by sources \(E_1 and E_2\), respectively. Since both \(I_1 and I_2\) enter node a, \(I_3\) is leaving.

Step 2: Polarities for each resistor are drawn to agree with assumed current directions, as indicated in Fig. 8.26.

Step 3: Kirchhoff’s voltage law is applied around each closed loop (1 and 2) in the clockwise direction:

\(loop 1: \sum\limits_{\circlearrowright }{V} =\overset{\underset{\downarrow}{Rise \ in \ Potintial}}{+} E_1\underset{\underset{Drop\; in \; Potential}{\uparrow}}{-}V_{R_1}\underset{\underset{Drop\; in \; Potential}{\uparrow}}{-}V_{R_3}=0\)

 

and \(loop 2: \sum\limits_{\circlearrowright }{V} =\overset{\underset{\downarrow}{Rise \ in \ Potintial}}{+} V_{R_3}\overset{\underset{\downarrow}{Rise \ in \ Potintial}}{+}V_{R_2}\underset{\underset{Drop\; in \; Potential}{\uparrow}}{-}E_{2}=0\)

 

\(loop 1: \sum\limits_{\circlearrowright }{V} =+\underbrace{2V}_{Battery \> Potintial}-\underbrace{(2\Omega)I_1}_{\begin{matrix} Voltage\ drop\ across \\ 2 \ \Omega \ resistor \end{matrix} }-\underbrace{(4\Omega)I_3}_{\begin{matrix} Voltage\ drop\ across \\ 4 \ \Omega \ resistor \end{matrix}}=0\)

 

\(loop 2: \sum\limits_{\circlearrowright }{V} =(4\Omega)I_3+(1\Omega)I_2-6V=0\)

Step 4: Applying Kirchhoff’s current law at node a (in a two-node network, the law is applied at only one node) gives

\(I_1 + I_2 = I_3\)

Step 5: There are three equations and three unknowns (units removed for clarity):

\(2-2I_1-4I_3=0\qquad Rewritten:2I_1+0+4I_3=2\\[0.5cm] 4I_3+1I_2-6=0\hspace{30 pt} \hspace{30 pt} \quad \enspace 0+I_2+4I_3=6\\[0.5cm] \qquad \enspace I_1+I_2=I_3\hspace{30 pt} \quad \enspace \qquad \quad I_1+I_2-I_3=0\)

Using third-order determinants (Appendix B), we have

 

\(I_1= \frac{\quad \left|\begin{matrix} 2 & 0 & 4 \\ 6 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 \end{matrix} \right| }{D=\left|\begin{matrix} 2 & 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 \end{matrix}\right| } =\overset{\underset{\downarrow }{\begin{matrix} A\ negative\ sign\ in\ front\ of\ a \\ branch\ current\ indicates\ only\\ that\ the\ actual\ current\ is\\ in\ the\ direction\ opposite\ to \\ that\ assumed. \end{matrix}}}{-}1A\)

 

\(I_2=\frac{\left|\begin{matrix} 2 & 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 6 & 4 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \end{matrix} \right|}{D} =2A \)

 

\(I_3=\frac{\left|\begin{matrix} 2 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 6 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right|}{D} =1A\)

Solution 2: Instead of using third-order determinants as in Solution 1, we can reduce the three equations to two by substituting the third equation in the first and second equations:

\(\left. \begin{matrix} 2-2I_1-4\overbrace{(I_1+I_2)}^{I_3}=0 \\ \underline{4\overbrace{(I_1+I_2)}^{I_3}+I_2-6=0} \end{matrix} \right\} \begin{matrix}2-2I_1-4I_1-4I_2=0 \\ \underline{4I_1+4I_2+I_2-6=0} \end{matrix} \)

 

or \(\begin{matrix}-6I_1-4I_2=-2 \\ \underline{+4I_1+5I_2=+6} \end{matrix}\)

Multiplying through by -1 in the top equation yields

\(\begin{matrix}6I_1-4I_2=+2 \\ \underline{4I_1+5I_2=+6} \end{matrix} \)

and using determinants gives

\(I_1=\frac{\begin{vmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 6 & 5 \end{vmatrix} }{\begin{vmatrix} 6 & 4 \\ 4 & 5 \end{vmatrix} } =\frac{10-24}{30-16}=\frac{-14}{14}=-1A \)
