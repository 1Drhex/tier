Guest Question.

Replace the cable of , which is loaded uniformly along the horizontal, by a cable which has a mass of 12 kg per meter of its own length and supports its own weight only. The cable is suspended between two points on the same level 300 m apart and has a sag of 60 m. Find the tension at midlength, the maximum tension, and the total length of the cable.

Admin
Solution

With a load distributed uniformly along the length of the cable, the solution of part (c) of Art. 5/8 applies, and we have a catenary shape of the cable. Equations 5/20 \(s=\frac{T_{0} }{\mu }\sinh \frac{\mu x}{T_{0}}\) and 5/21 \(T=T_{0} \cosh \frac{\mu x}{T_{0}}\) for the cable length and tension both involve the minimum tension \(T_{0} \) at midlength, which must be found from Eq. 5/19 \(y=\frac{T_{0} }{\mu } (\cosh \frac{\mu x}{T_{0}} -1)\) . Thus, for x = 150 m, y = 60 m, and \(\mu = 12(9.81)(10^{-3}) = 0.1177 \)kN/m, we have

 

\(60=\frac{T_{0} }{0.1177} [\cosh \frac{(0.1177)(150)}{T_{0}}-1 ]\)

 

or \( \frac{7.06}{T_{0}}=\cosh \frac{17.66}{T_{0}}-1\)

This equation can be solved graphically. We compute the expression on each side of the equals sign and plot it as a function of \(T_{0}\) . The intersection of the two curves establishes the equality and determines the correct value of \(T_{0}\) . This plot is shown in the figure accompanying this problem and yields the solution

\(T_{0}= 23.2 \) kN

Alternatively, we may write the equation as

\(ƒ(T_{0})=\cosh \frac{17.66}{T_{0}}-\frac{7.06}{T_{0}}-1=0\)

and set up a computer program to calculate the value(s) of \(T_{0}\) which renders \(ƒ(T_{0}) = 0\) . See Art. C/11 of Appendix C for an explanation of one applicable numerical method.
The maximum tension occurs for maximum y and from Eq. 5/22 \(T=T_{0} +\mu y \) is

\(T_{max}=23.2 + (0.1177)(60) = 30.2 \) kN

From Eq. 5/20 the total length of the cable becomes

\(2s=2\frac{23.2}{0.1177}\sinh \frac{(0.1177)(150)}{23.2} =330 \) m

Helpful Hint
1) Note that the solution of for the parabolic cable gives a very close approximation to the values for the catenary even though we have a fairly large sag. The approximation is even better for smaller sag-to span ratios.
