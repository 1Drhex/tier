Guest Question.

Consider the nonlinear oscillator described by

\( \ddot{X}(t)+c\dot{X}(t)\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]+kX^{3}(t)\dot{X}^{2}(t)=F(t) \)

in which \( \left\{F(t)\right\} \) is a mean-zero, stationary, delta-correlated process with autospectral density \( S_{0} \) . Write the general state-space moment equations for the system, and find the approximate stationary solution that results from Gaussian closure.

Admin
Solution

Using the alternative approach presented in , we can obtain appropriate state-space moment equations by multiplying the equation of motion by the components of the \( \vec{Y}(t) \equiv [X(t),\dot{X}(t)]^{T} \) state vector, then taking the expected values. The resulting equations are

\( E[X(t)\ddot{X}(t)]+cE\left(X(t)\dot{X}(t)\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right) +kE[X^{4}(t)\dot{X}^{2}(t)]=E[X(t)F(t)]=0 \)

And

\( E[\dot{X}(t)\ddot{X}(t)]+cE\left(\dot{X}^{2}(t)\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right) +kE[X^{3}(t)\dot{X}^{3}(t)]=E[\dot{X}(t)F(t)]=\pi S_{0} \)

For stationary response, though, we can use the simplifications that \( E[\dot{X}(t)\ddot{X}(t)]=0,E[X (t)\ddot{X}(t)]=-E[\dot{X}^{2}(t)] \) , and \( E(X(t)\dot{X}(t) \exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)])=0 \) . The last of these three relationships follows from the fact that the term has the form \( E(\dot{X}(t)\acute{f} [X(t)]) \) , which is exactly the derivative with respect to \( t \) of \( E(f [X(t)]) \) . Using these conditions gives the exact state-space equations for stationary response as

\( -E[\dot{X}^{2}(t)]+kE[X^{4}(t)\dot{X}^{2}(t)]=0 \)

And

\( cE\left(\dot{X}^{2}(t)\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right)+kE[X^{3}(t)\dot{X}^{3}(t)] =\pi S_{0} \)

Now we introduce the Gaussian assumption to further simplify the equations. In particular, we presume that \( X(t) \) and \( \dot{X}(t) \) are jointly Gaussian, which requires that they also be independent inasmuch as they are uncorrelated. This gives

\( E[X^{4}(t)\dot{X}^{2}(t)]=E[X^{4}(t)]E[\dot{X}^{2}(t)]=3\sigma ^{4}_{X} \sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}} \) \( E[X^{3}(t)\dot{X}^{3}(t)]=E[X^{3}(t)]E[\dot{X}^{3}(t)]=0 \)

And

\( E\left(\dot{X}^{2}(t)\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right)=E[\dot{X}^{2}(t)]E\left(\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right) \)

and the Gaussian distribution allows the final term to be evaluated as

\( E\left(\exp [-\alpha X^{2}(t)]\right) =\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma _{X}} \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{\exp \left(-u^{2}\left[\frac{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}\alpha ^{2}+1}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}} \right] \right) } du \) \( =\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1/2}(2\sigma ^{2}_{X}\alpha ^{2}+1^{1/2})}\int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{e^{-w^{2}/2}}dw=\frac{1}{(2\sigma ^{2}_{X}\alpha ^{2}+1)^{1/2}} \)

Thus, the state-space equations become

\( -\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}+3k\sigma ^{4}_{X}\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}=0 \)

And

\( \frac{c\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}}{(2\sigma ^{2}_{X}\alpha ^{2}+1)^{1/2}} =\pi S_{0} \)

From the first of these equations, we determine that \( \sigma ^{2}_{X}=(3k)^{-1/2} \) , the second equation then gives

\( \sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{c} (2\sigma ^{2}_{X}\alpha ^{2}+1)^{1/2}=\frac{\pi S_{0}}{c}\left(\frac{2\alpha ^{2}}{(3k)^{1/2}}+1 \right)^{1/2} \)
