LIFT AND DRAG COEFFICIENTS OF A SUPERSONIC AIRFOIL

The airplane of has a symmetric diamond cross section (sharp leading and trailing edges of angle \(δ = 6\)°). For a speed of 600 m/s in air at 4°C and 100 kPa, find the pressure distribution on the upper and lower surfaces, and the lift and drag coefficients for an angle of attack of \(α = 6\)°.

Admin
Solution

Given: Air flow over symmetric section shown with:

\(\begin{array}{lrl}p_{1} & =100 \mathrm{kPa} & \delta & =6^{\circ} \\\quad\\T_{1} & =4^{\circ} \mathrm{C} & \alpha & =6^{\circ} \\\quad\\V_{1} & =600 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} & &\end{array}\)

 

Find: Pressure distribution, and lift and drag coefficients.
Solution:
We first need to obtain the pressures on the four surfaces of the airfoil. We have already obtained in the data for Region \(2_{u}\) and Region \(2_{l}\) :

\(\begin{aligned}M_{2_{\text {(upper) }}}=1.90 \quad\quad p_{2_{\text {(upper) }}}=85.8 \mathrm{kPa} \quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_{2_{\text {(upper) }}}}\\\quad\\M_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=1.489 \quad\quad p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=158 \mathrm{kPa}\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}}\end{aligned}\)

(Note that \(M_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=1.489\) is obtained from \(M_{1_{n}}=1.223\) in by direct use of Eqs. 13.48 a and 13.47 b.)

\(M_{2_{n}}^{2}=\frac{M_{1_{n}}^{2}+\frac{2}{k-1}}{\frac{2 k}{k-1} M_{1_{n}}^{2}-1}\) (13.48 a)

\(M_{2_N}=\frac{V_{2_n}}{c_2}=M_2 sin (\beta-\theta)\) (13.47 b)

In addition, for Region \(2_{u}\), we found the Prandtl-Meyer function to be \(23.7^{\circ}\). Hence, for Region \(3_{u}\), we can find the value of the Prandtl-Meyer function from the deflection angle. For \(6^{\circ}\) leading and trailing edges, the airfoil angles at the upper and lower surfaces are each \(174^{\circ} .\) Hence, at the upper and lower surfaces the deflections are each \(6^{\circ} .\)
For Region \(3_{u}\),

\(\omega_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}=\omega_{2_{\text {(upper) }}}+\theta=23.7^{\circ}+6^{\circ}=29.7^{\circ}\)

For this Prandtl-Meyer function value, \(M_{3_\text{(upper)}}\) is obtained from Eq. 13.55,

 

\(\omega_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}=\sqrt{\frac{k+1}{k-1}} \tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{\frac{k-1}{k+1}\left(M_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}^{2}-1\right)}\right)-\tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{M_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}^{2}-1}\right)\)

 

This can be solved using manual iteration or interpolation, or by using, for example, Excel’s Goal Seek function,

\(M_{3(\text { upper })}=2.12\)

Finally, we can find \(p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}\) from repeated use of Eq. 12.21a,

\(\begin{aligned}p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}} &=\frac{p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}}{p_{0}} \frac{p_{0}}{p_{1}} p_{1}=\left\{\left[1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1}^{2}\right]^{k /(k-1)} /\left[1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}^{2}\right]^{k /(k-1)}\right\} p_{1} \\\quad\\&=\left\{\left[1+(0.2) 1.80^{2}\right]^{3.5} /\left[1+(0.2) 2.12^{2}\right]^{3.5}\right\} \times 100 \mathrm{kPa}\end{aligned}\)

so

\(p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}=60.9 \mathrm{kPa}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}}\)

For Region \(3_{l}\), we first need to find the Prandtl-Meyer function in the previous region, Region \(2_{l}\). For \(M_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=1.489\), we find from Eq. 13.55,

\(\begin{aligned}\omega_{2_{\text {(lower) }}} &{=\sqrt{\frac{k+1}{k-1}} \tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{\frac{k-1}{k+1}\left(M^{2}_{2_{\text {(lower) }}-1}\right)}\right)}-\tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{M^{2}_{2_{\text {(lower) }}-1}}\right) \\\quad\\&=\sqrt{\frac{1.4+1}{1.4-1}} \tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{\frac{1.4-1}{1.4+1}\left(1.489^{2}-1\right)}\right)-\tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{1.489^{2}-1}\right)\end{aligned}\)

so

 

\(\omega_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=11.58^{\circ}\)
Hence, for Region \(3_{l}\),

\(\omega_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}=\omega_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}+\theta=11.58^{\circ}+6^{\circ}=17.6^{\circ}\)

and \(M_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}\) is obtained from Eq. 13.55,

\(\omega_{3_{(\text {lower })}}=\sqrt{\frac{k+1}{k-1}} \tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{\frac{k-1}{k+1}\left(M_{3_{\text {(lower) }}^{2}}-1\right)}\right)-\tan ^{-1}\left(\sqrt{M_{3_{\text {(lower) }}^{2}}^{2}-1}\right)\)

Once again, this can be solved using manual iteration or interpolation, or by using, for example, Excel’s Goal Seek function,

\(M_{3_{(\text {lower })}}=1.693\)
Finally, we can find \(p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}\) from repeated use of Eq. 12.21 a,

\(\begin{gathered}p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}=\frac{p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}}{p_{0_{2}}} \frac{p_{0_{2}}}{p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}} p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}=\left\{\left[1+\frac{k-1}{2} M^{2}_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}\right]^{k /(k-1)} /\left[1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}^{2}\right]^{k /(k-1)}\right\} p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}} \\\quad\\=\left\{\left[1+(0.2) 1.489^{2}\right]^{3.5} /\left[1+(0.2) 1.693^{2}\right]^{3.5}\right\} \times 158 \mathrm{kPa}\end{gathered}\)

Hence,

\(p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}=117 \mathrm{kPa}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}}\)

 

(Note that we cannot use \(p_0\), the stagnation pressure of the incoming flow for computing this pressure, because the flow experienced a shock before reaching the lower surface.)
To compute the lift and drag coefficients, we need the lift and drag force.
First we find the vertical and horizontal forces with respect to coordinates orthogonal to the airfoil.
The vertical force (assuming the chord \(c\) and span \(s\) are in meters) is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}&F_{V}=s \frac{c}{2}\left\{\left(p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}+p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}\right)-\left(p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}+p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}\right)\right\} \\\quad\\&F_{V}=s(\mathrm{~m}) \frac{c(\mathrm{~m})}{2}\{(158+117)-(85.8+60.9)\}(\mathrm{kPa})=64.2 s c \mathrm{kN}\end{aligned}\)

and the horizontal force by

\(\begin{aligned}&F_{H}=s \frac{c}{2} \tan 3^{\circ}\left\{\left(p_{2_{\text {(upper) }}}+p_{2_{\text {(lower) }}}\right)-\left(p_{3_{\text {(upper) }}}+p_{3_{\text {(lower) }}}\right)\right\} \\\quad\\&F_{H}=s(\mathrm{~m}) \frac{c(\mathrm{~m})}{2} \tan 3^{\circ}\{(85.8+158)-(60.9+117)\}(\mathrm{kPa})=1.73 \mathrm{sc} \mathrm{kN}\end{aligned}\)

The lift and drag forces (per unit plan area) are then

\(F_{L}=F_{V} \cos 6^{\circ}-F_{H} \sin 6^{\circ}=63.6 \mathrm{sc} \mathrm{kN}\)

and

\(F_{D}=F_{V} \sin 6^{\circ}+F_{H} \cos 6^{\circ}=8.42 \mathrm{sc} \mathrm{kN}\)

The lift and drag coefficients require the air density,

\(\rho=\frac{p}{R T}=100 \times 10^{3} \frac{\mathrm{N}}{\mathrm{m}^{2}} \times \frac{1}{287} \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}}{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \times \frac{1}{277 \mathrm{~K}}=1.258 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\)

 

The lift coefficient is then

\(C_{L}= \frac{F_{L}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} s c}=2 \times 63.6 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~N} \times \frac{1}{1.258} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg}}\times \frac{1}{(600)^{2}} \frac{\mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~m}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}=0.281 \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad C_L}\)

 

and the drag coefficient is

 

\(C_{D}= \frac{F_{D}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} s c}=2 \times 8.42 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~N} \times \frac{1}{1.258} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{\mathrm{~kg}} \frac{1}{(600)^{2}} \frac{\mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~m}^{2}}\times \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}=0.037 \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad C_D}\)

 

(Note that instead of using \(\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}\) in the denominator of the coefficients, we could have used \(\frac{1}{2} k p M^{2}\).) The lift-drag ratio is approximately 7.6.

 

This Example illustrates the use of oblique shock and isentropic expansion wave equations to determine the pressure distribution on an airfoil.

  • We did not need to analyze the flow after the trailing expansion waves and oblique shock—unlike subsonic flow, the downstream condition has no effect on the airfoil.
  • Unlike a subsonic flow, a supersonic flow can generate drag even in the absence of boundary layers and flow separation.
  • Note that, unlike a subsonic flow, a supersonic flow can negotiate a sharp corner, even if we include the effect of a viscous boundary layer (as we have not done here). This is because an expanding supersonic flow has a negative pressure gradient, i, e., it is not adverse!
  • An actual airfoil is not likely to have planar surfaces, so more sophisticated techniques than we can cover here are needed. However, this example illustrates the kind of results to be expected when analyzing a supersonic airfoil.

The Excel workbooks for oblique shocks and isentropic expansion waves are convenient for performing these calculations. (Alternatively, the normal and oblique shock relations, and the isentropic expansion wave Excel add-ins, available on the Web site, are useful for these calculations.)
