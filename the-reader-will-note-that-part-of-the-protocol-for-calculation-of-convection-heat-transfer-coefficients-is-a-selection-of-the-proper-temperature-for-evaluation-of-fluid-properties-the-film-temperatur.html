Guest Question.

The reader will note that part of the protocol for calculation of convection heat-transfer coefficients is a selection of the proper temperature for evaluation of fluid properties. The film temperature is usually selected for flow over external surfaces, while the average bulk temperature is usually elected for flow in tubes and the like. The stronger the dependence of fluid properties on temperature, the more important is the selection of the proper value for calculation of the properties. It turns out that when convection with ideal gases is involved, the selection of temperature for property evaluation is not as critical as it is for liquids.

Admin
Solution

First, recall that an ideal gas is one that obeys the equation of state \(p = \rho RT\) . Over a pressure range of a few atmospheres a gas like air will have properties like thermal conductivity, k, dynamic viscosity,\( \mu \), and specific heat \(c_{p}\), essentially independent of pressure. Furthermore, we find that the Prandtl number \(Pr = c_{p}\mu /k\) is very nearly constant with temperature and equal to about 0.7. This implies that \(\mu\) varies directly as k.

If we plot \(\mu\) as a function of absolute temperature for air, as obtained from Table A-5, we obtain the curve shown in Figure Example 12-3a. Performing a curve fit to the data indicates that \(\mu\) varies with the absolute temperature to the 0.76 power. This means that k also varies approximately with T to the 0.76 power. For a constant pressure with an ideal gas we also have

\(\rho≈ \frac{1}{T}\)
\(\beta = \frac{1}{T}\) (exactly, for ideal gas only)
\( \mu≈ T^{ 0.76}\)
\(k ≈ T^{ 0.76}\)

Examining the different correlations for convection we find they take the following forms:

Laminar free convection:

\( h ≈ k × f(body dimensions,\Delta T) ×\left[\left(\frac{\rho ^{2}\beta }{ \mu ^{2}}\right) Pr\right]^{ 1/4} \)

Laminar forced convection over a flat plate:

\( h ≈ k × f(body dimensions, free-stream velocity) × \left(\frac{\rho }{ \mu}\right)^{1/2} Pr^{ 1/3}\)

Turbulent forced convection in smooth tubes:

\( h ≈ k × f(mass flow, tube diameter) × \left(\frac{1 }{ \mu}\right)^{0.8} Pr^{0.4}\)

Forced convection across tubes (4,000 < Re < 40,000):

\( h ≈ k × f(body dimension, free-stream velocity) × \left(\frac{\rho }{ \mu}\right)^{0.618} Pr^{ 1/3} \)

Forced convection across tubes (40,000 < Re < 400,000):

\( h ≈ k × f(body dimensions, free-stream velocity) × \left(\frac{\rho }{ \mu}\right)^{0.805} Pr^{ 1/3}\)

Inserting the temperature dependence of \(\rho, \beta , \mu, \) and k in these relations, along with an assumed constant value of Pr gives

\(h ≈ T^{ −0.37} \) laminar free convection
\(≈ T^{ −0.12}\) laminar forced convection over a flat plate
\(≈ T ^{0.15} \) turbulent forced convection in a smooth tube
\(≈ T ^{−0.328} \) forced convection over tubes(4,000 < Re < 40,000)
\(≈ T^{ −0.657 }\) forced convection across tubes(40,000 < Re < 400,000)

Plots of the temperature dependence of the convection coefficient h for these different flow circumstances over the temperature range of 200 to 400 K are shown in Figure Example 12-3b. While the variation is large at the outer temperature ranges for some cases, it is surprisingly small for others. For modest variations of ± 25 K from the mean of 300 K, the variation for all cases is small. The net result of these calculations is that in preliminary design studies of convection heat transfer employing ideal gases, one need not be overly careful about the temperature used for property determination. For operation near room temperature a value of 300 K will probably be satisfactory Please note that the foregoing analysis and conclusions do not apply to convection problems involving liquids.
