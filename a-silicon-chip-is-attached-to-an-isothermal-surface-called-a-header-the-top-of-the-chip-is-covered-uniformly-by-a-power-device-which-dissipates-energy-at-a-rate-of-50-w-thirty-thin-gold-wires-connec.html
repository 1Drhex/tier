Guest Question.

A silicon chip is attached to an isothermal surface called a header. The top of the chip is covered uniformly by a power device which dissipates energy at a rate of 50 W. Thirty thin gold wires connect the top with the header. Assume the entropy to be conducted down through the chip in one direction only. What will the steady-state temperature be at the top of the chip, if the header is kept at a temperature of 25°C?

The chip has a surface area of 0.51 cm by 0.51 cm. It is composed of three layers. The first is made out of silicon with a thickness of 0.051 cm. The chip carries a thin layer of gold at the bottom; its thickness is 0.010 cm. Between these two a thin layer of silicon dioxide forms, which has a thickness of 0.00013 cm. The thermal conductivities with respect to energy are 88 W/ (K · m), 312 W/(K · m), and 0.157 W/(K · m), respectively. The gold wires are 0.130 cm long and have a diameter of 0.0254 cm.

Admin
Solution

The device represents a thermal circuit with elements in parallel and in series (see figure). A constant thermal driving force is maintained over the circuit. We must figure out the total thermal resistance offered by the circuit to the flow of entropy and energy. The chip has a resistance of

\(\begin{aligned} R_{E, \text { chip }} &=\sum_{i=1}^{3} \frac{1}{k_{E, i}} \frac{\Delta x_{i}}{A} \\ &=\frac{1}{0.0051^{2}}\left(\frac{0.051 \cdot 10^{-2}}{88}+\frac{1.3 \cdot 10^{-6}}{0.157}+\frac{0.010 \cdot 10^{-2}}{312}\right) \frac{\mathrm{K}}{\mathrm{W}}=0.554 \mathrm{~K} / \mathrm{W} \end{aligned}\)

 

This device is connected in parallel with thirty identical wires. Therefore, the total resistance is calculated to be

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{R_{E, \text { tot }}} &=\sum_{i=1}^{31} \frac{1}{R_{E, i}}=30 \frac{k_{E} A}{\Delta x}+\frac{1}{R_{E, \text { chip }}} \\ &=30 \frac{312 \cdot \pi\left(1.27 \cdot 10^{-4}\right)^{2}}{0.0013} \frac{\mathrm{W}}{\mathrm{K}}+\frac{1}{0.554} \frac{\mathrm{W}}{\mathrm{K}}=2.17 \frac{\mathrm{W}}{\mathrm{K}} \end{aligned}\)

 

This corresponds to a resistance of \(0.461 K/W\). With the energy flux given, we can calculate the thermal driving force, i.e., the difference of temperatures between the top and the bottom of the chip:

\(\begin{aligned} \Delta T &=R_{E} I_{E, t h} \\ &=0.461 \mathrm{~K} / \mathrm{W} \cdot 50 \mathrm{~W}=23.0 \mathrm{~K} \end{aligned}\)

 

The temperature at the top of the chip is \(48°C.\) Without the gold wires it would be \(53°C.\)
