Guest Question.

Derive a simplified governing equation of motion to investigate the whirling phenomenon.

Admin
Solution

Consider the schematic of a rotating shaft in Figure 3.44 showing a thin disk of mass \(m\) attached to a shaft with a mass that is so small when compared to \(m\) that it can be ignored. If damping is neglected, the only force acting on \(m\) is due to the elastic restoring force of the bending shaft.

The simplest and most useful model in the study of the flexural behavior of rotors is called the Jeffcott43 rotor. The support structure is assumed to be rigid. In addition to the rotation, the shaft will oscillate in bending as a beam, although this behavior is ignored here.

How does the originally straight shaft bend as it rotates due to its operating speed? Imperfections in the shaft and the disk can create an unbalance of forces resulting in a net centripetal force and a bent shaft. It is the rotation about the bearing axis of the plane that contains the bent shaft, shown shaded in Figure 3.44.

Figure 3.45 shows an exaggerated view of the disk cross section where \(C_{m}\) is the disk center of mass and\(C_{s}\) is the geometric center of the shaft. The distance between them is the eccentricity ε due to imperfections in the disk. If the disk is perfect, the eccentricity equals zero, and, in theory, the shaft does not experience vibration at any speed. In practice, whirling behavior may still occur
due to shaft imperfections.

We define the following position vectors:44

\(\begin{aligned}r _{ O C _{ m }} &=(x+\varepsilon \cos \Omega) i +(y+\varepsilon \sin \Omega) j \\r &=x i +y j \\\left| r _{ C _{z} C _{ m }}\right| &=\varepsilon\end{aligned}\)

where the unit vectors are i and j, ω is the rotational
speed of the disk, which is considered a general function
of time, and Ω = ωt. Figure 3.45 defines the angular position of \(C_{s}\) by the angleθ, with ˙θ as the rotation speed of the plane formed by the bent shaft and its axis. If ˙θ is a constant then θ = ωt. In general \(\dot{\theta} \neq \omega\). To derive the equations of motion45 in the x and y directions, we use the same procedure as for the rotating unbalance problem, leading to the two governing equations,

\(\begin{aligned}&m \frac{d^{2}}{d t^{2}}(x+\varepsilon \cos \Omega)+k_{x} x=0 \\&m \frac{d^{2}}{d t^{2}}(y+\varepsilon \sin \Omega)+k_{y} y=0\end{aligned}\)

where \(k_{x}\) and \(k_{y}\) represent the support stiffnesses in the x and y directions, respectively. The two equations governing x and y motions are uncoupled, meaning that in
theory motions in the two directions are independent. This is a result of the prior assumptions and may generally not be the case. Carrying out the derivatives on the left-hand side where Ω is a function of time, we find

\(\begin{aligned}&m\left(\ddot{x}-\varepsilon \dot{\Omega}^{2} \cos \Omega-\varepsilon \ddot{\Omega} \sin \Omega\right)+k_{x} x=0 \\&m\left(\ddot{y}-\varepsilon \dot{\Omega}^{2} \sin \Omega+\varepsilon \ddot{\Omega} \cos \Omega\right)+k_{y} y=0\end{aligned}\)

Assuming that ω is constant and that there is synchronous
whirl, that is, ˙Ω = ω and therefore ¨Ω = ω˙ = 0,

Equations 3.55 and 3.56 become

\(\begin{aligned}&m\left(\ddot{x}-\varepsilon \omega^{2} \cos \omega t\right)+k_{x} x=0 \\&m\left(\ddot{y}-\varepsilon \omega^{2} \sin \omega t\right)+k_{y} y=0\end{aligned}\)

Dividing by \(m\), the equations of motion are

\(\begin{aligned}&\ddot{x}+\omega_{n x}^{2} x=\varepsilon \omega^{2} \cos \omega t \\&\ddot{y}+\omega_{n y}^{2} y=\varepsilon \omega^{2} \sin \omega t\end{aligned}\)

which can be solved using the techniques presented in
this chapter.

For the case where \(k_{x}\) = \(k_{y}\) = k and therefore \(\omega_{n x}^{2}=\) \(\omega_{n y}^{2}=\omega_{n}^{2}\), with \(z=x+i y\), Equations 3.57 and 3.58 can be added and combined into one complex parametrized equation,

\(\ddot{x}+i \ddot{y}+\omega_{n}^{2}(x+i y)=a \omega^{2}(\cos \omega t+i \sin \omega t)\),

where each original equation can be recovered by taking the real or imaginary parts of Equation 3.59. From the definition for \(z\) and Euler’s formula, Equation 3.59 becomes

\(\ddot{z}+\omega_{n}^{2} z=\varepsilon \omega^{2} e^{i \omega t}\),

with the particular solution \(z=z_{0} e^{i \omega t}\), where

\(z_{0}=\frac{g \omega^{2}}{\omega_{n}^{2}-\omega^{2}}\).

The critical value of the disk rotation frequency ω is
\(\omega_{c r}=\omega_{n}\). For the undamped case, the critical flexural speed is defined as the speed at which the frequency of
rotation equals the natural frequency of the system.46 If
\(z\) represents the rotation of the plane of the bent shaft
about the bearing axis and rotates with speed ω in the
\(x y\) plane, Equation 3.60 can be recast as

\(\frac{z_{0}}{a}=\frac{1}{\left(\omega_{n} / \omega\right)^{2}-1}\)

or

\(\frac{z_{0}}{\varepsilon}=\frac{\left(\omega / \omega_{n}\right)^{2}}{1-\left(\omega / \omega_{n}\right)^{2}}\)

The graph of \(\left|z_{0}\right| / \varepsilon \text { va }\). \(\omega / \omega_{n}\) is shown in Figure 3.46
and depicts the response magnitude as a function of ω, where the magnitude \(\left|z_{0}\right|=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\).

In the subcritical range, \(\omega<\omega_{n}\), the amplitude \(Z_{0}\) grows from zero and the limit approaches infinity as \(\omega \rightarrow\) \(\omega_{n}\) since no damping has been included in the model. In the supercritical range, \(\omega>\omega_{n},\)

always negative, although is shown as positive in Figure 3.46 due to the absolute value. With increasing ω the magnitude of the amplitude decreases monotonically to −ε as \(\omega \rightarrow \infty\). This phenomenon, where the disk begins to rotate about its mass center, is referred to as selfcentering since the rotor tends to rotate about its center of mass instead of its geometric center.
When damping is included in the model, there is the added possibility that the system may become unstable due to a phenomenon where energy is fed into the system by the damping, rather than the usual removal of energy. In this case the response amplitude grows exponentially, leading to failure. Such a phenomenon is known as selfexcited whirling. An introduction to self-excited oscillations is given in Section 4.9 and Chapter 11.
