Guest Question.

For the formation of \(Cu_{2}O, \Delta H^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0} \right) = −168.6 kJ/mol and \Delta G^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0} \right) = −1582.3 kJ/mol. \) For the formation of \(Al_{2}O_{3}, \Delta H^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0} \right) = −1675.7 kJ/mol and \Delta G^{0}\left(T_{0} , p_{0} \right) = −1582.3 kJ/mol.\) In both cases, the metal and its oxide are solids, not in solution, and the oxygen can be treated as an ideal gas. Write chemical equations for the two reactions. Assume that \(\Delta H^{0} \left(T , p_{0} \right) \) can be treated as a constant equal to \(\Delta H^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0} \right). \) Determine the equilibrium constants \(K(T, p_{0})\) as a function of temperature and then determine the equilibrium pressures of oxygen for each reaction at 1000 K.

Admin
Solution

The relevant reactions are \(2Cu(solid) + \left(1/2\right) O_{2} (gas) \rightarrow Cu_{2}O (solid) and 2Al(solid) + \left(3/2\right) O_{2} (gas) \rightarrow Al_{2} O_{3} (solid).\)

From Eq. (12.71),

\(\Delta G^{0} \left(T , p_{0}\right) = \Delta H^{0}_{0} + \frac{T}{T_{0}} \left[\Delta G^{0} \left(T_{0} , p_{0}\right) – \Delta H^{0}_{0} \left(T_{0} , p_{0}\right) \right] = \Delta H^{0}_{0} – T \Delta S^{0}\left(T_{0} , p_{0}\right).\) (12.71)

\(K\left(T , p_{0}\right) = exp \left[\left(\Delta H^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0}\right) – \Delta G^{0}_{f} \left(T_{0} , p_{0}\right) \right) / RT_{0} \right] exp\left(-\Delta H^{0}_{f} / RT\right).\) (12.72)

For the oxidation of copper,

\(K\left(T , p_{0}\right) = 1.098 \times 10^{-4} exp\left(20,280 K/T\right),\) (12.73)

so \(K\left(1000 K , p_{0}\right) = 2.018 \times 10^{10}.\)

From Eq. (12.59), we see that \(\left(p_{O_{2} } / p_{O} \right)^{-1/2} = K, so we obtain p_{O_{2} } = 2002 \times 10^{-10} atm.\)

\(\prod\limits_{i}^{gases}{\left(p_{i} / p_{0} \right)^{v_{i}} } = K\left(T , p_{0}\right).\) (12.59)

For the oxidation of aluminum,

\(K\left(T , p_{0}\right) = 4.326 \times 10^{-17} exp\left(201,550 K/T\right),\) (12.74)

so \(K\left(1000 K , p_{0}\right) = 1.475 \times 10^{71}.\)

For this reaction, \(\left(p_{O_{2} } / p_{O} \right)^{-3/2} = K, so we obtain p_{O_{2} } = 3.58\times 10^{-48} atm.\)

Both of these oxygen pressures are very small but their relative values indicate that aluminum oxide is much more stable than copper oxide; an extremely low oxygen pressure would be needed to reduce aluminum oxide to the metallic state.
