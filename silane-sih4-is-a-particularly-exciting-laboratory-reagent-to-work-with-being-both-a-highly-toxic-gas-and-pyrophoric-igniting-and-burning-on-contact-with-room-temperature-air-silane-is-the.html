Guest Question.

CONTENT Silane, SiH4, is a particularly exciting laboratory reagent to work with, being both a highly toxic gas and pyrophoric—igniting and burning on contact with room-temperature air. Silane is the silicon-based version of methane and has a similar tetrahedral molecular structure but with much weaker bonds. Low concentrations of impurities can catalyze the oxidation of silane, releasing enough heat to trigger the direct reaction of O2 with SiH4 to form SiO2 and H2O. Despite its hazards, silane is an important industrial compound, providing a quick route to get silicon atoms into the gas phase for numerous applications including the formation of integrated circuits by chemical vapor deposition.

PROBLEM Predict \(\ln K_{eq}\) for the following reaction at 450 K:

\(SiH_{4}(g)+ 2O_{2}(g)\rightarrow SiO_{2}(s)+ 2H_{2}O(g) \)

 

Admin
Solution

We need the following constants:

\(SiH_{4}(g)\) \(O_{2}(g)\) \(SiO_{2}(s)\) \(H_{2}O(g)\)
\(\Delta _{f}H^{\Theta }( KJ mol^{-1} )\) 34.3 0.0 -903.49 -241.818
\(\Delta _{f}G^{\Theta }( KJ mol^{-1} )\) 56.9 0.0 -850.73 -228.572
\(C_{Pm} ( JK^{-1} mol^{-1} )\) 42.84 29.355 44.4 -228.572

The equilibrium constant at 298.15 K is obtained in the usual way from the \(\Delta _{f}G^{\Theta }\) values:

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta_{\operatorname{ran}} G^{\theta}(298.15 \mathrm{~K}) &=\{[-850.73+2(-228.572)]-[56.9+2(0.0)]\} \mathrm{kJ} \mathrm{mol}^{-1} \\&=-1364.7 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\ln K_{\mathrm{eq}}(298.152 \mathrm{~K}) &=-\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{rxn}} G^{\ominus}(298.152 \mathrm{~K})}{R T} \\&=-\frac{\left(-1364.7 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right)}{\left(0.0083145 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right)(298.15 \mathrm{~K})} \\&=550.5 .\end{aligned}\)

If we first assume that the enthalpy of reaction is constant at its 298.15 K value,

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta_{\mathrm{rxn}} H^{\ominus}(298.15 \mathrm{~K}) &=\{[-903.49+2(-241.818)]-[34.3+2(0.0)]\} \mathrm{kJ} \mathrm{mol}^{-1} \\&=-1421.4 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

then we can estimate the equilibrium constant using Eq. 12.46:

\( ln K_{eq}(T_{2} )\approx ln K_{eq}(T_{1} ) – \frac{\Delta _{rxn}H^{\Theta }}{R} (\frac{1}{T_{2}} -\frac{1}{T_{1}} )\) (12.46)

\(ln K_{eq}(450 K)= 550.5 – \frac{-1421.4 kJ mol^{-1}}{0.0083145 kJ K^{-1} mol^{-1} }(\frac{1}{450 K} – \frac{1}{298.15 K} )= 550.5 – (193.5) = 357.0\)

As required for an exothermic reaction, heat drives the equilibrium down, toward the reactants.
Now, to correct for the temperature dependence of the enthalpy of reaction, we combine the heat capacities

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta_{\operatorname{ran}} C_{P \mathrm{~m}} &=\{[44.4+2(33.577)]-[42.84+2(29.355)]\} \mathrm{J} \mathrm{K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1} \\&=10.0 \mathrm{~J} \mathrm{~K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}=0.0100 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~K}^{-1}\mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

and use Eq. 12.45:

\(ln K_{eq}(T_{2} )= ln K_{eq}(T_{1} )+ \frac{1}{R} [\Delta _{rxn}H^{\Theta }(T_{1})- T_{1}\Delta _{rxn}C_{Pm} (\frac{1}{T_{1}} -\frac{1}{T_{2}} )+ \Delta _{rxn}C_{Pm} \ln \frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}} ]\) (12.45)

\(\begin{aligned}\ln K_{\mathrm{eq}}(450 \mathrm{~K})=& 550.5+\frac{1}{0.0083145 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}}\left\{\left[-1421.4 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right.\right.\\\\&\left.-(298.15 \mathrm{~K})\left(0.0100 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right)\right]\left(\frac{1}{298.15 \mathrm{~K}}-\frac{1}{450 \mathrm{~K}}\right) \\\\&\left.+\left(0.0100 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~K}^{-1} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}\right) \ln \left(\frac{450}{298.15}\right)\right\} \\\\=& 550.5+(-193.4)=357.1\end{aligned}\)

In this case, the correction for the temperature dependence of \(\Delta _{rxn}H^{\Theta }\) is small, and Eq. 12.46 works nearly as well.
