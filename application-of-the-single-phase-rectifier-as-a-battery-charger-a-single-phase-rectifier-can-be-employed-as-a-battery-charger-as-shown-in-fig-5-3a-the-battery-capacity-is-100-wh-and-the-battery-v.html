Guest Question.

Application of the single-phase rectifier as a battery charger A single-phase rectifier can be employed as a battery charger, as shown in Fig. 5.3(a). The battery capacity is 100 Wh, and the battery voltage is E = 12 V. The average charging current should be \( I_{ o ( av )}=5 A \). The primary AC input voltage is \( V_{ p }=120 V \) (rms), 60 Hz, and the transformer has a turns ratio of n = 2:1.

(a) Calculate the angle \( \delta \) over which the diode conducts, the current-limiting resistance R, the power rating \( P_{ R } \) of R, the charging time h in hours, the rectification efficiency \( \eta_{ R } \), and the peak inverse voltage PIV of the diode.

(b) Use PSpice/SPICE to plot \( P_{ o ( ac )} \text { and } P_{ o ( dc )} \) as a function of time. Assume model parameters of diode D1N4148:

IS=2.682N CJO=4P M=.3333 VJ=.5 BV=100 IBV=100U TT=11.54N

Admin
Solution

(a) If the secondary input voltage is \( v_{ S }>E, \text { diode } D _{1} \) will conduct. The angle \( \theta_{1} \) at which the diode starts conducting can be found from the condition

\( V_{ m } \sin \theta_{1}=E \)

 

or \( \theta_{1}=\sin ^{-1}\left(\frac{E}{V_{ m }}\right) \) (5.20)

 

Diode \( D _{1} \) will be turned off when \( v_{ S } \leq E \) at

 

\( \theta_{2}=\pi-\theta_{1} \)

 

The charging current \( i_{ O } \), which is shown in Fig. 5.3(b), can be found from

\( i_{ O }=\frac{v_{ S }-E}{R}=\frac{V_{ m } \sin \theta-E}{R} \quad \text { for } \theta_{1} \leq \theta \leq \theta_{2} \) (5.21)

 

Since \( V_{ s }=V_{ p } / 2=120 / 2=60 V, \)

 

\( V_{ m }=\sqrt{2} V_{ s }=\sqrt{2} \times 60=84.85 V \)

 

From Eq. (5.20), \( \theta_{1}=\sin ^{-1}(12 / 84.85)=8.13^{\circ} \text {, or } 0.1419 rad \). Thus,

 

\( \theta_{2}=180-8.13=171.87^{\circ} \)

 

The interval over which the diode will conduct is called the conduction angle and is given by

 

\( \delta=\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}=171.87-8.13=163.74^{\circ} \)

 

The average charging current \( I_{ D ( av )} \) is

 

\(\begin{aligned}I_{ o ( av )} &=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{\theta_{1}}^{\theta_{2}=\pi-\theta_{1}} \frac{V_{ m } \sin \theta-E}{R} d \theta \\&=\frac{1}{2 \pi R}\left(2 V_{ m } \cos \theta_{1}+2 E \theta_{1}-\pi E\right)\end{aligned}\) (5.22)

 

which gives the limiting resistance R as

 

\( \begin{aligned} R &=\frac{1}{2 \pi I_{ o ( av )}}\left(2 V_{ m } \cos \theta_{1}+2 E \theta_{1}-\pi E\right) \\ &=\frac{1}{2 \pi \times 5}\left(2 \times 84.85 \cos 8.13^{\circ}+2 \times 12 \times 0.1419-\pi \times 12\right)=4.26 \Omega \end{aligned} \)

 

The rms battery current \( I_{ o ( rms )} \) is

 

\(\begin{aligned}I_{ n ( rms )}^{2} &=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{\theta_{1}}^{\theta_{2}=\pi-\theta_{1}} \frac{\left(V_{ m } \sin \theta-E\right)^{2}}{R^{2}} d \theta \\&=\frac{1}{2 \pi R^{2}}\left[\left(\frac{V_{ m }^{2}}{2}+E^{2}\right)\left(\pi-2 \theta_{1}\right)+\frac{V_{ m }^{2}}{2} \sin 2 \theta_{1}-4 V_{ m } E \cos \theta_{1}\right]\end{aligned}\) (5.23)

 

\( =67.31 A ^{2} \)

 

which gives \( I_{ o ( rms )}=\sqrt{67.31}=8.2 A \). The power rating of R is

\( P_{ R }=I_{ o ( rms )}^{2} R=8.2^{2} \times 4.26=286.4 W \)

 

The power delivered to the battery \( P_{ o ( dc )} \) is

 

\( P_{ o ( dc )}=E I_{ o ( av )}=12 \times 5=60 W \)

 

For 100 Wh,

 

\( h P_{ o ( dc )}=100 \)

 

or \( h=\frac{100}{P_{ o ( dc )}}=\frac{100}{60}=1.667 h \)

 

The rectification efficiency \( \eta_{ R } \) is

 

\( \eta_{ R }=\frac{\text { Power delivered to the battery }}{\text { Total input power }}=\frac{P_{ o ( dc )}}{P_{ o(dc )}+P_{ R }}=\frac{60}{60+286.4}=17.32 \% \)

 

The peak inverse voltage PIV of the diode is

\( PIV =V_{ m }+E=84.85+12=96.85 V \) (5.24)

 

(b) The battery charger circuit for PSpice simulation is shown in Fig. 5.4. Since inductance is proportional to the square of the number of turns, the primary and the secondary leakage inductances of the input transformer are selected with a ratio of \( 2^{2} \) (or 4) to 1. That is, \( L_{1}=40 mH \text { and } L_{2}=10 mH \) for a linear transformer. The PSpice plots of \( I_{ o ( rms )}, P_{ o ( dc )}, \text { and } P_{ o ( rms )} \) are shown in Fig. 5.5, which gives \( I_{ o ( rms )} \approx 7.3 A \), \( P_{ o ( dc )} \approx 53.5 W , \text { and } P_{ o ( rms )}=86.7 W \). The value of \( I_{ O ( rms )} \) is equal to the rms current through resistance R—that is, I(R). These plots reach their steady-state values after a transient interval of approximately 80 ms.
