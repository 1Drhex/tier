Guest Question.

A square-thread power screw has a major diameter of 32 mm and a pitch of 4 mm with double threads, and it is to be used in an application similar to that in Fig. 8–4.
The given data include\( f = f_c= 0.08 \ , \ dc= 40 \ mm \ , \ and \ F = 6.4 \ kN\) per screw.
(a) Find the thread depth, thread width, pitch diameter, minor diameter, and lead.
(b) Find the torque required to raise and lower the load.
(c) Find the efficiency during lifting the load.
(d) Find the body stresses, torsional and compressive.
(e) Find the bearing stress.
(f) Find the thread bending stress at the root of the thread.
(g) Determine the von Mises stress at the root of the thread.
(h) Determine the maximum shear stress at the root of the thread.

Admin
Solution

(a) From Fig. 8–3a the thread depth and width are the same and equal to half thepitch, or 2 mm. Also\(d_m= d −{ p}/{2} = 32 − {4}/{2} = 30 \ mm d_r= d − p = 32 − 4 = 28 \ mm l = np = 2\left(4\right) = 8 \ mm\)
(b) Using Eqs. (8–1)

\(T_R=\frac{Fd_m}{2}\left(\frac{1+\pi fd_m}{\pi d_m-fl} \right) \)

and (8–6),

\(T_c=\frac{Ff_cd_c}{2} \)

the torque required to turn the screw against the load is
\(\begin{aligned} T_{R} &=\frac{F d_{m}}{2}\left(\frac{l+\pi f d_{m}}{\pi d_{m}-f l}\right)+\frac{F f_{c} d_{c}}{2} \\ &=\frac{6.4(30)}{2}\left[\frac{8+\pi(0.08)(30)}{\pi(30)-0.08(8)}\right]+\frac{6.4(0.08) 40}{2} \\ &=15.94+10.24=26.18 \mathrm{~N} \cdot \mathrm{m} \end{aligned}\)

Using Eqs. (8–2)

\(T_L=\frac{Fd_m}{2} \left(\frac{\pi fd_m-l}{\pi d_m+fl} \right) \)

and (8–6), we find the load-lowering torque is
\(T_L=\frac{Fd_m}{2} \left(\frac{\pi fd_m-l}{\pi d_m+fl} \right) +\frac{Ff_cd_c}{2} =\frac{6.4\left(30\right) }{2} \left[\frac{\pi \left(0.08\right)30-8 }{\pi \left(30\right)+0.08\left(5\right) } \right] +\frac{6.4\left(0.08\right)\left(40\right) }{2} \\=-0.466+10.24=9.77 \ N.m\)
The minus sign in the first term indicates that the screw alone is not self-locking and would rotate under the action of the load except for the fact that the collar friction is present and must be overcome, too. Thus the torque required to rotate the screw “with”the load is less than is necessary to overcome collar friction alone.
(c) The overall efficiency in raising the load is

\( e =\frac{Fl}{2\pi T_R}= \frac{6.4 \left(8\right)}{2 \pi \left(26.18\right)}=0.311 \)

(d) The body shear stress \(\tau\) due to torsional moment TR at the outside of the screw
body is

\( \tau=\frac{16 T_{R}}{\pi d_{r}^{3}}=\frac{16(26.18)\left(10^{3}\right)}{\pi\left(28^{3}\right)}=6.07 \mathrm{MPa}\)

The axial nominal normal stress \(\sigma \) is

\( \sigma=-\frac{4 F}{\pi d_{r}^{2}}=-\frac{4(6.4) 10^{3}}{\pi\left(28^{2}\right)}=-10.39 \mathrm{MPa}\)
(e) The bearing stress \(\sigma_B\) is, with one thread carrying 0.38F,
\(\sigma_B= − \frac{2 \left(0.38F\right)}{ \pi d_m \left(1\right) p}= − \frac{2 \left(0.38\right) \left(6.4\right)10^3}{ \pi \left(30\right) \left(1\right) \left(4\right)}= −12.9 \ MPa\)

 

(f) The thread-root bending stress σbwith one thread carrying 0.38F is
\( \sigma_{b}=\frac{6(0.38 F)}{\pi d_{r}(1) p}=\frac{6(0.38)(6.4) 10^{3}}{\pi(28)(1) 4}=41.5 \mathrm{MPa} \)

(g) The transverse shear at the extreme of the root cross section due to bending is zero.

However, there is a circumferential shear stress at the extreme of the root cross section of the thread as shown in part (d) of 6.07 MPa. The three-dimensional stresses,
after Fig. 8–8, noting the y coordinate is into the page, are
\( \sigma_x= 41.5 \ MPa \quad\quad\quad \tau_{τxy}= 0 \\ \sigma_y = −10.39 \ MPa \quad\quad\quad \tau_{yz}= 6.07 \ MPa \\ \sigma_z= 0 \quad\quad\quad \tau_{xz}=0 \)

For the von Mises stress, Eq. (5–14 ) of Sec. 5–5

\(\acute{\sigma }=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \left[\left(\sigma _x-\sigma _y\right)^2+\left(\sigma _y-\sigma _z\right)^2 \left(\sigma _z-\sigma _x\right)^2+6\left(\tau^2 _{xy}+\tau^2 _{yz}+\tau^2 _{zx}\right) \right] ^{{1}/{2}}\)

can be written as
\(\acute{\sigma } =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \left\{\left(41.5-0\right)^2+\left[0-\left(-10.39\right) \right]^2+\left(-10.339-41.5\right)^2+6\left(6.07\right)^2 \right\}^{{1}/{2}} =48.7 \ MPa \)

Alternatively, you can determine the principal stresses and then use Eq. (5–12)

\(\acute{\sigma }= \left[\frac{\left(\sigma _1-\sigma _2\right)^2+\left(\sigma _2-\sigma _3\right)^2 +\left(\sigma _3-\sigma _1\right)^2}{2} \right] ^{{1}/{2}}\)

to findthe von Mises stress. This would prove helpful in evaluating\( \tau_{max}\) as well.

The principal stresses can be found from Eq. (3–15);

\(\sigma ^3-\left(\sigma_x +\sigma_y +\sigma_z \right)\sigma ^2 +\left(\sigma_x \sigma_y +\sigma_x \sigma_z +\sigma_y \sigma_z -\tau^2 _{xy}-\tau^2_{yz}-\tau^2_{zx} \right) \sigma -\left(\sigma_x \sigma_y \sigma_z +2\tau_{xy} \tau_{yz} \tau_{zx} -\sigma_x \tau^2_{yz} -\sigma_y \tau^2_{zx} -\sigma_z \tau^2_{xy} \right) =0\) however, sketch the stress element and note that there are no shear stresses on the x face. This means that σx is a principal stress.

The remaining stresses can be transformed by using the plane stress equation,Eq. (3–13).

\(\sigma _1,\sigma _2=\frac{\sigma _x+\sigma _y}{} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _x-\sigma _y}{2} \right)^2+\tau ^2_{xy} } \)

Thus, the remaining principal stresses are

\(\frac{-10.39}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{-10.39}{2}\right) +6.07^2} =2.79-13.18 \ MPa\)

Ordering the principal stresses gives

\( \sigma_1 \ , \sigma_2 , \sigma_3 = 41.5, 2.79, −13.18 \ MPa\).

Substituting these into Eq. (5–12) yields

\(\acute{\sigma } =\left\{\frac{\left[41.5-2.79\right]^3+\left[2.97-\left(13.18\right)^2 )+\left[-13.18-41.5\right]^2 \right] }{2} \right\} ^{{1}/{2}} = 48.7 \ MPa\)

(h) The maximum shear stress is given by Eq. (3–16), where \(\tau_{max}= \tau_{{1}/{3}}\), giving

\(\tau _{max} =\frac{\sigma _1-\sigma _2}{2} =\frac{41.5-\left(-13.18\right) }{2} =27.3 \ MPa \)
