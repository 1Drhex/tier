Guest Question.

Consider an electrically heated house whose walls are 9 ft high and have an R-value of insulation of 13 (i.e., a thickness-to-thermal conductivity ratio of L/k = 13 h·\(ft ^{2}\)·°F/Btu). Two of the walls of the house are 40 ft long and the others are 30 ft long. The house is maintained at 75°F at all times, while the temperature of the outdoors varies. Determine the amount of heat lost through the walls of the house on a certain day during which the average temperature of the outdoors is 45°F. Also, determine the cost of this heat loss to the home owner if the unit cost of electricity is $0.075/kWh. For combined convection and radiation heat transfer coefficients, use the ASHRAE (American Society of Heating, Refrigeration, and Air Conditioning Engineers) recommended values of \( h_{i}=1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \) for the inner surface of the walls and \( h_{o}=6.0 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2}.^{\circ} F \) for the outer surface of the walls under 15 mph wind conditions in winter.

 

 

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION An electrically heated house with R-13 insulation is considered. The amount of heat lost through the walls and its cost are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The indoor and outdoor air temperatures have remained at the given values for the entire day so that heat transfer through the walls is steady. 2 Heat transfer through the walls is one-dimensional since any significant temperature gradients in this case exist in the direction from the indoors to the outdoors. 3 The radiation effects are accounted for in the heat transfer coefficients.

Analysis This problem involves conduction through the wall and convection at its surfaces and can best be handled by making use of the thermal resistance concept and drawing the thermal resistance network, as shown in Fig. 3–54. The heat transfer area of the walls is

\( A=\text { Circumference } \times \text { Height }=(2 \times 30 ft +2 \times 40 ft )(9 ft )=1260 ft ^{2} \)

Then the individual resistances are evaluated from their definitions to be

\( R_{i}=R_{\text {conv }, i}=\frac{1}{h_{i} A}=\frac{1}{\left(1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot F \right)\left(1260 ft ^{2}\right)}=0.00054 h \cdot F / B tu \)

 

\( R_{\text {kall }}=\frac{L}{k A}=\frac{R \text { value }}{A}=\frac{13 h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot F / Btu }{1260 ft ^{2}}=0.01032 h \cdot F / Btu \)

 

\( R_{o}=R_{\text {conv }, o}=\frac{1}{h_{o} A}=\frac{1}{\left(6.0 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(1260 ft ^{2}\right)}=0.00013 h \cdot F / Btu \)

 

Noting that all three resistances are in series, the total resistance is

\( R_{\text {total }}=R_{i}+R_{\text {wall }}+R_{o}=0.00054+0.01032+0.00013=0.01099 h \cdot F / Btu \)

Then the steady rate of heat transfer through the walls of the house becomes

\( \dot{Q}=\frac{T_{\infty 1}-T_{\infty 2}}{R_{\text {total }}}=\frac{(75-45)^{\circ} F }{0.01099 h \cdot F / Btu }=2730 Btu / h \)

Finally, the total amount of heat lost through the walls during a 24-h period and its cost to the home owner are

\( Q=\dot{Q} \Delta t=(2730 B tu / h )(24- h / day )=65,514 B tu / day =19.2 kWh / day \)

since 1 kWh = 3412 Btu, and

\( \text { Heating cost }=(\text { Energy lost)(Cost of energy) }=(19.2 kWh / \text { day })(\$ 0.075 / kWh ) \) = $1.44/day

Discussion The heat losses through the walls of the house that day cost the home owner $1.44 worth of electricity. Most of this loss can be saved by insulation.
