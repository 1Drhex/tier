Guest Question.

Derive the equation of motion of the spring-pendulum system depicted in Figure 5.19. Demonstrate the utility of Hamilton’s principle for the derivation of the equations of motion of complex dynamic systems. The system is assumed to have no dissipation.

Admin
Solution

Solution: The Lagrangian is

 

\(\begin{aligned}L &=T-V \\&=\frac{1}{2} m\left[\dot{r}^{2}+(r \dot{\theta})^{2}\right]-\left[-m g h+\frac{1}{2} k\left(r-r_{0}\right)^{2}\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

where \(h=r \cos \theta\) and \(r_{0}\) is the undeformed spring length. Substituting the variation of the Lagrangian into Hamilton’s principle yields the following expression,

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \delta L d t \\&=\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\left[m \dot{r} \delta \dot{r}+m\left(r \dot{\theta}^{2} \delta r+r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta}\right)\right. \\&\left.\quad+m g \delta r \cos \theta-m g r \delta \theta \sin \theta-k\left(r-r_{0}\right) \delta r\right] d t\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}=\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} &\left\{m \dot{r} \delta \dot{r}+m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta}\right.\\&+\left[m\left(r \dot{\theta}^{2}+m g \cos \theta-k\left(r-r_{0}\right)\right] \delta r\right.\\&-m g r \sin \theta \delta \theta\} d t .\end{aligned}\) (5.33)

 

To proceed we must convert variations of time derivatives of parameters into variations of the parameters themselves. There are two such terms in Equation 5.33,

 

\(m \dot{r} \delta \dot{r} d t, \quad m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta} d t\)

 

that need to be converted.
The approach is relatively straightforward and involves an “inverse” chain rule or “integration by parts.” We show the procedure here. For the first case above, we transform a term such as (· · · )\(\delta \dot{r} d t\) to one such as (· · · )\(\delta r d t\). We begin with the time derivative

 

\(\frac{d}{d t}(m \dot{r} \delta r)=m \ddot{r} \delta r+m \dot{r} \delta \dot{r}\)

 

from which we obtain

 

\(m \dot{r} \delta \dot{r} d t=d(m \dot{r} \delta r)-m \ddot{r} \delta r d t\)

 

For the expression \(m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta} d t\)

 

which is a product of three functions of time, we begin with the time derivative

 

\(\frac{d}{d t}\left(m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \theta\right)=m(2 r \dot{\theta} \dot{\theta} \delta \theta)+m\left(r^{2} \ddot{\theta} \delta \theta\right)+m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta}\)

 

and find

 

\(m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \dot{\theta} d t=d\left(m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \theta\right)-\left(m r^{2} \ddot{\theta}+2 m r \dot{\theta} \dot{\theta}\right) \delta \theta d t\)

 

Substituting these expressions into Equation 5.33, we have

 

\(\begin{aligned}&-\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \delta L d t \\&=\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\left[\left\{m \ddot{r}-m r \dot{\theta}^{2}-m g \cos \theta+k\left(r-r_{0}\right)\right\} \delta r\right.\end{aligned}\)

\(\begin{aligned}&\left.+\left\{m r^{2} \ddot{\theta}+2 m r \dot{\theta} \dot{\theta}+m g r \sin \theta\right\} \delta \theta\right] d t \\&-\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\left[d(m \dot{r} \delta r)+d\left(m r^{2} \dot{\theta} \delta \theta\right)\right]=0\end{aligned}\) (5.34)

 

Since \(\delta r\) =0 and \(\delta \theta\) =0 at \(t_{1}\) and \(t_{2}\), the second integral on the right-hand side of Equation 5.34 equals zero. Further, since \(\delta r\) is independent of \(\delta \theta\) and both are arbitrary, it must be that

 

\(\begin{array}{r}m \ddot{r}-m r \dot{\theta}^{2}-m g \cos \theta+k\left(r-r_{0}\right)=0 \\m r^{2} \ddot{\theta}+2 m r \dot{r} \dot{\theta}+m g r \sin \theta=0\end{array}\)

 

which are the nonlinear equations of motion for the “elastic pendulum.” It is assumed that the spring behaves linearly. Even if we assume small angles, that is \(\cos \theta \simeq 1, \sin \theta \simeq \theta\), we cannot remove the nonlinearity\(\dot{\theta}^{2}\) without additional assumptions.
