Guest Question.

Hot-water extraction of woodchips
Hot-water extraction is an excellent technique to remove hemicelluloses from wood. Hemicellulose extracted this way can be converted to valuable products at low cost. Kate carefully prepared a batch of woodchips to conduct hot-water extraction experiment. She loaded 500 g of sugar maple chips and 2 L water into an M/K digester, quickly ramped up the temperature to 160°C.
After 2 h at 160°C, she then quenched the reaction mixture down to room temperature. After thoroughly washing the woodchips that were collected from the hot-water extraction experiments, Kate found that the wood chips are weighing 385 g.

1. How much mass loss (in %) for the woodchips occurred in the hot-water extraction experiment?

2. If Kate were to carry out the experiment at 180°C, what is the extraction time she should keep in order to obtain roughly the same level of extraction?

Admin
Solution

1. Initially, the woodchips weigh 500 g and after extraction they weigh 385 g. Therefore 500 – 385 g = 115 g of wood was extracted or removed to the liquid phase. The mass loss is thus 115/500 = 23%.

2. Let us assume that the extraction reaction can be approximated by a single temperature varying reaction rate constant. That is the rate of mass removal may be approximated by

\(r=k(T) f(C)\), (E4.7.1)

where C is the concentration of components inside the wood.

For reaction occurring in a batch reactor, mole balance of reactant A leads to

\(\overset{\overset{0}{\nwarrow } }{In} – \overset{\overset{0}{ \nearrow } }{Out}+Generation=Accumulation\)

\(0-0+v_{A} r V=\frac{d n_{A}}{d t}\). (E4.7.2)

Since the volume is constant (constant volume batch reactor), we further obtain

\(v_{A} k f(C)=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). (E4.7.3)

Since the rate constant k is only a function of temperature, separation of variables for Eq. (E4.7.3) \(v_{A} k f(C)=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). leads to

\(\frac{d C_{A}}{f(C)}=v_{A} k d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup d t.\) (E4.7.4)

For a desired reaction end condition or conversion, the reaction time can be obtained by integrating Eq. (E4.7.4) \(\frac{d C_{A}}{f(C)}=v_{A} k d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup d t.\) .

\(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{f(C)}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T} \right\rgroup d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup t\), (E4.7.5)

if the temperature is constant. For the reaction to take place at two different temperatures to have the same conversion (or concentration change), we obtain from Eq. (E4.7.5) \(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{f(C)}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T} \right\rgroup d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup t\),

\(v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup-\frac{E}{R T_{1}} \right\rgroup t_{1}=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{2}} \right\rgroup t_{2}\), (E4.7.6)

which can be reduced to

\(t_{2}=\exp \left\lgroup \frac{E}{R T_{2}}-\frac{E}{R T_{1}}\right\rgroup t_{1}.\) (E4.7.7)

We can now estimate the time needed at a different temperature. Let us assume that \(\frac{E}{R}\) = 10800K, substitute into Eq. (E4.7.7) \(t_{2}=\exp \left\lgroup \frac{E}{R T_{2}}-\frac{E}{R T_{1}}\right\rgroup t_{1}.\) to yield

\(t_{2}=\exp \left\lgroup \frac{E}{R T_{2}}-\frac{E}{R T_{1}} \right\rgroup t_{1}=\exp \left\lgroup\frac{10800}{180+273.15}-\frac{10800}{160+273.15}\right\rgroup \times 2 h \text { . }\) (E4.7.8)

\(=0.6654 h =39.93 min\)

That is, at 180°C an extraction time of 39.93 min is expected to yield the same level of extraction as 2 h at 160°C.
