Guest Question.

A flat plate 70 cm long and 1.0 m wide is placed in a wind tunnel where the flow conditions
are\(M =3\> , p=\frac{1}{20} \; atm\) atm, and \(T =−40^{◦} C\). How much cooling must be used to maintain the plate
temperature at \(T =35^{◦} C\)?

Admin
Solution

We must consider the laminar and turbulent portions of the boundary layer separately because the recovery factors, and hence the adiabatic wall temperatures, used to establish the heat flow will be different for each flow regime. It turns out that the difference is rather small in this problem, but we shall follow a procedure that would be used if the difference were appreciable, so that the general method of solution may be indicated. The free-stream acoustic velocity is calculated from:

\(a=\sqrt{γg_{c} RT_{∞} }=[(1.4)(1.0)(287)(233)]^{1/2} =306\; m/s\enspace [1003\enspace ft/s] \)

so that the free-stream velocity is

\(u_{∞}=(3)(306)=918 \; m/s\enspace [3012 \enspace ft/s] \)

The maximum Reynolds number is estimated by making a computation based on properties evaluated at free-stream conditions:

\(ρ_{∞}=\frac{(1.0132×10^{5} )(\frac{1}{20} )}{(287)(233)} =0.0758 \enspace kg/m^{3} \enspace [4.73×10^{-3} lb_{m} /ft^{3} ] \)

 

\(μ_{∞}=1.434×10^{-5} \enspace kg/m· s \quad [0.0347\enspace lb_{m} /h · ft] \)

 

\(Re_{L,∞}=\frac{(0.0758)(918)(0.70)}{1.434×10^{-5} } =3.395×10^{6} \)

Thus we conclude that both laminar and turbulent-boundary-layer heat transfer must be considered. We first determine the reference temperatures for the two regimes and then evaluate properties at these temperatures.

Laminar portion

\(T_{0}=T_{∞} (1+\frac{γ −1}{2} M^{2}_{∞} ) =(233)[1+(0.2)(3)^{2} ]=652\; K \)

Assuming a Prandtl number of about 0.7, we have

\(r=Pr^{1/2}=(0.7)^{1/2} =0.837 \)

 

\(r=\frac{T_{aw}-T_{∞} }{T_{0} -T_{∞} } =\frac{T_{aw}-233}{652−233} \)

 

and \(T_{aw}=584\> K =311^{◦} C \; [592^{◦} F]. \)Then the reference temperature from Equation (5-123)

\(Turbulent\enspace flow:\hspace{30 pt} r= Pr^{1/3} \)

is :

\(T ^{*} =233+(0.5)(308−233)+(0.22)(584−233)=347.8 \> K \)

 

Checking the Prandtl number at this temperature, we have

\(Pr ^{*} =0.697 \)

so that the calculation is valid. If there were an appreciable difference between the value of \(Pr ^{*} \) and the value used to determine the recovery factor, the calculation would have to be repeated until agreement was reached.

The other properties to be used in the laminar heat-transfer analysis are:

\(ρ^{*}=\frac{(1.0132×10^{5} )(1/20)}{(287)(347.8)}=0.0508\, kg/m^{3} \)

 

\(μ^{*}= 2.07×10^{-5} \, kg/m· s \)

 

\(k^{*}= 0.03 W/m· ^{◦} C \; [0.0173\; Btu/h · ft ·^{◦} F] \)

 

\(c_{p} ^{*} = 1.009\; kJ/kg ·^{◦} C \)

Turbulent portion

Assuming Pr =0.7 gives

\(r=Pr^{1/3}=0.888=\frac{T_{aw}-T_{∞} }{T_{0} -T_{∞} } =\frac{T_{aw}-233}{652−233} \)

 

\(T_{aw}=605\> K =332^{◦} C \; . \)

 

\(T^{*} =233+(0.5)(308−233)+(0.22)(605−233)=352.3 \; K. \)

 

\(Pr^{*} =0.695 \)

The agreement between \(Pr^{*} \) and the assumed value is sufficiently close. The other properties to be used in the turbulent heat-transfer analysis are

\(ρ^{*} =\frac{(1.0132×10^{5} )(1/20)}{(287)(352.3)} =0.0501\; kg/m^{3} \)

 

\(μ^{*} =2.09×10^{-5} \enspace kg/m· s \)

 

\(k^{*} =0.0302 \enspace W/m·^{◦} C \qquad c_{ p}\, ^{*} =1.009 \; kJ/kg ·^{◦} C \)

Laminar heat transfer

We assume

\(Re_{crit} ^{*} =5×10^{5} =\frac{ρ^{*} u_{∞} xc}{μ^{*} }\)

 

\(x_{c}=\frac{(5×10^{5} )(2.07×10^{-5} )}{(0.0508)(918)} =0.222\; m \)

 

\(\overline{Nu^{*} } =\frac{\bar{h}x_{c} }{k^{*} } =0.664(Re^{*}_{crit} )^{1/2} Pr^{*1/3} =(0.664)(5×10^{5} )^{1/2} (0.697)^{1/3} =416.3\)

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{(416.3)(0.03)}{0.222} =56.25\; W/m^{2} ·^{◦} C \enspace [9.91\enspace Btu/h · ft^{2}·^{◦} F]\)

This is the average heat-transfer coefficient for the laminar portion of the boundary layer, and the heat transfer is calculated from

\(q=\bar{h}A(T_{w} −T_{aw} ) =(56.26)(0.222)(308−584)=−3445\> W [−11,750\> Btu/h]\)

so that 3445 W of cooling is required in the laminar region of the plate per meter of depth in the z direction.

Turbulent heat transfer:

To determine the turbulent heat transfer we must obtain an expression for the local heat-transfer coefficient from

\(St_{x} ^{*}\, Pr^{*2/3}=0.0296 Re_{x} ^{*-1/5} \)

and then integrate from x=0.222 m to x=0.7 m to determine the total heat transfer:

\(h_{x}=Pr^{*-2/3} ρ^{*} u_{∞} cp(0.0296)(\frac{ ρ^{*} u_{∞} x}{μ^{*} } )^{-1/5} \)

Inserting the numerical values for the properties gives

\(h_{x}=94.34x^{-1/5} \)

The average heat-transfer coefficient in the turbulent region is determined from

\(\bar{h}=\frac{\int_{0.222}^{0.7}{h_{x} dx} }{\int_{0.222}^{0.7}{dx} }=111.46 W/m^{2}·^{◦} C \; [19.6 \> Btu/h · ft^{2}·^{◦} F] \)

Using this value we may calculate the heat transfer in the turbulent region of the flat plate:

\(q=\bar{h}A(T_{w} −T_{aw} ) =(111.46)(0.7−0.222)(308−605)=−15,823\> W \; [−54,006\> Btu/h]\)

The total amount of cooling required is the sum of the heat transfers for the laminar and turbulent portions:

\(Total \enspace cooling=3445+15,823=19,268\enspace W \enspace [65,761\enspace Btu/h]\)

These calculations assume unit depth of 1 m in the z direction.
