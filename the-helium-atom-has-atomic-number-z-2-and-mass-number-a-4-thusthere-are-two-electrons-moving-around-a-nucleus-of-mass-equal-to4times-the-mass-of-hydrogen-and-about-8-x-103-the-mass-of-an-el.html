Guest Question.

The Helium atom has atomic number \(Z=2\) and mass number \(A=4 ;\) thus, there are two electrons moving around a nucleus of mass equal to 4 times the mass of Hydrogen and about \(8 \times 10^{3}\) the mass of an electron. Assuming this mass to be infinite, we refer to the coordinates of the two electrons with respect to the nucleus with \(\mathbf{r}_{1}\) and \(\mathbf{r}_{2}\) and to their relative position with \(\mathbf{r}_{12}=\mathbf{r}_{1}-\mathbf{r}_{2}\). The Helium atom Hamiltonian is, therefore,\(\mathcal{H}=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left(\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right)-\frac{Z e^{2}}{r_{1}}-\frac{Z e^{2}}{r_{2}}+\frac{e^{2}}{r_{12}}\)
If there were no repulsive interaction between the two electrons, in the Schrödinger equation, there would be a separation between the variables of the two electrons and, \(E\) being the energy of the ground state, we would have
\(\psi_{E}^{0}\left(\mathbf{r}_{1}, \mathbf{r}_{2}\right)=\psi_{E_{1}}\left(\mathbf{r}_{1}\right) \psi_{E_{1}}\left(\mathbf{r}_{2}\right)=\frac{Z^{3}}{\pi a_{0}^{3}} e^{-\frac{Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)}{a_{0}}}\)
Here, we denoted by \(E_{1}\) and \(\psi_{E_{1}}(r)=(2 / \sqrt{4 \pi})\left(Z / a_{0}\right)^{\frac{3}{2}} \cdot e^{-\frac{Z r}{a_{0}}},\) respectively, the ground state energy of a Hydrogenlike atom with \(Z=2\) and the corresponding eigenfunction. In this very rough approximation, neglecting the positive term of attraction between the electrons, the ground state energy would be
\(E^{0}=2 E_{1}=2\left[-\frac{m\left(Z e^{2}\right)^{2}}{2 \hbar^{2}}\right]=-8 \cdot 13.6 \mathrm{eV}=-108.8 \mathrm{eV}\)
Experimentally, it is found that the Helium ground state has energy \(E=-78.98 \mathrm{eV}\), a much higher value. Use the Variational method to improve this approximation. Hint: the trial functions will again be given by (11.1), however, considering \(Z\) as a parameter to be determined by minimizing the functional
\(E(Z)=\left(\frac{Z^{3}}{\pi a_{0}^{3}}\right)^{2} \int d \mathbf{r}_{1} d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\frac{Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)}{a_{0}}} \mathcal{H} e^{-\frac{Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)}{a_{0}}}\)

Solution

The Hamiltonian can be rewritten in the form
\(\mathcal{H}=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left(\nabla_{1}^{2}+\nabla_{2}^{2}\right)-\frac{Z e^{2}}{r_{1}}-\frac{(2-Z) e^{2}}{r_{1}}-\frac{Z e^{2}}{r_{2}}-\frac{(2-Z) e^{2}}{r_{2}}+\frac{e^{2}}{r_{12}}\)
Taking into account that \(\psi_{E_{1}}\) is the solution of the Schrödinger equation in correspondence with the eigenvalue \(E_{1}\), i.e.,
\(\left(-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla_{1}^{2}-\frac{Z e^{2}}{r_{1}}\right) e^{-\frac{z r_{1}}{a_{0}}}=-\frac{Z^{2} e^{2}}{2 a_{0}} e^{-\frac{z_{r_{1}}}{a_{0}}},\)
it is possible to eliminate the differential operators and so we find
\(\begin{aligned}E(Z) &=\left(\frac{Z^{3}}{\pi a_{0}^{3}}\right)^{2} \int d \mathbf{r}_{1} d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\frac{2 Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)}{a_{0}}} e^{2}\left[-\frac{Z^{2}}{a_{0}}-\frac{(2-Z)}{r_{1}}-\frac{(2-Z)}{r_{2}}+\frac{1}{r_{12}}\right]=\\&=\left(\frac{Z^{3} e}{\pi a_{0}^{3}}\right)^{2}\left[-\frac{Z^{2}}{a_{0}} K_{1}-2(2-Z) K_{2}+K_{3}\right]\end{aligned}\)
Here, \(K_{1}, K_{2}\) and \(K_{3}\) are the following integrals:
\(-\mathbf{K}_{1}\)
\(K_{1}=\int d \mathbf{r}_{1} d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\frac{2 Z\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)}{a_{0}}}=\left(4 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty} d r r^{2} e^{-\beta r}\right)^{2}=\left(4 \pi \frac{2}{\beta^{3}}\right)^{2},\)
where \(\beta=\frac{2 Z}{a_{0}}\) and \((\mathrm{A} .8)\) have been used. \(-\mathbf{K}_{2}\)
\(\begin{aligned}K_{2} &=\int d \mathbf{r}_{1} d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\beta\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)} \frac{1}{r_{1}}=(4 \pi)^{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} d r_{2} r_{2}^{2} e^{-\beta r_{2}} \int_{0}^{\infty} d r_{1} r_{1} e^{-\beta r_{1}}=\\&=(4 \pi)^{2} \frac{2}{\beta^{3}} \frac{1}{\beta^{2}}=\frac{2(4 \pi)^{2}}{\beta^{5}}\end{aligned}\)

\(K_{3}=\int d \mathbf{r}_{1} d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\beta\left(r_{1}+r_{2}\right)} \frac{1}{\left|\mathbf{r}_{1}-\mathbf{r}_{2}\right|}=\int d \mathbf{r}_{1} e^{-\beta r_{1}} f\left(r_{1}\right)\)
where
\(f\left(r_{1}\right)=\int d \mathbf{r}_{2} e^{-\beta r_{2}} \frac{1}{\left|\mathbf{r}_{1}-\mathbf{r}_{2}\right|}\)
is a function of \(\mathbf{r}_{1}\) only, because, by integrating over the whole \(\mathbf{r}_{2}\) solid angle, the dependence on the angles disappears. To calculate \(f\left(r_{1}\right),\) we are free, therefore, to position \(\mathbf{r}_{1}\) in the direction of the \(z\) axis. We get
\(\begin{aligned}f\left(r_{1}\right) &=2 \pi \int d r_{2} r_{2}^{2} e^{-\beta r_{2}} \int_{-1}^{+1} d \cos \theta \frac{1}{\sqrt{r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}-2 r_{1} r_{2} \cos \theta}}=\\&=-2 \pi \int d r_{2} r_{2}^{2} e^{-\beta r_{2}} \frac{1}{r_{1} r_{2}}\left[\sqrt{r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}-2 r_{1} r_{2} \cos \theta}\right]_{-1}^{+1}=\\&=\frac{2 \pi}{r_{1}} \int d r_{2} r_{2} e^{-\beta r_{2}}\left(r_{1}+r_{2}-\left|r_{1}-r_{2}\right|\right)=\end{aligned}\)
\(=\frac{4 \pi}{r_{1} \beta^{3}}\left[2-e^{-\beta r_{1}}\left(2+\beta r_{1}\right)\right]\)

\(I_{2}\left(0, r_{1}\right)=\frac{1}{\beta^{3}}\left[2-\left(2+2 r_{1} \beta+r_{1}^{2} \beta^{2}\right) e^{-\beta r_{1}}\right]\)
and
\(I_{1}\left(r_{1}, \infty\right)=\frac{1}{\beta^{2}}\left(1+\beta r_{1}\right) e^{-\beta r_{1}}\)
Returning to the calculation of \(K_{3},\) we obtain
\(K_{3}=4 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty} d r_{1} r_{1}^{2} e^{-\beta r_{1}} \frac{4 \pi}{r_{1} \beta^{3}}\left[2-e^{-\beta r_{1}}\left(2+\beta r_{1}\right)\right]=\frac{(4 \pi)^{2}}{\beta^{5}} \frac{5}{4}\)
By inserting these results into the expression for \(E(Z),\) we obtain
\(E(Z)=-\frac{e^{2}}{a_{0}}\left(\frac{27}{8} Z-Z^{2}\right)\)
which takes its minimum value for
\(Z=Z_{e f f}=\frac{27}{16}\)
given by
\(E\left(Z_{e f f}\right)=-\frac{e^{2}}{a_{0}}\left(\frac{27}{8} Z_{e f f}-Z_{e f f}^{2}\right)=-77.5 e V\)
This value is very close to the already mentioned experimental value \(-78.98 \mathrm{eV}\) and provides a better approximation compared to the calculation in Perturbation Theory. Both approximations calculate the energy of the ground state as the expectation value of the Hamiltonian in the unperturbed state, but the variational method modifies the value of \(Z\) in such a way as to minimize the difference from the exact value. Notice, moreover, that \(Z_{\text {eff }}<2\)
Taking into account that wave functions are factored, we can consider the approximation as resulting from a model in which each of the electrons moves in the mean field of a nucleus with an effective charge \(Z_{\text {eff }} \cdot e\), lower than the real one due to the screen effect produced by the other electron.
