Guest Question.

An important application of the shunt–shunt feedback formulas is in the noise analysis of a network consisting of N identical two-ports connected in parallel. This situation occurs often in RF circuits when connecting N identical MOSFET gate fingers in parallel to form a large MOSFET.

Admin
Solution

The most elegant solution to derive the noise parameters of such a network is to employ the noise admittance correlation matrix expressions. The noise admittance correlation matrix and the Y-matrix of the resulting two-port becomes

\(\left[C_{y}\right]=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{i=N}\left[C_{y}\right]_{i}=N \times\left[C_{y}\right]_{i} \text { and } Y=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{i=N}[Y]_{i}=N \times[Y]_{i}\)

where \(\left[C_{y}\right]_{i}\) and \([Y]_{i}\) are the noise admittance and Y-parameter matrices, respectively, of two-port i.

From (3.137)-(3.139), we obtain, successively

\(R_{n}=\frac{C_{y 22}}{\left|Y_{21}\right|^{2}}=\frac{N \times C_{i y 22}}{N^{2} \times Y_{i 21}}=\frac{R_{n i}}{N} \)

 

\(Y_{c o r}=Y_{11}-\frac{C_{y 12}}{C_{y 22}} \times Y_{21}=N \times Y_{i 11}-\frac{N \times C i_{y 12}}{N \times C i_{y 22}} \times N \times Y i_{21}=N \times Y i_{c o r}\)

 

\(G_{u}=C_{y 11}-R_{n}\left|Y_{11}-Y_{c o r}\right|^{2}=N \times C i_{y 11}-\frac{R_{n i}}{N}\left|N \times Y i_{11}-N \times Y i_{c o r}\right|^{2}=N \times G_{u i} \)

 

\(G_{s o p t}=\sqrt{G_{c o r}^{2}+\frac{G_{u}}{R_{n}}}=\sqrt{N^{2} G_{c o r i}^{2}+\frac{N G_{u i}}{\frac{R_{\mathrm{ni}}}{N}}}=N G_{s o p t i} \)

 

\(F_{M I N}=1+2 R_{n}\left(G_{c o r}+G_{s o p t}\right)=1+2 \frac{R_{\mathrm{ni}}}{N}\left(N G_{c o r i}+N G_{s o p t i}\right)=1+2 R_{c}\left(G_{c o r i}+G_{s o p t i}\right)=F_{M I N i}\)

By connecting transistors or transistor gate fingers in parallel, the minimum noise factor is preserved while the optimum source impedance and sensitivity to source mismatch are reduced. This provides the IC designer with a powerful tool for realizing noise matching simply by controlling the size of transistors and without (to first order) compromising the noise factor. The example above illustrates a practical case in which we can derive the exact noise parameters of a combination of shunt-connected two-ports, without resorting to approximations. However, for most practical applications, as in amplifiers with negative feedback, we need to apply reasonable approximations in order to arrive at simplified noise parameter equations that can be used in the hand design of low-noise amplifiers.

In general, if the unilateral amplifier approximation holds

\(Y_{21} \approx Y_{21 a} ; \quad Y_{12} \approx Y_{12 f} ; \quad D_{f} \approx 0 ; \quad D_{a} \approx 1 ; \quad E_{a}=-E_{f} \approx Y_{11 f}\) (3.166)

Equations (3.156) and (3.158) simplify to

\(v_{n} \approx v_{n a} ; i_{n}=i_{n u f}+i_{n u a}+\left(Y_{c o r f}-Y_{11 f}\right) v_{n f}+\left(Y_{c o r a}+Y_{11 f}\right) v_{n a}\)

(3.167)

Next, by using (3.159)-(3.161) and algebraic manipulations of statistical signals, the noise parameters of the amplifier with feedback can be cast as [11]

\(R_{n} \approx R_{n a} ; G_{u}=G_{u f}+G_{u a}+\left|Y_{c o r f}-Y_{11 f}\right|^{2} R_{n f} \geq G_{u a}\) (3.168)

 

\(Y_{c o r}=Y_{c o r a}+Y_{11 f} ; G_{c o r}=G_{c o r a}+\Re\left(Y_{11 f}\right) \geq G_{c o r a}\) (3.169)

 

\(Y_{s o p t}=\sqrt{G_{s o p t a}^{2}+\frac{G_{u f}}{R_{n a}}+2 G_{c o r a} \Re\left(Y_{11 f}\right)+\Re^{2}\left(Y_{11 f}\right)+\frac{\left|Y_{c o r f}-Y_{11 f}\right|^{2} R_{n f}}{R_{n a}}}+j\left[B_{s o p t a}-\Im\left(Y_{11 f}\right)\right] \) (3.170)

 

\(F_{M I N}=1+2 R_{n a}\left[G_{c o r a}+G_{s o p t}+\Re\left(Y_{11 f}\right)\right] \geq F_{M I N a}\) (3.171)

We note that the noise voltage of the amplifier with parallel feedback is practically equal to that of the main amplifier. The noise currents of the amplifier and feedback networks add, while \(Y_{S O P T}\) increases. \(F_{M I N}\) increases if the feedback network has resistive elements. If it is purely reactive \(\left(R_{n f}=0, G_{u f}=0, g_{11 f}=0\right.\) ), it will not degrade the noise figure and \(G_{S O P T}\) will remain largely unaffected by feedback. For lossy feedback, we can conclude that:

• Shunt lossy feedback can be used for noise matching in situations where the noise impedance of the original two-port is higher than that of the source impedance. Lossy shunt feedback at the input of an amplifier decreases both the input impedance and the optimal noise impedance.

• Since \(R_{n}\) remains approximately equal to that of the main amplifier, the sensitivity to noise impedance mismatch remains practically the same.

• More importantly, since, according to \((3.170), G_{S O P T}\) increases and \(B_{S O P T}\) decreases or remains the same, the quality factor of the optimum noise admittance, \(Q=B_{\text {SOPT }} / G_{\text {SOPT }}\), decreases, implying that the bandwidth over which the noise impedance can be matched increases when negative feedback is applied. This behavior is similar to the signal bandwidth extension property of negative feedback.

• Just as the gain of an amplifier with negative feedback decreases, the minimum noise factor of an amplifier with lossy negative feedback increases compared to that of the main amplifier.
