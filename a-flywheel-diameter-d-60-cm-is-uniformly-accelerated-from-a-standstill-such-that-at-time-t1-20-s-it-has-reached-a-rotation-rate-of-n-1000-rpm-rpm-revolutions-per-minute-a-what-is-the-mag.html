Guest Question.

A flywheel (diameter d = 60 cm) is uniformly accelerated from a standstill such that at time\(\ t_{1} = 20 s\) it has reached a rotation rate of n = 1000 rpm (rpm = revolutions per minute).
a) What is the magnitude of the angular acceleration \(\ \dot{\omega}\) of the flywheel?
b) How many revolutions N has the flywheel made at time \(t_1\)?
c) What are the velocity and acceleration of a point on the perimeter at time \(\ t_{2} = 1 s\)?

Admin
Solution

a) For a uniformly accelerated circular motion the angular acceleration is constant:\(\ \dot{\omega } =\dot{\omega }_{0}=const \). Noting the initial condition \(\ \omega\left(0\right)=0,\) the angular velocity is \(\ \omega=\dot\omega_{0}t.\) With \(\ \omega\left(t_{1}\right)=\omega_{1}\) it follows that
\(\ \dot\omega_{0}=\frac{\omega_{1}}{t_{1}} \) .

Rotation rate n (in rpm) and angular velocity ω are easily related to one another: with a rotation rate n one has an angle of revolution of \(\ n.2\pi\) after one minute. Angular velocity is usually given in units of 1/s (or equivalently rad/s); in this case

\(\ \omega=\frac{n.2\pi}{60}.\)

With the given rotation rate, it follows that

\(\ \underline{\underline{\dot{\omega }_{0} } } =\frac{1000·2\pi}{60 · 20} =\underline{\underline{5.24s^{-2}} }. \)

b) Integration of \(\ \omega=\dot{\omega_{0}}t\) with initial condition \(\ \varphi \left(0\right) =0\) gives the rotation angle

\(\ \varphi=\frac{1}{2}\dot{\omega_{0}}t^{2}.\)

With the given values, we have at \(\ t=t_{1} \) a rotation angle

\( \varphi_{1}=\varphi\left(t_{1}\right)=\frac{1}{2}· 5.24 · 400 = 1048 rad .\)

Thus the number of revolutions is

\(\ \underline{\underline{N} }=\frac{\varphi_{1}}{2\pi}= \underline{\underline{166 } } \) .

c) From (1.26) the angular velocity component is obtained as

\(v=v_{\varphi }=r\omega \) (1.26)

\(\ v=r\omega=r\dot\omega_{0}t.\)

The radial velocity is zero. From (1.27) and (1.28) the two acceleration components are

\(a_{\varphi }=r\dot{\omega } \) (1.27)

\(a_{r }=-r\omega ^2 . \) (1.28)

\(\ a_{\varphi}=r\dot\omega_{0},a_{r}=-r\omega^{2}=-r\left(\dot\omega_{0}t\right)^{2}.\)

For the given numerical values, at \(\ t = t_{2}\) we have

\(\ \underline{\underline{ v}}=r\dot{\omega_{0}t_{2} } = 30 · 5.24 · 1 =\underline{\underline{157.2 cm/s} }, \\ \underline{\underline{a_{\varphi}} } = 30 · 5.24 · 1 =\underline{\underline{157.2 cm/s^{2}} }, \\ \underline{\underline{a_{r}} }=- 30 ·\left( 5.24 · 1\right)^{2} =\underline{\underline{-823.7cm/s^{2}} }, \)

and

\(\ \underline{\underline{a} }=\sqrt{a^{2}_{\varphi }+a^{2}_{r } } =\underline{\underline{838.6 cm/s^{2}} }\)

The centripetal acceleration \(\ a_{r}\), which points towards the center of the flywheel, grows quadratically with t and is thus, after a short time, much larger than the time-independent angular acceleration \(\ a_{\varphi}\)
