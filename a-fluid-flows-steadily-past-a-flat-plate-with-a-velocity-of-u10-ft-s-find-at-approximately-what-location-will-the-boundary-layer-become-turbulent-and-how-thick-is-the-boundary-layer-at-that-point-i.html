Guest Question.

A fluid flows steadily past a flat plate with a velocity of U = 10 ft/s.

FIND At approximately what location will the boundary layer become turbulent, and how thick is the boundary layer at that point if the fluid is water at 60 °F, standard air, or glycerin at 68 °F?

Admin
Solution

For any fluid, the laminar boundary layer thickness is found
from \(\delta =5\sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U} } \) as

\(\delta =5\sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U} } \)

The boundary layer remains laminar up to

\(x_{cr}=\frac{\nu Re_{x,cr}}{U} \)

Thus, if we assume \(Re_{x,cr}=5\times 10^{5}\) we obtain

\(x_{cr}=\frac{5\times 10^{5}}{10{ft}/{s}} \nu =5\times 10^{4}\nu \)

and

\(\delta _{cr}\equiv \delta \mid _{x=x_{cr}}=5[\frac{\nu }{10}(5\times 10^{4}\nu ) ]^{{1}/{2}}=354\nu \)

where \(\nu\) is in \({ft^{2}}/{s}\) and \(x_{cr}\) and \(\delta _{cr}\) are in feet. The values of the kinematic viscosity obtained from Tables 1.4 to 1.6 are listed in Table E9.3 along with the corresponding \(x_{cr}\) and \(\delta _{cr}\).

Table E9.3

Fluid \(\nu ({ft^{2}}/{s})\) \(x_{cr}(ft)\) \(\delta _{cr}(ft)\)
Water \(1.21\times 10^{-5}\) 0.605 0.00428
Air \(1.57\times 10^{-4}\) 7.85 0.0556
Glycerin \(1.28\times 10^{-2}\) 640.0 4.53

COMMENT Laminar flow can be maintained on a longer portion of the plate if the viscosity is increased. However, the boundary layer flow eventually becomes turbulent, provided the plate is long enough. Similarly, the boundary layer thickness is greater if the viscosity is increased.
