Guest Question.

A 25- μm-diameter polished-platinum wire 6 mm long is to be used for a hot-wire anemometer to measure the velocity of 20°C air in the range between 2 and 10 m/s (see Fig. 7.13). The wire is to be placed into the circuit of the Wheatstone bridge shown in Fig. 7.12(a). Its temperature is to be maintained at 230°C by adjusting the current using the rheostat. To design the electrical circuit, it is necessary to know the required current as a function of air velocity. The electrical resistivity of platinum at 230°C is 17.1 μΩ cm.

Admin
Solution

Since the wire is very thin, conduction along it can be neglected; also, the temperature gradient in the wire at any cross section can be disregarded. At the free-stream temperature, the air has a thermal conductivity of 0.0251 W/m °C and a kinematic viscosity of \(1.57 × 10^{-5} m^2/s\). At a velocity of 2 m/s, the Reynolds number is

\(Re_D =\frac{(2 m/s)(25 \times 10^{-6} m)}{1.57 \times 10^{-5}m^2/s} = 3.18\)

The Reynolds number range of interest is therefore 1 to 40, so the correlation equation from Eq. (7.3)

\(\overline{Nu} _D =\frac{\overline{h} _{c} D}{k} = C\left(\frac{U_\infty D}{\nu } \right)^mPr^n\left(\frac{Pr}{Pr_s} \right) ^{0.25}\) (7.3)

and Table 7.1

\(40 – 1 × 10^3\) 0.51 0.5

is

\(\frac{\overline{h} _{c}D }{k} =0.75Re_D^{0.4}Pr^{0.37}\left(\frac{Pr}{Pr_s} \right)^{0.25}\)

Neglecting the small variation in Prandtl number from 20° to 230°C, the average convection heat transfer coefficient as a function of velocity is

\(\overline{h} _{c}=(0.75)(3.18)^{0.4}\left(\frac{U_\infty }{2} \right)^{0.4}(0.71)^{0.37}\left(\frac{0.0251 W/m K}{25 \times 10^{-6} m} \right)\)

 

\( =799 U^{0.4}_\infty W/m^2 °C\)

At this point, it is necessary to estimate the heat transfer coefficient for radiant heat flow. According to Eq. (1.21), we have

\(\overline{h}_r=\frac{K_r}{A_1} =\frac{\mathscr{F}_{1-2}\sigma \left(T^4_1 – T^4_2\right) }{T_1-T_2^\prime } W/m^2K(Btu/h ft^2 {^\circ }F ) \) (1.21)

\(\overline{h} _{r}=\frac{q_r}{A(T_s – T_\infty )}=\frac{\sigma \epsilon (T_s^4 – T_\infty ^4 )}{T_s – T_\infty }= \sigma \epsilon \left(T_s^2 + T_\infty ^ 2\right) \left( T_s + T_\infty \right)\)

or, since

\(\left(T_s^2 + T_\infty ^ 2 \right) \left(T_s + T_\infty \right)\thickapprox 4\left(\frac{T_s + T_\infty }{2} \right)^3\)

we have approximately

\(\overline{h} _{r} = \sigma \epsilon 4\left(\frac{T_s + T_\infty }{2} \right)^3\)

is about 0.05, so \(\overline{h} _{r}\) is about \(0.05 W/m^2 °C\). This shows that the amount of heat transferred by radiation is negligible compared to the heat transferred by forced convection.
The rate at which heat is transferred from the wire is therefore

\(q_c = \overline{h} _{c}A(T_s – T_\infty )=(799U^{0.4}_\infty )(\pi )(25 \times 10^{-6})(6 \times 10^{-3})(210)\) \( =0.0790U^{0.4}_\infty W\)

which must equal the rate of dissipation of electrical energy to maintain the wire at 230°C. The electrical resistance of the wire, \(R_e\), is

\(R_e =(17.1 \times 10^{-6} ohm cm)\frac{0.6 cm}{\pi(25 \times 10{-4}cm)^2/4 } = 2.09 ohm\)

A heat balance with the current i in amperes gives

\(i^2R_e=0.0790U^{0.4}_\infty\)

Solving for the current as a function of velocity, we get

\(i = \left(\frac{0.0790}{2.09} \right)^{1/2}U^{0.2}_\infty= 0.19U^{0.2}_\infty amp\)
