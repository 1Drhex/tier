Guest Question.

Golf Ball in Flight A 1.68 in. diameter golf ball is driven off a tee at 70 mph. Determine the drag force acting on the golf ball by (a) approximating it as a smooth sphere and (b) using the actual drag coefficient of 0.27.To find the drag force in part (a), we will begin by calculating the Reynolds number [Equation (6.6)] with the density and viscosity of air listed in Table 6.1. If Refor this situation is less than one, then it is acceptable to apply Equation (6.16). On the other hand, if the Reynolds number is larger, that equation can’t be used, and we will instead find the drag force from Equation (6.14) with \(C_{D}\) determined from Figure 6.23. We will use this latter approach to find the drag force in part (b).

 

\(R e=\frac{\rho v l}{\mu}\) (Equation 6.6)

 

Table 6.1 Density and Viscosity Values for Sevweral Gases and Liquid at Room Temperature and Pressure
Air 1.20 2.33 × 10-3 1.8 × 10-5 3.8 × 10-7
Helium 0.182 3.53 × 10-4 1.9 × 10-5 4.1 × 10-7
Freshwter 1000 1.94 1.0 × 10-3 2.1 × 10-5
Seawater 1026 1.99 1.2 × 10-3 2.5 × 10-5
Gasoline 680 1.32 2.9 × 10-4 6.1 × 10-6
SAE 30 oil 917 1.78 0.26 5.4 × 10-3

 

\(F_{ D } \approx 3 \pi \mu d v \quad(\text { Special case for a sphere: } \operatorname{Re}<1)\) (Equation 6.16)

 

\(F_{ D }=\frac{1}{2} \rho A v^{2} C_{ D }\) (Equation 6.14)

Solution

(a) In dimensionally consistent units, the golf ball’s speed is

 

\(\begin{aligned}v &=\left(70 \frac{ mi }{ h }\right)\left(5280 \frac{ ft }{ mi }\right)\left(\frac{1}{3600} \frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=102.7\left(\frac{ mi }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ ft }{ mi }\right)\left(\frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=102.7 \frac{ ft }{ s }\end{aligned}\)

 

Because the diameter is d =1.68 in. =0.14 ft, the Reynolds number becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}\operatorname{Re} &=\frac{\left(2.33 \times 10^{-3} \operatorname{slug} / ft ^{3}\right)(102.7 ft / s )(0.14 ft )}{3.8 \times 10^{-7} slug /( ft \cdot s )} \leftarrow\left[\operatorname{Re}=\frac{\rho v l}{\mu}\right] \\&=8.813 \times 10^{4}\left(\frac{ slug }{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left(\frac{ ft }{ s }\right)( ft )\left(\frac{ ft \cdot s }{ slug }\right) \\&=8.813 \times 10^{4}\end{aligned}\)

 

Since this value is much greater than one, we can’t apply Equation (6.16) or Equation (6.15) for the drag coefficient. We refer to Figure 6.23 to see that this value for Relies in the flat portion of the curve where \(C_{D} \approx 0.5\) The frontal area of the ball is

 

\(C_{D} \approx \frac{24}{R e} \quad(\text { Special case for a sphere: } \operatorname{Re}<1)\) (Equation 6.15)

 

\(F_{ D } \approx 3 \pi \mu d v \quad(\text { Special case for a sphere: } \operatorname{Re}<1)\) (Equation 6.16)

 

\(\begin{aligned}A &=\frac{\pi(0.14 ft )^{2}}{4} \leftarrow\left[A=\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}\right] \\&=1.539 \times 10^{-2} ft ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

The drag force then becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}F_{ D } &=\frac{1}{2}\left(2.33 \times 10^{-3} \frac{\text { slug }}{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left(1.539 \times 10^{-2} ft ^{2}\right)\left(102.7 \frac{ ft }{ s }\right)^{2}(0.5) \\&=9.452 \times 10^{-2}\left(\frac{\text { slug }}{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left( ft ^{2}\right)\left(\frac{ ft ^{3}}{ s ^{2}}\right) \leftarrow\left[F_{ D }=\frac{1}{2} \rho A v^{2} C_{ D }\right] \\&=9.452 \times 10^{-2} \frac{\text { slug } \cdot ft }{ s ^{2}} \\&=9.452 \times 10^{-2} lb\end{aligned}\)

 

where the final grouping of USCS dimensions is equivalent to the pound [Equation (3.3)].

 

\(1 lb =(1 slug )\left(1 \frac{ ft }{ s ^{2}}\right)=1 \frac{\text { slug } \cdot ft }{ s ^{2}}\) (Equation 3.3)

 

(b) With \(C_{ D }=0.27 \text { instead, }\) the drag force is reduced to

 

\(\begin{aligned}F_{ D } &=\frac{1}{2}\left(2.33 \times 10^{-3} \frac{\text { slug }}{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left(1.539 \times 10^{-2} ft ^{2}\right)\left(102.7 \frac{ ft }{ s }\right)^{2}(0.27) \\&=5.104 \times 10^{-2}\left(\frac{\text { slug }}{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left( ft ^{2}\right)\left(\frac{ ft ^{2}}{ s ^{2}}\right) \leftarrow\left[F_{ D }=\frac{1}{2} \rho A v^{2} C_{ D }\right] \\&=5.104 \times 10^{-2} \frac{\text { slug } \cdot ft }{ s ^{2}} \\&=5.104 \times 10^{-2} lb\end{aligned}\)
