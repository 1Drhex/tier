Guest Question.

Isentropic Expansion of an Ideal Gas

Air enters an isentropic turbine at 150 psia and 900°F through a \(0.5-ft^2\) inlet section with a velocity of 500 ft/s (Fig. 7–39). It leaves at 15 psia with a velocity of 100 ft/s. Calculate the air temperature at the turbine exit and the power produced, in hp, by this turbine.

Admin
Solution

Air is expanded in an isentropic turbine. The exit temperature of the air and the power produced are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 This is a steady-flow process since there is no change with time. 2 The process is isentropic (i.e., reversible-adiabatic). 3 Air is an ideal gas with constant specific heats.

Propertles The properties of air at an anticipated average temperature of \(600^{\circ} \mathrm{F}\) are \(c_{p}=0.250 \mathrm{Btu} / \mathrm{lbm} \cdot \mathrm{R}\) and \(k=1.377\) . The gas constant of air is \(R=0.3704 \mathrm{psia} \cdot \mathrm{ft}^{3} / \mathrm{lbm} \cdot \mathrm{R}\) .

Analysis There is only one inlet and one exit, and thus \(m_{1}=\dot{m}_{2}=\dot{m} .\) We take the turbine as the system, which is a control volume since mass crosses the boundary. The energy balance for this steady-flow system can be expressed in the rate form as

\(\begin{aligned}\underbrace{\dot{E}_{\mathrm{in}}-\dot{E}_{\mathrm{out}}}_{\begin{array}{c}\text { Rate of net energy transfer } \\\text { by heat, work, and mass }\end{array}}&=\underbrace{dE_{\mathrm{system}}dt^{\nearrow 0 ( steady )} }_{\begin{array}{r}\text { Rate of change in internal, kinetic, } \\\text { potential, etc., energies }\end{array}}= 0 \\\dot{E}_{\text {in }} &=\dot{E}_{\text {out }} \\\dot{m}\left(h_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2}\right) &=\dot{m}\left(h_{2}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2}\right)+\dot{W}_{\text {out }} \\\dot{W}_{\text {out }} &=\dot{m}\left(h_{1}-h_{2}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}-V_{2}^{2}}{2}\right) \\&=\dot{m}\left[c_{p}\left(T_{1}-T_{2}\right)+\frac{V_{1}^{2}-V_{2}^{2}}{2}\right]\end{aligned}\)

The exit temperature of the air for this isentropic process is

\(T_{2}=T_{1}\left(\frac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}\right)^{(k-1) / k}=(900+460 \mathrm{R})\left(\frac{15 \mathrm{psia}}{150 \mathrm{psia}}\right)^{0.377 / 1.377}=724 \mathrm{R}\)

The specific volume of air at the inlet and the mass flow rate are

\(\begin{array}{c}v_{1}=\frac{R T_{1}}{P_{1}}=\frac{\left(0.3704 \mathrm{psia} \cdot \mathrm{ft}^{3} / \mathrm{lbm} \cdot \mathrm{R}\right)(900+460 \mathrm{R})}{150 \mathrm{psia}}=3.358 \mathrm{ft}^{3} / \mathrm{lbm} \\\dot{m}=\frac{A_{1} V_{1}}{v_{1}}=\frac{\left(0.5 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\right)(500 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s})}{3.358 \mathrm{ft}^{3} / \mathrm{lbm}}=74.45 \mathrm{lbm} / \mathrm{s}\end{array}\)

Substituting into the energy balance equation gives

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{W}_{\text {out }}=& \dot{m}\left[c_{p}\left(T_{1}-T_{2}\right)+\frac{V_{1}^{2}-V_{2}^{2}}{2}\right] \\\\=&(74.45 \mathrm{lbm} / \mathrm{s})[(0.250 \mathrm{Btu} / \mathrm{lbm} \cdot \mathrm{R})(1360-724) \mathrm{R}\\\\&\left.+\frac{(500 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s})^{2}-(100 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}{2}\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{Btu} / \mathrm{lbm}}{25,037 \mathrm{ft}^{2} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}\right)\right] \\\\=& 12,194 \mathrm{Btu} / \mathrm{s}\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{hp}}{0.7068 \mathrm{Btu} / \mathrm{s}}\right)=17,250 \mathrm{hp}\end{aligned}\)

Discussion An actual adiabatic turbine will produce less power due to irreversibilities. Also, the exit temperature of the air will be higher in the actual turbine corresponding to a smaller enthalpy change between the inlet and the exit.
