Guest Question.

Imagine that a particle can exist in one of two states, which we will label \(\mid 0〉\) and \(\mid 1〉.\) We now consider two such particles, and describe their joint behaviour by a product state. Thus \(\mid 0〉\mid 1〉\) (29.7) describes the state in which the first particle is in state 0 and the second particle is in state 1. What are the possible states for this system if the particles are (a) distinguishable, (b) indistinguishable, but classical, (c) indistinguishable bosons, and (d) indistinguishable fermions?

Admin
Solution

(a) Distinguishable particles There are four possible states, which are

\( \mid 0〉\mid 0〉, \mid 1〉\mid 0〉, \mid 0〉\mid 1〉, \mid 1〉\mid 1〉. \) (29.8)

(b) Indistinguishable, but classical, particles There are now only three possible states, which are

\( \mid 0〉\mid 0〉, \mid 1〉\mid 0〉, \mid 1〉\mid 1〉.\) (29.9)

Since the particles are indistinguishable, there is no way of distin-guishing the state \(\mid 1〉\mid 0〉\) and \( \mid 0〉\mid 1〉.\)

(c) Indistinguishable bosons There are also only three possible states. Clearly both \(\mid 0〉\mid 0〉\) and \(\mid 1〉\mid 1〉\) are eigenstates of the exchange operator, but \(\mid 1〉\mid 0〉\) and \(\mid 0〉\mid 1〉\) are not. However, if we make a linear combination\(^{2}\)

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid 1〉\mid 0〉+\mid 0〉\mid 1〉),\) (29.10)

this will be an eigenstate of the exchange operator with eigen-value 1. Thus the three possible states are (all symmetric under exchange):

\( \mid 0〉\mid 0〉, \mid 1〉\mid 1〉, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid 1〉\mid 0〉+\mid 0〉\mid 1〉). \) (29.11)

(d) Indistinguishable fermions No two fermions can be in the same quantum state (by the Pauli exclusion principle), so \(\mid 0〉\mid 0〉\) and \(\mid 1〉\mid 1〉\) are not allowed. Thus only one state is possible, which is antisymmetric under exchange:

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid 1〉\mid 0〉-\mid 0〉\mid 1〉). \) (29.12)

This wave function is an eigenstate of the exchange operator with eigenvalue −1.

\(^{2}\)This example demonstrates quantum-mechanical entanglement. The states of the two particles are entangled be-cause they cannot be separated into a product of two single-particle states. In this case, if one particle is in the \(\mid 0〉\) state, the other particle has to be in the \(\mid 1〉\) state, and vice versa. Thus the behaviour of the two particles are cor-related. An example of a non-entangled state is \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid 0〉\mid 0〉+\mid 0〉\mid 1〉),\) which can be separated into \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(\mid 0〉\mid 0〉+\mid 1〉),\) a prod-uct state.
