Guest Question.

Consider the linkage in when the final position specified there is the starting configuration, so that \(L_{o} = 1.5 m\), \(L_{\mathrm{o}}=1.5 \mathrm{~m}, \theta_{\mathrm{o}}=50^{\circ}, \beta_{\mathrm{o}}=30^{\circ},\gamma_{o} = 60º\). From that position the following increments are applied: \(\Delta{L}= 12 mm\), \(\Delta \theta=0.5^{\circ}, \Delta \beta=-0.5^{\circ}, \Delta \gamma=-1^{\circ}\) . Determine the associated displacement using the exact matrix transformation technique, and compare it with the approximate value obtained by considering the rotation to be infinitesimal.

Admin
Solution

An objective of this example is to demonstrate the simplifications encountered in the transition from finite displacement to velocity. To apply the developments in the previous example here, we recognize that the final configuration obtained there is now the initial, so we have

\(\begin{array}{c}{\left[R_{1}\right]_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 0 & 0 \\\\0 & \cos \beta_{\mathrm{o}} & \sin \beta_{\mathrm{o}} \\\\0 & -\sin\beta_{\mathrm{o}} &\cos\beta_{\mathrm{o}}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \theta_{\mathrm{o}} & \sin\theta_{\mathrm{o}} & 0 \\\\-\sin \theta_{\mathrm{o}} & \cos \theta_{\mathrm{o}} & 0\\\\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right],} \\\\{\left[R_{2}\right]_{\mathrm{o}}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \gamma_{\mathrm{o}} & 0 & \sin \gamma_{\mathrm{o}} \\\\0 & 1 & 0 \\\\-\sin \gamma_{\mathrm{o}} & 0&\cos\gamma_{\mathrm{o}}\end{array}\right]\left[R_{1}\right]_{\mathrm{o}} .}\end{array}\)

The transformations \([R_{1}]_{f} and [R_{1}]_{f}\) for the final location are obtained by replacing the angles in the preceding by \(\theta_{\mathrm{o}}+\Delta \theta, \beta_{\mathrm{o}}+\Delta \beta, \text { and } \gamma_{\mathrm{o}}+\Delta \gamma\) Relative to \(x_{2} y_{2}z_{2}\),which is attached to arm BC, the position and displacement are \(\left(\bar{r}_{C / B}\right)_{0}=1.5 \bar{i}_{2}\) and \(\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{C}\right)_{x_{2} y_{2} z_{2}}=(\Delta L) \bar{i}_{2}\) It still is true that \(\bar{r}_{B / A}=2 \bar{i} \text { and } \Delta \bar{r}_{A}=\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{B}\right)_{x_{1} y_{1} z_{1}}=\overline{0}\). Substitution of these terms into Eq. (3.2.7) to evaluate \(\Delta\bar{r}_{B}\), and then \(\Delta\bar{r}_{C}\), leads to

\(\begin{aligned}\left\{\begin{array}{c}\Delta \bar{r}_{P} \cdot \bar{I} \\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{P} \cdot \bar{J} \\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{P} \cdot \bar{K}\end{array}\right\}=&\left\{\begin{array}{l}\Delta \bar{r}_{O^{\prime}} \cdot \bar{I} \\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{O^{\prime}} \cdot \bar{J} \\\\\Delta\bar{r}_{O^{\prime}} \cdot \bar{K}\end{array}\right\}+[R]_{\mathrm{f}}^{\mathrm{T}}\left\{\begin{array}{l}\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{P}\right)_{x y z} \cdot \bar{i} \\\\\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{P}\right)_{x y z} \cdot \bar{j} \\\\\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{p}\right)_{x y z} \cdot \bar{k}\end{array}\right\}\\\\\\&+\left[[R]_{\mathrm{f}}^{\mathrm{T}}-[R]_{\mathrm{o}}^{\mathrm{T}}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\left(x_{P}\right)_{\mathrm{o}} \\\\\left(y_{P}\right)_{\mathrm{o}}\\\\\left(z_{P}\right)_{\mathrm{o}}\end{array}\right\}\end{aligned}\) (3.2.7)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}\left\{\begin{array}{c}\Delta\bar{r}_{B} \cdot \bar{I} \\\\\Delta\bar{r}_{B} \cdot \bar{J} \\\\\Delta\bar{r}_{B} \cdot \bar{K}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}13.42 \\\\11.16 \\\\0\end{array}\right\}\mathrm{mm}, \\\\\left\{\begin{array}{c}\Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{I}\\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{J} \\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{K}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}-5.28 \\\\ 49.08 \\\\ 3.01\end{array}\right\}\mathrm{mm}.\end{array}\)

 

To perform the analysis based on the infinitesimal approximation we use Eq. (3.3.6) to relate the displacements of points \(A, B\), and \(C .\) The rotation angles from the initial to final positions are small, but finite, so these relations are approximate. We let \(\overline{\Delta \theta}_{1}\) and \(\overline{\Delta \theta}_{2}\) denote small rotation vectors for the respective reference frames. Because \(\Delta \bar{r}_{A}=\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{B}\right)_{x_{1} y_{1} z_{1}}=\overline{0}\), we have

\(\Delta \bar{r}_{B} \approx \overline{\Delta \theta}_{1} \times \bar{r}_{B / A}, \quad \Delta \bar{r}_{C} \approx \Delta \bar{r}_{B}+\left(\Delta \bar{r}_{C}\right)_{x_{2} y_{2} z_{2}}+\overline{\Delta \theta}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{C / B}\).

 

Each small rotation vector is the vector sum of the scalar angles in the directions of the respective axes. The rotation of \(x_{1} y_{1} z_{1}\) consists of \(\Delta \theta\) about the \(Z\) axis, followed by \(\Delta \beta\) about the \(x_{1}\) axis. (Because \(\beta\) is not zero, the \(z_{1}\) axis is not vertical, so we cannot say that \(\Delta \theta\) is about the zuxis.) The vector sum of these rotations is

\(\overline{\Delta \theta}_{1}=\Delta \theta \bar{K}+\Delta \beta \bar{i}_{1}.\)

Because \(x_{2} y_{2} z\), rotates by \(\beta\) about the negative \(y_{2}\) axis relative to \(x_{1} y_{1} z_{1}\), its rotation vector is the sum of the rotation of \(x_{1} y_{1} z_{1}\) and \(\Delta \gamma\) in the direction of \(-\bar{j_{2}}:\)

 

\(\overline{\Delta \theta}_{2}=\Delta \theta \bar{K}+\Delta \beta \bar{i}_{1}-\Delta \gamma \bar{j}_{2}.\)

Note that the rotation angles must be expressed in units of radians.

To form the sums we need to express all quantities in terms of components rela. tive to the same set of coordinate axes. We could use \(\bar{I} \bar{J} \bar{K}\) components for this with the aid of rotation transformations. However, if we use \(\bar{i}_{1} \bar{j}_{1} \bar{k}_{1}\) components we can find the required vectors by inspection. We refer back to the sketch of the coordinate aves in Example 3.6. Because \(k_{1}\) remains coincident with \(R\) in the \(\theta\) rotation, \(K\) is situated in the \(y_{1} z_{1}\) plane, such that \(\bar{K} =\sin \beta_{0} \bar{j}_{1}+\cos \beta_{\circ}\bar k_{1}\). (Note that, because \(\beta\) changes little, we may use the initial geometrical arrangement to describe components.) The \(y_{1}\) and \(y_{2}\) axes are parallel, so \(\bar{j}_{2}=\bar{j}_{1}\). Thus we find that

\(\begin{array}{c}\overline{\Delta \theta}_{1}=\Delta \theta\left(\sin \beta_{\circ} \bar{j}_{1}+\cos \beta_{\circ} \bar{k}_{1}\right)+\Delta \beta \bar{i}_{1}, \\\\\overline{\Delta \theta}_{2}=\Delta \theta\left(\sin \beta_{a}\bar {j}_{1}+\cos \beta_{e} \bar{k}_{1}\right)+\Delta \beta \bar{t}_{1}-\Delta\gamma\bar{j}_{1}.\end{array}\)

The positions and relative displacements were described earlier as \(\bar{r}_{B / A}=2 \bar{i}_{1}\), \(\bar{r}_{C / B}=1.5 \bar{i}_{2},(\Delta \bar{r} c)_{x_{1} / 2}=(\Delta L) \bar{i}_{2}\). We convert the last two to \(\bar{i}_{1} \bar{j}_{1} \bar{k}_{1}\) components by observing that the \(\gamma\) rotation leaves \(\bar{i}_{2}\) in the \(x_{1} z_{1}\) plane, so that \(\bar{i}_{2}=\cos \gamma_{0} \bar{i}_{1}+\) \(\sin \gamma_{\circ} k_{1}\). The result of substitution of the various terms corresponding to the given values of the angles and their increments into the earlier vector expressions for \(\bar{u}_{B}\) and \(\bar{u}_{C}\) is

\(\begin{array}{c}\Delta \bar{r}_{B}=0.01511 \bar{j}_{1}-0.00873 \bar{k}_{1} \mathrm{~m}, \\\\\Delta \bar{r}_{C}=0.03434 \bar{i}_{1}+0.03212 \bar{j}_{1}-0.01470 \bar{k}_{1} \mathrm{~m} .\end{array}\)

The first evaluation of \(\Delta \bar{r}_{C}\) gave the result in terms of components relative to \(X Y Z\). There is little difference between the initial and the final orientation of \(x y z\) in the present situation, so we may use either the inital or final \(\left[R_{1}\right]\) to convert the preceding approximate displacement to those components. Using the initial transformation gives

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l} \Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{I} \\\\ \Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{J} \\\\ \Delta \bar{r}_{C} \cdot \bar{K} \end{array}\right\}=\left[R_{1}\right]_{0}^{\mathrm{T}}\left\{\begin{array}{c} 0.03434 \\\\ 0.03212 \\\\-0.01470 \end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c} -4.86 \\\\ 48.91 \\\\ 3.33 \end{array}\right\} \mathrm{mm}.\)

These values are quite close to the previous; decreasing the values of \(\Delta \theta, \Delta \beta, \Delta \gamma \) , and \(\Delta L\) would improve the agreement.

An interesting aspect of the analysis based on the infinitesimal displacement approximation is that the only place where a rotation transformation matrix was required was to convert the displacement to the components found from the exact transformation procedure. The steps we followed to carry out the approximate analysis are like those by which we will analyze velocity and acceleration.
