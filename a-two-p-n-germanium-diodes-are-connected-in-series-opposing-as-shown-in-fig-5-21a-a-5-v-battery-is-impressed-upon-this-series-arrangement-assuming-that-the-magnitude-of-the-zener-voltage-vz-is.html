Guest Question.

(a) Two p-n germanium diodes are connected in series opposing as shown in Fig. 5.21a. A 5 V battery is impressed upon this series arrangement. Assuming that the magnitude of the Zener voltage, \(V_{Z}\) , is greater than 5.0 V, find the voltage across each junction at room temperature. Show that it is independent of the reverse saturation current. Is it dependent on temperature?

(b) If the magnitude of the Zener voltage is 9 V and reverse saturation current is 5 \(\mu \)A, what will be the current in the circuit?

(c) Suppose that the Zener break down voltage of D2 is 2V and the reverse saturation current is 5 \(\mu \)A If the diode resistances could be neglected, what would be the current in the circuit?

(d) If the ohmic resistance of D1 in part (c) is 100 Ω and the Zener resistance of D2 is neglected, what would be the current?

Admin
Solution

(a) Since the diodes are connected in series, same current, say I, should flow through each diode in the circuit. Clearly, I through D1 is in the forward direction while it is in the reverse direction in D2.

Let V (volt) be the voltage across D1. Clearly, the voltage drop across D2 is 5 2 V (volt) which makes D2 reverse‑biased. However, the condition (5 – V) <VZ ensures that D2 is not in the breakdown region and hence will operate as a normal reverse‑biased diode. Thus, the currents of D1 and D2 are obtained from Eq. (5.33) , \(I=I_{o}[exp(V/\eta V_{T})-1]\) as

\(I_{1}=I_{o}\left\lgroup exp \left\lgroup\frac{V}{\eta V_{T}} \right\rgroup -1 \right\rgroup \) and \(I_{2}=I_{o}\left\lgroup exp \left\lgroup\frac{5-V}{\eta V_{T}} \right\rgroup -1 \right\rgroup \) respectively. Since, \(I=I_{1}=-I_{2} \) , we can write

\(exp\left\lgroup\frac{11600 V}{T} \right\rgroup +exp\left\lgroup-\frac{11600(5-V)}{T} \right\rgroup =2 \)

where we have used η = 1 (for germanium diodes), and \(V_{T}=\frac{T}{11600} \) The voltage V can thus be given by

\(V(in volt) =\frac{T}{11600}1n\left[\frac{2}{1+exp(-58000 /T)} \right] \approx 5.98\times 10^{-5} T \)

where we have used 1+exp(258000/T) ≈ 1 for any practical value of the operating temperature T. Using T = 300°K for room temperature, we get V = 0.018 V. Thus, the voltages appearing across the diodes D1 and D2 are 0.018 V and 5.0 – 0.018 ≈ 4.98 V respectively. Note that V is independent of \(I_{o} \) but function of T, which implies that the voltages across the junctions are independent of the reverse saturation current but function of the operating temperature of the diodes.

(b) Since, the voltage 98 V across D2 is greater than \(V_{Z} \) = 4.9 V, the diode D2 will work in the Zener breakdown region and hence voltage across D2 becomes fixed at \(V_{Z} \) = 4.9 V (Fig. 5.21b). Thus, the voltage appearing across D1 now becomes 5.0 – 4.9 = 0.1 V. Note that the current in circuit will depend on the forward‑biased diode D1 which can be obtained as

\(I=5\times 10^{-6}\left\lgroup exp \left\lgroup\frac{0.1}{26\times 10^{-3}} \right\rgroup -1 \right\rgroup =5\times 10^{-6}(46.5 -1)=228 \mu A \)

where we have used \(I_{o}=5\times 10^{-6} \) A and \(V_{T}=26\times 10^{-3} \) V (at room temperature ) for the above calculation.

(c) Since D2 works in the breakdown region with \(V_{Z} \)= 2 V, the voltage appearing across D1 is 5 –\(V_{Z} \)= 3V (Fig. 21b). Note that as the forward bias voltage, say V, of a diode is increased, the potential barrier of the p-n junction is reduced by V amount from its unbiased value V0 (i.e. built‑in voltage), which in turn reduces the total width of the depletion layer W (see part (a) of the ). For V = V0 ; W = 0, and hence carriers can freely move from one side to the other of the p-n junction. Clearly, the maximum voltage that can be applied across the junction of a diode for its normal operation under forward bias condition would be equal to V0 (which is less than unity in the case of a germanium diode). If the diode resistance is neglected, the p-n junction ceased to operate as a diode for V > V0 and Eq. (5.33) to obtain the current flowing through the diode is no longer valid. Thus for, V = 3 V, diode would behave as a conductor with negligible resistance resulting in a very large current (theoretically infinite !!) flowing through the circuit which could damage both the diodes and power supplies in the circuit.

(d) Since the diode resistance 100 Ω of D1 appears in series of the diode and diode D2 is in the breakdown region with Zener voltage \(V_{Z} \) = 2V (Fig. 21c), a part \(V_{R}=IR_{f} \) of the total voltage \(V_{1}=5-V_{Z}=3 \) V appearing across \(D_{1} \) would be dropped across the resistance \(R_{f} \) = 100 Ω due to the diode current I, and \(V_{1}-V_{R}=Vjun\) (say) would be appeared across the p-n junction of D1. Thus, the current I in the circuit can now be obtained by solving the equation

\(I=I_{o}\left[exp \left\lgroup\frac{V_{jun}}{\eta V_{T}} \right\rgroup -1 \right] =I_{o}\left[exp \left\lgroup\frac{3-100 I}{0.026} \right\rgroup -1 \right] \)

A suitable numerical method can be employed to solve the above equation which gives I = 27.8 mA. Note that voltage drop across the p-n junction Vjun = 3 2 2.78 = 0.22 V which gives also the diode current I = 27.8 mA.
