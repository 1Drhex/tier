Guest Question.

Bevel gear A is rolling around the fixed horizontal bevel gear B, as shown in Fig. 12.31. Gear A also rotates about the shaft OG with angular velocity ω1. The shaft OG, attached to a vertical shaft by clevis pin O, is initially at rest when it is given a constant angular acceleration, \(\dot{\Omega}\) . If the weight of gear A is 20 lb, and the weight of shaft OG is negligible, determine the tangential force and the normal force between the two gears as a function of the angle θ.

Admin
Solution

The coordinate system shown by the x and y axes in Fig. 12.31 is fixed to shaft OG and it is not stationary. The origin of this system is chosen at fixed point O, and the axes are principal axes of inertia. Since gear A is symmetric about the x axis, its mass properties remain constant.

The mass moments of inertia of gear A about point O are

\(I_{O}^{x x}=\frac{1}{2} m R^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{20 lb }{386 in / s ^{2}}\right)(8 in )^{2}=1.658 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\) (1)

 

\(\begin{aligned}I_{O}^{y y} &=I_{O}^{z z}=I_{G}^{y y}+m l^{2}=1 / 4 m R^{2}+m l^{2} \\&=\left(\frac{20 lb }{386 in / s ^{2}}\right)\left(\frac{(8 in )^{2}}{4}+(16 in )^{2}\right)=14.093 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\end{aligned}\) (2)

and, since these are principal axes, the products of inertia are

\(I_{O}^{x y}=I_{O}^{y z}=I_{G}^{x z}=0\) (3)

The angular velocity and angular acceleration of gear A, respectively, are

\(\omega =\left(\omega_{1}+\Omega \cos \theta\right) \hat{ i }-\Omega \sin \theta \hat{ j } rad / s\) (4)

 

\(\alpha=\left(\dot{\omega}_{1}+\dot{\Omega} \cos \theta\right) \hat{ i }-\dot{\Omega} \sin \theta \hat{ j }+\omega_{1} \Omega \sin \theta \hat{ k } \operatorname{rad} / s ^{2}\) (5)

 

The velocity of the point of contact, C, between the two gears can be written as

\(V _{C}= V _{O}+ \omega \times R _{C O}\)

 

where \(R _{C O}=16 \hat{ i }-8 \hat{ j } \text { in. }\) Since the velocity of points O and C are zero then this equation can be written, with the aid of Eq. (4), as

\(\omega \times R _{C O}=\left[-(8 \text { in })\left(\omega_{1}+\Omega \cos \theta\right)+(16 \text { in }) \Omega \sin \theta\right] \hat{ k }= 0\)

Rearranging this equation, the angular velocity of gear A relative to shaft OG is

\(\omega_{1}=\Omega(2 \sin \theta-\cos \theta)\) (6)

Differentiating this equation with respect to time gives

\(\dot{\omega}_{1}=\dot{\Omega}(2 \sin \theta-\cos \theta)\) (7)

Substituting Eqs. (6) and (7) into Eqs. (4) and (5), respectively, gives

\(\omega=\Omega \sin \theta(2 \hat{ i }-\hat{ j })\) (8)

 

\(\alpha =\dot{\Omega} \sin \theta(2 \hat{ i }-\hat{ j })+\Omega^{2} \sin \theta(2 \sin \theta-\cos \theta) \hat{ k }\) (9)

From Eq. (12.125), the time rate of change of the angular momentum of gear A can be written as

\(\dot{ H }_{O}=\left(\frac{d H_{O}}{d t} \hat{ H }_{O}\right)_{x y z}+ \Omega \times H _{O}\) (12.125)

 

\(\dot{ H }_{O}=\left(\frac{d H_{O}}{d t} \hat{ H }_{O}\right)_{x y z}+ \omega \times H _{O}\) (10)

From Eqs. (12.117) and (12.118), the angular momentum of gear A about point O can be written as

\(H _{G}=H_{G}^{x} \hat{ i }+H_{G}^{y} \hat{ j }+H_{G}^{z} \hat{ k }\) (12.117)

 

\(\begin{aligned}H_{G}^{x} &=+I_{G}^{x x} \omega^{x}-I_{G}^{x y} \omega^{y}-I_{G}^{x z} \omega^{z} \\H_{G}^{y} &=-I_{G}^{y x} \omega^{x}+I_{G}^{y y} \omega^{y}-I_{G}^{y z} \omega^{z} \\H_{G}^{z} &=-I_{G}^{z x} \omega^{x}-I_{G}^{z y} \omega^{y}+I_{G}^{z z} \omega^{z}\end{aligned}\) (12.118)

 

\(H _{O}=I_{O}^{x x} \omega^{x} \hat{ i }+I_{O}^{y y} \omega^{y} \hat{ j }+I_{O}^{z z} \omega^{z} \hat{ k }\) (11)

Substituting Eqs. (1), (2), and (8) into this equation gives

\(\begin{aligned}H _{O} &=\left(1.658 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)(2 \Omega \sin \theta) \hat{ i }+\left(14.093 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)(-\Omega \sin \theta) \hat{ j } \\&=\Omega \sin \theta\left(3.316 \hat{ i }-14.093 \hat{ j } in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)\end{aligned}\) (12)

Differentiating Eq. (11) with respect to time gives

\(\left(\frac{d H_{O}}{d t} \hat{ H }_{O}\right)_{x y z}=I_{O}^{x x} \alpha^{x} \hat{ i }+I_{O}^{y y} \alpha^{y} \hat{ j }+I_{O}^{z z} \alpha^{z} \hat{ k }\)

Substituting Eqs. (1), (2), and (9) into this equation gives

\(\begin{aligned}\left(\frac{d H_{O}}{d t} \hat{ H }_{O}\right)_{x y z}=&\left(1.658 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)(2 \dot{\Omega} \sin \theta) \hat{ i }+\left(14.093 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)(-\dot{\Omega} \sin \theta) \hat{ j } \\&+\left(14.093 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)\left[\Omega^{2} \sin \theta(2 \sin \theta-\cos \theta)\right] \hat{ k }\end{aligned}\)

which can be written as

\(\left(\frac{d H_{O}}{d t} \hat{ H }_{O}\right)_{x y z}=\sin \theta\left[3.316 \dot{\Omega} \hat{ i }-14.093 \dot{\Omega} \hat{ j }+14.093 \Omega^{2}(2 \sin \theta-\cos \theta) \hat{ k }\right] \text { in } \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\) (13)

Substituting Eqs. (8), (12), and (13) into Eq. (10) gives

\(\dot{ H }_{O}=\sin \theta\left[3.316 \dot{\Omega} \hat{ i }-14.093 \dot{\Omega} \hat{ j }+\Omega^{2}(3.316 \sin \theta-14.093 \cos \theta) \hat{ k }\right] \text { in } \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\) (14)

From the free-body diagram of the shaft and the gear shown in Fig. 12.32, the sum of the moments about point O can be written as

\(\begin{aligned}\sum M _{O} &=M_{O}^{y} \hat{ j }+ R _{G O} \times(20 lb )(\cos \theta \hat{ i }-\sin \theta \hat{ j })+ R _{G O} \times(N \hat{ j }+f \hat{ k }) \\&=-(8 \text { in }) f \hat{ i }+\left[M_{O}^{y}-(16 in ) f\right] \hat{ j }+[-(320 in \cdot lb ) \sin \theta+(16 in ) N] \hat{ k }\end{aligned}\) (15)

where f is the tangential force between the two gears at the point of contact C.

Equating the \(\hat{ i }\) components of Eqs. (14) and (15) gives

\(-(8 \text { in }) f=\left(3.316 in \cdot 1 b \cdot s ^{2}\right) \sin \theta \dot{\Omega}\)

Therefore, the tangential force between the two gears is

\(f=-\left(0.415 lb \cdot s ^{2}\right) \sin \theta \dot{\Omega}\)

Equating the \(\hat{ j }\) components of Eqs. (14) and (15) gives

\(M_{O}^{y}-(16 in ) f=-\left(14.093 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right) \sin \theta \dot{\Omega}\)

Substituting the tangential force into this equation, and rearranging, the moment about point O can be written as

\(M_{O}^{y}=-\left(20.725 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right) \sin \theta \dot{\Omega}\)

Finally, equating the \(\hat{ k }\) components of Eqs. (14) and (15) gives

\(-(320 in \cdot lb ) \sin \theta+(16 in ) N=\Omega^{2} \sin \theta(3.316 \sin \theta-14.093 \cos \theta) in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\)

Rearranging this equation, the normal reaction force can be written as

\(N=20 \sin \theta lb +\Omega^{2} \sin \theta(0.207 \sin \theta-0.881 \cos \theta) lb \cdot s ^{2}\)

Note that the first term on the right-hand side of this equation represents the static gravitational effect, and the second term represents two opposing inertial effects. If gear A did not mesh with the fixed gear B, then the angular velocity ω1 would be zero, and the centripetal acceleration of point G would decrease the normal reaction force N. Due to ω1, however, a gyroscopic moment is created that has the effect of forcing gear A downward. Note that if θ = tan-1 (0.881/0.207) = 76.78°, then the two dynamic effects counterbalance each other and the normal reaction force reduces to its static value of N = 20 sinθ lb.
