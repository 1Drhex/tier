Guest Question.

Two bars, pinned at joint B, move in the horizontal plane subject only to the restriction that the velocity of end C must be directed toward end A. Determine the corresponding velocity constraint. Is this constraint holonomic?

Admin
Solution

The objective here is to demonstrate the procedure for analyzing constraint conditions that are more complicated than the one in the . Determining whether the velocity constraint is holonomic will require more ingenuity on our part. The position of each bar is uniquely specified by the coordinates of pin \(B\) and the angle of rotation of each bar. Hence we define \(q_{1}=x_{B}, q_{2}=y_{B}\), \(q_{3}=\theta, q_{4}=\phi .\) The given condition on the velocity of point \(C\) may be written in vector form as

\(\bar{v}_{C}=v_{C} \bar{e}_{A / C}.\)

We must express this condition in terms of the generalized coordinates, which means that the speed \(v_{c}\) should be eliminated. Thus we restate the constraint condition as

\(\bar{v}_{C} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}=\overline{0} (1)\)

Points \(B\) and \(C\) are common to the same body, so their velocities are related by

\(\bar{v}_{C}=\bar{v}_{B}+\bar{\omega}_{B C} \times \bar{r}_{C / B}.\)

We express each of the terms on the right side in terms of the generalized coordinates to find

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{v}_{C} &=\dot{X}_{B} \bar{I}+\dot{Y}_{B} \bar{J}+\dot{\phi} \bar{K} \times\left(-L_{2} \cos \phi \bar{I}-L_{2} \sin \phi \bar{J}\right) \\\\&=\left(\dot{X}_{B}+\dot{\phi} L_{2} \sin \phi\right) \bar{I}+\left(\dot{Y}_{B}-\dot{\phi} L_{2} \cos \phi\right) \bar{J}.\end{aligned} \) (2)

Also, the position vector is

\(\bar{r}_{A / C}=\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right) \bar{I}+\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right) \bar{J} .\) (3)

Substitution of Eqs. (2) and (3) into Eq. (1) leads to

\(\begin{aligned}\left(\bar{v}_{C} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}\right) \cdot \bar{K}=&\left(\dot{X}_{B}+\dot{\phi} L_{2} \sin \phi\right)\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right) \\\\&-\left(\dot{Y}_{B}-\dot{\phi} L_{2} \cos \phi\right)\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right)=0 .\end{aligned} \)

Collecting the coefficients of each generalized velocity converts this equation to the standard form of a linear velocity constraint:

\(\begin{array}{c}a_{11} \dot{X}_{B}+a_{12} \dot{Y}_{B}+a_{13} \dot{\theta}+a_{14} \dot{\phi}=0, \\\\a_{11}=L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi, \quad a_{12}=-\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right), \quad a_{13}=0, \\\\a_{14}=L_{2} \sin \phi\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right)+L_{2} \cos \phi\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right) \\\\\equiv L_{1} L_{2} \cos (\theta-\phi)+L_{2}^{2}.\end{array} \)

To determine whether the constraint equation is integrable we convert it to Pfaffian form by multiplying by \(d t\), which gives

\(\begin{array}{l}\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right) d X_{B}-\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right) d Y_{B} \\\\\quad+\left[L_{1} L_{2} \cos (\theta-\phi)+L_{2}^{2}\right] d \phi=0.\end{array} \)

In view of the appearance of \(\theta\) and \(\phi\) in the terms containing \(d X_{B}\) and \(d Y_{B}\) and the absence of \(X_{B}\) and \(Y_{B}\) in the coefficient of \(d \phi\), it certainly does not appear that this differential constraint equation can be integrated. This, however, is not a proof. We proceed to the alternative strategy described by Eqs. (7.2.8) Thus we assume that the velocity constraint equation is equivalent to \(f_{1}\left(q_{n}, t\right)=0\), and then seek a configuration function that is consistent with all of the conditions. Equations (7.2.7) in the present case require that

\(\begin{aligned} f_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right)=& \int g_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right) a_{i j}\left(q_{n}, t\right) d q_{j}+h_{i j}\left(q_{n}, t\right), \quad j=1,2, \ldots, N, \\\\ & f_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right)=\int g_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right) b_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right) d t+h_{i t}\left(q_{n}\right). \end{aligned} (7.2.8)\)

 

\(g_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right) a_{i j}\left(q_{n}, t\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial q_{j}} f_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right), \quad j=1,2, \ldots, N, (7.2.7a)\)

 

\(g_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right) b_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial t} f_{i}\left(q_{n}, t\right). (7.2.7b)\)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}\frac{\partial f_{1}}{\partial X_{B}}=g_{1}\left(X_{B}, Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right)\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right), \\\\\frac{\partial f_{1}}{\partial Y_{B}}=-g_{1}\left(X_{B}, Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right)\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right), \\\\\frac{\partial f_{1}}{\partial \theta}=0, \\\\\frac{\partial f_{1}}{\partial \phi}=g_{1}\left(X_{B}, Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right)\left[L_{1} L_{2} \cos (\theta-\phi)+L_{2}^{2}\right].\end{array} \)

Integrating the first two conditions gives

\(\begin{array}{l}f_{1}=\left(L_{1} \sin \theta+L_{2} \sin \phi\right) \int g_{1}\left(X_{B}, Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right) d X_{B}+\beta_{1}\left(Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right), \\\\f_{1}=-\left(L_{1} \cos \theta+L_{2} \cos \phi\right) \int g_{1}\left(X_{B}, Y_{B}, \theta, \phi\right) d Y_{B}+\beta_{2}\left(X_{B}, \theta, \phi\right),\end{array} \)

where \(\beta_{n}\) with associated arguments indicates the functions of integration. The integration factor \(g_{1}\) cannot be zero, so both conditions state that \(f_{1}\) must depend on \(\theta\). However, the third condition, \(\partial f_{1} / \partial \theta=0\), requires that it not depend on \(\theta\). This is a contradiction, so we conclude that the constraint equation is not holonomic.
