Guest Question.

Water at 20°C flows between two reservoirs at the rate of \(0.06 m^3/s\) as shown in Fig. E7.17. Sketch the HGL and the EGL.What is the minimum diameter \(D_{B}\) allowed to avoid the occurrence of cavitation?

Admin
Solution

The EGL and the HGL are sketched on the figure, including sudden changes at the entrance, contraction, enlargement, and the exit. Note the large velocity head (the difference between the EGL and the HGL) in the smaller pipe because of the high velocity. The velocity, Reynolds number, and relative roughness in the 20-cm-diameter pipe are calculated to be

\(\begin{aligned}V &=\frac{Q}{A}=\frac{0.06}{\pi \times 0.20^{2} / 4}=1.91 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \\\mathrm{Re} &=\frac{V D}{\nu}=\frac{1.91 \times 0.2}{10^{-6}}=3.8 \times 10^{5} \\\frac{e}{D} &=\frac{0.26}{200}=0.0013\end{aligned}\)

Thus \(f=0.022\) from Fig. 7.13. The velocity, Reynolds number, and relative roughness in the smaller pipe are

\(\begin{array}{l}V_{B}=\frac{0.06}{\pi D_{B}^{2} / 4}=\frac{0.0764}{D_{B}^{2}} \\\\\operatorname{Re}=\frac{0.0764 \times D_{B}}{D_{B}^{2} \times 10^{-6}}=\frac{76400}{D_{B}} \\\\\frac{e}{D_{B}}=\frac{0.00026}{D_{B}}\end{array}\)

The minimum possible diameter is established by recognizing that the water vapor pressure ( 2450 Pa absolute) at \(20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\) is the minimum allowable pressure. Since the distance between the pipe and the HGL is an indication of the pressure in the pipe, we can conclude that the minimum pressure will occur at section 2 . Hence the energy equation applied between section 1, the reservoir surface, and section 2 gives

\(\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2g}+\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma}+z_{1}=\frac{V_{B}^{2}}{2 g}+\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma}+z_{2}+K_{\mathrm{ent}} \frac{V_{A}^{2}}{2 g}+K_{\mathrm{cont}} \frac{V_{B}^{2}}{2 g}+f_{A} \frac{L_{A}}{D_{A}} \frac{V^{2}}{2 g}+f_{B} \frac{L_{B}}{D_{B}} \frac{V_{B}^{2}}{2 g}\)

where the subscript \(A\) refers to the 20 -cm-diameter pipe. This simplifies, using absolute pressure, to

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{101000}{9810}+20=& \frac{\left(0.0764 / D_{B}^{2}\right)^{2}}{2 \times 9.81}\left(1+0.25+f_{B} \frac{20}{D_{B}}\right)+\frac{2450}{9810} \\&+\left(0.5+0.022 \frac{30}{0.2}\right) \frac{1.91^{2}}{2 \times 9.81} \\98600=& \frac{1.25}{D_{B}^{4}}+f_{B} \frac{20}{D_{B}^{5}}\end{aligned}\)

where we have used \(K_{\text {ent }}=0.5\) and assumed that \(K_{\text {cont }}=0.25\). This requires a trial-anderror solution. The following illustrates the procedure.

Let \(D_{B}=0.1 \mathrm{~m} .\) Then \(e / D=0.0026\) and \(\mathrm{Re}=7.6 \times 10^{5}\). Therefore, \(f=0.026\) :

\(98600 \stackrel{?}{=} 12500+52000\)

Let \(D_{B}=0.09 \mathrm{~m}\). Then \(e / D=0.0029\) and \(\mathrm{Re}=8.4 \times 10^{5}\). Therefore, \(f=0.027\) :

\(98600 \stackrel{\text { ? }}{=} 19000+91000\)

We see that \(0.1 \mathrm{~m}\) is too large and \(0.09 \mathrm{~m}\) is too small. In fact, the value of \(0.09 \mathrm{~m}\) is only slightly too small. Consequently, to be safe we must select the next larger pipe size of \(0.1 \mathrm{~m}\) diameter. If there were a pipe size of \(9.5 \mathrm{~cm}\) diameter, that could be selected. Assuming that that size is not available, we select

\(D_{B}=10 \mathrm{~cm}\)

Note that the assumption of a \(2: 1\) area ratio for the contraction is too small. It is actually \(4: 1 .\) This would give \(K_{\text {cont }} \simeq 0.4\). After a quick check we conclude that this value does not significantly influence the result.
