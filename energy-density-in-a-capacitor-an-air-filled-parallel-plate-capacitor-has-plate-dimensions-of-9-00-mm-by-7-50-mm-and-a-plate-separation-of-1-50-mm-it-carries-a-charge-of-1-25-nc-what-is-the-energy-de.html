Energy Density in a Capacitor

An air-filled parallel-plate capacitor has plate dimensions of 9.00 mm by 7.50 mm and a plate separation of 1.50 mm. It carries a charge of 1.25 nC. What is the energy density in the region between the plates?

Admin
Solution

We can first find the capacitance from the relationship for a parallel-plate capacitor. After we know the capacitance (and the charge, which is given in the problem) we can use Q = CV to find the electric potential difference across the capacitor. Once we know the electric potential difference and the plate separation, the electric field between the plates can be determined. Finally, we can use this to find the energy density in the electric field:

 

\(C=\frac{\varepsilon_{0} A}{d}=\frac{\varepsilon_{0} \times 0.00900 m \times 0.00750 m }{0.00150 m }=3.98 \times 10^{-13} F\)

 

\(V=\frac{Q}{C}=\frac{1.25 \times 10^{-9} C }{3.98 \times 10^{-13} F }=3140 V\)

 

\(E=\frac{V}{d}=\frac{3140 V }{0.00150 m }=2.09 \times 10^{6} V / m\)

 

\(u=\frac{1}{2} \varepsilon_{0} E^{2}=19.4 J / m ^{3}\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

We can check this result by multiplying the energy density by the volume of the space between the plates to find the total stored energy:

 

\(U=u A d=19.3 J / m ^{3} \times 0.00900 m \times 0.00750 m\) \(\times 0.00150 m =1.95 \times 10^{-6} J\)

 

This should be equal, within rounding, to the energy stored in the capacitor:

 

\(U=\frac{1}{2} C V^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(3.98 \times 10^{-13} F \right) \times(3140 V )^{2}=1.96 \times 10^{-6} J\)

 

We might also point out that the field is just less than that for electrical breakdown in air (see Table 22-1), so the capacitor is near its maximum charge and potential difference.
