Guest Question.

Using the data of Table, compare the axial heat flux in a heat pipe using water as the working fluid (at about \(200^{\circ}C\)) with the heat flux in a solid copper bar 8 cm long experiencing a temperature differential of \(100^{\circ}C\).

Admin
Solution

The heat flux per unit area is expressed as
\(q/A = −k(\Delta T/\Delta x)\)
From Table the thermal conductivity of copper is \(374 W/m · ^{\circ}C\), so that for an 8-cm length

\(q/A = −(374)(−100)/0.08 = 467.5 kW/m^{2} = 0.04675 kW/cm^{2}\)

From Table the typical axial heat flux for a water heat pipe is

\((q/A)_{axial} = 0.67 kW/cm^{2}\)

Thus the heat pipe transfers more than 10 times the heat of a pure copper rod with a substantial temperature gradient. This example illustrates why the heat pipe enjoys wide application possibilities.
