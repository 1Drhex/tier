Guest Question.

Water is boiled at atmospheric pressure by a horizontal polished copper heating element of diameter D=5 mm and emissivity \( \varepsilon=0.05\) immersed in water, as shown in Fig. (10-17). If the surface temperature of the heating wire is \( 350^{\circ} C\), determine the rate of heat transfer from the wire to the water per unit length of the wire.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION Water is boiled at 1 atm by a horizontal polished copper heating element. The rate of heat transfer to the water per unit length of the heater is to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Steady operating conditions exist. 2 Heat losses from the boiler are negligible.

Properties The properties of water at the saturation temperature of \( 100^{\circ} C\) are \( h_{ fg }=2257 \times 10^{3} J / kg\) and \( \rho_{l}=957.9 kg / m ^{3}\) (Table.A -9) The properties of vapor at the film temperature of \( T_{f}=\left(T_{\text {sat }}+T_{s}\right) / 2=(100+350) / 2=225^{\circ} C\) are, from Table (A-16),

\( \rho_{v}=0.444 kg / m ^{3} \)

 

\( c_{p v}=1951 J / kg \cdot K \)

 

\( \mu_{\nu}=1.75 \times 10^{-5} kg / m \cdot s \)

 

\( k_{v}=0.0358 W / m \cdot K\)

 

Note that we expressed the properties in units that cancel each other in boiling heat transfer relations. Also note that we used vapor properties at atm pressure from Table A- 16 instead of the properties of saturated vapor from Table (A-9) at \( 225^{\circ} C\) since the latter are at the saturation pressure of (2.55 MPa)

Analysis The excess temperature in this case is \( \Delta T=T_{s}-T_{\text {sat }}=350-100= 250^{\circ} C _{1}\) which is much larger than \( 30^{\circ} C\) for water. Therefore, film boiling will occur. The film boiling heat flux in this case can be determined from Eq. (10-5)

\(\dot{q}_{ film }=C_{ film }\left[\frac{g k_{v}^{3} \rho_{\nu}\left(\rho_{l}-\rho_{\nu}\right)\left[h_{f g}+0.4 c_{p v}\left(T_{s}-T_{ sat }\right)\right]}{\mu_{v} D\left(T_{s}-T_{ sat }\right)}\right]^{1 / 4}\left(T_{s}-T_{ sat }\right)\)

to be

\( \dot{q}_{\text {film }} =0.62\left[\frac{g k_{\nu}^{3} \rho_{\nu}\left(\rho_{l}-\rho_{\nu}\right)\left[h_{f g}+0.4 c_{p v}\left(T_{s}-T_{s a t}\right)\right]}{\mu_{v} D\left(T_{s}-T_{s a t}\right)}\right]^{1 / 4}\left(T_{s}-T_{\text {sat }}\right) \)

 

\( =0.62\left[\frac{9.81(0.0358)^{3}(0.444)(957.9-0.441)}{\left(1.75 \times 10^{-5}\right)\left(5 \times 10^{-3}\right)(250)}\right]^{1 / 4} \times 250 \) \( =5.93 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2}\)

 

The radiation heat flux is determined from Eq. (10-6)

\(\dot{q}_{ rad }=\varepsilon \sigma\left(T_{s}^{4}-T_{ sat }^{4}\right)\)

to be

\( \dot{q}_{ rad } =\varepsilon \sigma\left(T_{s}^{4}-T_{\text {sat }}^{4}\right) \) \( =(0.05)\left(5.67 \times 10^{-8} W / m ^{2} \cdot K ^{4}\right)\left[(350+273 K )^{4}-(100+273 K )^{4}\right] \) \( =372 W / m ^{2}\)

 

Note that heat transfer by radiation is negligible in this case because of the low emissivity of the surface and the relatively low surface temperature of the heating element. Then the total heat flux becomes Eq. (10-7)

\(\dot{q}_{\text {total }}=\dot{q}_{ film }+\frac{3}{4} \dot{q}_{ rad }\)

 

\(\dot{q}_{\text {total }}=\dot{q}_{ film }+\frac{3}{4} \dot{q}_{ rad }=5.93 \times 10^{4}+\frac{3}{4} \times 372=5.96 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2}\)

 

Finally, the rate of heat transfer from the heating element to the water is determined by multiplying the heat flux by the heat transfer surface area,

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {total }} =A \dot{q}_{\text {total }}=(\pi D L) \dot{q}_{\text {total }} \) \( =(\pi \times 0.005 m \times 1 m )\left(5.96 \times 10^{4} W / m ^{2}\right) \) \( =936 W\)

 

Discussion Note that the 5 -mm-diameter copper heating element consumes about 1 kW of electric power per unit length in steady operation in the film boiling regime. This energy is transferred to the water through the vapor film that forms around the wire.
