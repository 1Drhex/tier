Guest Question.

Consider glass particles with a complex index of refraction w = 1.5 – 0.1i and a density of \(\rho _{glass}=2g/cm^{3}\) suspended in an inert gas, with a particle loading ratio of 1 kg of particles per \(m^{3}\) of suspension volume. Particle sizes range between lOOμm and 1000μm, with an equal distribution over all sizes by weight-%. Determine the absorption coefficient, the scattering coefficient, and the phase function for the infrared (3μm < λ< lOμm).

Admin
Solution

First, we need to determine the particle distribution function by number (rather than mass). Since the mass distribution function is a constant we get

\(m(a)=\frac{1kg/m^{3}}{(1000-100)\mu m}=\frac{4}{3}\pi a^{3} \rho _{glass}n(a), 100\mu m \leq a\leq 1000\mu m ,\)

\(n(a)=\frac{3m(a)}{4\pi a^{3}\rho _{glass}}= 1.3226\times 10^{-7}\mu m^{-1}/a^{3} , 100\mu m \leq a\leq 1000\mu m\) .

Next we need to determine the range of the size parameter x to see whether Rayleigh scattering, Mie scattering or large-particle scattering must be considered. The minimum value for x will occur for the smallest particle at the longest wavelength, or

\(x_{min }= \frac{2\pi a_{min}}{\lambda _{max}}=\frac{2\pi 100}{10}=62.83\gg 1,\)

\((kx)_{min }= 6.283\gg 1,\)

that is, the large-particle assiimption will be acceptable for all conditions encountered in this example.
Thus, the absorption and scattering coefficients may be related to the hemispherical emissivity of the glass. Since for this glass k \(\ll\) n , the material behaves essentially like a dielectric, and the hemispherical emissivity may be found from Fig. 3-19 or equation (3.82) \(\epsilon _{||}=\frac{4(2n+1)}{3(n+1)^{2}}, \epsilon _{\bot }=\frac{4n^{3}(n^{2}+2n-1)}{(n^{2}+1)(n^{4}-1)} +\frac{2n^{2}(n^{2}-1)^{2}}{(n^{2}+1)^{3}}\ln \left(\frac{n+1}{n-1} \right)-\frac{16n^{4}(n^{4}+1)\ln n}{(n^{2}+1)(n^{4}-1)^{2}}, \epsilon _{\lambda }=\frac{1}{2}(\epsilon _{||}+\epsilon _{\bot })\) . Either method leads to ε=α=1-ρ =0.91.
The absorption and scattering coefficients may then be calculated from equations (11.39) \(\kappa _{\lambda }=\int_{0}^{\infty }{}C_{abs}n(a)da =\pi \int_{0}^{\infty }{Q_{abs}a^{2}n(a)da}\) and (11.38) \(\sigma _{s\lambda }=\int_{0}^{\infty }{}C_{sca}n(a)da =\pi \int_{0}^{\infty }{Q_{sca}a^{2}n(a)da}\) as

\(\kappa _{\lambda }=\pi \int_{0}^{\infty }{}\alpha a^{2}n(a)da =\pi \alpha \int_{100\mu m}^{1000\mu m}{a^{2}}\frac{1.3226\times 10^{-7}}{\mu m a^{3}}da\)

\(=1.3226\times 10^{-7}\mu m^{-1}\pi \alpha ln \frac{1000}{100}=9.60 \times 10^{-3}\alpha cm^{-1}\)

\(=8.74\times 10^{-3}cm^{-1},\)

\(\sigma _{s\lambda }=9.60\times 10^{-3}\rho cm^{-1}=0.86\times 10^{-3}cm^{-1}\) .

The scattering phase function must be evaluated from equation (11.75) \(\Phi (\Theta )=\rho ^{s}\left(\frac{\pi -\Theta }{2} \right)/\rho ^{s}\) and is also included in Fig. 11-13.
Because of the small value for k, the directional behavior is very similar to that of the perfect dielectric (m = 2), but the forward-scattering peak is more pronounced because of the smaller refractive index.
