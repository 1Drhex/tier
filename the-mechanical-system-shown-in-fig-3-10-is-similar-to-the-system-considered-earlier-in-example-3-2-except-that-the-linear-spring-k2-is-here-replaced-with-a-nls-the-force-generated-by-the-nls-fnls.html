Guest Question.

The mechanical system shown in Fig. 3.10 is similar to the system considered earlier in , except that the linear spring \(k_{2}\) is here replaced with a NLS. The force generated by the NLS, \(F_{NLS}\) , is approximated by

\(F_{NLS} =f_{NL} \left(z_{2}- z_{1} \right) =c\left|z_{2} - z_{1} \right| \left(z_{2} - z_{1} \right),\)

where c is a constant and \(z_{1}\) and \(z_{2}\) are displacements of the two masses measured from their respective spring-relaxed positions, \(r_{1}\) and \(r_{2}\):

\(z_{1}=\Delta _{1} + x_{1},\)

 

\(z_{2}=\Delta _{2} + x_{2},\)

where \(\Delta _{1}\) and \(\Delta _{2}\) are displacements that are due to gravity and \(x _{1}\) and \(x _{2}\) are displacements that are due to force \(F_{1}\left(t\right)\) measured from the static-equilibrium positions of the masses, \(se_{1}\) and \(se_{2}\), respectively. Recall that a similar notation was used for vertical displacements in .

The square-law expression embodied in the function \(f_{NL}\) is a fairly common type of nonlinearity, although the specific form of the nonlinear function is of no significance here.

Derive an input–output equation by using \(x_{2}\) as the output variable and a set of state equations for this system.

Admin
Solution

The equations of motion for masses \(m_{1}\) and \(m_{2}\) are

\(m_{1} \overset{\cdot \cdot }{z} _{1}+ b_{1} \overset{\cdot }{z} _{1}+ k_{1} {z} _{1}- c\left|z_{1} – z_{2} \right| \left(z_{1} – z_{2} \right) =F_{1} \left(t\right) + m_{1} g ,\)

 

\(m_{2} \overset{\cdot \cdot }{z} _{2}+ c\left|z_{1} – z_{2} \right| \left(z_{1} – z_{2} \right)=m_{2} g .\)

One would normally obtain the input–output equation relating output \(x_{2}\) to input \(F_{1} \left(t\right)\) by combining the preceding equations of motion and eliminating \(z_{1}\) (and subtracting \(\Delta _{2}\) to get \(x_{2}\)). However, in this case, the substitution is impossible because of the nonlinear term present in both equations!

To obtain a state model, select displacements \(z_{1}\) and \(z_{2}\) and their derivatives \(v_{1}\) and \(v_{2}\) as the state variables. Based on the definitions of the state variables and the equations of motion, the state-variable equations are

\(\overset{\cdot }{z} _{1} =v_{1} ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{v} _{1}=- \frac{k_{1} }{m_{1} } z_{1} – \frac{b_{1} }{m_{1} } v_{1}+ \frac{c}{m_{1} }\left|z_{2}- z_{1} \right| \left(z_{2}- z_{1} \right) + \frac{1}{m_{1} } F_{1} \left(t\right) + g ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{z} _{2} ={v} _{2} ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{v} _{2} =- \frac{c}{m_{2} } \left|z_{2} – z_{1} \right| \left(z_{2} – z_{1} \right) + g,\)

or, with general symbols used for the state variables,

\(\overset{\cdot }{q} _{1} =q_{2} ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{q} _{2}=- \frac{k_{1} }{m_{1} } q_{1} – \frac{b_{1} }{m_{1} } q_{2}+ \frac{c}{m_{2} }\left|q_{3}- q_{1} \right| \left(q_{3}- q_{1} \right) + \frac{1}{m_{1} } F_{1} \left(t\right) + g ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{q} _{3} ={q} _{4} ,\)

 

\(\overset{\cdot }{q} _{4} =- \frac{c}{m_{2} } \left|q_{3} – q_{1} \right| \left(q_{3} – q_{1} \right) + g,\)

where \(q_{1} =z_{1} ,q_{2} =v_{1},q_{3} =z_{2},\) and \(q_{4} =v_{2}\). Note that two inputs are used in this model, \(F_{1}\left(t\right)\) and g.
The preceding state-variable equations can be solved numerically by use of one of the methods described in Chap. 5.
