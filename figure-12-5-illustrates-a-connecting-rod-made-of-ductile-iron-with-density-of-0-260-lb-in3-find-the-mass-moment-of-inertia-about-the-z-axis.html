Guest Question.

Figure 12.5 illustrates a connecting rod made of ductile iron with density of 0.260 lb/in3. Find the mass moment of inertia about the z axis.

Admin
Solution

First we find the mass moment of inertia of each of the annular cylinders at the ends of the rod and of the central prismatic bar, each taken about its own mass center. Then we use the parallel-axis theorem to transfer each to the z axis.

The mass of each annular cylinder is

\(\begin{aligned}m_{\text {cyl }} &=\rho \pi\left(r_{o}^{2}-r_{i}^{2}\right) l \\&=\frac{\left(0.260 lb / in ^{3}\right) \pi\left[(1.5 in )^{2}-(0.5 in )^{2}\right](0.75 in )}{386 in / s ^{2}} \\&=0.00317 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in\end{aligned}\)

The mass of the central prismatic bar is

\(\begin{aligned}m_{ bar } &=\rho w h l=\frac{\left(0.260 lb / in ^{3}\right)(13 in )(1 in )(0.75 in )}{386 in / s ^{2}} \\&=0.00657 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in\end{aligned}\)

From Table 5, Appendix A, we find the mass moment of inertia of each cylinder and the bar as

\(\begin{aligned}I_{\text {cyl }}&=\frac{m\left(r_{o}^{2}+r_{i}^{2}\right)}{2} \\&=\frac{\left(0.00317 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in \right)\left[(1.5 in )^{2}+(0.5 in )^{2}\right]}{2} \\&=0.00396 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

and

\(\begin{aligned}I_{ bar } &=\frac{m\left(w^{2}+h^{2}\right)}{12} \\&=\frac{\left(0.00657 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in \right)\left[(1 in )^{2}+(13 in )^{2}\right]}{12} \\&=0.09308 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

Then using Eq. (12.11) to transfer the axes, the mass moment of inertia about the z axis is

\(I=I_{G}+m d^{2}\) (12.11)

 

\(\begin{aligned}I^{z z}=& I_{ cyl }+\left(I_{ bar }+m_{ bar } d_{ bar }^{2}\right)+\left(I_{ cyl }+m_{ cyl } d_{ cyl }^{2}\right) \\=&\left(0.00396 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)+\left[\left(0.09308 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)+\left(0.00657 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in \right)(8 in )^{2}\right] \\&+\left[\left(0.00396 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)+\left(0.00317 lb \cdot s ^{2} / in \right)(16 in )^{2}\right] \\=& 1.333 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

It is noted that only one mass moment of inertia, Izz, was requested or found in this example. This does not mean that Ixx and Iyy are zero, but rather that they are likely not needed for further analysis. In problems with only planar motion, only Izz is needed since Ixx and Iyy are used only with rotations out of the xy plane. These other mass moments and products of inertia are used in Secs. 12.10 and later, where we treat problems with spatial motion, and they are found in identical fashion.
