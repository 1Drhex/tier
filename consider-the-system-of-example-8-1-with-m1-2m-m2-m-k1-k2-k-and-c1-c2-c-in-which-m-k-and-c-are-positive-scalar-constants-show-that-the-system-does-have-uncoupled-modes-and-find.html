Guest Question.

Consider the system of with: \( m_{1} = 2m , m_{2} = m , k_{1} = k_{2} = k \) , and \( c_{1} = c_{2} = c \) , in which \( m, k \) , and \( c \) are positive scalar constants. Show that the system does have uncoupled modes, and find the \( \theta ,\lambda ,\hat{m} \) , and \( \beta \) matrices and the values of the modal frequencies and damping values.

Admin
Solution

Substituting the parameter values into \( m, k \) , and \( c \) from gives

m\( =m\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \) , k\( =k\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \) , c\( =c\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \)

Inverting m is almost trivial, and matrix multiplication then gives

k m\(^{-1}\)c = c m\(^{-1}\)k=\(\frac{k c}{m} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -2 \\ -2 & 1.5 \end{pmatrix} \)

The equality of \( km^{-1}c \) and \( cm^{-1}k \) assures us that uncoupled modes do exist. Eigenanalysis of \( m^{-1}k \) gives

\( \theta =\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1.414 & -1.414 \end{pmatrix}, \lambda =\frac{k}{m} \begin{pmatrix} 0.2929 & 0 \\ 0 & 1.707 \end{pmatrix} \)

Note that the columns of \( \theta \) are the eigenvectors of \( m^{-1}k \) and their ordering is arbitrary. We have followed the common convention of putting the lower frequency mode in the first column. Note also that although the “directions” of the eigenvectors are unique, their “lengths” are arbitrary. A different scaling of these eigenvectors will affect the values of the components of \( \hat{m} \) , but not of \( \beta \) . Using \( \theta \) as written gives

\( \hat{m} =m\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix} , \beta =\frac{c}{m}\begin{pmatrix} 0.2929 & 0 \\ 0 & 1.707 \end{pmatrix} \)

Taking the square root of the elements of \( \lambda \) gives the modal frequencies as \( \omega _{1}=0.5412(k/m)^{1/2} \) and \( \omega _{2}=1.307(k/m)^{1/2} \) . Dividing the elements of \( \beta \) by \( 2\omega _{j} \) gives the modal damping values as \( \zeta _{1}=0.2706c/(km)^{1/2} \) and \( \zeta _{2}=0.6533c/(km)^{1/2} \) .

It may also be noted that one could have performed this latter analysis without checking to see if \( km^{-1}c \) and \( cm^{-1}k \) were equal. That is, one can perform the eigenanalysis to find \( \theta \) , then evaluate \( \beta \) regardless of whether the system has uncoupled modes. The form of \( \beta \) , in fact, then provides the information about the existence of uncoupled modes. A diagonal \( \beta \) matrix is sufficient evidence of uncoupled modes. Checking to see whether \( km^{-1}c=cm^{-1}k \) allows one to avoid doing the eigenanalysis if the equations will not uncouple anyway.
