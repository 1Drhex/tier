Describing Function for a Saturation Nonlinearity
A saturation nonlinearity is shown in Fig. 9.25(a) and is the most common nonlinearity in control systems. The saturation function (sat) is defined as

\( \operatorname{sat}(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc} +1, & x>1 \\ x, & |x| \leq 1 \\ -1, & x<-1 \end{array}\right. \)

If the slope of the linear region is k and the final saturated values are \( ±N, \) then the function is

\( y = N \text{sat} \left( \frac{k}{N} x \right). \)

Find the describing function for this nonlinearity.

Admin
Solution

Consider the input and output signals of the saturation element shown in Fig. 9.25. For an input sinusoid of \( u = a \sin ωt \) with amplitude \( a ≤ \frac{N}{k} , \) the output is such that the \( \text{ DF} \) is just a gain of unity. With \( a ≥ \frac{N}{k} , \) we need to compute the amplitude and phase of the fundamental component of the output. Since saturation is an odd function, all the cosine terms in Eq. (9.21) are zeros and \( a_1 = 0. \) According to Eq. (9.27),

\( y(t)=a_{0}+\sum\limits_{i=1}^{\infty }a_{i} \cos (j \omega t)+b_{i} \sin (j \omega t) \) (9.21)

\( DF = K_{eq}(a) = \frac{b_1}{a} \) (9.27)

\( K_{eq}(a) = \frac{b_1}{a} ,\)

so that

\( b_{1}=\frac{2}{\pi} \int_{0}^{\pi} N \operatorname{sat}\left(\frac{k}{N} a \sin \omega t\right) \sin \omega t \ d(\omega t) \),

since the integral for the coefficient b1 over the interval \( ωt = [0, π] \) is simply twice that of the interval \( ωt = [0, π/2]. \) Then

\( b_{1}=\frac{4 N}{\pi} \int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \text { sat }\left(\frac{k}{N} a \sin \omega t\right) \sin \omega t \ d(\omega t) \)

We can now divide the integral into two parts corresponding to the linear and saturation parts. Let us define the saturation time \( t_s \) as the time when

\( t_s = \frac{1}{ω} \sin^{−1} \left( \frac{N}{ak} \right) \) or \( ωt_s = \sin^{−1} \left( \frac{N}{ak} \right). \) (9.28)

Then

\( b_{1}=\frac{4 N \omega}{\pi a}\left(\int_{0}^{\omega t_{s}} \operatorname{sat}\left(\frac{k}{N} a \sin \omega t\right) \sin \omega t \ d t+\int_{\omega t_{s}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \sin \omega t \ d t\right) \)

\( =\frac{4 N \omega}{\pi a}\left(\int_{0}^{\omega t_{s}} \frac{k}{N} a \sin^2 \omega t \ d t+\int_{\omega t_{s}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \sin \omega t \ d t\right) \)

\( =\frac{4 N \omega}{\pi a}\left(\int_{0}^{\omega t_{s}} \frac{k}{2 N} a(1-\cos 2 \omega t) \ d t+\int_{\omega t_{s}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \sin \omega t \ d t\right) \)

\( =\frac{4 N \omega}{\pi a}\left(\left.\frac{k}{2 N} a t\right|_{0} ^{t_{s}}-\left.\frac{k}{2 N} a \sin 2 \omega t\right|_{0} ^{t_{s}}-\frac{1}{\omega}\left(\cos \frac{\pi}{2}-\cos t_{s}\right)\right) \)

But using Eq. (9.28), we have

\( \sin ωt_s = \frac{N}{ka} \) and \( \cos ωt_s = \sqrt{1 − \left( \frac{N}{ka} \right)^2 } . \)

We finally obtain

\( K_{e q}(a)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc} \frac{2}{\pi}\left(k \sin ^{-1}\left(\frac{N}{a k}\right)+\frac{N}{a} \sqrt{1-\left(\frac{N}{k a}\right)^{2}}\right), & \frac{k a}{N}>1 \\ k, & \frac{k a}{N} \leq 1 \end{array}\right. \) (9.29)

Figure 9.26 shows a plot of \( K_{eq}(a) \) indicating that it is a real function independent of frequency and results in no phase shifts. It is seen that the describing function is initially a constant and then decays essentially as a function of the reciprocal of the input signal amplitude, \( a.\)
