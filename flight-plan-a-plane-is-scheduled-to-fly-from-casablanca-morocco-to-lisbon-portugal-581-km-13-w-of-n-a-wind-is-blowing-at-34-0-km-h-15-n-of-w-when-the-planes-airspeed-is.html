Flight Plan

A plane is scheduled to fly from Casablanca, Morocco, to Lisbon, Portugal, 581 km [\(13^{\circ}\) W of N]. A wind is blowing at 34.0 km/h [\(15^{\circ}\) N of W]. When the plane’s airspeed is 560 km/h, and the pilot flies on a direct bearing to Lisbon, how long does the flight take?

Admin
Solution

If the pilot were to point the plane along the straight line from Casablanca to Lisbon, the wind would push the plane off course to the west. To compensate, the pilot needs to point the plane somewhat to the east of the direct heading to Lisbon.

The coordinate systems we will employ are one attached to the ground and one that moves with the air at the velocity of the wind.

Let us denote the velocity of the plane with respect to the ground as \(\vec{v}_{ PC }\), the velocity of the plane with respect to the air as \(\vec{v}_{ PA }\), and the velocity of the air with respect to the ground as \(\vec{V}_{ AC }\). We can then write

 

\(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PG }=\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PA }+\overrightarrow{ V }_{ AG }\) (1)

 

The velocity of the plane with respect to the ground equals the sum of the velocity of the plane with respect to the air and the velocity of the air with respect to the ground (i.e., the wind velocity). Let us construct a diagram of these velocities (Figure 4-28).

Although we do not know yet in which direction the plane should be oriented, we do know that the sum of the velocities of the plane and the wind point along the line connecting the two cities. Since the wind speed is small compared to the airspeed of the plane, the airspeed and the speed of the plane with respect to the ground will not be very different, as we can see from our scale diagram.

We label the angles between the velocities \(\alpha, \beta \text {, and } \gamma\) as shown. In Figure 4-28, \(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PG }\) makes an angle of 13° with the vertical, and \(\vec{V}_{ AG }\) makes an angle of 15° with the horizontal.

Then,

 

\(\alpha=90^{\circ}-\left(13^{\circ}+15^{\circ}\right)=62^{\circ}\)

 

Applying the sine law to the velocity triangle, we have

 

\(\frac{\sin \alpha}{v_{ PA }}=\frac{\sin \beta}{V_{ AG }}\)

 

\(\sin \beta=\frac{V_{ AG } \sin \alpha}{v_{ PA }}=\frac{34.0 km / h \left(\sin 62.0^{\circ}\right)}{560 km / h }=0.0536\)

 

\(\beta=3.07^{\circ}\)

 

Therefore,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\gamma &=180^{\circ}-(\alpha+\beta)=180^{\circ}-\left(3.07^{\circ}+62^{\circ}\right) \\&=114.93^{\circ}\end{aligned}\)

 

To three significant digits, the value is 115°.

Using the sine law once again, we have

 

\(\frac{\sin \alpha}{v_{ PA }}=\frac{\sin \gamma}{v_{ PG }}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}v_{ PG } &=\frac{v_{ PA } \sin \gamma}{\sin \alpha}=\frac{560 . km / h \left(\sin 115^{\circ}\right)}{\sin 62.0^{\circ}} \\&=575 km / h\end{aligned}\)

 

The time it takes the plane to arrive at its destination is

 

\(t=\frac{581 km }{575 km }=1.01 h\)

 

Another way to tackle this problem is using vector components. If we choose our x-axis to point in the direction of \(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PG }\) , we can write that vector as \(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PG }=v_{ PG } \hat{ i }\) . The other two vectors will have both x- and y-components and can be written as \(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PA }=v_{ PA , x} \hat{ i }+v_{ PA , y} \hat{ j } \text { and } \overrightarrow{ V }_{ AG }=v_{ AG , x} \hat{ i }+V_{ AG , y} \hat{ j }\) . We substitute these into Equation (1) to get

 

\(v_{ PG , x} \hat{ i }=v_{ PA , x} \hat{ i }+v_{ PA , y} \hat{ j }+V_{ AG , x} \hat{ i }+V_{ AG , y} \hat{ j }\) (2)

 

Notice that the left-hand side of this equation only has x-components. This means that the y components on the righthand side must add up to zero:

 

\(\begin{aligned}0 &=v_{ PA , y}+V_{ AG , y} \\v_{ PA , y} &=-V_{ AG , y}\end{aligned}\)

 

This result makes physical sense. The plane must fly slightly into the wind to counteract the effect of the wind velocity that is perpendicular to the desired direction of flight.

The desired direction of flight is [13° W of N] and the wind is blowing [15° N of W], which is [75° W of N]. Therefore, the wind makes an angle with the desired direction of 62°. The perpendicular component of the wind velocity is thus \(v_{ AG , y}=\) \(\left(34.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\right) \sin 62.0^{\circ}=30.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\) and the parallel component is \(v_{ AG , x}=\left(34.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\right) \cos 62.0^{\circ}=16.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\) .

To find the time of flight, we need to know the parallel component of the velocity of the plane over the ground, which is given by the x-components of Equation (1):

 

\(v_{ PG , x}=v_{ PA , x}+V_{ AG , x}\) (3)

 

We already know what \(V_{ AG , x}\) is, so we need to find \(v_{ PA , x}\). We know the magnitude of \(\overrightarrow{ v }_{ PA }\) and we know that the y-component has a magnitude of \(v_{ PA , y}=v_{ AG , y}=30.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\) . Therefore,

 

\(\begin{aligned}v_{ PA , x} &=\sqrt{v_{ PA }^{2}-v_{ PA , y}^{2}}=\sqrt{\left(560 . km \cdot h ^{-1}\right)^{2}-\left(30.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}\right)^{2}} \\&=559 km \cdot h ^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

 

We can substitute this into Equation (3) to find \(v_{ PG , x}=\) \(559 km \cdot h ^{-1}+16.0 km \cdot h ^{-1}=575 km \cdot h ^{-1}\) . We need to travel a distance of 581 km at this speed, so it takes

 

\(t=\frac{\text { distance }}{\text { speed }}=\frac{581 km }{575 km \cdot h ^{-1}}=1.01 h\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

The plane’s speed is very large compared to the speed of the wind, so the small heading correction of 3.07° is reasonable.
