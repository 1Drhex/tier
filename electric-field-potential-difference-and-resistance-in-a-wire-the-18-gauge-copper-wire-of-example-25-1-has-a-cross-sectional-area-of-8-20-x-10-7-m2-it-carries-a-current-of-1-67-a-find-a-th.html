ELECTRIC FIELD, POTENTIAL DIFFERENCE, AND RESISTANCE IN A WIRE

The 18-gauge copper wire of has a cross-sectional area of 8.20 × \(10^{-7}\) \(m^2\). It carries a current of 1.67 A. Find (a) the electric-field magnitude in the wire; (b) the potential difference between two points in the wire 50.0 m apart; (c) the resistance of a 50.0-m length of this wire.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

We are given the cross-sectional area A and current I. Our target variables are the electric-field magnitude E, potential difference V, and resistance R. The current density is J = I/A. We find E from Eq. (25.5), E = ρJ (Table 25.1 gives the resistivity ρ for copper). The potential difference is then the product of E and the length of the wire. We can use either Eq. (25.10) or Eq. (25.11) to find R.

\(\rho=\frac{E}{J}\) (25.5)

\(R=\frac{\rho L}{A}\) (25.10)

\(V=I R\) (25.11)

TABLE 25.1 resistivities at room temperature (20°C)
Substance \(\rho(\Omega \cdot \mathrm{m})\) Substance \(\rho(\Omega \cdot \mathrm{m})\)
Conductors Semiconductors
Metals Silver \(1.47 \times 10^{-8}\) Pure carbon (graphite) \(3.5 \times 10^{-5}\)
Copper \(1.72 \times 10^{-8}\) Pure germanium 0.6
Gold \(2.44 \times 10^{-8}\) Pure silicon 2300
Aluminum \(2.75 \times 10^{-8}\) Insulators
Tungsten \(5.25 \times 10^{-8}\) Amber \(5 \times 10^{14}\)
Steel \(20 \times 10^{-8}\) Glass \(10^{10}-10^{14}\)
Lead \(22 \times 10^{-8}\) Lucite \(>10^{13}\)
Mercury \(95 \times 10^{-8}\) Mica \(10^{11}-10^{15}\)
Alloys Manganin (Cu 84%, Mn 12%, Ni 4%) \(44 \times 10^{-8}\) Quartz (fused) \(75 \times 10^{16}\)
Constantan (Cu 60%, Ni 40%) \(49 \times 10^{-8}\) Sulfur \(10^{15}\)
Nichrome \(100 \times 10^{-8}\) Teflon \(>10^{13}\)
Wood \(10^{8}-10^{11}\)

EXECUTE:

(a) From Table 25.1, \(\rho=1.72 \times 10^{-8} \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m}\). Hence, from Eq. (25.5),

\(E=\rho J=\frac{\rho I}{A}=\frac{\left(1.72 \times 10^{-8} \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m}\right)(1.67 \mathrm{~A})}{8.20 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{2}}=0.0350 \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m}\)

(b) The potential difference is

\(V=E L=(0.0350 \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m})(50.0 \mathrm{~m})=1.75 \mathrm{~V}\)

(c) From Eq. (25.10) the resistance of 50.0 m of this wire is

\(R=\frac{\rho L}{A}=\frac{\left(1.72 \times 10^{-8} \Omega \cdot \mathrm{m}\right)(50.0 \mathrm{~m})}{8.20 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{2}}=1.05 \Omega\)

Alternatively, we can find R from Eq. (25.11):

\(R=\frac{V}{I}=\frac{1.75 \mathrm{~V}}{1.67 \mathrm{~A}}=1.05 \Omega\)

 

EVALUATE: We emphasize that the resistance of the wire is defined to be the ratio of voltage to current. If the wire is made of nonohmic material, then R is different for different values of V but is always given by R = V/I. Resistance is also always given by R = ρL/A; if the material is nonohmic, ρ is not constant but depends on E (or, equivalently, on V = EL).
