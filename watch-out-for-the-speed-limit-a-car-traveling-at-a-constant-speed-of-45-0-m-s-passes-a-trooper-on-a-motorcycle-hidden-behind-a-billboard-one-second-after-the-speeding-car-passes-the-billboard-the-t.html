Guest Question.

Watch Out for the Speed Limit!

A car traveling at a constant speed of 45.0 m/s passes a trooper on a motorcycle hidden behind a billboard. One second after the speeding car passes the billboard, the trooper sets out from the billboard to catch the car, accelerating at a constant rate of \(3.00 m/s^2\). How long does it take the trooper to overtake the car?

Admin
Solution

A pictorial representation (Fig. 2.13) helps clarify the sequence of events. The car is modeled as a particle under constant velocity, and the trooper is modeled as a particle under constant acceleration.

First, we write expressions for the position of each vehicle as a function of time. It is convenient to choose the position of the billboard as the origin and to set \(t_{B} = 0\) as the time the trooper begins moving. At that instant, the car has already traveled a distance of 45.0 m from the billboard because it has traveled at a constant speed of \(v_{x} = 45.0 m/s\) for 1 s. Therefore, the initial position of the speeding car is \(x_{B} = 45.0 m\).

Using the particle under constant velocity model, apply Equation 2.7 to give the car’s position at any time t :

\(x_{f} = x_{i}+v_{x}t (for constant v_x) \) (2.7)

\(x_{car} = x_{B}+v_{x car}t\)

A quick check shows that at t = 0, this expression gives the car’s correct initial position when the trooper begins to move: \(x_{car} = x_{B} = 45.0 m\).

The trooper starts from rest \(t_{B} = 0\) and accelerates at \(a_{x} = 3.00 m/s^2\) away from the origin. Use Equation 2.16 to give her position at any time t :

\(x_{f} =x_{i}+v_{x i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2} (for constant a_x)\) (2.16)

\(\begin{aligned}x_{f} &=x_{i}+v_{x i} t+\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2} \\x_{\text {trooper }} &=0+(0) t+\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2}=\frac{1}{2} a_{x} t^{2}\end{aligned}\)

Set the positions of the car and trooper equal to represent the trooper overtaking the car at position C:

\(\begin{aligned}x_{\text {trooper }} &= x_{\text {car}}\\\frac{1}{2}a_{x} t^{2} &=x_{B} +v_{x \text {car}}t\end{aligned}\)

Rearrange to give a quadratic equation:

\(\frac{1}{2}a_{x} t^{2} – v_{x \text {car}}t – x_{B} = 0\)

Solve the quadratic equation for the time at which the trooper catches the car (for help in solving quadratic equations, see Appendix B.2.):

Evaluate the solution, choosing the positive root because that is the only choice consistent with a time t > 0:

\(\begin{aligned}t&=\frac{v_{x \mathrm{car}} \pm \sqrt{v_{x \mathrm{car}}^{2}+2 a_{x} x_{B}}}{a_{x}}\\(1)\quad t&=\frac{v_{x \mathrm{car}}}{a_{x}} \pm \sqrt{\frac{v_{x \mathrm{car}}^{2}}{a_{x}^{2}}+\frac{2 x_{B}}{a_{x}}}\\t&=\frac{45.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{3.00 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}+\sqrt{\frac{(45.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}{\left(3.00 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)^{2}}+\frac{2(45.0 \mathrm{~m})}{3.00 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}}=31.0 \mathrm{~s}\end{aligned}\)

Why didn’t we choose t = 0 as the time at which the car passes the trooper? If we did so, we would not be able to use the particle under constant acceleration model for the trooper. Her acceleration would be zero for the first second and then \(3.00 m/s^2\) for the remaining time. By defining the time t = 0 as when the trooper begins moving, we can use the particle under constant acceleration model for her movement for all positive times.
