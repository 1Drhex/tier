Guest Question.

H-factor
A reaction with one single temperature-dependent reaction rate constant,

\(\begin{gathered}-v_{ A } A +\left(-v_{ B }\right) B \rightarrow \text { products } \quad r= \\k(T) C _{ A }^{ O _{ R A}} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }} k C _{ A }^{ O _{ RA }} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }} k C _{ A }^{ O _{ R }} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }} k C _{ A }^{ O _{ RA }} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }}\end{gathered}\) (4-6.1)

is carried out in a constant volume batch reactor. Each time when the reactor is loaded and discharged, the temperature profile, that is temperature change with time during the intended reaction period

\(T=f(t)\) (E4.6.2)

is different. For comparison purposes, we would like to develop a method to quantify the effect so that operators can control the reaction effectively. A concept of H-factor was developed, which is the effective reaction time equivalent to that at a constant temperature TR. Fig. E4.6.1 illustrates this concept. Derive a formula for the H-factor.

Admin
Solution

For reaction occurring in a batch reactor, mole balance of reactant A leads to

\(\overset{\overset{0}{\nwarrow } }{In} – \overset{\overset{0}{ \nearrow } }{Out}+Generation=Accumulation\)

 

\(0-0+v_{A} r V=\frac{d n_{A}}{d t}\). (E4.6.3)

Since the volume is constant (constant volume batch reactor), we further obtain

\(v_{A} k C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). (E4.6.4)

Based on stoichiometry, we have

\(\frac{n_{A}-n_{A 0}}{v_{A}}=\frac{n_{B}-n_{B 0}}{v_{B}}\). (E4.6.5)

For constant volume reactor, Eq. (E4.6.5) \(\frac{n_{A}-n_{A 0}}{v_{A}}=\frac{n_{B}-n_{B 0}}{v_{B}}\). is reduced to

\(C_{B}=C_{B 0}+\frac{v_{B}}{v_{A}}\left(C_{A}-C_{A 0}\right)\). (E4.6.6)

Therefore \(C_{B}\) and \(C_{A}\) are related. As the rate constant k is only a function of temperature (Arrhenius relationship), separation of variables for Eq. (E4.6.4) \(v_{A} k C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). leads to

\(\frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left(-\frac{E}{R T}\right) d t.\) (E4.6.7)

For a desired reaction end condition or conversion, the reaction time can be obtained by integrating Eq. (E4.6.7) \(\frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left(-\frac{E}{R T}\right) d t.\) .

\(\int_{C_{A0} }^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left(-\frac{E}{R T}\right) d t\), (E4.6.8)

where \(C_{B}\) is related to \(C_{ A }\) via Eq. (E4.6.6) \(C_{B}=C_{B 0}+\frac{v_{B}}{v_{A}}\left(C_{A}-C_{A 0}\right)\). . Eq. (E4.6.8) \(\int_{C_{A0} }^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left(-\frac{E}{R T}\right) d t\), is applicable to reaction occurring isothermally at a constant temperature, \(T_{ R }\) as well:

\(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t_{R}} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{R}}\right\rgroup d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{R}} \right\rgroup t_{R}\), (E4.6.9)

where \(t_{ R }\) is the time required to reach the reaction end condition (concentration \(C_{ A }\) from \(C_{ A0 }\)) isothermally at a reaction temperature of \( T_{ R }\). Substituting Eq. (E4.6.9) \(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t_{R}} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{R}}\right\rgroup d t=v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{R}} \right\rgroup t_{R}\), into Eq. (E4.6.8) \(\int_{C_{A0} }^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left(-\frac{E}{R T}\right) d t\), , we obtain

\(v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T_{R}}\right\rgroup t_{R}=\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}} C_{B}^{O_{R B}}}=\int_{0}^{t} v_{A} k_{0} \exp \left\lgroup -\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup d t\), (E4.6.10)

which can be reduced to

\(t_{R}=\int_{0}^{t} \exp \left\lgroup \frac{E}{R T_{R}}-\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup d t\). (E4.6.11)

Thus we have obtained a time scale that would give rise to the same conversion for different temperatures and/or different temperature progressions. Integration for \(t_{ R }\) can be achieved numerically (Liu, 2010)

In chemical pulping, this temperature \(T_{ R }\) is usually taken as 100°C (or 373.15 K) and the resulting \(t_{ R }\) is called H-factor. Once the activation energy is known, the effect of temperature and time can be easily lumped together with this single parameter, \(t_{ R }\), or H-factor. This turned out to be smart idea as operators can control the reaction effectively even the temperature varied during the process. Frequently quoted values of the constants are of those of Vroom (1957) for kraft pulping of woodchips as shown below.

\(H F=\int_{0}^{t} \exp \left(43.2-\frac{16115 K}{T}\right) d t\). (E4.6.12)

As temperature rises, more solvent evaporates to the vapor phase. As pressure increases, more solvent returns to the liquid phase. Therefore for liquid or liquid-solid reactions at high temperature, the effective reaction volume V changes with temperature and pressure if not with concentration or composition. The constant volume assumption is only approximate. The complexity further arises as the reaction kinetics is not the same when temperature is increased causing different components in the plant biomass to be susceptible to reaction. Thus the H-factor treatment is only an approximation of the reaction system. One variant of the H-factor is the severity factor. Eq. (E4.6.11) \(t_{R}=\int_{0}^{t} \exp \left\lgroup \frac{E}{R T_{R}}-\frac{E}{R T}\right\rgroup d t\). can also be written as

\(t_{R}=\int_{0}^{t} \exp \left\lgroup \frac{T-T_{R}}{R T_{R} T / E} \right\rgroup d t\). (E4.6.13)

If one approximates

\(\frac{R T_{R} T}{E} \approx T_{E}\) (E4.6.14)

as a constant, then Eq. (E4.2.13) \(t_{R}=\int_{0}^{t} \exp \left\lgroup \frac{T-T_{R}}{R T_{R} T / E} \right\rgroup d t\). can be written as

\(t_{R} \approx \int_{0}^{t} \exp \left\lgroup \frac{T-T_{R}}{T_{E}} \right\rgroup d t=\operatorname{texp} \left\lgroup\frac{T-T_{R}}{T_{E}} \right\rgroup\). (E4.6.15)

The severity factor is defined as the log of \( t_{ R } \), that is

\(S F=\log \left\lgroup t \exp \frac{T-T_{R}}{T_{E}}\right\rgroup =\log t+\frac{T-T_{R}}{T_{E} \ln 10}\) (E4.6.16)

For hot-water extraction of woody biomass, the frequently used \(T_{ E }\) is 14.75°C or 14.75 K to be precise (Kabel et al. 2007; Montané et al., 1994; Overend and Chornet, 1986). This is equivalent to \(\frac{E}{R}\)= 10800 K for hot-water extraction of woody biomass or hemicellulose reaction.
