Guest Question.

Consider a perturbation to a two-level system with matrix elements

\( H_{a b}^{\prime}=H_{b a}^{\prime}=\frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{\pi} \tau} e^{-(t / \tau)^{2}}, \quad H_{a a}^{\prime}=H_{b b}^{\prime}=0 \) .

where τ and α are positive constants with the appropriate units.

(a) According to first-order perturbation theory, if the system starts off in the state \( c_{a}=1, c_{b}=0 \text { at } t=-\infty \), what is the probability that it will be found in the state b at t = ∞?

(b) In the limit that \( \tau \rightarrow 0, H_{a b}^{\prime}=\alpha \delta(t) \) Compute the τ → 0 limit of your expression from part (a) and compare the result of .

(c) Now consider the opposite extreme: \( \omega_{0} \tau \gg 1 \). What is the limit of your expression from part (a)? Comment: This is an example of the adiabatic theorem (Section 11.5.2).

Admin
Solution

(a) Using Equation (11.21),

\( \frac{d c_{a}^{(1)}}{d t}=0 \Rightarrow c_{a}^{(1)}(t)=1 \);

\( \frac{d c_{b}^{(1)}}{d t}=-\frac{i}{\hbar} H_{b a}^{\prime} e^{i \omega_{0} t} \Rightarrow c_{b}^{(1)}=-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{0}^{t} H_{b a}^{\prime}\left(t^{\prime}\right) e^{i \omega_{0} t^{\prime}} d t^{\prime} \) (11.21).

\( c_{b}^{(1)}(t)=-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{-\infty}^{t} H_{b a}^{\prime}\left(t^{\prime}\right) e^{i \omega_{0} t^{\prime}} d t^{\prime}=-\frac{i \alpha}{\hbar \tau \sqrt{\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{t} e^{-\left(t^{\prime} / \tau\right)^{2}} e^{i \omega_{0} t^{\prime}} d t^{\prime} \).

Complete the square: \( -\left(\frac{t^{\prime}}{\tau}\right)^{2}+i \omega_{0} t^{\prime}=-\left(\frac{t^{\prime}}{\tau}-i \frac{\omega_{0} \tau}{2}\right)^{2}-\left(\frac{\omega_{0} \tau}{2}\right)^{2} . \text { Let } t \rightarrow \infty \text { and } t^{\prime} / \tau \equiv u \text { : } \)

\( c_{b}^{(1)}(\infty)=-\frac{i \alpha}{\hbar \tau \sqrt{\pi}} e^{-\left(\omega_{0} \tau / 2\right)^{2}} \tau \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-u^{2}} d u=-\frac{i \alpha}{\hbar \sqrt{\pi}} e^{-\left(\omega_{0} \tau / 2\right)^{2}} \sqrt{\pi}=-\frac{i \alpha}{\hbar} e^{-\left(\omega_{0} \tau\right)^{2} / 4}\).

The probability of a transition, then, is

\( P_{a \rightarrow b} \approx\left|c_{b}^{(1)}(\infty)\right|^{2}= \left(\frac{\alpha}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \exp \left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\omega_{0} \tau\right)^{2}\right] \).

(b) In the limit \( \tau \rightarrow 0 \),

\( P_{a \rightarrow b} \approx\left(\frac{\alpha}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \)

The exact result (from ) is \( \sin ^{2}(\alpha / \hbar) \), and expanding this as a power series we get \( P_{a \rightarrow b}= (\alpha / \hbar)^{2}+\cdots \) First-order perturbation theory is precisely the lowest-order term.

(c) In this limit \( P_{a \rightarrow b}=0 \) (no transition occurs). [Interestingly, it is not zero for all times-only as \( t \rightarrow \infty \) , (where the perturbation turns off). In fact, if you compute the probability of a transition at a finite time you get:

\( P_{a \rightarrow b}(t)=\left(\frac{\alpha}{\hbar}\right)^{2} e^{-\left(\omega_{0} \tau\right)^{2} / 2}\left|1-\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{erfc}\left(\frac{t}{\tau}-i \frac{\omega_{0} \tau}{2}\right)\right|^{2} \).

For large \( \omega_{0} \tau \), the rise and fall of the transition probability can also be computed from the overlap of the instantaneous energy eigenstate with the unperturbed state \( \psi_{b} \).]
