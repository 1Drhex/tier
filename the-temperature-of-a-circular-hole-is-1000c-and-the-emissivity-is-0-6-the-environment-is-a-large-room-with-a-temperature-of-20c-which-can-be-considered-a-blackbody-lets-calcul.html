Guest Question.

The temperature of a circular hole is 1000°C, and the emissivity is 0.6. The environment is a large room with a temperature of 20°C, which can be considered a blackbody. Let’s calculate the radiation heat transfer from the circular hole. The simplified solution to this question is to regard the radiosity of the entire inner heating surface as uniform, and work through the problem integrally. The radiosity on the surface is variable. The inner surface can be divided into four parts (hole bottom 1, and side surfaces 2, 3, and 4) for analysis (Fig. 3.8). Let’s assume an imaginary black surface 5 covers the hole, and solve the radiosity of every part before calculating the final amount of heat transfer—as such, this is a precise, stepwise solution.

Admin
Solution

The following works through the problem first stepwise, then in an integral manner for the sake of illustration.

1. Stepwise solution
 For precalculation preparation, first consider the emissive power of each part of the surface:

\(E_{b1}=E_{b2} =E_{b3} =E_{b4}=\sigma T_{1}^{4}\)

\(= (5.67 ×10^{-8} ) × (1000 + 273)^{4} = 1.4887 ×10^{5}\) [W/m²]

\(E_{b5}=\sigma T_{5}^{4}= (5.67 ×10^{-8} ) × (20 + 273)^{4} = 417.8\) [W/m²]

and its emissivity:

\(\epsilon _{1}=\epsilon _{2}=\epsilon _{3}=\epsilon _{4}=0.6\)

\(\epsilon _{5}=1.0\)

its area:

\(A_{1}=A_{5}=A_{6}=A_{7}=\pi \)[cm²]

\(A_{2}=A_{3}=A_{4}=2\pi \)[cm²]

and the configuration factors, which can be calculated based on the example circular plate surface shown in Fig. 1.25. For the configuration factors between two parallel circular planes with the same radius, utilize Eq. (1.93):

\(\begin{cases} R_{1} = \frac{r_{1}}{h}\\ R_{2} = \frac{r_{2}}{h}\\ X =1 + \frac{1 + R_{2}^{2}}{R_{1}^{2}}\\ \varphi _{12} = \frac{1}{2}\left[X – \sqrt{X^2-4(\frac{R_{2}}{R_{1}})^{2} } \right] \end{cases}\)

\(\Phi =\frac{1 + 2R^{2} – \sqrt{1 + 4R^{2}} }{2R^{2}}, R=r/h\)

where r is the radius of the circular plane, and h is the distance between the two planes.
All the configuration factors can be calculated as follows:

\(\Phi _{11}=\Phi _{55}=0 \\ \Phi _{16}=0.37,\Phi _{17}=0.175,\Phi=0.1 \\\Phi _{12}=\Phi _{16}=0.63=\Phi _{54} \\\Phi _{13}=\Phi _{16}-\Phi _{17}=0.195=\Phi _{53} \\\Phi _{14}=\Phi _{17}-\Phi _{15}=0.075=\Phi _{52} \\\Phi _{21}=\Phi _{26}=\Phi _{12}A_{1}/A_{2}=\Phi _{12}/2=0.315=\Phi _{45}=\Phi _{36}=\Phi _{37} \\\Phi _{22}=1 -\Phi _{21}-\Phi _{26}=1 -0.315 -0.315=0.37=\Phi _{33}=\Phi _{44} \\\Phi _{31}=\Phi _{13}A_{1}/A_{3}=\Phi _{13}/2=0.0975=\Phi _{27}=\Phi _{46} \\\Phi _{32}=\Phi _{36}\Phi – 31=0.2175=\Phi _{34}=\Phi _{43}=\Phi _{23} \\\Phi _{41}=\Phi _{14}A_{1}/A_{4}=\Phi _{14}/2=0.0375=\Phi _{25} \\\Phi _{42}=\Phi _{46}-\Phi _{41}=0.06=\Phi _{24}\)

Next, calculate the emissive power of each part of the surface using Eq. (3.12) \(E_{Ri}=\frac{1}{1 – \varphi_{ii}}\left(\sum\limits_{j\neq i}{\varphi_{ij}}E_{Rj}+q_{i} \right) \):

\(E_{R1}=(1 – \epsilon_{1})(\Phi _{12}E_{R2}+\Phi _{14}E_{R4}+\Phi _{15}E_{R5})+\epsilon_{1}E_{b1} \\ E_{R2}=\frac{1}{1 – \Phi _{22}(1 – \epsilon_{2})} [(1 – \epsilon_{2})(\Phi _{21}E_{r1}+\Phi _{23}E_{R3}+\Phi _{24}E_{R4}+\Phi _{25}E_{b5}+\epsilon_{2}E_{b2})] \\ E_{R3}=\frac{1}{1 – \Phi _{33}(1 – \epsilon_{3})} [(1 – \epsilon_{3})(\Phi _{31}E_{R1}+\Phi _{32}E_{R2}+\Phi _{34}E_{R4}+\Phi _{35}E_{b5}+\epsilon_{3}E_{b3})] \\ E_{R4}=\frac{1}{1 – \Phi _{44}(1 – \epsilon_{4})} [(1 – \epsilon_{4})(\Phi _{41}E_{R1}+\Phi _{42}E_{R2}+\Phi _{43}E_{R3}+\Phi _{45}E_{b5}+\epsilon_{4}E_{b4})]\)

Substitute all the values to obtain:

\(E_{R1}=0.252E_{R2}+0.078E_{R3}+0.03E_{R4}+89341 \\ E_{R2}=0.1479E_{R1}+0.1021E_{R3}+0.2817E_{R4}+ 104 484 \\ E_{R3}=0.04577E_{R1}+0.1021E_{R2}+0.1021E_{R4}+ 104 484 \\ E_{R4}=0.01761E_{R1}+0.02817E_{R2}+0.1021E_{R3}+ 104 484\)

Then the equation set can be solved by the Gauss Iteration Method:

\(E_{R1}=1.4003×10^{5}\)[W/m²]

\(E_{R2}=1.4326×10^{5}\)[W/m²]

\(E_{R3}=1.3872×10^{5}\)[W/m²]

\(E_{R4}=1.2557×10^{5}\)[W/m²]

Finally, calculate the total amount of radiation heat transfer. The outward radiation heat transfer values of each part are:

\(Q_{1}=\frac{E_{oi}-E_{Ri}}{(1 – \epsilon_{i})/\epsilon_{i}A_{i}} =\frac{\epsilon_{i}A_{i}}{1 – \epsilon_{i}}(E_{bi}-E_{Ri}) \)

\(Q_{1}=\frac{\epsilon_{1}A_{1}}{1 – \epsilon_{1}}(E_{b1}-E_{R1})=\frac{0.6×\pi ×10^{-4}}{1 – 0.6}(1.4887 -1.4003)×10^{5} =4.1658[W] \\ Q_{2}=\frac{\epsilon_{2}A_{2}}{1 – \epsilon_{2}}(E_{b2}-E_{R2})=\frac{0.6×2\pi ×10^{-4}}{1 – 0.6}(1.4887 -1.4326)×10^{5} =5.2873[W] \\ Q_{3}=\frac{\epsilon_{3}A_{3}}{1 – \epsilon_{3}}(E_{b3}-E_{R3})=\frac{0.6×2\pi ×10^{-4}}{1 – 0.6}(1.4887 -1.3872)×10^{5} =9.5661[W] \\ Q_{4}=\frac{\epsilon_{4}A_{4}}{1 – \epsilon_{4}}(E_{b4}-E_{R4})=\frac{0.6×2\pi ×10^{-4}}{1 – 0.6}(1.4887 -1.2557)×10^{5} =21.959[W]\)

and the total amount of outward, radiation heat transfer of the circular hole is:

\(\sum{Q}=Q_{1}+Q_{2}+Q_{3}+Q_{4}=40.979[W] \)

2. Integral solution
Assuming the radiosity is uniform, consider the inner surface in its entirety:

\(A_{1}=\pi +3×2\pi =7\pi \)[cm²], \(A_{2}=\pi\)[cm²]

\(\Phi _{51}=1.0\), \(\epsilon_{1}=0.6\), \(\epsilon_{5}=1.0\)

\(Q=\frac{E_{b1}-E_{b5}}{\frac{1 – \epsilon_{1}}{\epsilon_{1}A_{1}}+\frac{1}{A_{5}}+\frac{1 – \epsilon_{5}}{\epsilon_{5}A_{5}}}=\frac{A_{5}(E_{b1}-E_{b5})}{\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon_{1}}-1 \right)\frac{A_{5}}{A_{1}}+\frac{1}{\epsilon_{5}}} \\ =\frac{\pi ×10^{-4}(1.4887×10^{-5}-417.8)}{\left(\frac{1}{0.6}-1 \right)\frac{1}{7}+1} \\ = 42.581[W] \)

Comparing the above two solutions, the relative error is \(\Delta =\frac{42.581 – 40.979}{40.979}= 3.9 \)%.

Therefore, the integral solution can be utilized to simplify the calculation in practice.

If the circular hole surface 5 is small enough compared to hole depth, surface 5 can be considered a blackbody surface and the outward radiation of the circular hole can be replaced by the outward radiation of surface 5 at the same temperature. In this case, because the size of surface 5 is not small compared to hole depth, this solution is not appropriate.

\(Q_{1} =A_{5}\sigma T_{1}^{4}= \pi ×10^{-4}×5.67×10^{-8}×1273^{4}\)= 46.78[W]

Compared to the stepwise calculation result for the same scenario, the error is:

\(\Delta =\frac{46.78 – 40.98}{40.98} \)= 14.15%

Now, let’s consider a case where \(\lambda _{w}\) = ∞. For a water cooling hole wall, \(T _{1}\) >\(T _{2}\) ≈ \(T _{3}\). Because the hole wall approximates a blackbody (due to ash deposition), and flame surface \(A _{1}\) and outer surfac \(A _{1}\) both can be considered blackbodies, we have the following:

\(\begin{cases} Q_{13}=E_{b1}\varphi _{13}A_{1} \\ Q_{12}=E_{b1}(1-\varphi _{13})A_{1} \\ \end{cases} \) (3.14)

where \(E _{b1}=\epsilon T_{1}^{4}\). The configuration of a circular hole is:

\(\varphi _{13}=\frac{2\left(1+\frac{d^{2}}{2h^{2}}-\sqrt{1+\frac{d^{2}}{h^{2}} } \right) }{\frac{d^{2}}{h^{2}}} \)

where d and h are the diameter and length of the circular hole, respectively.
Let’s consider a metal hole wall, now – hole wall 2 is an insulating surface with uniform temperature, thus we obtain the following by applying the network method:

\(Q=\frac{E_{b1}-E_{b3}}{\frac{1 – \epsilon_{1}}{\epsilon_{1}}+R\frac{1 – \epsilon_{3}}{\epsilon_{1}A_{3}}}\) (3.15)

where R is the thermal resistance between node 1 and node 3.
The detailed calculation process is not listed here. If we consider the system to be three blackbodies, the calculation is simpler.
