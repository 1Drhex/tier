Guest Question.

A metal sphere of radius a carries a charge Q (Fig. 4.20). It is surrounded, out to radius b, by linear dielectric material of permittivity ε. Find the potential at the center (relative to infinity).

Admin
Solution

To compute V, we need to know E; to find E, we might first try to locate the bound charge; we could get the bound charge from P, but we can’t calculate P unless we already know E (Eq. 4.30). We seem to be in a bind. What we do know is the free charge Q, and fortunately the arrangement is spherically symmetric, so let’s begin by calculating D, using Eq. 4.23:

\(\oint{D · da = Q _{fenc} ,}\) (4.23)

\(P = \epsilon _0χ_eE.\) (4.30)

\(D=\frac{Q}{4\pi r^{2}}\hat{r}, \) for all points r > a.

(Inside the metal sphere, of course, E = P = D = 0.) Once we know D, it is a trivial matter to obtain E, using Eq. 4.32:

\(D = \epsilon E,\) (4.32)

\(E=\begin{cases}\frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon r^{2}}\hat{r}, for a < r < b, \\ \frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon_{0} r^{2}}\hat{r}, for r > b. \end{cases} \)

The potential at the center is therefore

\(V=-\int_{\infty }^{0}{E.dI}=- \int_{\infty }^{b}{\left(\frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon _{0}r^{2}} \right) } dr-\int_{b}^{a}{\left(\frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon _{0}r^{2}} \right) } dr-\int_{a}^{0}{(0)dr}\\=\frac{Q}{4\pi}\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{0}b}+\frac{1}{\epsilon a}-\frac{1}{\epsilon b} \right). \)

As it turns out, it was not necessary for us to compute the polarization or the bound charge explicitly, though this can easily be done:

\(P = \epsilon _0χ_eE =\frac{\epsilon _0χ_eQ}{4\pi \epsilon r^{2}}\hat{r} ,\)

in the dielectric, and hence

\(ρ_{b} = −∇ · P = 0,\)

while

\(σ_b = P ·\hat{n}=\begin{cases} \frac{\epsilon _0χ_eQ}{4\pi \epsilon b^{2}}, at the outer surface,\\\frac{-\epsilon _0χ_eQ}{4\pi \epsilon a^{2}}, at the inner surface.\end{cases} \)

Notice that the surface bound charge at a is negative (\(\hat{n}\) points outward with respect to the dielectric, which is \(+\hat{r}\) at b but \(-\hat{r}\) at a). This is natural, since the charge on the metal sphere attracts its opposite in all the dielectric molecules. It is this layer of negative charge that reduces the field, within the dielectric, from \(1/4\pi \epsilon _{0}(Q/r^{2})\hat{r} to 1/4\pi \epsilon (Q/r^{2})\hat{r} \). In this respect, a dielectric is rather like an imperfect conductor: on a conducting shell the induced surface charge would be such as to cancel the field of Q completely in the region a < r < b; the dielectric does the best it can, but the cancellation is only partial.
