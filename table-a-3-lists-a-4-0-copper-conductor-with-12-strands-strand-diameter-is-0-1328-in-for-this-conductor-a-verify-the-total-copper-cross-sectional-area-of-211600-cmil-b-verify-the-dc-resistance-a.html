Solution

a. The strand diameter is \(d=\left(0.1328in.\right)\left(1000mil/in.\right) =132.8mil \), and, from (4.2.2), the strand area is \(d^{2} \) cmil. Using four significant figures, the cross-sectional area of the 12-strand conductor is

\(A=\left(\frac{\pi }{4} D^{2}in.^{2} \right)\left(1000\frac{mil}{in.} \right)=\frac{\pi }{4}\left(1000D\right)^{2}=\frac{\pi }{4}d^{2} \) sq mil

or

\(A=\left(\frac{\pi }{4} d^{2} sq mil\right) \left(\frac{1cmil}{\pi /4sq mil} \right)=d^{2} \) cmil (4.2.2)

\(A=12d^{2}=12\left(132.8\right)^{2}=211,600 \) cmil

which agrees with the value given in Table A.3.

b. Using (4.2.3) and hard-drawn copper data from Table 4.3,

\(\rho _{T2}=\rho _{T1}=\left(\frac{T_{2}+T }{T_{1} +T} \right)\) (4.2.3)

\(\rho _{50°C}=10.66\left(\frac{50+241.5}{20+241.5} \right)=11.88 \) Ω-cmil/ft

From (4.2.1), the dc resistance at 50°C for a conductor length of 1 mile (5280 ft) is

\(R_{dc,T} =\frac{\rho _{T^{l} } }{A}\Omega\) (4.2.1)

\(R_{dc,50°C} =\frac{\left(11.88\right)\left(5280\times 1.02\right) }{211,600}=0.302 \) Ω/mi

which agrees with the value listed in Table A.3.

c. From Table A.3,

\(\frac{R_{60Hz,50°C} }{R_{dc,50°C} }=\frac{0.303}{0.302}\)=1.003 \(\frac{R_{60Hz,25°C} }{R_{dc,25°C} }=\frac{0.278}{0.276}=1.007 \)
Thus, the 60-Hz resistance of this conductor is about 0.3 to 0.7% higher than the dc resistance. The variation of these two ratios is due to the fact that resistance in Table A.3 is given to only three significant figures.
