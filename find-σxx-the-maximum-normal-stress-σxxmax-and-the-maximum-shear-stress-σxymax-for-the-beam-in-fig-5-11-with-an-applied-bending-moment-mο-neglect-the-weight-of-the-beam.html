Guest Question.

Find \(\sigma _{xx},\) the maximum normal stress \(\sigma ^{\prime }_{xx})_{\max },\) and the maximum shear stress \(\sigma ^{\prime }_{xy})_{\max }\) for the beam in Fig. 5.11 with an applied bending moment \(M_{\omicron }.\) Neglect the weight of the beam.

Admin
Solution

A free-body diagram of the whole structure reveals that we have

\( \sum{F_{x}}=0\rightarrow R_{x}=0,\)

 

\( \sum{F_{y}}=0\rightarrow R_{y}=0,\)

 

\( \sum{M_{z}})_{B}=0\rightarrow M_{\omicron }-M_{w}-R_{y}L=0,\)

and, thus,

\( M_{w}=M_{\omicron }.\)

A free-body diagram of part of the beam similarly requires FIGURE 5.11 A cantilever beam having a rectangular cross section and subjected to an applied moment at the end. Free-body diagrams of the whole and the parts isolate reaction and internal forces and moments as needed. One cut is sufficient because the loads are applied only at the ends.

\( \sum{F_{x}}=0\rightarrow f=0,\)

 

\( \sum{F_{y}}=0\rightarrow V(x)=0 \forall x\)

 

\( \sum{M_{z}})_{A}=0\rightarrow -M_{\omicron }+M_{z}(x)-Vx=0,\)

and thus, as expected,

\( M_{z}(x)=M_{\omicron }.\)

Next, we could draw the shear force and bending moment diagrams, which are trivial in this case of pure bending. From Eq. (5.23), therefore, we have

\( \sigma _{xx}=-\frac{M_{z}(x)y}{I_{zz}}. \) (5.23)

 

 

\( \sigma _{xx}=-\frac{M_{0}y}{I_{zz}} \forall x\epsilon \left[0,L\right],y\epsilon \left[-\frac{h}{2},\frac{h}{2} \right],z\epsilon \left[-\frac{b}{2},\frac{b}{2} \right],\)

where Beam Bending and Column Buckling

\(I_{zz}=\int{y^{2}dA}=\int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{}\int_{-h/2}^{h/2}{y^{2}dydz}=\int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{\frac{h^{3}}{12}dz=\frac{h^{3}b}{12} }.\)

Because \(\sigma _{xx}\) balances the applied load at each cross section, there is no need for any other component of stress relative to (x, y, z). The largest compressive and tensile loads, relative to (x, y, z), occur at \(y=\pm h/2\); hence,

\( \sigma _{xx}=\left(y=\pm \frac{h}{2} \right)=\mp \frac{12M_{\omicron }}{bh^{3}}\left(\frac{h}{2} \right)=\mp \frac{6M_{\omicron }}{bh^{2}}.\)

Considering only a 2-D state of stress here (e.g., \(σ_{xx}, σ_{yy},\) and \(σ_{xy}=σ_{yx}),\) we recall from Chap. 2 that the maximum/minimum normal stresses are called principal stresses. They are computed via

\( \sigma ^{\prime }_{xx})_{\begin{matrix} \max \\ \min \end{matrix} }=\frac{\sigma _{xx}+\sigma _{yy}}{2}\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _{xx}-\sigma _{yy}}{2} \right)^{2}+\sigma ^{2}_{xy} },\)

where \(σ_{yy}=0=σ_{xy}.\) Using the largest value of \(σ_{xx}\) (i.e., at y=h/2), we find that

\( \sigma ^{\prime }_{xx})_{\max }=\frac{\sigma _{xx}} {2}+\sqrt{\frac{\sigma ^{2}_{xx} }{2} }=\frac{6M_{\omicron }}{bh^{2}},\)

 

\( \sigma ^{\prime }_{xx})_{\min }=\frac{\sigma _{xx}}{2}-\sqrt{\frac{\sigma ^{2}_{xx} }{2} }=0.\)

Hence, x and y are principal directions \((α_{p}=0).\) The maximum/minimum shear stress, however, is

\( \sigma ^{\prime }_{xy})_{\begin{matrix}\max \\ \min \end{matrix} }=\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _{xx}-\sigma _{yy}}{2} \right)^{2}+\sigma ^{2}_{xy} }=\pm \frac{\sigma _{xx}}{2}\)

and, therefore,

\( \sigma ^{\prime }_{xy})_{\begin{matrix} \max \\ \min \end{matrix} }=\pm \frac{3M_{\omicron }}{bh^{2}},\)

which occurs at \(α_{s}=π/4\) or \(45^{\circ}\) and \(y=\pm h/2,\) not at \(y=0\) (the centroid), where \(σ_{yx}\) is largest in general. We see, therefore, that we must pay particularly close attention to the coordinate system to which quantities are referred as well as possible failure mechanisms (e.g., ductile materials yield in response to shear stresses and brittle materials fracture in response to normal stresses).
