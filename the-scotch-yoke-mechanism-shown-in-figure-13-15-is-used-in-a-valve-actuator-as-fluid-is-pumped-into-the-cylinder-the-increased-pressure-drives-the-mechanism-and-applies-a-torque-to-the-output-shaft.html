Guest Question.

The scotch-yoke mechanism shown in Figure 13.15 is used in a valve actuator. As fluid is pumped into the cylinder, the increased pressure drives the mechanism and applies a torque to the output shaft. This torque can be used to actuate (open and close) valves. At the instant shown, the pressure load on the piston is 25 lb. Determine the torque generated on the output shaft. The coefficient of friction between the follower pin and crosshead slot is 0.15.

1.Sketch a Kinematic Diagram of the Mechanism.

2.Sketch Free-Body Diagrams of the Mechanism Links.

3.Solve the Equilibrium Equations for Link 2.

4.Solve the Equilibrium Equations for Link 4.

Admin
Solution

1: The kinematic diagram of this scotch-yoke mechanism is shown in Figure 13.16.

2: Link 2 is the piston/rod assembly/crosshead slot. Link 4 is the follower. Notice that link 3 is not a tangible link. It is used in a kinematic simulation to separate the revolute joint on the follower and the sliding joint in the crosshead slot. Thus, the mechanism is modeled with all lower-order joints. The kinematic diagram has four links, two pin joints, and two sliding joints, and consequently one degree of freedom. The driver for this mechanism is the movement of the fluid into the cylinder.

The free-body diagrams for links 2 and 4 are shown in Figure 13.17. Link 3 is not required for force analysis. Notice that a friction force is shown opposing relative motion. The directions may seem confusing and warrant further explanation.

Consider link 4 (Figure 13.17b). The pin moves upward relative to the crosshead slot. Therefore, friction will act to prevent this motion of the pin by acting downward. Similarly, consider link 2 (Figure 13.17a). The slot moves downward relative to the pin (recall the definition of relative motion). Therefore, friction will act to prevent this motion of the slot by acting upward.

3: Link 2 (Figure 13.17a) is examined first because it contains the applied force. For this analysis, only the x-equilibrium equation is required.

\(\overset{+}{\rightarrow }\ \Sigma F^x=0:\)

\(F_{24}\) = 300 lb ←

4: The free-body diagram of link 4 (Figure 13.17b) will reveal the torque on the output shaft. Of course, Newton’s first law dictates that \(F_{42} = F_{24}\).

\(F_{f42}\) = μ\(F_{42}\) = (0.15) (300 lb) = 45 lb

The torque can be determined by using the moment equilibrium equation.

\(+\Lsh\ \Sigma M_A=0:\)

– (\(F_{42}\) cos 45°) (3.5 in.) + (μ\(F_{42}\) cos 45°) (3.5 in.) + \(T_{21}\) = 0

– [(300 lb) cos 45°] (3.5 in.) + [(45 lb) cos 45° (3.5 in.)] + \(T_{21}\) = 0

Finally, the torque exerted on the output shaft is

\(T_{21}\) = + 631 lb-in. = 631 lb-in., cw
