Guest Question.

A CONICAL PENDULUM An inventor designs a pendulum clock using a bob with mass m at the end of a thin wire of length L. Instead of swinging back and forth, the bob is to move in a horizontal circle at constant speed v, with the wire making a fixed angle β with the vertical direction (Fig. 5.32a). This is called a conical pendulum because the suspending wire traces out a cone. Find the tension F in the wire and the period T (the time for one revolution of the bob).

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

To find our target variables, the tension F and period T, we need two equations. These will be the horizontal and vertical components of Newton’s second law applied to the bob. We’ll find the radial acceleration of the bob from one of the circular motion equations.
Figure 5.32b shows our free-body diagram and coordinate system for the bob at a particular instant. There are just two forces on the bob: the weight mg and the tension F in the wire. Note that the center of the circular path is in the same horizontal plane as the bob, not at the top end of the wire. The horizontal component of tension is the force that produces the radial acceleration \(a_{rad}\).

 

EXECUTE:

The bob has zero vertical acceleration; the horizontal acceleration is toward the center of the circle, which is why we use the symbol \(a_{rad}\). Newton’s second law, Eqs. (5.2) (\(\sum \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}=m \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{a}}\), \(\sum F_{x}=m a_{x}\), \(\sum F_{y}=m a_{y}\)), says

\(\sum F_{x}=F \sin \beta=m a_{\mathrm{rad}}\)

 

\(\sum F_{y}=F \cos \beta+(-m g)=0\)

These are two equations for the two unknowns F and β. The equation for \(\sum F_{y}\) gives F = mg/cos β; that’s our target expression for F in terms of β. Substituting this result into the equation for \(\sum F_{x}\) and using sin β/cos β = tan β, we find

\(a_{\mathrm{rad}}=g \tan \beta\)

To relate b to the period T, we use Eq. (5.15) (\(a_{\mathrm{rad}}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} R}{T^{2}}\))for \(a_{\mathrm{rad}}\), solve for T, and insert \(a_{\mathrm{rad}}=g \tan \beta\):

\(a_{\mathrm{rad}}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} R}{T^{2}} \quad \text { so } \quad T^{2}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} R}{a_{\mathrm{rad}}}\)

 

\(T=2 \pi \sqrt{\frac{R}{g \tan \beta}}\)

Figure 5.32a shows that R = L sin β. We substitute this and use sin β/tan β = cos β:

\(T=2 \pi \sqrt{\frac{L \cos \beta}{g}}\)

 

EVALUATE: For a given length L, as the angle b increases, cos β decreases, the period T becomes smaller, and the tension F = mg/cos β increases. The angle can never be 90°, however; this would require that T = 0, F = ∞, and v = ∞. A conical pendulum would not make a very good clock because the period depends on the angle β in such a direct way.
