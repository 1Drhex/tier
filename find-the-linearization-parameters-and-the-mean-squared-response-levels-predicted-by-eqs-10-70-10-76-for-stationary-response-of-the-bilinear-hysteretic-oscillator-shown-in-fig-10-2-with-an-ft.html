Guest Question.

Find the linearization parameters and the mean-squared response levels predicted by Eqs. 10.70 \( \hat{g}[X(t), \dot{X}(t), Z(t) ]=kZ(t) \) 10.76 \( b_{3}=-2\int_{0}^{\infty }{\nu p_{\dot{X}(t)Z(t)}(\nu ,x_{y})d\nu } \) for stationary response of the bilinear hysteretic oscillator shown in Fig. 10.2 with an \( \left\{F(t)\right\} \) excitation that is a stationary, mean-zero, Gaussian white noise with autospectral density \( S_{0} \) .

Admin
Solution

As previously noted, the system is described by Eqs. 10.77 \( m\ddot{X} (t)+c\dot{X} (t)+k_{2}X(t)+ k_{1}Z(t)= F(t) \) and 10.78 \( \dot{Z} (t)=b_{2}\dot{X} (t)+b_{3}Z(t) \) and the \( b_{2} \) and \( b_{3} \) linearization parameters are related to the response levels by Eqs. 10.75 \( b_{2}=1-2 \int_{x_{y}}^{\infty } \int_{0}^{\infty } {p_{\dot{X}(t)Z(t)}(\nu ,w)d\nu dw} \) and 10.76. \( b_{3}=-2\int_{0}^{\infty }{\nu p_{\dot{X}(t)Z(t)}(\nu ,x_{y})d\nu } \) Writing these two expressions as integrals of the jointly Gaussian probability density function gives

\( b_{2}=1-2\int_{x_{y}/\sigma _{Z}}^{\infty }{\frac{e^{-r^{2}/2}}{(2\pi)^{1/2}} }\Phi \left(\rho _{\dot{X},Z}r/[1-\rho ^{2}_{\dot{X}Z}]^{1/2}\right) dr \)

And

\( b_{3}=-2\frac{\sigma _{\dot{X}}}{\sigma _{Z}}\hat{b} _{3} \)

With

\( \hat{b}_{3}=\frac{\rho _{\dot{X}Z}x_{y}}{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma _{Z}} \exp \left(\frac{-x^{2}_{y}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{Z}} \right)\Phi \left(\frac{\rho _{\dot{X}Z}x_{y}}{\sigma _{Z}(1-\rho ^{2}_{\dot{X}Z})^{1/2}} \right) +\frac{(1-\rho ^{2}_{\dot{X}Z})^{1/2}}{2\pi}\exp \left(\frac{-x^{2}_{y}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{Z}(1-\rho ^{2}_{\dot{X}Z})} \right) \)

Note that \( b_{2} \) and \( \hat{b} _{3} \) are dimensionless quantities that depend only on the two parameters \( \rho _{\dot{X}Z} \) and \( x_{y}/\sigma _{Z} \) , whereas \( b_{2} \) has units of frequency and depends on the value \( \sigma _{\dot{X}}/\sigma _{Z} \) .

To evaluate parameters and response levels, we must use expressions giving the response levels of the linearized system of Eqs. 10.77 \( m\ddot{X} (t)+c\dot{X} (t)+k_{2}X(t)+ k_{1}Z(t)= F(t) \) and 10.78 \( \dot{Z} (t)=b_{2}\dot{X} (t)+b_{3}Z(t) \)

We will do this by using the state-space formulation of the covariance equations, as given in Section 9.5. Thus, we rewrite the equations of motion in the form of \( \vec{Y} (t)+B\vec{Y}(t)=\vec{Q}(t), \) with \( \vec{Y} (t)=[X(t),\dot{X}(t),Z(t)]^{T} , \vec{Q} (t)=[0,F(t)/m,0]^{T} \) , and

\( B=\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\k_{2}/m & c/m & k_{1}/m \\ 0 & -b_{2} & -b_{3} \end{matrix} \right] \)

Because we are interested only in stationary response, we can simplify Eq. 9.18 \( \frac{d}{dt} \phi _{YY}(t,t)+A^{-1}B\phi _{YY}(t,t)+\phi _{YY}(t,t)(A^{-1}B)^{T}= 2\pi A^{-1}S_{0}(t)(A^{-1})^{T}+ A^{-1}\vec{\mu} _{Q}(t)\vec{\mu} _{Y}^{T}(t)+ \vec{\mu} _{Y}(t)\vec{\mu} _{Q}^{T}(t) (A^{-1})^{T} \) to be

\( BK_{YY}+K_{YY}B^{T}=2\pi S_{0} \)

with the only nonzero element of the \( S_{0} \) matrix being the scalar \( S_{0}/m^{2} \) in the (2,2) position. After some very minor simplification, the six distinct components of this relationship can be written as

\( K_{X \dot{X}}=0 \)

 

\( cK_{X \dot{X}}+k_{1}K_{X Z}+k_{2}K_{X X}-mK_{\dot{X} \dot{X}}=0 \)

 

\( b_{2}K_{X \dot{X}}+b_{3}K_{X Z}+K_{\dot{X}Z}=0 \)

 

\( k_{2}K_{X \dot{X}}+cK_{\dot{X} \dot{X}}+k_{1}K_{\dot{X}Z}=\pi S_{0}/m \)

 

\( k_{2}K_{XZ}+(c-b_{3}m)K_{\dot{X}Z}-b_{2}mK_{\dot{X}\dot{X}}+k_{1}K_{ZZ}=0 \)

And

\( -b_{2}K_{\dot{X}Z}-b_{3}K_{ZZ}=0 \)

The first of these equations is simply a well-known property of any stationary process, and this can be used to give a slight simplification in the second, third, and fourth equations. Similarly, Eq. 10.78 \( \dot{Z} (t)=b_{2}\dot{X} (t)+b_{3}Z(t) \) shows that the sixth equation is the corresponding condition that \( K_{Z\dot{Z}}=0 \) . Using the linearization condition of \( b_{3}=-2(\sigma _{\dot{X}}/\sigma _{Z})\hat{b} _{3} \) along with this stationarity condition gives \( -b_{2}\rho _{\dot{X}Z}+2\hat{b}_{3}=0 \) as a necessary condition for the joint probability density function of \( \dot{X}(t) \) and \( Z(t).q2\) One can use this relationship along with the A expressions for \( b_{2} \) and \( \hat{b}_{3} \) to derive a unique relationship between \( \rho _{\dot{X}Z} \) and \( x_{y}/\sigma _{Z} \) . In particular, for a selected value of \( x_{y}/\sigma _{Z} \) one can use an initial guess for \( \rho _{\dot{X}Z} \) , evaluate estimates of \( b_{2} \) and \( \hat{b}_{3} \) , reevaluate \( \rho _{\dot{X}Z} \) , and so on. After finding these values for the selected \( x_{y}/\sigma _{Z} \) value, one can use the third response equation to write

\( K_{XZ}=-K_{\dot{X}Z}/b_{3}=\rho _{\dot{X}Z}\sigma ^{2}_{Z}/(2\hat{b}_{3} )=\sigma ^{2}_{Z}/b_{2} \)

Rewriting \( K_{\dot{X}\dot{X}} \) and \( K_{ZZ} \) as \( \sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}} \) . and \( \sigma ^{2}_{Z} \) , respectively, allows relatively simple solution of the fourth and fifth response equations to find these quantities. The second response equation then gives the value of \( K_{XX}\equiv \sigma ^{2}_{X} \) . Although there are multiple solution branches for these equations, there seems to be only one solution that gives real positive values of \( \sigma ^{2}_{X}, \sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}} \) , and \( \sigma ^{2}_{Z} \) for any given value of \( x_{y}/\sigma _{Z} \) . The following sketches show the results of this linearization for the situations with \( k_{2}=k_{1} \) and \( k_{2}=0.05k_{1} \) . The form of the plots is the same as in Example 10.10. For \( k_{2}=0.05k_{1} \) and intermediate values of \( \sigma ^{\ast }_{F}/x_{y} \) , it is observed that the fit of this linearization to the numerical data is significantly better than for the linearization of Example 10.10. For larger values of \( \sigma ^{\ast }_{F}/x_{y} \) , though, this linearization is less accurate than the one of Example 10.10, for either value of \( k_{2}/k_{1} \)
