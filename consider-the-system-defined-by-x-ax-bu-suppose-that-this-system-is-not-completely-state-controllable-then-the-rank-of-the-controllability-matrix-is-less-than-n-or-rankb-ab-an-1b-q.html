Guest Question.

Consider the system defined by

\(\dot{\mathbf{x}}=\mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}+\mathbf{B} u\)

Suppose that this system is not completely state controllable. Then the rank of the controllability matrix is less than \(n\), or

\(\operatorname{rank}\left[\begin{array}{c:c:c:c}\mathbf{B}& \mathbf{A B}& \cdots& \mathbf{A}^{n-1} \mathbf{B}\end{array}\right]=q<n\) (10.136)

This means that there are \(q\) linearly independent column vectors in the controllability matrix. Let us define such \(q\) linearly independent column vectors as \(\mathbf{f}_{1}, \mathbf{f}_{2}, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_{q} .\) Also, let us choose \(n-q\) additional \(n\)-vectors \(\mathbf{v}_{q+1}, \mathbf{v}_{q+2}, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{n}\) such that

\(\mathbf{P}=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{f}_{1} & \mathbf{f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{f}_{q} & \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \mathbf{v}_{q+2} & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_{n}\end{array}\right]\)

is of rank \(n\). By using matrix \(\mathbf{P}\) as the transformation matrix, define

\(\mathbf{P}^{-1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{P}=\hat{\mathbf{A}}, \quad \mathbf{P}^{-1} \mathbf{B}=\hat{\mathbf{B}}\)

Show that \(\hat{\mathbf{A}}\) can be given by

\(\hat{\mathbf{A}}=\left[\begin{array}{c:c}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\\hdashline\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{array}\right]\)

where \(\mathbf{A}_{11}\) is a \(q \times q\) matrix, \(\mathbf{A}_{12}\) is a \(q \times(n-q)\) matrix, \(\mathbf{A}_{22}\) is an \((n-q) \times(n-q)\) matrix, and \(\mathbf{0}\) is an \((n-q) \times q\) matrix. Show also that matrix \(\mathbf{B}\) can be given by

\(\hat{\mathbf{B}}=\left[\frac{\mathbf{B}_{11}}{\mathbf{0}}\right]\)

where \(\mathbf{B}_{11}\) is a \(q \times 1\) matrix and \(\mathbf{0}\) is an \((n-q) \times 1\) matrix.

Admin
Solution

Notice that

\(\mathbf{A P}=\mathbf{P} \hat{\mathbf{A}}\)

or

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{A f}_{1} & \mathbf{A f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{A f}_{q} & \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_{n}\end{array}\right]} \\&=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{f}_{1} & \mathbf{f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{f}_{q} & \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_{n}\end{array}\right] \hat{\mathbf{A}}\end{aligned}\) (10.137)

 

Also,

\(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{P \hat B}\) (10.138)

 

Since we have \(q\) linearly independent column vectors \(\mathbf{f}_{1}, \mathbf{f}_{2}, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_{q}\), we can use the Cayley-Hamilton theorem to express vectors \(\mathbf{A f}_{1}, \mathbf{A f}_{2}, \ldots, \mathbf{A f}_{q}\) in terms of these \(q\) vectors. That is,

\(\begin{aligned}\mathbf{A f}_{1}&=a_{11} \mathbf{f}_{1}+a_{21} \mathbf{f}_{2}+\cdots+a_{q 1} \mathbf{f}_{q} \\\mathbf{A f}_{2}&=a_{12} \mathbf{f}_{1}+a_{22} \mathbf{f}_{2}+\cdots+a_{q 2} \mathbf{f}_{q}\\&\cdot\\&\cdot\\&\cdot\\\mathbf{A f}_{q}&=a_{1 q} \mathbf{f}_{1}+a_{2 q} \mathbf{f}_{2}+\cdots+a_{q q} \mathbf{f}_{q}\end{aligned}\)

 

Hence, Equation (10-137) may be written as follows:

\(\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{A f}_{1} & \mathbf{A f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{A f}_{q} & \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{A v}_{n}\end{array}\right]\)

 

\(=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{A f}_{1} & \mathbf{A f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{A f}_{q} & \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{A v}_{n}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ccc:ccc}a_{11}&\cdots &a_{1q}&a_{1q+1} & \cdots&a_{1n}\\a_{21}&\cdots &a_{2q}&a_{2q+1} & \cdots&a_{2n}\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\a_{q1}&\cdots &a_{qq}&a_{qq+1} & \cdots&a_{qn}\\\hdashline 0&\cdots &0&a_{q+1q+1} & \cdots&a_{q+1n}\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\\cdot&&\cdot&\cdot&&\cdot\\0&\cdots &0&a_{nq+1} & \cdots&a_{nn}\end{array}\right]\)

 

Define

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\begin{array}{ccc}a_{11} & \cdots & a_{1 q} \\a_{21} & \cdots & a_{2 q} \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\a_{q 1} & \cdots & a_{q q}\end{array}\right]=\mathbf{A}_{11}} \\&{\left[\begin{array}{ccc}a_{1 q+1} & \cdots & a_{1 n} \\a_{2 q+1} & \cdots & a_{2 n} \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\a_{q q+1} & \cdots & a_{q n}\end{array}\right]=\mathbf{A}_{12}} \\&{\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & \cdots & 0 \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\0 & \cdots & 0\end{array}\right]=\mathbf{A}_{21}=(n-q) \times q \text { zero matrix }} \\&{\left[\begin{array}{ccc}a_{q+1 q+1} & \cdots & a_{q+1 n} \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & & \cdot\\a_{nq+1} & \cdots & a_{nn}\end{array}\right]}\end{aligned}\)

 

Then Equation (10-137) can be written as

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{A f}_{1} & \mathbf{A f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{A f}_{q} & \mathbf{A v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{A v}_{n}\end{array}\right]} \\&=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{f}_{1} & \mathbf{f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{f}_{q} & \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_{n}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c:c}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\\hdashline \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{array}\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

Thus,

\(\mathbf{A P}=\mathbf{P}\left[\begin{array}{c:c}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\\hdashline \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{array}\right]\)

 

Hence,

\(\mathbf{P}^{-1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{P}=\hat{\mathbf{A}}=\left[\begin{array}{c:c}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\\hdashline \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{array}\right]\)

 

Next, referring to Equation (10-138), we have

\(\mathbf{B}=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l:l:l:l}\mathbf{f}_{1} & \mathbf{f}_{2} & \cdots & \mathbf{f}_{q} & \mathbf{v}_{q+1} & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_{n}\end{array}\right] \hat{\mathbf{B}}\) (10.139)

 

Referring to Equation (10-136), notice that vector B can be written in terms of \(q\) linearly independent column vectors \(\mathbf{f}_{1}, \mathbf{f}_{2}, \ldots, \mathbf{f}_{q}\). Thus, we have

\(\mathbf{B}=b_{11} \mathbf{f}_{1}+b_{21} \mathbf{f}_{2}+\cdots+b_{q 1} \mathbf{f}_{q}\)

 

Consequently, Equation (10-139) may be written as follows:

 

\(b_{11}+f_1+b_{21}f_2+\cdots+b_{q1}f_q=\left[\begin{array}{c:c:c:c:c:c:c}\mathbf{f}_1&\mathbf{f}_2&\cdots&\mathbf{f}_q&\mathbf{v}_{q+1}&\cdots&\mathbf{v}_n\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}b_{11}\\b_{21}\\\cdot\\\cdot\\\cdot\\b_{q1}\\o\\\cdot\\\cdot\\\cdot\\0\end{array}\right]\)

 

Thus,

\(\hat{\mathbf{B}}=\left[\begin{array}{c}\mathbf{B}_{11}\\\hdashline\mathbf{0}\end{array}\right]\)

 

where

\(\mathbf{B}_{11}=\left[\begin{array}{c}b_{11} \\b_{21} \\\cdot \\\cdot \\\cdot \\b_{q 1}\end{array}\right]\)
