Guest Question.

A 1-by-2-cm ceramic strip \([k=3.0W/m\cdot^{◦} C, p=1600kg/m^{3},\, and \, c=0.8 kJ/kg\cdot^{◦} C ]\) is embedded in a high-thermal-conductivity material, as shown in Figure Example 3-10, so that the sides are maintained at a constant temperature of \(900 ^{◦} C\). The bottom surface of the ceramic is insulated, and the top surface is exposed to a convection and radiation environment at \(T_{∞} =50 ^{◦} C,h=50W/m^{2}\cdot^{◦} C \) , and the radiation heat loss is calculated from:

\( q=\sigma A\epsilon (T^{4}-T^{4}_{∞} )\) where:

A= surface area

\(\sigma =5.669\times 10^{-8} \, W/m^{2} \cdot^{◦} K^{4} \)

ε= 0.7

Solve for the steady-state temperature distribution of the nodes shown and the rate of heat loss.
The radiation temperatures are in degrees Kelvin.

Admin
Solution

We shall employ the resistance formulation and note that the radiation can be written as:

\(q=\sigma \epsilon A(T^{4}-T^{4}_{∞} )=\frac{T-T_{∞} }{R_{rad} }\hspace{30 pt} [a] \)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{rad}}=\sigma \epsilon A(T^{2}-T^{2}_{∞} )=(T+T_{∞}) \hspace{30 pt} [b]\)

 

From symmetry \(T_{1}= T_{3} ,T_{4}= T_{6}\; and, \> T_{7}= T_{9}\) so we have only six unknown nodes. The resistances
are now computed:

Nodes 1, 2:

\(\frac{1}{R_{m+} }=\frac{1}{R_{m-} } =\frac{kA}{\Delta x} =\frac{(3)(0.0025)}{0.005}=1.5 \hspace{30 pt}\enspace \frac{1}{R_{n-} } =\frac{(3)(0.005)}{0.005}=3.0 \)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{n+,conv}}=hA=(50)(0.005)=0.25 \hspace{30 pt} [c]\)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{n+,rad}}=\sigma \epsilon A(T^{2}-T^{2}_{∞} )(T+T_{∞}) \)

 

The radiation term introduces nonlinearities and will force us to employ an iterative solution.
Nodes 4, 5:

 

\(A11\frac{1}{R}=\frac{KA}{\Delta x} =\frac{(3.0)(0.005)}{0.005} =3.0 \)

 

Nodes 7, 8:

\(\frac{1}{R_{m+} }=\frac{1}{R_{m-} } =1.5\hspace{30 pt} \qquad\frac{1}{R_{n+} }=3.0 \)

 

Because the bottom surface is insulated ,\(1/R_{n-}=0 \).We now use Equatio(3-32):\(T_{i}=\frac{q_{i}+\sum\limits_{j}{T_{j}/R_{ij} } }{\sum\limits_{j}{(1/R_{ij} )} } \)

 

\(T_{i}=\frac{\sum\limits({T_{j}/R_{ij}) } }{\sum\limits{(1/R_{ij} )} }\hspace{30 pt} [3-33] \)

 

and tabulate:

Node ∑(1/Rij )
1 6.25+1/Rrad
2 6.25+1/Rrad
4 12

7 6
8 6

 

Our nodal equations are thus expressed in degrees Kelvin because of the radiation terms and become :

\(T_{1} =\frac{1}{\sum{(1/R_{ij} )} }=[1.5T_{2} +3T_{4} (1.5)(1173)+(323)(0.25)+\sigma \epsilon (0.005)(T^{2}_{1}+323^{2} )(T_{1}+323 )(323)] \)

 

\(T_{1} =\frac{1}{\sum{(1/R_{ij} )} }=[1.5T_{1}(2) +3T_{5} (1.5)(1173)+(323)(0.25)+\sigma \epsilon (0.005)(T^{2}_{2}+323^{2} )(T_{2}+323 )(323)] \)

 

\(T_{4} =\frac{1}{12}[(1173)(3.0)+3T_{1} +3T_{7} +3T_{5} ]\hspace{30 pt} T_{5} =\frac{1}{12}[2T_{4} (3.0)+3T_{2} +3T_{8} ] \)

 

\(T_{7} =\frac{1}{6}[(1173)(1.5)+3T_{4} +1.5T_{8} ]\hspace{30 pt} T_{5} =\frac{1}{6}[2T_{7} (1.5) +3T_{5} ] \)

 

The radiation terms create a very nonlinear set of equations. The computational algorithm we shall use is outlined as follows:

1. Assume \(T_{1} =T_{2} =1173 K. \) .

2.Compute \(1/R_{rad} \) and \(1/R_{ij} \) for nodes 1 and on the basis of this assumption.

3.Solve the set of equations for\(T_{1}\) through \(T_{8}\).

4. Using new values of \(T_{1}\) and\(T_{2}\), recalculate 1/Rrad values.

5. Solve equations again, using new values.

6. Repeat the procedure until answers are sufficiently convergent.

The results of six iterations are shown in the table. As can be seen, the convergence is quite rapid.

At this point we may note that in a practical problem the value of will only be known within a tolerance of several percent, and thus there is nothing to be gained by carrying the solution to unreasonable limits of accuracy.

The heat loss is determined by calculating the radiation and convection from the top surface
(nodes 1, 2, 3):

\(q_{rad}=\sum{\sigma \epsilon A_{i}(T^{4}_{i}-323^{4} ) } \)

 

\(=(5.669×10^{-8} )(0.7)(0.005)[(2)(1020.88^{4} −323^{4} )+984.313^{4} −323^{4} ]=610.8W/m \> depth\)

 

\(q_{conv}=\sum{h A_{i}(T_{i}-323 ) } \)

 

\(= (50)(0.005)[(2)(1020.88−323)+984.313−323]=514.27 W \)

 

\(q_{total}=610.8+514.27=1125.07 \; W/m\> depth \)

 

This can be checked by calculating the conduction input from the \(900 ^{◦} C\) surfaces:

\(q_{cond}=\sum{k A_{i}\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x} } \)

 

\( =\frac{(2)(3.0)}{0.005}[(0.0025)(1173−1020.879)+(0.005)(1173−1092.369) +(0.0025)(1173−1111.384)]= 1124.99\, W/m\> depth\)

The agreement is excellent.
