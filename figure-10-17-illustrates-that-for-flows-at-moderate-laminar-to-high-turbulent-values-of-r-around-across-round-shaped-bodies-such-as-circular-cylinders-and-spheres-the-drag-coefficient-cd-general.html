Guest Question.

Figure 10.17 illustrates that for flows at moderate (laminar) to high (turbulent) values of R around/across round-shaped bodies such as circular cylinders and spheres, the drag coefficient, \( C_{D} \) generally decreases with an increase in R. Consider a 5-milelong oil pipeline with a diameter of 3 ft that is submerged in the ocean. Assume the ocean water is at \( 68^{\circ }F \) flows over the pipeline, as illustrated in Figure EP 10.14. (a) Determine the drag coefficient and the drag force if the velocity of flow over the pipeline is 0.00025 ft/sec. (b) Determine the drag coefficient and the drag force if the velocity of flow over the pipeline is 2.5 ft/sec.

Admin
Solution

(a)–(b) The frontal area is used to compute the drag force for the circular pipeline, and the drag force is determined by applying Equation 10.12 \( F_{D} = C_{D} \frac{1}{2} \rho v^{2}A \). The fluid properties for the ocean water (saltwater) are given in Table A.4 in Appendix A.
(a) The drag coefficient and the drag force for a velocity of flow over the cylinder of 0.00025 ft/sec is determined as follows:

\( slug: = 1 lb \frac{sec^{2}}{ft} \) \( L: = 5 mile = 2.64 \times 10^{4} ft \) \( D: = 3 ft \)

\( A_{front}: = LD = 7.92 \times 10^{4} ft^{2} \) \( \rho : = 1.985 \frac{slug}{ft^{3}} \) \(\mu: = 22.50 \times 10^{-6} lb \frac{slug}{ft^{2}} \)

\( V: = 0.00025 \frac{ft}{sec} \) \( R: = \frac{\rho .V .D}{\mu} = 66.167 \) \( C_{D}: =2 \)

Guess value: \( F_{D}: = 1 lb \)

Given

\( F_{D} = C_{D} \frac{1}{2} \rho.V^{2} . A_{front} \)
\( F_{D}: = Find (F_{D}) = 9.826 \times 10^{-3} lb \)

(b) The drag coefficient and the drag force for a velocity of flow over the cylinder of 2.5 ft/sec are determined as follows:

\( V: = 2.5 \frac{ft}{sec} \) \( R := \frac{\rho .V .D}{\mu} = 6.167 \times 10^{5} \) \( C_{D}: = 0.35 \)

Guess value: \( F_{D}: = 1 lb \)

Given

\( F_{D} = C_{D} \frac{1}{2} \rho.V^{2} . A_{front} \)
\( F_{D}: = Find (F_{D}) = 1.72 \times 10^{5} lb \)

Therefore, although the drag coefficient, \( C_{D} \) decreases (from 2 to 0.35) with an increase in the velocity, v (from 0.00025 ft/sec to 2.5 ft/sec) and thus an increase in the Reynolds number, R (from 66.167 to 6.617 \( \times 10^{5} \)), the drag force, \( F_{D} \) increases (from 9.826 \( \times 10^{-3} \) lb to 1.72\( \times 10^{5} \) lb) with an increase in velocity, as \( F_{D} \) is directly proportional to \( V^{2} \).
