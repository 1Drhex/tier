Guest Question.

Imagine an infinite square well (Equation 2.22) into which we introduce a delta-function perturbation,

\( V(x)= \begin{cases}0, & 0 \leq x \leq a \\ \infty, & \text { otherwise }\end{cases} \) (2.22).

\( H^{\prime}(x)=\lambda \delta\left(x-x_{0}\right) \) ,

where λ is a positive constant, and \( 0<x_{0}<a \) (to simplify matters, let \( x_{0}=p a \) , where 0 < p <1 ).

(a) Find the first-order correction to the nth allowed energy (Equation 2.30), assuming λ is small. (What does “small” mean, in this context?)

\( E_{n}=\frac{\hbar^{2} k_{n}^{2}}{2 m}=\frac{n^{2} \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m a^{2}} \) (2.30).

(b) Find the second-order correction to the allowed energies. (Leave your answer as a sum.)

(c) Now solve the Schrödinger equation exactly, treating separately the regions \( 0 \leq x<x_{0} \text { and } x_{0}<x<a \), and imposing the boundary conditions at \( x_0\) ,Derive the transcendental equation for the energies:

\( u_{n} \sin \left(u_{n}\right)+\Lambda \sin \left(p u_{n}\right) \sin \left[(1-p) u_{n}\right]=0 \quad(E>0) \) (7.116).

\( \text { Here } \Lambda \equiv 2 m a \lambda / \hbar^{2}, u_{n} \equiv k_{n} a, \text { and } k_{n} \equiv \sqrt{2 m E_{n}} / \hbar \).

Check that Equation 7.116 reproduces your result from part (a), in the appropriate limit.

(d) Everything so far holds just as well if λ is negative, but in that case there may be an additional solution with negative energy. Derive the transcendental equation for a negative-energy state:

\( v \sinh (v)+\Lambda \sinh (p v) \sinh [(1-p) v]=0 \quad(E<0) \) (7.117).

\( \text { where } v \equiv \kappa a \text { and } \kappa \equiv \sqrt{-2 m E} / \hbar \).

Specialize to the symmetrical case p = 1/2 , and show that you recover the energy of the delta-function well (Equation 2.132), in the appropriate regime.

\( \psi(x)=\frac{\sqrt{m \alpha}}{\hbar} e^{-m \alpha|x| / \hbar^{2}} ; \quad E=-\frac{m \alpha^{2}}{2 \hbar^{2}} \) (2.132).

(e) There is in fact exactly one negative-energy solution, provided that \( |\Lambda|>1 /[p(1-p)] \). First, prove this (graphically), for the case p = 1/2. (Below that critical value there is no negative-energy solution.) Next, by computer, plot the solution v, as a function of p, for \( \Lambda=-4.1,-5, \text { and }-10 \). Verify that the solution only exists within the predicted range of p.

(f) For p = 1/2 , plot the ground state wave function, \( \psi_{1}(x) \) , for \( \Lambda=0,-2,-3,-3.5,-4.5,-5, \text { and }-10 \), to show how the sinusoidal shape (Figure 2.2) evolves into the exponential shape (Figure 2.13), as the delta function well “deepens.”

Solution

(a) The first-order correction to the nth energy is

\( E_{n}^{1}=\left\langle\psi_{n}^{0}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{n}^{0}\right\rangle \).

\( =\lambda \int_{0}^{a}\left|\psi_{n}^{0}(x)\right|^{2} \delta\left(x-x_{0}\right) \).

\( =\lambda\left|\psi_{n}^{0}\left(x_{0}\right)\right|^{2} \).

\( =\frac{2 \lambda}{a} \sin ^{2}\left(\frac{n \pi x_{0}}{L}\right) \).

Here small means that

\( \left|\frac{V_{m n}}{E_{m}^{0}-E_{n}^{0}}\right| \ll 1 \).

The matrix element \( \left|V_{m n}\right|=(2 \lambda / a)\left|\sin \left(m \pi x_{0} / a\right) \sin \left(n \pi x_{0} / a\right)\right| \text { is of order } 2 \lambda / a \) and the spacing between energy levels is of order \( \pi^{2} \hbar^{2} / 2 m a^{2} \) so that we need to have

\( \lambda \ll \frac{a}{2} \frac{\pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m a^{2}} \).

(b) To compute the second-order correction we first need

\( V_{m n}=\left\langle\psi_{m}^{0}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{n}^{0}\right\rangle \).

\( =\lambda \int\left[\psi_{m}^{0}(x)\right]^{*} \delta\left(x-x_{0}\right) \psi_{n}^{0}(x) d x \).

\( =\frac{2 \lambda}{a} \sin \left(\frac{m \pi x_{0}}{a}\right) \sin \left(\frac{n \pi x_{0}}{a}\right) \).

Then the second-order correction to the allowed energies is then

\( E_{n}^{2}=\sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\left|V_{m n}\right|^{2}}{E_{n}^{0}-E_{m}^{0}} \).

\( =\frac{(2 \lambda / a)^{2}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{2} /\left(2 m a^{2}\right)} \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(\frac{m \pi x_{0}}{a}\right) \sin ^{2}\left(\frac{n \pi x_{0}}{a}\right)}{n^{2}-m^{2}} \).

\( =\frac{8 m \lambda^{2}}{\pi^{2} \hbar^{2}} \sin ^{2}\left(\frac{n \pi x_{0}}{a}\right) \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(\frac{m \pi x_{0}}{a}\right)}{n^{2}-m^{2}} \).

(c) We construct a piecewise solution. The time-independent Schrödinger equation for \( 0<x<x_{0} \) or for \( x_{0}<x<a \) reads

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \psi^{\prime \prime}=E \psi \).

for which the general solution is \( \psi(x)=A \sin (k x)+B \cos (k x) \text { where } k=\sqrt{2 m E / \hbar^{2}} \) o that our piecewise solution is

\( \psi(x)= \begin{cases}A \sin (k x)+B \cos (k x) & 0<x<x_{0} \\ C \sin (k x)+D \cos (k x) & x_{0}<x<a\end{cases} \).

The boundary condition at x = 0 gives B = 0 and the boundary condition at x = a gives D = -C tan(k a) so with these two boundary conditions satisfied

\( \psi(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{cc} A \sin (k x) & 0<x<x_{0} \\ C^{\prime} \sin [k(a-x)] & x_{0}<x<a \end{array}\right. \).

where (unimportantly) we’ve defined a new constant \( C^{\prime}=-C / \cos (k a) \), Now we look at the boundary conditions at \( x = x_0\) . Continuity of the wave function requires

\( A \sin \left(k x_{0}\right)=C^{\prime} \sin \left[k\left(a-x_{0}\right)\right] \) \(\star\).

and the condition on the derivative (Equation 2.128) is

\( \Delta\left(\frac{d \psi}{d x}\right)=-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2}} \psi(0) \) (2.128).

\( \Delta \psi^{\prime}=\frac{2 m \lambda}{\hbar^{2}} \psi\left(x_{0}\right) \).

\( -C^{\prime} k \cos \left[k\left(a-x_{0}\right)\right]-A k \cos \left(k x_{0}\right)=\frac{2 m \lambda}{\hbar^{2}} A \sin \left(k x_{0}\right) \).

\( A k \cos \left(k x_{0}\right)+\frac{2 m \lambda}{\hbar^{2}} A \sin \left(k x_{0}\right)=-C^{\prime} k \cos \left[k\left(a-x_{0}\right)\right] \) \( \blacklozenge \) .

Dividing Equation \( \blacklozenge \) by Equation \(\star\) gives

\( k \cot \left(k x_{0}\right)+\frac{2 m \lambda}{\hbar^{2}}=-k \cot \left[k\left(a-x_{0}\right)\right] \).

\( u \cot (p u)+\Lambda=-u \cot [u(1-p)] \).

\( u \cos (p u) \sin [u(1-p)]+\Lambda \sin (p u) \sin [u(1-p)]=-u \cos [u(1-p)] \sin (p u) \).

which simplifies to

\( u \sin (u)+\Lambda \sin (p u) \sin [u(1-p)]=0 \) \( \star \star \) .

Now, to calculate the corrections to the energy from this expression note that

\( u=\sqrt{\frac{2 m a^{2} E}{\hbar^{2}}}=\sqrt{\frac{2 m a^{2}\left(E_{n}^{0}+E_{n}^{1}+\ldots\right)}{\hbar^{2}}} \).

\( =\sqrt{\frac{2 m a^{2} E_{n}^{0}}{\hbar^{2}}}+\sqrt{\frac{m a^{2}}{2 E_{n}^{0} \hbar^{2}}} E_{n}^{1}+\ldots \).

\( =n \pi+\frac{m a^{2}}{n \pi \hbar^{2}} E_{n}^{1}+\ldots \).

\( =u_{0}+\delta u+\ldots \).

Plugging into Equation \( \star \star \) and keeping terms of order δu gives

\( \left(u_{0}+\delta u\right) \sin \left(u_{0}+\delta u\right)+\Lambda \sin \left(p u_{0}\right) \sin \left[u_{0}(1-p)\right] \approx 0 \).

where we can ignore the δu in the second term because the Λ ensures that the whole term is already order δ. Expanding this

\( u_{0} \underbrace{\sin \left(u_{0}\right)}_{0}+[u_{0} \underbrace{\cos \left(u_{0}\right)}_{(-1)^{n}}+\sin \left(u_{0}\right)] \delta u+\Lambda \sin \left(p u_{0}\right) \approx 0 \) \( \blacklozenge \blacklozenge\).

and

\( \delta u=\frac{m a^{2}}{n \pi \hbar^{2}} E_{n}^{1}=-(-1)^{n} \frac{\Lambda}{u_{0}} \sin \left(p u_{0}\right) \sin \left[u_{0}(1-p)\right] \).

\( E_{n}^{1}=-(-1)^{n} \frac{n \pi \hbar^{2}}{m a^{2}} \frac{2 m a \lambda}{n \pi \hbar^{2}} \sin \left(p u_{0}\right)\left[\sin u_{0} \cos \left(p u_{0}\right)\right. \)

\( \left.-\sin \left(p u_{0}\right) \cos u_{0}\right] \).

\( =\frac{2 \lambda}{a}\left|\sin \left(p u_{0}\right)\right|^{2} \)

which is precisely the result obtained above in part (a) since \( p u_{0}=n \pi x_{0} / a \).

(d) In this case we want negative-energy solutions and we write

\( u=\sqrt{\frac{2 m E a^{2}}{\hbar^{2}}}=i \sqrt{\frac{2 m|E| a^{2}}{\hbar^{2}}} \equiv i v \).

Plugging this into Equation \( \star \star \) and noting that \( \sin (i z)=i \sinh z \) we have

\( v \sinh v+\Lambda \sinh (p v) \sinh [(1-p) v]=0 \).

For the case p = 1/2,

\( v \sinh v+\Lambda \sinh ^{2}\left(\frac{v}{2}\right)=0 \).

\( v=-\frac{1}{2} \Lambda \tanh \left(\frac{v}{2}\right) \).

If a is huge so that the boundaries don’t matter, then \( \tanh (v / 2)=\tanh (\kappa a / 2) \approx 1 \) and

\( v \approx-\frac{\Lambda}{2} \)

and then

\( E=-\frac{\hbar^{2} v^{2}}{2 m a^{2}} \approx-\frac{\hbar^{2} \Lambda^{2}}{8 m a^{2}}=-\frac{m \lambda^{2}}{2 \hbar^{2}} \).

which is precisely the energy of the delta-function well.

(e) For the case of p = 1/2,

\( v=\frac{1}{2}|\Lambda| \tanh \left(\frac{v}{2}\right) \).

as shown above. From the graph (below) it’s clear that the functions on the right- and left-hand sides of this equation intersect at most once. In order to intersect at all the slope of the function on the right-hand side must be greater than 1 at the origin.

\( \left.\frac{d}{d v} \frac{1}{2}|\Lambda| \tanh \left(\frac{v}{2}\right)\right|_{0}>1 \)

\( \frac{|\Lambda|}{4}>1 \).

as claimed for p = 1/2.
