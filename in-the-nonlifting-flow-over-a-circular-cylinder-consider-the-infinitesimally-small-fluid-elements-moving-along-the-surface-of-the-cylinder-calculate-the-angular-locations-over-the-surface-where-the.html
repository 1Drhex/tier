Guest Question.

In the nonlifting flow over a circular cylinder, consider the infinitesimally small fluid elements moving along the surface of the cylinder. Calculate the angular locations over the surface where the acceleration of the fluid elements are a local maximum and minimum. For the case where the radius of the cylinder is 1 m and the freestream flow velocity is 50 m/s, calculate the values of the local maximum and minimum accelerations.

Admin
Solution

From Equation (3.100) the local velocity of the fluid elements on the surface as a function of angular location Θ a is

\(V_{\Theta }=-2V_{\infty }\sin \Theta (3.100)\)

The acceleration of the fluid elements is dV Θ /dt. From Equation (3.100),

\(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt}=-2V_{\infty }(\cos \Theta )\frac{d\Theta }{dt} (E3.1)\)

Return to Figure 3.28. Let d? be an incremental change in ?. The corresponding incremental distance on the cylinder surface subtended by d Θ a is ds, given by

\(d_{s}=Rd\Theta\)

Hence,

\(\frac{d\Theta }{dt}=\frac{1}{R}\frac{ds}{dt} (E3.2)\)

Substituting Equation (E3.2) into (E3.1), we have

\(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt}=-2V_{\infty }(\cos \Theta )(\frac{1}{R}\frac{ds}{dt}) (E3.3)\)

For a fluid element moving over an incremental distance ds in time dt, its linear velocity is by definition ds/dt. Thus, VΘ ta = ds/dt. Replacing ds/dt in Equation (E3.3) with V Θ , we have

\(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt}=-2V_{\infty }(\cos \Theta )(\frac{V_{\Theta }}{R}) (E3.4)\)

Substitute Equation (3.100) into (E3.4).

\(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt}=\frac{4v_{\infty }^{2}}{R}\sin \Theta \cos \Theta (E3.5)\)

From the trigonometric identity

\(\sin 2\Theta =2\sin \Theta \cos \Theta\)

Equation (E3.5) becomes

\(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt}=\frac{2v_{\infty }^{2}}{R}\sin 2\Theta (E3.6)\)

Equation (E3.6) gives the flow acceleration along the surface, dV Θa /dt, as a function of angular location Θ along the surface. To find the Θ locations at which the acceleration is a maximum or minimum, differentiate Equation (E3.6) with respect to Θ , and set the result equal to zero.

\(\frac{d}{d\Theta }(\frac{dV_{\Theta }}{dt})=\frac{4v_{\infty }^{2}}{R}\cos 2\Theta =0 (E3.7)\)

Solving Equation (E3.7) for Θ , we have the location where acceleration is either a local maximum or minimum:

\(\Theta =45^{\circ},135^{\circ},225^{\circ},315^{\circ} (E3.8)\)

From Equation (E3.6), the values of the local flow acceleration at each one of these locations are respectively

\(\frac{2V_{\infty }^{2}}{R},-\frac{2V_{\infty }^{2}}{R},\frac{2V_{\infty }^{2}}{R},-\frac{2V_{\infty }^{2}}{R} (E3.9)\)

Interpretation: Return to Figures 3.27 and 3.28, and note our sign convention that Θ is zero at the rearward stagnation point and increases in the counterclockwise direction; thisis the conventional polar coordinate system where Θ increases in the counterclockwise direction, and we have followed this convention throughout the present chapter. That is, as Θ sweeps counterclockwise,Θ = 90? is the top of the cylinder, Θ = 180? is the location of the forward stagnation point, and Θ = 270? is the bottom of the cylinder. The consistent sign convention for V Θ , as seen in Figure 3.27, is positive in the direction of increasing Θ.

The direction of V∞ in Figure 3.28, however, is left to right. Hence the actual left-to-right flow over the top of the cylinder has a negative velocity because it is running counter to the positive direction of V Θ shown in Figure 3.27. The left-to-right flow over the bottom of the cylinder, however, has a positive velocity because it is running in the positive direction of V Θ . This is totally consistent with the result given for V Θ in Equation (3.100).

\(V_{\Theta }=2V_{\infty }\sin \Theta (3.100)\)

Over the top of the cylinder, where Θ varies from 0º to 180º, Equation (3.100) gives a negative value of V Θ , whereas over the bottom of the cylinder, where Θ varies from 180º to 360º , Equation (3.100) gives a positive value of V Θ .With this in mind, now consider the location and values of the local maximum and minimum acceleration as given by relations (E3.8) and (E3.9), respectively. At Θ = 45º, the acceleration is a positive value; i.e., the time rate of change V Θ is positive. However, at Θ = 45º, the velocity is a negative value, and with a positive time rate of change, the velocity is becoming less negative; i.e., the absolute value of V Θ is becoming smaller. The fluid element at Θ = 45º is slowing down.
The point Θ = 45º is therefore a point of maximum deceleration (minimum acceleration).

In contrast, at Θ = 135º the velocity is a negative value, but from (E3.9), the local acceleration is also negative. This means that at Θ = 135º, with a negative time rate of change of velocity, the velocity itself is becoming more negative, and its absolute value is increasing. At Θ = 135º, the fluid element is speeding up, and Θ = 135º is a point of maximum acceleration.

Over the bottom surface of the cylinder, VΘ is positive. At Θ = 225º (which is on the front face of the cylinder) the acceleration from (E3.9) is also positive; i.e., at Θ = 225º the fluid element is speeding up and hence Θ = 225º is a point of maximum acceleration. At Θ = 315º (which is on the back face of the cylinder) the acceleration from (E3.9) is negative; i.e., at Θ = 315º the fluid element is slowing down. Hence, Θ = 315º is a point of maximum deceleration.

In short, again examining Figure 3.28, the flowover the front face of the cylinder starts out at zero velocity at the front stagnation point (Θ = 180º), accelerates to a maximum velocity of 2V∞ at the top (Θ = 90º) and bottom (Θ = 270º) of the cylinder, with a maximum acceleration occurring at Θ = 135º and 225º. The flow then decelerates over the back face of the cylinder, coming to zero velocity at the rear stagnation point (Θ = 0), with maximum deceleration occurring at Θ = 45º and 315º.

It is interesting, and it also makes intuitive sense, that the maximum acceleration and deceleration occur geometrically at the points on the cylinder surface halfway between the stagnation points and the points of maximum velocity at the top and bottom. We might have guessed this right from the beginning of this worked example. But frequently in physical science intuition can betray us, and it is necessary to make an analysis as we have done here to find the correct result.

Note: The interpretation given above is rather long and protracted. It was carried out in order to identify which of the positive or negative values in (E3.9) corresponded to an acceleration or deceleration, and at all times we had to keep our mind focused on the sign convention for the polar coordinate system shown in Figures 3.27 and 3.28. This polar coordinate system with ? increasing in the counterclockwise direction is standard mathematical convention, and we have chosen to use it throughout this chapter.
Finally, let us calculate the actual value of maximum acceleration and deceleration for R = 1 m and V∞ = 50 m/s. From (E3.9),

\(\frac{2V_{\infty }^{2}}{R}=\frac{2(50)^{2}}{(1)}=5000m/s^{2}\)

Since the standard sea level value of the acceleration of gravity on earth is \(9.8 ms/s^{2}\), a fluid element flowing over the surface of the circular cylinder in this example experiences a mind-boggling maximum acceleration and deceleration of

\(\frac{5000}{9.8}=510.2g\)

So, a fluid element in an otherwise seemingly benign flow field can experience tremendously large accelerations.
