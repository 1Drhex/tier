Guest Question.

An \(AC\) voltage source (i.e., a sinusoidal source) \(v(t) = 325 sin\) \(100\pi t V\) delivers \(1 kW\) of average power to a load through a resistance of \(5 \Omega \). This resistance is the sum of the internal resistance of the source and the resistance of cable connecting the load to the source. Assume that the load draws a current \(i(t) = I_{m} sin(100\pi t + q)\) and that \(I_{m}\) and \(q\) can be varied keeping the power delivered by the source at \(1 kW\) itself. Find the amplitude of current, power dissipated in the series resistance and the efficiency of power transfer when \((i) q = -80^{o}\) (ii) \(q = 45^{o}\) (iii) \(q = -30^{o}\) and (iv) \(q = 0^{o}.\)

Admin
Solution

Let \(v(t) = V_{m}\) \(sin \theta t\) and \(i(t) = I_{m}\) \(sin (ωt +\theta )\). Then the average power delivered by the source \(=\frac{V_{\mathrm{m}} I_{\mathrm{m}}}{2} \cos \theta \mathrm{W}\) (We have derived this many times in earlier sections.). The average power delivered depends on magnitude of phase difference between voltage and current waveforms. But it does not depend on the sign of phase difference.

The power delivered by the source is kept constant at \(1000 W\) in this example. Therefore, \(I_{m} cos \theta\) \(= 1000/325 = 3.077 A\). Let \(P_{R}\) be the average power dissipated in the resistor. Then,

\(\begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{R}} &=\frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{0.5 T} R[i(t)]^{2} d t=\frac{\omega}{\pi} \int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{\omega}} R I_{\mathrm{m}}^{2} \sin ^{2}(\omega t+\theta) d t \\ &=\frac{\omega}{\pi} \int_{0}^{\frac{\pi}{\omega}} R I_{\mathrm{m}}^{2}\left[\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2} \cos (2 \omega t+2 \theta)\right] d t=\frac{R I_{\mathrm{m}}^{2}}{2} \text { watts } \end{aligned}\)

 

But \(I_{m} = 3.077/cos \theta\). Therefore, \(P_{R} = 4.734\) \(R/cos2\theta\). Now we evaluate the numbers for the various cases:

\(\begin{aligned} &\text { (i) } \theta=-80^{\circ} . \text { Then, } \cos \theta=0.1736, \therefore I_{\mathrm{m}}=3.077 / 0.1736=17.72 \mathrm{~A}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{R}}=4.734 \times 5 / 0.1736^{2}=\\ &785.4 \mathrm{~W}, P_{\mathrm{S}}=1000 \mathrm{~W}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{L}}=214.6 \mathrm{~W} \text { and efficiency }=21.46 \% \end{aligned}\)

 

\(\text { (ii) } \begin{array}{l} \theta=45^{\circ} . \text { Then }, \cos \theta=0.707, \therefore I_{\mathrm{m}}=3.077 / 0.707=4.35 \mathrm{~A}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{R}}=4.734 \times 5 / 0.707^{2}=47.3 \mathrm{~W}, \\ P_{\mathrm{s}}=1000 \mathrm{~W}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{I}}=952.7 \mathrm{~W} \text { and efficiency }=95.27 \% \end{array}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned} &\text { (iii) } \theta=-30^{\circ} . \text { Then, } \cos \theta=0.866, \therefore I_{\mathrm{m}}=3.077 / 0.1736=3.55 \mathrm{~A}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{R}}=4.734 \times 5 / 0.866^{2}=\\ &31.6 \mathrm{~W}, P_{\mathrm{S}}=1000 \mathrm{~W}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{L}}=968.4 \mathrm{~W} \text { and efficiency }=96.84 \% \end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned} &\text { (iv) } \theta=0^{\circ} . \text { Then, } \cos \theta=1, \therefore I_{\mathrm{m}}=3.077 \mathrm{~A}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{R}}=4.734 \times 5 / 1^{2}=23.7 \mathrm{~W}, P_{\mathrm{S}}=1000 \mathrm{~W}, \therefore P_{\mathrm{L}}=\\ &976.3 \mathrm{~W} \text { and efficiency }=97.63 \% \end{aligned}\)

 

Increasing magnitude of phase difference between voltage and current makes power transfer to the load highly inefficient
