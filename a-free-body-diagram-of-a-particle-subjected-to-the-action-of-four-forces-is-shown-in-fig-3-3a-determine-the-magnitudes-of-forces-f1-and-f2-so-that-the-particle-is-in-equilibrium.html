Guest Question.

A free-body diagram of a particle subjected to the action of four forces is shown in Fig. 3-3a. Determine the magnitudes of forces \(F_1\) and \(F_2\) so that the particle is in equilibrium.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION (using x- and y-components of equilibrium)
In this example problem the particle has already been isolated from its surroundings. Figure 3-3a is the free-body diagram, and it shows all forces acting on the particle by the external world. The particle is subjected to a system of coplanar, concurrent forces, two of which are known (\(F_3\) and \(F_4\)) and two of which are unknown (\(F_1\) and \(F_2\)). For a concurrent system of forces in a plane, the vector equation of equilibrium \(R=\sum{F}=0\) has only two independent scalar components. Writing the x-component of the equilibrium equation for the freebody diagram of Fig. 3-3a results in

\(+\rightarrow \sum{F_x=0:} \quad \quad \quad \quad F_{1x}+F_{2x}+F_{3x}+F_{4x}=0\)

 

\(F_1cos60^\circ +F_2cos30^\circ -40cos56^\circ -10cos15^\circ =0\)

 

\(0.5000F_1+0.8660F_2-22.368-9.659=0\)

from which

\(F_1+1.732F_2=64.054\) (a)

Writing the y-component of the equilibrium equation results in

\(+\uparrow \sum{F_y}=0: \quad \quad \quad \quad F_{1y}+F_{2y}+F_{3y}+F_{4y}=0\)

 

\(F_1sin60^\circ +F_2sin30^\circ -40sin56^\circ +10sin15^\circ =0\)

 

\(0.8660F_1+0.5000F_2-33.161+2.588=0\)

from which

\(F_1+0.5774F_2=35.304\) (b)

Solving Eqs. (a) and (b) simultaneously yields

\(F_1=20.9\) kip

 

\(F_2=24.9\) kip

SOLUTION (using n- and x-components of equilibrium)
Using the x- and y-components of the equilibrium equation \(R=\sum{F}=0\) resulted in a pair of equations that had to be solved simultaneously. An alternate choice of a coordinate system would be along and perpendicular to one of the unknown forces (such as the nt-coordinate system of Fig. 3-3b). Since the force \(F_2\) has no n-component, it does not enter into the equation \(\sum{F_n}= 0\), and the difficulty of solving simultaneous equations can be avoided. Writing the n-component of the equilibrium equation results in

\(+\nwarrow \sum{F_n}=0: \quad \quad \quad \quad F_{1n}+F_{2n}+F_{3n}+F_{4n}=0\)

 

\(F_1sin30^\circ +0-40sin26^\circ +10sin45^\circ =0\)

from which

\(F_1=20.93\cong 20.9\) kip

Once force \(F_1\) is known, any other component of the equilibrium equation can be used to obtain the force F2. For example, writing the x-component of the equilibrium equation results in

\(+\rightarrow \sum{F_x}=0:\quad \quad \quad \quad F_{1x}+F_{2x}+F_{3x}+F_{4x}=0\)

 

\(20.93cos60^\circ +F_2cos30^\circ -40cos56^\circ -10cos15^\circ =0\)

from which

\(F_2=24.9\) kip

Summing forces in a direction perpendicular to one of the unknown forces eliminates the need to solve simultaneous equations in two-dimensional problems.

Depending on the complexity of the problem and the complexity of the geometry, it may be more appropriate to solve the simultaneous equations.
