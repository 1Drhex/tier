Guest Question.

Electric Field Due to Two Charges

Charges \(q_{1}\) and \(q_{2}\) are located on the \(x\) axis, at distances \(a\) and \(b\), respectively, from the origin as shown in Figure 23.12.

(A) Find the components of the net electric field at the point \(P\), which is at position \((0, y)\).

(B) Evaluate the electric field at point \(P\) in the special case that \(\left|q_{1}\right|=\left|q_{2}\right|\) and \(a=b\).

(C) Find the electric field due to the electric dipole when point P is a distance y >> a from the origin.

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize Compare this example with . There, we add vector forces to find the net force on a charged particle. Here, we add electric field vectors to find the net electric field at a point in space. If a charged particle were placed at P, we could use the particle in a field model to find the electric force on the particle.

Categorize We have two source charges and wish to find the resultant electric field, so we categorize this example as one in which we can use the superposition principle represented by Equation 23.10.

\(\overrightarrow{ E }=k_{e} \sum_{i} \frac{q_{i}}{r_{i}^{2}} \hat{ r }_{i}\) (23.10)

Analyze Find the magnitude of the electric field at \(P\) due to charge \(q_{1}\) :

\(E_{1}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{r_{1}^{2}}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{a^{2}+y^{2}}\)

Find the magnitude of the electric field at \(P\) due to charge \(q_{2}\) :

\(E_{2}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{r_{2}^{2}}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{b^{2}+y^{2}}\)

Write the electric field vectors for each charge in unit-vector form:

\(\begin{aligned}&\overrightarrow{ E }_{1}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \phi \hat{ i }+k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \phi \hat{ j } \\&\overrightarrow{ E }_{2}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{b^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \theta \hat{ i }-k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{b^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \theta \hat{ j }\end{aligned}\)

Write the components of the net electric field vector:

\(\text { (1) } E_{x}=E_{1 x}+E_{2 x}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \phi+k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{b^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \theta\)

\(\text { (2) } E_{y}=E_{1 y}+E_{2 y}=k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{1}\right|}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \phi-k_{e} \frac{\left|q_{2}\right|}{b^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \theta\)

Conceptualize Figure 23.13 shows the situation in this special case. Notice the symmetry in the situation and that the charge distribution is now an electric dipole.

Categorize Because Figure 23.13 is a special case of the general case shown in Figure 23.12, we can categorize this example as one in which we can take the result of part (A) and substitute the appropriate values of the variables.

Analyze Based on the symmetry in Figure 23.13, evaluate Equations (1) and (2) from part (A) with \(a=b,\left|q_{1}\right|=\left|q_{2}\right|=q\), and \(\phi=\theta\) :

\(\text { (3) } E_{x}=k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \theta+k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \theta=2 k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \cos \theta\)

\(E_{y}=k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \theta-k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}} \sin \theta=0\)

From the geometry in Figure 23.13, evaluate \(\cos \theta\):

\(\text { (4) } \cos \theta=\frac{a}{r}=\frac{a}{\left(a^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}}\)

Substitute Equation (4) into Equation (3):

\(E_{x}=2 k_{e} \frac{q}{a^{2}+y^{2}}\left[\frac{a}{\left(a^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}}\right]=k_{e} \frac{2 a q}{\left(a^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}}\)

In the solution to part (B), because \(y \gg a\), neglect \(a^{2}\) compared with \(y^{2}\) and write the expression for \(E\) in this case:

\(\text { (5) } E \approx k_{e} \frac{2 a q}{y^{3}}\)

Finalize From Equation (5), we see that at points far from a dipole but along the perpendicular bisector of the line joining the two charges, the magnitude of the electric field created by the dipole varies as \(1 / r^{3}\), whereas the more slowly varying field of a point charge varies as \(1 / r^{2}\) (see Eq. 23.9). That is because at distant points, the fields of the two charges of equal magnitude and opposite sign almost cancel each other. The \(1 / r^{3}\) variation in \(E\) for the dipole also is obtained for a distant point along the \(x\) axis and for any general distant point.

\(\overrightarrow{ E }=k_{e} \frac{q}{r^{2}} \hat{ r }\) (23.9)
