Guest Question.

WHAT’S COOKING?

A hot copper pot of mass 2.0 kg (including its copper lid) is at a temperature of 150°C. You pour 0.10 kg of cool water at 25°C into the pot, then quickly replace the lid so no steam can escape. Find the final temperature of the pot and its contents, and determine the phase of the water (liquid, gas, or a mixture). Assume that no heat is lost to the surroundings.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

The water and the pot exchange heat. Three outcomes are possible: (1) No water boils, and the final temperature T is less than 100°C; (2) some water boils, giving a mixture of water and steam at 100°C; or (3) all the water boils, giving 0.10 kg of steam at 100°C or greater. We use Eq. (17.13) for the heat transferred in a temperature change and Eq. (17.20) for the heat transferred in a phase change.

Q = mc ∆T (17.13)

Q = ± mL (17.20)

EXECUTE:

First consider case (1), which parallels exactly. The equation that states that the heat flow into the water equals the heat flow out of the pot is

\(Q_{\mathrm{W}}+Q_{\mathrm{Cu}}=m_{\mathrm{W}} c_{\mathrm{W}}\left(T-T_{0 \mathrm{~W}}\right)+m_{\mathrm{Cu}} c_{\mathrm{Cu}}\left(T-T_{0 \mathrm{Cu}}\right)=0\)

Here we use subscripts W for water and \(C_u\) for copper, with \(m_W\) = 0.10 kg, \(m_{Cu}\) = 2.0 kg, T0W = 25°C, and \(T_{0Cu}\) = 150°C. From Table 17.3, \(c_W\) = 4190 J/kg . K and \(c_{Cu}\) = 390 J/kg . K.

Solving for the final temperature T and substituting these values, we get

\(T=\frac{m_{\mathrm{W}} c_{\mathrm{W}} T_{0 \mathrm{~W}}+m_{\mathrm{Cu}} c_{\mathrm{Cu}} T_{0 \mathrm{Cu}}}{m_{\mathrm{W}} c_{\mathrm{W}}+m_{\mathrm{Cu}} c_{\mathrm{Cu}}}=106^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\)

But this is above the boiling point of water, which contradicts our assumption that no water boils! So at least some of the water boils.
So consider case (2), in which the final temperature is T = 100°C and some unknown fraction x of the water boils, where (if this case is correct) x is greater than zero and less than or equal to 1. The (positive) amount of heat needed to vaporize this water is \(x m_{\mathrm{W}} L_{\mathrm{v}}\). The energy-conservation condition \(Q_{\mathrm{W}}+Q_{\mathrm{Cu}}=0\) then

\(m_{\mathrm{W}} c_{\mathrm{W}}\left(100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}-T_{0 \mathrm{~W}}\right)+x m_{\mathrm{W}} L_{\mathrm{v}}+m_{\mathrm{Cu}} c_{\mathrm{Cu}}\left(100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}-T_{0 \mathrm{Cu}}\right)=0\)

We solve for the target variable x:

\(x=\frac{-m_{\mathrm{Cu}} c_{\mathrm{Cu}}\left(100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}-T_{0 \mathrm{Cu}}\right)-m_{\mathrm{W}} c_{\mathrm{W}}\left(100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}-T_{0 \mathrm{~W}}\right)}{m_{\mathrm{W}} L_{\mathrm{v}}}\)

With \(L_v\) = 2.256 × \(10^6\) J from Table 17.4, this yields x = 0.034. We conclude that the final temperature of the water and copper is 100°C and that 0.034(0.10 kg) = 0.0034 kg = 3.4 g of the water is converted to steam at 100°C.
