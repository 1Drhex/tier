Guest Question.

In a lossless dielectric for which \(\eta=60\pi, \mu_{r}=1\), and \(H=-0.1\cos(\omega t-z)a_{x}+0.5\sin(\omega t-z)a_{y} A/m\), calculate \(\varepsilon_{r}, \omega\), and E.

Admin
Solution

In this case, \(\sigma=0, \alpha=0\), and \(\beta=1\), so

\(\eta =\sqrt{\mu/\varepsilon}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu_{o}}{\varepsilon_{o}}}\sqrt{\frac{\mu_{r}}{\varepsilon_{r}}}=\frac{120\pi}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_{r}}}\)

or

\(\sqrt{\varepsilon_{r}}=\frac{120\pi}{\eta}=\frac{120\pi}{60\pi}=2\rightarrow \varepsilon_{r}=4\)

\(\beta=\omega\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}=\omega\sqrt{\mu_{o}\varepsilon_{o}}\sqrt{\mu_{r}\varepsilon_{r}}=\frac{\omega}{c}\sqrt{4}=\frac{2\omega}{c}\)

or

\(\omega=\frac{\beta c}{2}=\frac{1(3\times 10^{8})}{2}=1.5\times 10^{8}rad/s\)

From the given H field, E can be calculated in two ways: by using the techniques (based on Maxwell’s equations) developed in this chapter or directly, by using Maxwell’s equations as in Chapter 9.

Method 1: To use the techniques developed in the present chapter, we let

\(H=H_{1}+H_{2}\)

where

\(H_{1}=-0.1\cos(\omega t-z)a_{x}\) and \(H_{2}=0.5\sin(\omega t-z)a_{y}\)

and the corresponding electric field

\(E=E_{1}+E_{2}\)

where

\(E_{1}=E_{1o}\cos(\omega t-z)a_{E_{1}}\) and \(E_{2}=E_{2o}\sin(\omega t-z)a_{E_{2}}\)

Notice that although H has components along \(a_{x}\) and \(a_{y}\), it has no component along the direction of propagation; it is therefore a TEM wave.

For \(E_{1}\)

\(a_{E_{1}}=-(a_{k}\times a_{H_{1}})=-(a_{z}\times -a_{x})=a_{y}\)

\(E_{1o}=\eta H_{1o}=60\pi(0.1)=6\pi\)

Hence

\(E_{1}=6\pi\cos(\omega t-z)a_{y}\)

For \(E_{2}\)

\(a_{E_{2}}=-(a_{k}\times a_{H_{2}})=-(a_{z}\times a_{y})=a_{x}\)

\(E_{2o}=\eta H_{2o}=60\pi(0.5)=30\pi\)

Hence

\(E_{2}=30\pi\sin(\omega t-z)a_{x}\)

Adding \(E_{1}\) and \(E_{2}\) gives \(E\); that is

\(E=94.25\sin(1.5\times 10^{8}t-z)a_{x}+18.85\cos(1.5\times10^{8}t-z)a_{y} V/m\)

Method 2: We may apply Maxwell’s equations directly

\(\nabla\times H=\sigma E+\varepsilon \frac{\partial E}{\partial t}\rightarrow E=\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\int\nabla\times Hdt\) (look figure↓)

because \(\sigma=0\). But

\(\nabla\times H=\begin{vmatrix} a_{x} & a_{y} & a_{z} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ H_{x}(z) & H_{y}(z) & 0 \\ \end{vmatrix}=-\frac{\partial H_{y}}{\partial z}a_{x}+\frac{\partial H_{x}}{\partial z}a_{y}\)

\(H_{2o}\cos(\omega t-z)a_{x}+H_{1o}\sin(\omega t-z)a_{y}\)

where

\(H_{1o}=-0.1\) and \(H_{2o}=0.5\)

Hence

\(E=\frac{1}{\varepsilon}\int\nabla\times Hdt=\frac{H_{2o}}{\varepsilon\omega}\sin(\omega t-z)a_{x}-\frac{H_{1o}}{\varepsilon\omega}\cos(\omega t-z)a_{y}\)

\(=94.25\sin(\omega t-z)a_{x}+18.85\cos(\omega t-z)a_{y} V/m\)

as expected.
