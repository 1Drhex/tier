Guest Question.

Orthogonally diagonalize the matrix \( A = \left [ \begin{matrix} 3 & -2 & 4 \\ -2 & 6 & 2 \\ 4 & 2 & 3 \end{matrix} \right ] \) , whose characteristic equation is

\( 0 = -\lambda^{3} + 12\lambda^{2} - 21\lambda - 98 = -(\lambda - 7)^{2}(\lambda + 2) \)

Admin
Solution

The usual calculations produce bases for the eigenspaces:

\( \lambda = 7 : v_{1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] , v_{2} = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1/2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right ] ; \lambda = -2 : v_{3} = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 \\ -1/2 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

Although \( v_{1} \) and \( v_{2} \) are linearly independent, they are not orthogonal. Recall from Section 6.2 that the projection of \( v_{2} \) onto \( v_{1} \) is \( \frac{v_{2} \cdot v_{1}}{v_{1} \cdot v_{1}}v_{1} \), and the component of \( v_{2} \) orthogonal to \( v_{1} \) is

\( z_{2} = v_{2} – \frac{v_{2} \cdot v_{1}}{v_{1} \cdot v_{1}}v_{1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1/2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right ] – \frac{-1/2}{2}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1/4 \\ 1 \\ 1/4 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

Then {\( v_{1},z_{2} \)} is an orthogonal set in the eigenspace for λ = 7. (Note that \( z_{2} \) is a linear combination of the eigenvectors \( v_{1} \) and \( v_{2} \), so \( z_{2} \) is in the eigenspace. This construction of \( z_{2} \) is just the Gram–Schmidt process of Section 6.4.) Since the eigenspace is two-dimensional (with basis \( v_{1} , v_{2} \)), the orthogonal set {\( v_{1},z_{2} \)} is an orthogonal basis for the eigenspace, by the Basis Theorem. (See Section 2.9 or 4.5.)

Normalize \( v_{1} \) and \( z_{2} \) to obtain the following orthonormal basis for the eigenspace for λ = 7:

\( u_{1} = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 0 \\ 1/\sqrt{2} \end{matrix} \right ] , u_{2} = \left [ \begin{matrix} -1/\sqrt{18} \\ 4/\sqrt{18} \\ 1/\sqrt{18} \end{matrix} \right ] \)

An orthonormal basis for the eigenspace for λ = -2 is

\( u_{3} = \frac{1}{\left\|2v_{3}\right\| }2v_{3} = \frac{1}{3}\left [ \begin{matrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 2 \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} -2/3 \\ -1/3 \\ 2/3 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

By Theorem 1, \( u_{3} \) is orthogonal to the other eigenvectors \( u_{1} \) and \( u_{2} \). Hence {\( u_{1},u_{2},u_{3} \)} is an orthonormal set. Let

\( P = \left [ \begin{matrix} u_{1} & u_{2} & u_{3} \end{matrix} \right ] = \left [ \begin{matrix} 1/\sqrt{2} & -1/\sqrt{18} & -2/3 \\ 0 & 4/\sqrt{18} & -1/3 \\ 1/\sqrt{2} & 1/\sqrt{18} & 2/3 \end{matrix} \right ] , D = \left [ \begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

Then P orthogonally diagonalizes A, and \( A = PDP^{-1} \).

THEOREM 1 :

If A is symmetric, then any two eigenvectors from different eigenspaces are orthogonal.
