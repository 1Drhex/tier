Guest Question.

Show that p² is hermitian, for hydrogen states with \( \ell=0 \) . Hint: For such states ψ is independent of θ and ϕ, so

\( p^{2}=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d}{d r}\right) \),

(Equation 4.13). Using integration by parts, show that

\( \nabla^{2}=\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} \sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\sin \theta \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta}\left(\frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial \phi^{2}}\right) \) (4.13).

\( \left\langle f \mid p^{2} g\right\rangle=-\left.4 \pi \hbar^{2}\left(r^{2} f \frac{d g}{d r}-r^{2} g \frac{d f}{d r}\right)\right|_{0} ^{\infty}+\left\langle p^{2} f \mid g\right\rangle \).

Check that the boundary term vanishes for \( \psi_{n 00} \), which goes like

\( \psi_{n 00} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}(n a)^{3 / 2}} \exp (-r / n a) \).

near the origin.

The case of \( p^{4} \) is more subtle. The Laplacian of 1/r picks up a delta function (see, for example, D. J. Griffiths, Introduction to Electrodynamics, 4th edn, Eq. 1.102). Show that

\( \nabla^{4}\left[e^{-k r}\right]=\left(-\frac{4 k^{3}}{r}+k^{4}\right) e^{-k r}+8 \pi k \delta^{3}( r ) \).

and confirm that \( p^{4} \) is hermitian.

Admin
Solution

Quoting the Laplacian in spherical coordinates (Eq. 4.13), we have, for states with no dependence on θ or Φ:

\( \nabla^{2}=\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} \sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\sin \theta \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta}\left(\frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial \phi^{2}}\right) \) (4.13).

\( p^{2}=-\hbar^{2} \nabla^{2}=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d}{d r}\right) \).

Question: Is it Hermitian?

Using integration by parts (twice), and test functions f(r) and g(r):

\( \left\langle f \mid p^{2} g\right\rangle=-\hbar^{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} f \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d g}{d r}\right) 4 \pi r^{2} d r=-4 \pi \hbar^{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} f \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d g}{d r}\right) d r \).

\( =-4 \pi \hbar^{2}\left\{\left.r^{2} f \frac{d g}{d r}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}-\int_{0}^{\infty} r^{2} \frac{d f}{d r} \frac{d g}{d r} d r\right\} \).

\( =-4 \pi \hbar^{2}\left\{\left.r^{2} f \frac{d g}{d r}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}-\left.r^{2} g \frac{d f}{d r}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}+\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d f}{d r}\right) g d r\right\} \).

The boundary term at infinity vanishes for functions f(r) and g(r) that go to zero exponentially; the boundary term at zero is killed by the factor r², as long as the functions (and their derivatives) are finite. So

\( \left\langle f \mid p^{2} g\right\rangle=\left\langle p^{2} f \mid g\right\rangle \).

and hence p² is Hermitian.

As for \( p^4\) , first note that

\( \nabla^{2}\left[e^{-k r}\right]=\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d}{d r} e^{-k r}\right)=\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d r}\left(-k r^{2} e^{-k r}\right) \).

\( =-\frac{k}{r^{2}}\left(2 r e^{-k r}-k r^{2} e^{-k r}\right)=\left(k^{2}-\frac{2 k}{r}\right) e^{-k r} \).

So

\( \nabla^{4}\left[e^{-k r}\right]=\nabla^{2}\left[\left(k^{2}-\frac{2 k}{r}\right) e^{-k r}\right]=k^{2} \nabla^{2}\left[e^{-k r}\right]-2 k \nabla^{2}\left(\frac{1}{r} e^{-k r}\right) \).

Now, for any functions f and g,

\( \nabla^{2}(f g)= \nabla \cdot \nabla (f g)= \nabla \cdot(f \nabla g+g \nabla f)=2 \nabla f \cdot \nabla g+f \nabla^{2} g+g \nabla^{2} f \).

\( \text { so (using } \left.\nabla^{2}(1 / r)=-4 \pi \delta^{3}( r )\right) \)

\( \nabla^{2}\left(\frac{1}{r} e^{-k r}\right)=2 \nabla \left(\frac{1}{r}\right) \cdot \nabla \left(e^{-k r}\right)+\frac{1}{r} \nabla^{2}\left(e^{-k r}\right)+e^{-k r} \nabla^{2}\left(\frac{1}{r}\right) \).

\( =2\left(-\frac{1}{r^{2}} \hat{ r }\right) \cdot\left(-k e^{-k r} \hat{ r }\right)+\frac{1}{r}\left[\left(k^{2}-\frac{2 k}{r}\right) e^{-k r}\right]+e^{-k r}\left[-4 \pi \delta^{3}( r )\right] \).

\( =\frac{2 k}{r^{2}} e^{-k r}+\frac{k^{2}}{r} e^{-k r}-\frac{2 k}{r^{2}} e^{-k r}-4 \pi \delta^{3}( r )=\frac{k^{2}}{r} e^{-k r}-4 \pi \delta^{3}( r ) \).

Therefore

\( \nabla^{4}\left[e^{-k r}\right]=k^{2}\left(k^{2}-\frac{2 k}{r}\right) e^{-k r}-2 k\left[\frac{k^{2}}{r} e^{-k r}-4 \pi \delta^{3}( r )\right] \).

\( =\left(k^{4}-\frac{4 k^{3}}{r}\right) e^{-k r}+8 \pi k \delta^{3}( r ) \).

Then

\( \left\langle e^{-j r} \mid \nabla^{4} e^{-k r}\right\rangle=\int e^{-j r}\left[\left(k^{4}-\frac{4 k^{3}}{r}\right) e^{-k r}+8 \pi k \delta^{3}( r )\right] d^{3} r \).

\( =4 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(k^{4}-\frac{4 k^{3}}{r}\right) e^{-(j+k) r} r^{2} d r+8 \pi k \).

\( =4 \pi k\left[k^{3} \int_{0}^{\infty} r^{2} e^{-(j+k) r} d r-4 k^{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} r e^{-(j+k) r} d r+2\right] \).

\( =4 \pi k\left[k^{3} \frac{2}{(j+k)^{3}}-4 k^{2} \frac{1}{(j+k)^{2}}+2\right] \).

\( =8 \pi k\left[\frac{k^{3}-2 k^{2}(j+k)+(j+k)^{3}}{(j+k)^{3}}\right] \).

\( =\frac{8 \pi k}{(j+k)^{3}}\left(k^{3}-2 k^{2} j-2 k^{3}+j^{3}+3 j^{2} k+3 j k^{2}+k^{3}\right) \).

\( =\frac{8 \pi j k}{(j+k)^{3}}\left(j^{2}+3 j k+k^{2}\right)=\left\langle\nabla^{4} e^{-j r} \mid e^{-k r}\right\rangle \).

(The final step follows immediately, because the penultimate expression is symmetric under j ↔ k.) Thus \( p^{4}=\left(\hbar^{4} / 4 m^{4}\right) \nabla^{4} \) is hermitian for functions of the form \( \psi_{n 00} \) [Actually, I’ve only proved it for functions of the form \( e^{-k r} \), whereas \( \psi_{n 00}=f_{n}(r) e^{-r / n a} \) (the exponential is multiplied by a polynomial of degree n – 1). But remember, the potential problems come from boundary terms at r → 0 , and higher powers of r are only going to make such terms go to zero even more rapidly. It’s \( e^{-k r} \) with no factor of r that’s going to cause trouble if anything does. So we’re safe.]
