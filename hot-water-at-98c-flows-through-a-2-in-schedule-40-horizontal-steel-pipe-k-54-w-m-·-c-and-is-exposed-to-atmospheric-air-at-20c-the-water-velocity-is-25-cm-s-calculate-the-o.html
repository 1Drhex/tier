Guest Question.

Hot water at \(98^{\circ }C\) flows through a 2-in schedule 40 horizontal steel pipe \([k = 54 W/m · ^{\circ }C]\) and is exposed to atmospheric air at \(20^{\circ }C\). The water velocity is 25 cm/s. Calculate the overall heat-transfer coefficient for this situation, based on the outer area of pipe.

Admin
Solution

From Appendix A the dimensions of 2-in schedule 40 pipe are

ID = 2.067 in = 0.0525 m
OD = 2.375 in = 0.06033 m

The heat-transfer coefficient for the water flow on the inside of the pipe is determined from the flow conditions with properties evaluated at the bulk temperature. The free-convection heat-transfer coefficient on the outside of the pipe depends on the temperature difference between the surface and ambient air. This temperature difference depends on the overall energy balance. First, we evaluate \(h_{i}\) and then formulate an iterative procedure to determine \(h_{o}\).
The properties of water at \(98^{\circ }C\) are

\(\rho = 960 kg/m^{3} \mu = 2.82 × 10^{−4 } kg/m ·s\)
\(k = 0.68 W/m ·^{\circ}C Pr = 1.76\)

The Reynolds number is

\(Re = \frac{\rho ud}{\mu} =\frac{(960)(0.25)(0.0525)}{2.82 × 10^{−4}}= 44,680\) [a]

and since turbulent flow is encountered, we may use Equation \( Nu_{d}=0.023Re_{d}^{0.8}Pr^{n}\):

\(Nu=0.023Re^{0.8}Pr^{0.4}\)

 

\(= (0.023)(44,680) ^{0.8}(1.76)^{0.4} = 151.4\)

 

\( h_{i} = Nu \frac{k}{ d} =\frac{ (151.4)(0.68)}{ 0.0525} = 1961 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C [345 Btu/h · ft^{2} · ^{\circ}F] \) [b]

For unit length of the pipe the thermal resistance of the steel is

\(R_{s} = \frac{ln(r_{o}/r_{i})}{ 2πk} = \frac{ln(0.06033/0.0525)}{2π(54)} = 4.097 × 10^{−4}\) [c]

Again, on a unit-length basis the thermal resistance on the inside is

\( R_{i} =\frac{ 1}{ h_{i}A_{i}} = \frac{1}{ h_{i}2πr_{i}} = \frac{1} {(1961)π(0.0525)} = 3.092 × 10^{−3}\) [d]

The thermal resistance for the outer surface is as yet unknown but is written, for unit lengths,

\( R_{o} = \frac{1}{ h_{o}A_{o}} = \frac{1}{ h_{o}2πr_{o}}\) [e]

From Table, for laminar flow, the simplified relation for \(h_{o}\) is

\( h_{o} = 1.32 \left(\frac{\Delta T }{d} \right)^{ 1/4} = 1.32\left( \frac{T_{o}− T_{∞}}{d}\right)^{ 1/4} \) [f]

where \(T_{o}\) is the unknown outside pipe surface temperature. We designate the inner pipe surface as \(T_{i}\) and the water temperature as \(T_{w}\); then the energy balance requires

\( \frac{T_{w} − T_{i}}{ R_{i}} = \frac{T_{i} − T_{o}}{ R_{s}} = \frac{T_{o} − T_{∞}} {R_{o}} \) [g]

Combining Equations (e) and ( f ) gives

\(\frac{T_{o} − T_{∞}}{ R_{o}} = 2πr_{o} \frac{1.32}{ d^{1/4}} (T_{o} − T_{∞}) ^{5/4} \) [h]

This relation may be introduced into Equation (g) to yield two equations with the two unknowns \(T_{i}\) and \(T_{o}\):

\(\frac{98 − T_{i}}{ 3.092 × 10^{−3}} = \frac{T_{i} − T_{o}}{ 4.097 × 10^{−4}}\)

 

\( \frac{T_{i} − T_{o}}{ 4.097 × 10^{−4}} = \frac{(π)(0.06033)(1.32)(T_{o} − 20)^{5/4}} {(0.06033)^{1/4}} \)

This is a nonlinear set that may be solved by iteration to give

\( T_{o} = 97.6^{\circ}C T_{i} = 97.65^{\circ}C\)

As a result, the outside heat-transfer coefficient and thermal resistance are

\(h_{o} =\frac{ (1.32)(97.6 − 20)^{1/4}} {(0.06033)^{1/4}} = 7.91 W/m^{2} ·^{\circ} C [1.39 Btu/h · ft^{2} · ^{\circ}F]\)

 

\(R_{o} = \frac{1}{ (0.06033)(7.91)π} = 0.667\)

The calculation clearly illustrates the fact that the free convection controls the overall heat-transfer because \(R_{o}\) is much larger than \(R_{i}\) or \(R_{s}\). The overall heat-transfer coefficient based on the outer area is written in terms of these resistances as

\(U_{o} =\frac{ 1}{A_{o}(R_{i} + R_{s} + R_{o})}\) [i]

With numerical values inserted,

\(U_{o} =\frac{ 1}{π(0.06033)(3.092 × 10^{−3}+ 4.097 × 10^{−4} + 0.667)} \)

 

\(= 7.87 W/Area · ^{\circ}C\)

In this calculation we used the outside area for a 1.0-m length as

\(A_{o} = π(0.06033) = 0.1895 m^{2}/m\)
\(U_{o} = 7.87 W/m^{2} · ^{\circ}C\)

Thus, we find that the overall heat-transfer coefficient is almost completely controlled by the value of \(h_{o}\). We might have expected this result strictly on the basis of our experience with the relative magnitude of convection coefficients; free-convection values for air are very low compared with forced convection with liquids.
