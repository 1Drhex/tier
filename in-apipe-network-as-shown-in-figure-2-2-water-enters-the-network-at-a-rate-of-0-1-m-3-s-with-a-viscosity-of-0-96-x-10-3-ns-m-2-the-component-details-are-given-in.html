Guest Question.

In apipe network as shown in Figure 2.2, water enters the network at a rate of \( 0.1 m ^{3} / s\) with a viscosity of \( 0.96 \times 10^{-3} Ns / m ^{2} . \) The component details are given in Table 2. 1 . Determine the pressure and flow distributions.

Table 2.1 Details of pipe network
Component number Diameter, cm Length, m
1 2.5.0 30.0
2 2.00 20.0
3 2.00 25.0
4 1.25 20.0

 

 

Admin
Solution

On substitution of the various values, we get the following resistances from Equation (2.16)

\(R_{k}=\frac{\Delta p}{Q}=\frac{128 L \mu}{\pi D^{4}}\) :

\(\begin{array}{l}R_{1}=0.3 \times 10^{7} \\R_{2}=0.5 \times 10^{7} \\R_{3}=0.6 \times 10^{7} \\R_{4}=3.2 \times 10^{7}\end{array}\left(\frac{N}{M^{2}} \quad \frac{S}{M^{3}}\right)\)

Now Equation (2.24)

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc}{\left[\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}\right]} & -\frac{1}{R_{2}} & -\frac{1}{R_{1}} \\-\frac{1}{R_{2}} & {\left[\frac{1}{R_{2}}+\frac{1}{R_{3}}+\frac{1}{R_{4}}\right]} & -\left[\frac{1}{R_{3}}+\frac{1}{R_{4}}\right] \\-\frac{1}{R_{1}} & -\left[\frac{1}{R_{3}}+\frac{1}{R_{4}}\right] &{\left[\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{3}}+\frac{1}{R_{4}}\right]}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{1} \\p_{2} \\p_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\) \(\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{1}+q_{2} \\-q_{2}+q_{3}+q_{4} \\-q_{1}-q_{3}-q_{4}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}Q \\0 \\-Q\end{array}\right\}\)

can be formulated as

\(10^{-7}\left[\begin{array}{rrr}5.33 & -2.00 & -3.33 \\-2.00 & 3.98 & -1.98 \\-3.33 & -1.98 & 5.31\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{r}p_{1} \\p_{2} \\p_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{r}0.1 \\0.0 \\-0.1\end{array}\right\}\) (2.25)

The reduction of the above simultaneous system of equations with \( p_{3}=0.0\) (assumed as reference atmospheric pressure) results in

\(10^{-7}\left[\begin{array}{rr}5.33 & -2.00 \\-2.00 & 3.98\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{1} \\p_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0.1 \\0.0\end{array}\right\}\) (2.26)

The solution to the above set of simultaneous equations (see Appendix A) gives the relative pressure (actual minus atmospheric pressure) as

\(\begin{array}{l}p_{1}=0.236 \times 10^{6} N / m ^{2} \\p_{2}=0.110 \times 10^{6} N / m ^{2}\end{array}\) \(\text { From Equations }(2.19),(2.20),(2.21) \text { and }(2.22) \) \(\frac{1}{R_{1}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{1} \\p_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{1} \\-q_{1}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{2}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{1} \\p_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{2} \\-q_{2}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{3}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{2} \\p_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{3} \\-q_{3}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(\frac{1}{R_{4}}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}p_{2} \\p_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}q_{4} \\-q_{4}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(\text { we can calculate the flow quantities as }\) \(\begin{aligned}q_{1} &=\frac{p_{1}-p_{2}}{R_{1}}=0.042 m^{3} / s \\q_{2} &=\frac{p_{1}-p_{3}}{R_{2}}=0.0472 m^{3} / s \\q_{3} &=\frac{p_{2}-p_{3}}{R_{3}}=0.0183 m^{3} / s \\q_{4} &=\frac{p_{2}-p_{3}}{R_{4}}=0.0034 m^{3} / s (2.27)\end{aligned}\)

It is possible to take into account the entrance loss, exit loss, bend loss etc. in the calculation of nodal pressures and flows in each circuit. If the fluid flow in the network is turbulent, it is still possible to define an element but the element equations are no longer linear as can be seen from an empirical relation governing fully developed turbulent pipe flow (Darcy-Weisbach formula (Shames 1982 ) :

\(p_{1}-p_{2}=\frac{8 f L Q^{2} \rho}{\pi^{2} D^{5}}\) (2.28)

where f is the Moody friction factor, which is a function of the Reynolds number and the pipe roughness. The fluidity matrix would contain known functions of the flow rate Q instead of constants. Hence, the problem becomes nonlinear.
