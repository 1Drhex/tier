Guest Question.

A 10 × 6 venturi meter is used in a flow line that conveys air at 5 kg/s. The line pressure is 150 kPa (gage pressure), and the air temperature is 25°C. (a) Determine the expected pressure reading if a gage were attached to the throat. (b) Determine the expected reading if we erroneously neglected compressibility effects. Take atmospheric pressure to be 101.3 kPa.

Admin
Solution

From the appendix tables we read
air R = 286.8 J/(kg·K) \(µ = 18 \times 10^{-6} N·s/m^2\)
\(C_p = 1 005 J/(kg·K)\) γ = 1.4 [App. Table C.1]
10-nom sch 40 D = 25.46 cm \(A = 509.1 cm^2\)
6-nom sch 40 D = 15.41 cm \(A = 186.5 cm^2 \)[App. Table D.1]
The actual mass flow rate through the meter is given by
\(\dot{m}_{ac} = C_vYρ_1A_2 \sqrt{\frac{ 2g_c(p_1 – p_2)}{ρ_1(1 – D_2^4/D_1^4)}}\)
We now evaluate each term. The actual flow rate is 5 kg/s. For an ideal gas,
\(ρ_1 = \frac{p_1}{RT_1} =\frac{ 150 000 + 101 300}{286.8(273 + 25) }= 2.94 kg/m^3\)
The average velocity in the pipeline is found as
\(V_1 = \frac{\dot{m}_{ac}}{ρ_1A_1} = \frac{5}{2.94(509.1 \times 10^{-4}) }= 33.4\) m/s
The Reynolds number then is
\(Re = \frac{ρ_1V_1D_1}{µg_c} =\frac{ 2.94(33.4)(0.254 6)}{18 \times 10^{-6}} = 1.39 \times 10^6\)
The flow is thus highly turbulent, and so the venturi discharge coefficient is \(C_v = 0.984\). The diameter ratio is
\(\frac{D_2}{D_1} =\frac{ 15.41}{25.46} = 0.605\)
Substituting into the mass flow equation gives
\(5 = 0.984Y(2.94)(186.5 \times 10^{-4}) \sqrt{\frac{2(150 000 + 101 300 – p_2)}{2.94(1 – 0.605^4)}}\)
Rearranging and solving for the pressure at the throat, we get
\(p_2 = 251 300 –\frac{1.093 \times 10^4}{Y^2}\) (i)
The compressibility factor Y is unknown and, because it depends on \(p_2\), an iterative solution method is required. We first assume a value for \(p_2\), then calculate the pressure ratio \(p_2/p_1\), and Y from Equation 5.35. Next, we substitute into Equation i of this example and find a new value of \(p_2\). The process is repeated until the solution converges. Substituting into Equation 5.35 gives
\(Y = \sqrt{ \frac{1.4}{1.4 – 1}\frac{[(p_2/p_1)^{2/1.4} – (p_2/p_1)^{(1.4 + 1)/1.4}](1 – 0.605^4)}{[1 – (0.605^4)(p_2/p_1)^{2/1.4}](1 – p_2/p_1)}}\) (5.35)
The results are

\(p_2\) \(p_2/p_1\) Y(Eq 5.35) \(p_2\)(Eq i)
160000 0.696 0.754 232115
232115 0.923 0.950 239198
239198 0.952 0.969 239653
239653 0.954 0.970 239682
239682 0.954 0.970 239683

Thus,
\(p_2 = 239 700 kPa\) (absolute)
or \(p_2 = 239 700 – 101 300\)
(a) \(p_2 = 138 400\) Pa (gage)
If we neglect compressibility, then Y = 1, and from Equation i,
\(p_2 = 240 400\) kPa (absolute)
or \(p_2 = 240 400 – 101 300\)
(b) \(p_2 = 139 100\) Pa (gage)
