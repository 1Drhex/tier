Guest Question.

Transform the basis \(\mathbf{v}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{l}1 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\) to an orthonormal (perpendicular unit) basis (axes) for \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\) with respect to the dot product. Also plot this orthonormal basis and the given basis on two different graphs.

Admin
Solution

Using the above Gram-Schmidt process (4.16) means that we need to find vectors \(\mathbf{p}_{1}\) and \(\mathbf{p}_{2}\) which are orthogonal (perpendicular) to each other.

 

We have \(\mathbf{p}_{1}=\mathbf{v}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) which is one of the given vectors. By the above Gram-Schmidt process (4.16) we have

\(\mathbf{p}_{2}=\mathbf{v}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}\) (*)

We need to evaluate each component of \((*)\). What is the inner product \(\left\langle\mathrm{v}_{2} \mid, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle\) equal to?

\( \left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle=\mathbf{v}_{2} \cdot \mathbf{p}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l} 2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right)=(1 \times 2)+(1 \times 1)=3 \)

What is \(\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}\) equal to?

\(\begin{aligned}\left\|\mathrm{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}=\left\langle\mathbf{p}_{1}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle &=\mathbf{p}_{1} \cdot \mathbf{p}_{1} \\ & =\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right) \cdot\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right)=2^{2}+1^{2}=5 \quad \text { [Pythagoras] }\end{aligned}\)

Substituting all these, \(\mathbf{v}_{2}=\left(\begin{array}{l}1 \\ 1\end{array}\right),\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle=3,\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}=5\) and \(\mathbf{p}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\), into \(\left({ }^{*}\right)\) gives

\( \begin{aligned} \mathbf{p}_{2} &=\mathbf{v}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1} \\ &=\left(\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right)-\frac{3}{5}\left(\begin{array}{l} 2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} \)

 

\( =\left(\begin{array}{l} 1-6 / 5 \\ 1-3 / 5 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 / 5 \\ 2 / 5 \end{array}\right)=\frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \end{array}\right) \)

Our vectors \(\mathbf{p}_{1}=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathrm{p}_{2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\ 2\end{array}\right)\) are an orthogonal (perpendicular) basis.

But how do we make these into an orthonormal (perpendicular unit) basis?

By normalizing these vectors (converting into unit vectors), which is achieved by dividing the vectors by their norms (lengths). Call these new unit vectors \(\mathbf{u}_{1}\) and \(\mathbf{u}_{2}\), and they are

\(\mathbf{u}_{1}=\frac{\mathbf{p}_{1}}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|} \quad \text { and } \quad \mathbf{u}_{2}=\frac{\mathbf{p}_{2}}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|}\)

From above \(\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}=5\) and taking square root gives \(\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|=\sqrt{5}\). We need to normalize the other perpendicular vector \(\mathbf{p}_{2}\), which means we need to convert it to a unit vector:

\(\begin{aligned}\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}=\left\langle\mathbf{p}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle &=\frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\2\end{array}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\2\end{array}\right) \\&=\frac{1}{25}\left[(-1)^{2}+2^{2}\right]=\frac{5}{25}=\frac{1}{5}\end{aligned}\)

Thus \(\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\). Our normalized (unit) vectors are

\(\mathbf{u}_{1}=\frac{\mathbf{p}_{1}}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|}=\frac{1}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|} \mathbf{p}_{1}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1\end{array}\right)\) \(\mathbf{u}_{2}=\frac{1}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|} \mathbf{p}_{2}=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}} \frac{1}{5}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\2\end{array}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\2\end{array}\right) \quad\left[\text { By Law of Indices } \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} 5=\sqrt{5}\right]\)

Our orthonormal (perpendicular unit) basis is the set \(\left\{\mathbf{u}_{1}, \mathbf{u}_{2}\right\}\) where these are given above:

\(\mathbf{u}_{1}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{l}2 \\1\end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad \mathbf{u}_{2}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\2\end{array}\right)\) \(\text { Plotting these orthonormal (perpendicular unit) basis and our given basis in } \mathbb{R}^{2} \text { (Fig. 4.18): }\)

process (4.16):

\(\begin{aligned} &\text { Let } \mathbf{p}_{1}=\mathbf{v}_{1} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{2}=\mathbf{v}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{2}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2} \mathbf{p}_{1}} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{3}=\mathbf{v}_{3}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{3}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{3}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2} \\ &\mathbf{p}_{4}=\mathbf{v}_{4}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left(\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{4}, \mathbf{p}_{3}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{3}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{3} \\ &\vdots \quad \vdots \quad \vdots \quad \vdots \\ &\mathbf{p}_{n}=\mathbf{v}_{n}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{1}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{2}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{2}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{2}-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{3}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{3}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{3}-\cdots-\frac{\left\langle\mathbf{v}_{n}, \mathbf{p}_{n-1}\right\rangle}{\left\|\mathbf{p}_{n-1}\right\|^{2}} \mathbf{p}_{n-1} \end{aligned}\)
