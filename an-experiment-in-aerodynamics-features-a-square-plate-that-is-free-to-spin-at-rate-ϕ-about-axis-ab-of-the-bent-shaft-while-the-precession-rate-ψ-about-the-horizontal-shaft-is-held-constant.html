Guest Question.

An experiment in aerodynamics features a square plate that is free to spin at rate \(\dot{\phi}\) about axis \(A B\) of the bent shaft, while the precession rate \(\dot{\psi}\) about the horizontal shaft is held constant by application of a torque \(\Gamma\) about the shaft. The angles are defined such that \(\psi=\phi=0\) when the plate coincides with the vertical plane. This system is situated in a wind tunnel whose flow is horizontal. The resultant of the aerodynamic pressure is a known force \(F(t)\) acting at the center of pressure \(P\) and always normal to the plane of the plate. Derive the differential equation of motion for \(\phi\). Include the effect of gravity in the derivation.

Admin
Solution

This example addresses situations in which a nonaxisymmetric body executes a spatial rotation. Aligning the body-fixed coordinate system to match the tabulated inertia properties fully specifies each axis, so we are not free to align the axes to simplify the kinematical analysis. Rather, we need to use rotation transformations. To construct the free-body diagram we recognize that the bent shaft applies a distributed force to the plate along edge AB. The resultant of this is arbitrary, except that it cannot exert a torque about that edge because the plate rotates freely. Thus we represent the reaction exerted by the shaft on the plate as a force–couple system at corner A, in which the couple has no component parallel to edge AB. In addition to the aerodynamic force, the free-body diagram shows the gravity force acting vertically downward.

In the free-body diagram the origin of body-fixed \(x y z\) coordinate system is placed at bearing \(A\). This point has a fixed position relative to the plate, and it is stationary, which means that the plate executes a pure rotation about point \(A\). The axes of \(x y z\) are aligned with the edges of the plate in order to use the tabulated inertia properties. The \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) coordinate systems depicted in the free-body diagram is introduced to aid in the description of the gravity force and the angular motion. The sole rotation it undergoes is the precession about the horizontal \(x^{\prime}\) axis. We define \(\psi=0\) to correspond to the \(z^{\prime}\) axis being vertical.

The transformation from \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) to \(x y z\) may be pictured as a pair of body-fixed rotations: \(\theta\) about the \(y^{\prime}\) axis, followed by \(-\phi\) about the \(x\) axis. The associated simple rotation transformations are

\(\begin{array}{l}{\left[R_{\theta}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \theta & 0 & -\sin \theta \\\\0 & 1 & 0 \\\\\sin \theta & 0 & \cos \theta\end{array}\right],} \\\\{\left[R_{\phi}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1 & 0 & 0 \\\\0 & \cos \phi & -\sin \phi \\\\0 & \sin \phi & \cos \phi\end{array}\right],}\end{array} \)

so the transformation of the unit vectors is

\(\left[\begin{array}{lll}\bar{i} & \bar{j} & \bar{k}\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}=[R]\left[\begin{array}{lll}\bar{i}^{\prime} & \bar{j}^{\prime} & \bar{k}^{\prime}\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}, \)

 

\([R]=\left[R_{\phi}\right]\left[R_{\theta}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{lll}\cos \theta & 0 & -\sin \theta \\\\-\sin \phi \sin \theta & \cos \phi & -\sin \phi \cos \theta \\\\\cos \phi \sin \theta & \sin \phi & \cos \phi \cos \theta\end{array}\right]. \)

We may now form the angular velocity and angular acceleration. Adding the precessional motion about the horizontal \(x^{\prime}\) axis and the spin about the \(x\) axis leads to

\(\bar{\omega}=-\dot{\psi} \bar{i}^{\prime}-\dot{\phi} \bar{i}.\)

The precession rate is specified to be constant and it is about a fixed axis, so the angular acceleration of the plate is

\(\bar{\alpha}=-\ddot{\phi} \bar{i}-\dot{\phi}(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{i}).\)

We need the components of \(\bar{\omega}\) and \(\bar{\alpha}\) relative to \(x y z\), and we need to express \(\bar{i}\) in terms of its \(x y z\) components. The transformation from \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) to \(x y z\) is described by \([R]\), so we extract the components of \(\bar{i}^{\prime}\) from the first row of \([R]^{\mathrm{T}}\), with the result that

\(\bar{i}^{\prime}=\cos \theta \bar{i}-\sin \phi \sin \theta \bar{j}+\cos \phi \sin \theta \bar{k},\)

which leads to

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{\omega}=(-\dot{\psi} \cos \theta-\dot{\phi}) \bar{i}+\dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta \bar{j}-\dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta \bar{k}, \\\\\bar{\omega}^{\prime}=-\dot{\psi} \cos \theta \bar{i}+\dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta \bar{j}-\dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta \bar{k}, \\\\\bar{\alpha}=-\ddot{\phi} \bar{i}+\dot{\phi} \dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta \bar{j}+\dot{\phi} \dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta \bar{k}.\end{array} \)

We find the inertia properties for the square plate by setting to zero the dimension along the \(y\) edge of the rectangular parallelepiped in the Appendix, and then invoking the parallel axis transformation. The result is

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{x x}=\frac{1}{3} m \ell^{2}, \quad I_{y y}=m\left(\frac{2}{3} \ell^{2}+\ell b+b^{2}\right), \quad I_{z z}=m\left(\frac{1}{3} \ell^{2}+\ell b+b^{2}\right) \\\\I_{x y}=I_{y z}=0, \quad I_{x z}=m\left(\frac{1}{4} \ell^{2}+\frac{1}{2} \ell b\right)\end{array} \)

We combine these properties and the components of \(\bar{\omega}\) and \(\bar{\alpha}\) to form \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t .\) For this we must use the the full equations (6.1.3) because some products of inertia are nonzero. Thus the angular momentum is

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{H}_{A}=&\left(I_{x x} \omega_{x}-I_{x y} \omega_{y}-I_{x z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \omega_{y}-I_{y x} \omega_{x}-I_{y z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(I_{z z} \omega_{z}-I_{z x} \omega_{x}-I_{z y} \omega_{y}\right) \bar{k}, \\\\\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}=&\left(I_{x x} \alpha_{x}-I_{x y} \alpha_{y}-I_{x z} \alpha_{z}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \alpha_{y}-I_{y x} \alpha_{x}-I_{y z} \alpha_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(I_{z z} \alpha_{z}-I_{z x} \alpha_{x}-I_{z y} \alpha_{y}\right) \bar{k} .\end{aligned}\) (6.1.3)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{H}_{A}=&\left(I_{x x} \omega_{x}-I_{x z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{i}+I_{y y} \omega_{y} \bar{j}+\left(I_{z z} \omega_{z}-I_{z x} \omega_{x}\right) \bar{k} \\\\=&\left[I_{x x}(-\dot{\psi} \cos \theta-\dot{\phi})-I_{x z}(-\dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta)\right] \bar{i}+I_{y y} \dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta \bar{j} \\\\&+\left[I_{z z}(-\dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta)-I_{x z}(-\dot{\psi} \cos \theta-\dot{\phi})\right] \bar{k}, \\\\\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}=&\left(I_{x x} \alpha_{x}-I_{x z} \alpha_{z}\right) \bar{i}+I_{y y} \alpha_{y} \bar{j}+\left(I_{z z} \alpha_{z}-I_{z x} \alpha_{x}\right) \bar{k} \\\\=&\left[I_{x x}(-\ddot{\phi})-I_{x z}(\dot{\phi} \dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta)\right] \bar{i}+I_{y y} \dot{\phi} \dot{\psi} \cos \phi \sin \theta \bar{j} \\\\&+\left[I_{z z}(\dot{\phi} \dot{\psi} \sin \phi \sin \theta)-I_{x z}(-\ddot{\phi})\right] \bar{k}.\end{aligned}\)

The algebraic operations required to form \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t\) according to the second of Eqs. (6.1.1) are somewhat tedious, but symbolic mathematical software lessens the difficulty. The result is

\(\begin{array}{c}\Sigma \bar{F}=m \bar{a}_{G} \\\Sigma \bar{M}_{A}=\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{H}_{A}.\end{array}\) (6.1.1)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{H}_{A}=&\left\{-I_{x x} \ddot{\phi}+\left[\left(I_{y y}-I_{z z}\right) \sin \phi \cos \phi(\sin \phi)^{2}+I_{x z} \sin \phi \sin \theta \cos \theta\right] \dot{\psi}^{2}\right\} \bar{i} \\\\&+\left\{\left[\left(I_{x x}-I_{y y}\right) \cos \phi \sin \theta \cos \theta+I_{x z}\left((\cos \theta)^{2}-(\cos \phi)^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}\right)\right] \psi^{2}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left[\left(I_{x x}+I_{y y}-I_{z z}\right) \cos \phi \sin \theta+2 I_{x z} \cos \theta\right] \psi \dot{\phi}-I_{x z} \dot{\phi}^{2}\right\} \bar{j} \\\\&+\left\{I_{x z} \ddot{\phi}+\left[\left(I_{x x}-I_{y y}\right) \sin \phi \sin \theta \cos \theta-I_{x z} \sin \phi \cos \phi(\sin \theta)^{2}\right] \psi^{2}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left[\left(I_{x x}-I_{y y}+I_{z z}\right) \sin \phi \sin \theta\right] \psi \dot{\phi}\right\} \bar{k}.\end{aligned} \)

We now are ready to form the equations of motion. We seek an equation of motion for \(\phi\) that does not contain unknown reactions. The force equations of motion will contain \(A_{x}, A_{y}\), and \(A_{z}\), so we will gain nothing by actually forming those equations. We also can see that the unknown couple reactions \(M_{y}\) and \(M_{z}\) would appear in the equations governing the moment sums about the \(y\) and \(z\) axes. In contrast, none of the reactions exert a moment about the \(x\) axis, so we focus solely on that term. Two forces exert a moment about that axis: the aerodynamic force \(F\), whose lever arm about the \(x\) axis is \(d\), and gravity, which acts in the (fixed) vertical direction. Rather than trying to visualize the moment of gravity about the \(x\) axis, we proceed formally by using a cross product. The angle between the vertical and the \(z^{\prime}\) axis is \(\psi\), so

\(m \bar{g}=m g\left(\sin \psi \bar{j}^{\prime}-\cos \psi \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)\).

The position of the center of mass is readily described in terms of components relative to \(x y z\) :

\(\bar{r}_{G / A}=\left(\frac{\ell}{2}+b\right) \bar{i}-\frac{\ell}{2} \bar{k} .\)

To evaluate the cross product of these vectors, both need to be described in terms of components relative to the same coordinate system. We select \(x y z\) for this purpose, because those are the axes for the moment sums. The transformation from \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) to \(x y z\) is described by \([R]\), so we have

\(\{m g\}=m g[R]\left\{\begin{array}{c}0 \\\\-\sin \psi \\\\\cos \psi\end{array}\right\}=m g\left\{\begin{array}{c}\sin \theta \cos \psi \\\\\cos \phi \sin \psi+\sin \phi \cos \theta \cos \psi \\\\\sin \phi \sin \psi-\cos \phi \cos \theta \cos \psi\end{array}\right\}. \)

The gravitational moment is \(\bar{r}_{G / A} \times m \bar{g} .\) We require only the \(x\) component of this moment. We add that result to the moment of \(\bar{F}\) about the \(x\) axis and equate that result to the \(x\) component of \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t\) obtained previously. This gives

\(\begin{aligned}\Sigma \bar{M}_{A} \cdot \bar{i} &=-F d+m g \frac{\ell}{2}(\cos \phi \sin \psi+\sin \phi \cos \theta \cos \psi)=\bar{H}_{A} \cdot \bar{i} \\\\&=-I_{x x} \ddot{\phi}+\left[\left(I_{y y}-I_{z z}\right) \sin \phi \cos \phi(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{x z} \sin \phi \sin \theta \cos \theta\right] \dot{\psi}^{2}.\end{aligned} \)

Substituting the values of the inertia properties yields

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{3} \ddot{\phi} &-\left[\frac{1}{3} \cos \phi \sin \theta+\left(\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{2} \frac{b}{\ell}\right) \cos \theta\right](\sin \phi \sin \theta) \dot{\psi}^{2} \\&+\frac{g}{2 \ell}(\cos \phi \sin \psi+\sin \phi \cos \theta \cos \psi)=\frac{F d}{m \ell}.\end{aligned} \)

It was stated that \(\dot{\psi}\) is constant, so we may set \(\psi=\dot{\psi} t\). The value of \(\theta\) presumably is known, so the preceding is the differential equation of motion governing \(\phi\). It is nonlinear, and its coefficients depend on time as a consequence of the known variation of \(\psi\). The solution of this differential equation satisfying specified initial conditions could be obtained numerically if all parameter values were provided. The case in which the shaft is straight, \(\theta=0\), provides a check for this expression. The equation of motion then reduces to \(\ddot{\phi} / 3+(g / 2 \ell) \sin \phi=F d / m \ell\). This is the equation of a pendulum subjected to an external moment, which is what the system reduces to when \(\theta=0\), because precession of the shaft becomes irrelevant.
