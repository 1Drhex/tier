Guest Question.

Optimum reactor size
Production of a secondary metabolite has been characterized using a small-batch reactor and the kinetic data is shown in Fig. E14.1.1. You are to design a reactor to produce 2 mg/year of the product P. Batch production mode is chosen based on the approved drug production method. Identical reaction conditions (same seed culture concentrations, pH, and temperature) will be used as those for generating the data in Fig. E14.1.1. Experience is that for each batch of operations, there are 6 h additional time needed for reactor loading, preparation, unloading after reaction, and reactor cleaning. Determine the minimum reactor size required for this production.

Admin
Solution

The kinetic data have not been treated to obtain mathematical expressions. This is a perfect example to use the Reactor Pinch Graph method as outlined in Chapter 4. Minimizing the reactor size is equivalent to maximize the productivity of P. The productivity of P is given by

\(P_{P}=\frac{P-P_{0}}{t_{B}}\) (E14.1.1)

where P is the product concentration at a total batch time \(t_{ B }\) (the sum of reaction time t and shifting time \(t_{ s }\)), while \(P_{0}\) is the product concentration in the feed (or initial stream). To maximize \(P_{ P }\), we set

\(0=\left.\frac{d P_{P}}{d t_{B}}\right|_{t_{B m i n}}=\frac{t_{B} \frac{d P}{d t_{B}}-\left(P-P_{0}\right)}{t_{B}^{2}}\) (E14.1.2)

Eq. (E14.1.2) can be reduced to

\(\left.\frac{d P}{d t_{B}}\right|_{t_{B min }}=\left.\frac{P-P_{0}}{t_{B}}\right|_{t_{B min }}\) (E14.1.3)

Eq. (E14.1.3) implies that the production rate (based on overall time required by the process) at the time of discharge (each batch) and the average production rate (based on total-batch time required by the process) are identical when the production rate is maximized (or process time at minimum). In other words, the line connects (t = \(t_{ B }-t_{ s }=-t_{ s }, P=P_{0}\)) and the optimum point (t, P) on the product generation curve, and the tangential line (in terms of \(t_{ B }\) and P) passing the optimum point (t, P) on the product generation curve have the same slope. Therefore, one can determine the optimum point by plot the pinch line to the product generation curve passing the “feed” or initial point (t = − \(t_{ s }, P=P_{0}\)).

The initial point I has a total batch time of zero and production concentration of zero. The total batch time zero corresponding to the growth time of − \(t_{ s }\), here \(t_{ s }\) is the shifting time. Therefore in Fig. E14.1.1 provided, the initial point is (t = − 6 hours, P = 0). We plot the pinch line passing point I (t = − 6 hours, P = 0), the pinch point is then the optimum point for the fermentation discharge. The graphical solution is illustrated in Fig. E14.1.2. Therefore, we can read out the fermentation time and the concentrations obtained from a batch operation:

\(P_{\max }=0.562 g / L , t_{ f }=16.66 h (t=22.66 h ), \text { and } X_{\max }=8 g / L \text { . }\)

The production rate is given by

\(F_{P}=\frac{P V}{t}\) (E14.1.4)

Since \(F_{P}\) = 2 mg/year, we have

\(V=\frac{F_{P} t}{P}=\frac{2 \times 10^{6}}{365 \times 24} \times \frac{22.66}{0.562} L=9205.54 L\)
