Guest Question.

A rock-bed thermal-energy storage unit is employed to remove energy from a hot airstream and store for later use. The schematic for the device is shown in Figure. The surface is covered with a material having an overall R value of \(2 h · ^{\circ}F · ft^{2}/Btu\). The inlet flow area is \(5 × 5 = 25 ft^{2}\), and the rock-bed length is 10 ft. Properties of the rock are

\(\rho _{r} = 80 lb_{m}/ft^{3} [1281.4 kg/m^{3}]\)
\(c_{r} = 0.21 Btu/lb_{m} · ^{\circ}F [0.880 kJ/kg · ^{\circ}C]\)
\(k_{r} = 0.5 Btu/h · ft · ^{\circ} F [0.87 W/m · ^{\circ}C]\)

As the air flows through the rock, it is in such intimate contact with the rock that the air and rock temperatures may be assumed equal at any x position

The rock bed is initially at \(40^{\circ}F\) and the air enters at 1 atm and \(100^{\circ}F\). The surroundings remain at \(40^{\circ}F\). Calculate the energy storage relative to \(40^{\circ}F\) as a function of time for inlet velocities of 1.0 and 3.0 ft/s.

Admin
Solution

It can be shown that the axial energy conduction is small compared to the mass-energy transport.
For a \(60^{\circ}F \) temperature difference over a 2-ft length

\(q_{cond }= kA\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta x} = (0.5)(25)\frac{60}{2} = 375 Btu/h [109.9 W]\) [a]

The density of the air at \(100^{\circ}F\) is

\(\rho_{a} = \frac{(14.696)(144)}{(53.35)(560)} = 0.07083 lb_{m}/ft^{3} [1.1346 kg/m^{3}]\) [b]

and the mass flow at 1.0 ft/s is

\( \dot{m}_{a} = \rho Av = (0.07083)(25)(1.0) = 1.7708 lb_{m}/s\)

\( = 6375 lb_{m}/h [2891.7 kg/h]\) [c]

The corresponding energy transport for a temperature difference of \(60^{\circ}F\) is

\(q = \dot{m}c_{pa} \Delta T = (6375)(0.24)(60) = 91,800 Btu/h [26,904 W]\) [d]

and this is much larger than the value in Equation (a).
We now write an energy balance for one of the axial nodes as
Energy transported in − energy transported out − energy lost to surroundings
= rate of energy accumulation of node

or

\(\dot{m}_{ a}c_{pa} (T^{ p}_{m−1} − T^{ p}_{m}) − \frac{(T^{ p}_{m} − T_{∞})P \Delta x}{R_{∞}} = \rho_{r}c_{r}\Delta V_{r}\frac{(T^{ p+1}_{ m} − T^{ p}_{m})}{\Delta \tau }\) [e]

where the air exit temperature from node m is assumed to be the rock temperature of that node \((T^{ p}_{m})\).

Equation (e) may be solved to give

\(T ^{p+1}_{ m} = F\dot{m}_{ a}c_{pa}T ^{p}_{m−1} +\left[1-F\left(\dot{m}_{ a}c_{pa}+\frac{P \Delta x}{R_{∞}} \right) \right] T ^{p}_{m} + \frac{FP \Delta x}{R_{∞}} T_{∞}\) [f]

where

\(F = \frac{\Delta r}{\rho_{r}c_{r} \Delta V_{r}}\)

Here P is the perimeter and \( \Delta x\) is the x increment (P = 4 × 5 = 20 ft for this problem). We are thus in a position to calculate the temperatures in the rock bed as time progresses.

The stability requirement is such that the coefficient on the \(T^{ p}_{ m}\) terms cannot be negative. Using \( \Delta x = 2 ft\), we find that the maximum value of F is \(6.4495 × 10^{−4}\), which yields a maximum time increment of 0.54176 h. With a velocity of 3 ft/s the maximum time increment for stability is 0.1922 h. For the calculations we select the following values of \(\Delta \tau\) with the resultant calculated values of F:

F \( \Delta \tau,h \) v
\(2.38095 × 10^{−4}\) 0.2 1.0
\(1.190476 × 10^{−4}\) 0.1 3.0

With the appropriate properties and these values inserted into Equation (f) there results

\(T^{p+1}_{ m} = 0.3642943T^{ p}_{m−1} + 0.630943T^{ p}_{m} + 0.1904762 for v = 1.0 ft/s\) [g]

\(T^{ p+1}_{ m} = 0.546430633T ^{p}_{m−1} + 0.451188T^{ p}_{m} + 0.0952381 for v = 3.0 ft/s\) [h]

The energy storage relative to \(40^{\circ}F\) can then be calculated from

\(E(\tau ) =\sum^{ 5}_{m=1} \rho_{r}c_{r} \Delta V_{r}[T_{m}(\tau) − 40]\) [i]

as a function of time. The computation procedure is as follows.
1. Initialize all \(T_{m}\) at \(40^{\circ}F\) with \(T_{m−1}\) for node 1 at \(100^{\circ}F\) for all time increments.
2. Compute new values of \(T_{m}\) from either Equation (g) or (h), progressing forward in time until a desired stopping point is reached or the temperature attains steady-state conditions.
3. Using computed values of \(T_{m}(\tau)\), evaluate \(E(\tau)\) from Equation (i).
Results of the calculations are shown in Figure (b). For v = 3.0 ft/s, steady state is reached at about \(\tau = 1.5 h\) while for v = 1.0 ft/s it is reached at about \(\tau = 5.5 h\). Note that the steady-state value of E for v = 1.0 ft/s is lower than for v = 3.0 ft/s because a longer time is involved and more of the energy “leaks out” through the insulation.
This example shows how a rather complex problem can be solved in a straightforward way by using a numerical formulation. The actual calculation may be performed in a very straightforward way using the transient Excel formulation discussed in Appendix D (Section D-5).
