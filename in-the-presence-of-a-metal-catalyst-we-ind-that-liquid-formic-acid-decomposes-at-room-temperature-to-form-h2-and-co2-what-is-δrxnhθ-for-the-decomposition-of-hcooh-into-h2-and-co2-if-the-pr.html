Guest Question.

CONTENT Formic acid is the simplest carboxylic acid, having formula HCOOH, but its chemistry upon heating is surprisingly complex. In water and on metal surfaces, heated formic acid decomposes into \(H_2\) and \(CO_2\), but in the gas phase and in solution at low pH the following reaction occurs instead:

HCOOH(g) → \(H_2O(g)\) + CO(g).

Calorimetry can help us understand how the interactions between HCOOH and its environment have such a substantial impact on the chemistry, beginning by telling us which reaction is more exothermic.

PROBLEM In the presence of a metal catalyst, we ind that liquid formic acid decomposes at room temperature to form \(H_2\) and \(CO_2\) . What is \(\Delta _{rxn} H^{\ominus }\) for the decomposition of HCOOH into \(H_2\) and \(CO_2\) if the process warms 500. g of water by 1.48 K at 298.15 K when 0.100 moles of HCOOH react in a calorimeter at 1.00 moles of HCOOH react in a calorimeter at 1.00 bar? For liquid water at 298.15 K, the speciic heat capacity is 4.186 J K-¹ g-¹.

Admin
Solution

The heat gained by the water has been released by the reaction. Writing the mass of the water as \(M_{H_{2}O } \) and the speciic heat capacity as \(c_{H_{2}O } \) , we set q for the reaction equal to -q for the water:

\(q_{rxn} = – q_{H_{2}O }\)

 

\(\Delta H^{\ominus } = – C_{H_{2}O }\Delta T = – M_{H_{2}O }C_{H_{2}O }\Delta T\)

 

\(\Delta _{rxn} H^{\ominus }(\frac{1}{n} )\Delta H^{\ominus }= – \frac{ M_{H_{2}O }C_{H_{2}O }\Delta T}{n} = \frac{(500. g)(4.186 J K^{-1}g^{-1} )(1.48 K)}{(0.100 mol)} = -3.10\cdot 10^{4} J mol^{-1}= -31.0 KJ mol^{-1}\)

This reaction is weakly exothermic. In contrast, the decomposition into \(HO_2\) and CO is endothermic with a \(\Delta _{rxn} H^{\ominus }\) value of 102.6 kJ, but it is the faster reaction at high temperature in the gas phase.
