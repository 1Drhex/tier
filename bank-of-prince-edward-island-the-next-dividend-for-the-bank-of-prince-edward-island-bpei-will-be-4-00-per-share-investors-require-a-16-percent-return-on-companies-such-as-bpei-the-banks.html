Guest Question.

Bank of Prince Edward Island

The next dividend for the Bank of Prince Edward Island (BPEI) will be $4.00 per share. Investors require a 16 percent return on companies such as BPEI. The bank’s dividend increases by 6 percent every year. Based on the dividend growth model, what is the value of BPEI stock today? What is the value in four years?

Admin
Solution

The only tricky thing here is that the next dividend, \(D_{1}\),
is given as $4.00, so we won’t multiply this by (1 + g). With this in mind, the price per share is given by:

\(P_{0}\) = \(D_{1}\)/(r – g)

= $4.00/(.16 – .06)

= $4.00/(.10)

= $40.00

Because we already have the dividend in one year, the dividend in four years is equal to \(D_{1}× (1 + g)^{3}= \$4.00 × (1.06)^3 = \$4.764\). The price in four years is therefore:

\(P_{4}\) = [\(D_{4}\)× (1 + g)]/(r – g)

= [$4.764 × 1.06]/(.16 – .06)

= $5.05/(.10)

= $50.50

Notice in this example that \(P_{4}\)is equal to \(P_{0}\) × \((1 + g)^{4}\):

\(P_{4}\) = $50.50 = $40.00 × \((1.06)^{4}\) = \(P_{0}\) × \((1 + g)^{4}\)

To see why this is so, notice that:

\(P_{4}\) = \(D_{5}\)/(r – g)

However, \(D_{5}\) is just equal to \(D_{1}\) × \((1 + g)^{4}\), so we can write \(P_{4}\) as:

\(P_{4}\) = \(D_{1}\)× \((1 + g)^{4}\)/(r – g)

= {\(D_{1}\)/(r – g)} × \((1 + g)^{4}\)

= \(P_{0}\) × \((1 + g)^{4}\)

This last example illustrates that the dividend growth model has the implicit assumption that the stock price will grow at the same constant rate as the dividend. This really isn’t too surprising. What it tells us is that if the cash flows on an investment grow at a constant rate through time, so does the value of that investment.
