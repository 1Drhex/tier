Heterogeneous Dissociation of Calcium Carbonate

Calcium carbonate can dissociate according to the following reaction:

\(CaCO _{3}(s) \rightleftarrows CaO (s)+ CO _{2}(g)\) (E9.11)

Consider a closed system with pure \(CaCO _{3}\) in vacuum at 1000 K. What is the equilibrium pressure of the system? Assume that the two solid phases are completely immiscible. At 1000 K, the following Gibbs energies of formation are reported:

Species \(\left(\Delta g_{f, 1000}^{o}\right)_{i}\)
\(CaCO _{3}\) -951.25
\(CaO\) -531.09
\(CO _{2}\) -395.81
Admin
Solution

Applying the definition of the equilibrium constant given by Equation (9.16):

 

\(\prod\left(\frac{\hat{f}_{i}}{f_{i}^{o}}\right)^{v_{i}} \equiv K\) (9.16)

 

\(K=\frac{\left(\frac{\hat{f}_{ CaO }}{f_{ CaO }^{o}}\right)\left(\frac{\hat{f}_{ CO _{2}}}{1 bar }\right)}{\left(\frac{\hat{ f } CaCO _{3}}{f_{ CaCO _{3}}^{o}}\right)}\)

 

We must treat each of the three pure phases distinctly. If we assume that the pressure will be low enough that we have an ideal gas at equilibrium, we can rewrite the equilibrium constant as:

 

\(K=\frac{\left(\frac{f_{ CaO }}{f_{ CaO }}\right)\left(\frac{y_{ CO _{2}} P}{1 bar }\right)}{\left(\frac{f_{ CaCO _{3}}}{f_{ CaCO _{3}}}\right)}=p_{ CO _{2}}\)

 

At low and moderate pressures, the terms corresponding to the pure species solids go to 1, since the Lewis/Randall reference fugacity equals the pure solid fugacity. At very high pressure, we would have to account for the Poynting correction, since the standard state is defined at 1 bar. Thus, the equilibrium constant equals the \(CO _{2}\) partial pressure.

Solving for K from the thermochemical data provided, we get:

 

\(K=\exp \left(-\frac{\Delta g_{ rxn }^{o}}{R T}\right)=\exp \left[-\frac{(-531.09-395.81+951.25)(1000)}{(8.314)(1000)}\right]=0.053\)

 

Thus, at 1000 K calcium carbonate will dissociate until the pressure reaches:

 

\(p_{ CO _{2}}=K=0.053 bar\)

 

or until we run out of \(CaCO _{3}\). Note that our ideal gas assumption is valid at this pressure. From this analysis, we can also deduce the following: If we had a system with solid \(CaO \text { and a } CO _{2}\) partial pressure greater than 0.053 bar at 1000 K, Reaction (E9.11) would have a tendency to occur backward and we would consume \(CO _{2}\) until its partial pressure reached 0.053 bar.
