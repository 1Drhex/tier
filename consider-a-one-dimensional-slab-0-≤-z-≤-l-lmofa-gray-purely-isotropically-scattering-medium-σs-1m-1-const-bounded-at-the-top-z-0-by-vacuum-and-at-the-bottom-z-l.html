Guest Question.

Consider a one-dimensional slab 0 ≤ z ≤ L = lmofa gray, purely isotropically scattering medium (\(σ_{s}= 1m^{-1}\) = const), bounded at the top (z = 0) by vacuum and at the bottom (z = L) by a cold, black surface. Collimated irradiation of strength Q = 100 W is normally incident on this nonreflecting layer, equally distributed over the disk 0 ≤ r ≤ R = 0.1 m, as shown in Fig. 20-10. A small detector 2 cm × 2 cm in size, with an acceptance angle of \(θ_{max}\) is located on the black surface at x = \(x_{o}\) = 02 m, y = 0. Determine the flux incident on the detector for varying acceptance angles, comparing forward and backward Monte Carlo implementations.

Admin
Solution

Forward Monte Carlo Emission points across the irradiation disk for N bundles are chosen as in ,

\(r=R\sqrt{R_{r}} , \phi =2\pi R_{\phi }, and x=r\cos \phi , y=r\sin \phi\).

Emission is always into the \(\widehat{s}=\widehat{k}\) or z-direction. Each bundle carries an amount of energy of Q/N and travels a distance of

\(l_{\sigma }=\frac{1}{\sigma _{s}}\ln \frac{1}{R_{\sigma }}\)

from equation (20.73) \(l_{\sigma }=\frac{1}{\sigma _{s\lambda }}\ln \frac{1}{R_{\sigma }}\) \(l_{\kappa }=r_{0}\ln (1/0.20)=1.61r_{0} , and l_{\sigma }=(r_{0}/2)\times \ln (1/0.082)=1.25 r_{0}\) , before being scattered into a new direction found from equations (20.77) \(R_{\psi }=\frac{\psi ^{\prime }}{2\pi } , or \psi ^{\prime }=2\pi R_{\psi },\) , \(R_{\theta }= \frac{1}{2}\left(1-\cos \theta ^{\prime } + \frac{A_{1}}{2}\sin ^{2} \theta ^{\prime } \right)\) . For isentropic scattering the incident direction is irrelevant and one may set the new direction to that given for isotropic emission, equations (20.66) \(R_{\psi }=\frac{\psi }{2\pi } , or \psi =2\pi R_{\psi },\) \(R_{\theta }=\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{\theta }{}sin\theta d\theta =\frac{1}{2}(1-\cos \theta )\) , \(\theta =\cos ^{-1}(1-2R_{\theta })\). The bundle is then traced along as many scattering paths as needed, until it leaves the layer (z < 0, or z > L). If the bundle strikes the bottom surface (z = I ) , incidence angle (\(\widehat{s}\cdot \widehat{k}\gt \cos ^{2}\theta _{max}\) ?) and location (x,y on detector?) are checked and a detector hit is recorded, if appropriate. Results are shown in Fig. 20-11. As the detector’s acceptance angle increases, more photon bundles are captured. Obviously, this results in a larger detector-absorbed flux. However, it also increases the fraction of statistically-meaningful samples, decreasing the variance of the results or the number of required photon bundles to achieve a given variance. All calculations were carried
out until the variance fell below 2% of the calculated flux, and the necessary number of bundles is also included in the figure. For the chosen variance about \(4 × 10^{6}\) bundles are required for large acceptance angles, rising to \(512 × 10^{6}\) for \(\theta _{max} = 10°\). Results are difficult to obtain for\(\theta _{max} < 10°\). Similar remarks can be made for detector area: as the detector area decreases, the necessary number of bundles increases.
Modeling a more typical detector 1 mm × 1 mm in size would almost be impossible.
Backward Monte Carlo In this case no direct radiation hits the detector (\(x_{0}\) > R) and the scattered irradiation is calculated from equations (20.107) \(I_{\lambda n}(r_{i}, – \widehat{s}_{i} )=\int_{0}^{1}{}S_{\lambda 1}(r^{\prime }, -\widehat{s}^{\prime } ) exp \left[-\int_{0}^{l^{\prime }}{\kappa _{\lambda }dl^{\prime \prime }} \right] dl^{\prime }\) and (20.106) \(S_{\lambda 1}(r,\widehat{s} )=\sigma _{s}(r)\frac{q_{coll}(r_{w})}{4\pi }exp \left[-\int_{0}^{l_{c}}{} (\kappa _{\lambda }+\sigma _{s\lambda })dl^{\prime }_{c} \right] \Phi (r,\widehat{s}_{0},\widehat{s} )\) with \(q_{coll}= Q/\pi R^{2}\) as

\(I_{n}(r_{i}, -\widehat{s}_{i} )=\int_{0}^{1}{} \frac{\sigma _{s}Q}{4\pi ^{2}R^{2}}e^{-\sigma _{s}z}H(R-r(l^{\prime }))dl^{\prime }\),

where l consists of a number of straight-line segments, for which \(dl^{\prime }= dz^{\prime }/\cos \theta\), and H is Heaviside’s unit step function. Therefore,

\(I_{n}(r_{i}, -\widehat{s}_{i} )=\frac{\sigma _{s}Q}{4\pi ^{2}R^{2}}\sum\limits_{j}{}\int_{z_{1j}}^{z_{2j}}{} e^{-\sigma _{s}z}\frac{dz}{s_{zj}} =\frac{Q}{4\pi ^{2}R^{2}} \sum\limits_{j}{} \frac{e^{-\sigma _{s}z_{1j}}-e^{-\sigma _{s}z_{2j}}}{s_{zj}}\), (20.108)

where \(s_{zj}=\cos \theta _{j}\) is the z-component of the direction vector for the j-th segment, and \(z_{1j} and z_{2j}\) are the z-locations between which the segment lies within the cylindrical column r ≤ R (note that some segments may lie totally inside this column, some partially, and some not at all).
