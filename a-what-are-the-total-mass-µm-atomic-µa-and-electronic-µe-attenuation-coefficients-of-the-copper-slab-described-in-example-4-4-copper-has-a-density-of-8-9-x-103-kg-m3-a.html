Guest Question.

a. What are the total mass \(\left(\mu _{m} \right)\), atomic \(\left(\mu _{a} \right)\), and electronic \(\left(\mu _{e} \right)\) attenuation coefficients of the copper slab described in ? Copper has a density of \(8.9\times 10^{3} \ kg/cm^{3}\), a gram-atomic mass of 63.6, and an atomic number of 29.

b. To the 0.01-m copper slab, 0.02 m of copper is added. How many photons remain in the beam emerging from the slab?

c. What is the thickness \(\chi _{e}\) in electrons per square meter for the 0.03-m slab?

d. Repeat the calculation in Part b, but use the electronic attenuation coefficient.

 

Admin
Solution

a.

\(\mu _{m}= \mu /\rho \)

 

\(\ \ \ \ \ =\frac{69.3 m^{-1} }{ 8.9 \times 10^{3} \ kg/cm^{3} } \)

 

\(\ \ \ \ \ = 0.0078 m^{2}/kg\)

 

\(\mu _{a}= \frac{\mu M}{\rho N}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = \frac{\left(69.3 m^{-1}\right) \left(63.6 g/gram-atomic \ mass \right) \left(10^{-3} \ kg/g \right) }{ \left( 8.9\times 10^{3} \ kg/cm^{3}\right) \left(6.02\times 10^{23} \ atoms/gram-atomic \ mass\right) }\) (4-15)

 

\( \ \ \ \ = 8.2 \times 10^{-28} \ m^{2}/atom\)

 

\(\mu _{e} = \frac{\mu _{a} }{Z}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = \frac{8.2 \times 10^{-28} \ m^{2}/atom}{ 29 electrons/atom}\) (4-16)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = 2.8 \times 10^{-29} \ m^{2}/electron\)

b.

\(I= I_{0}e^{-\mu \chi }\)

 

\( \ \ =\left(2000 \ photons \right) e^{-\left(69.3 \ m^{-1}\right) \left(0.03 \ m\right) }\)

 

\( \ \ =\left( 2000 \ photons \right) e^{-2.079}\) (4-17)

 

\( \ \ =\left( 2000 \ photons \right) \left( 0.125 \right)\)

 

\( \ \ = 250 \ photons\)

A thickness of 0.01 m of copper reduces the number of photons to half. Because the beam is narrow and monoenergetic, adding two more of the same thicknesses of copper reduces the number to one eighth. Only one eighth of the original number of photons remains after the beam has traversed three thicknesses of copper.

c.

\(\chi _{e}= \chi _{a}Z\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = \frac{\chi \ N_{a} \ \rho \ Z}{M}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = \frac{\left(0.03 \ m\right)\left( 8.9 \times 10^{3} kg/m^{3}\right) \left(6.02 \times 10^{23} atoms/gram-atomic \ mass\right)\left(29 \ electrons/atom\right) }{\left(63.3 g/gram-atomic \ mass\right)\left(10^{-3} kg/g\right) }\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ =7.3 \times 10^{28} electrons/m^{2}\)

d.

\(I= I_{0}e^{-\mu_{e} \chi _{e}}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ =\left( 2000 \ photons \right) e^{\left(-2.8 \times 10^{29} m^{2}/electro\right) \left(7.3\times 10^{28}electrons/m^{2} \right) }\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ =\left( 2000 \ photons \right) e^{-2.079}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ =\left( 2000 \ photons \right)\left(0.125\right) \)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = 250 \ photons\)

A narrow beam of monoenergetic photons is attenuated exponentially. Exponential attenuation is described by Eq. (4-6)\(I= I_{0}e^{-\mu \chi }\). From we have the equivalent:

\(\ln \frac{I}{I_{0}}= – \mu \chi \)

The logarithm of the number I of photons decreases linearly with increasing thickness of the attenuating slab. Hence a straight line is obtained when the logarithm of the number of x or γ rays is plotted as a function of thickness. When the log of the dependent (y-axis) variable is plotted as a function of the linear independent (x-axis) variable, the graph is termed a semilog plot. It should be emphasized that a semilogarithmic plot of the number of photons versus the thickness of the attenuating slab yields a straight line only if all photons possess the same energy and the conditions for attenuation fulfill requirements for narrow-beam geometry.
If broad-beam geometry were used, the falloff of the number of photons as a function of slab thickness would be less rapid. In broad-beam geometry, some scattered photons continue to strike the detector and are therefore not considered to have been “attenuated.” A broad-beam measurement is relevant when considering the design of wall shielding for radiation safety near x-ray equipment. Humans on the opposite side of barriers would encounter a broad beam. Narrow-beam geometry is more relevant to situations such as experimental studies of the properties of materials.

When an x-ray beam is polyenergetic (as is the beam emitted from an x-ray tube), a plot of the number of photons remaining in the beam as a function of the thickness of the attenuating material it has traversed does not yield a straight line on a semilogarithmic plot. This is because the attenuation of a polyenergetic beam cannot be represented by a single simple exponential equation with a single attenuation coefficient. It is represented by a weighted average of exponential equations for each of the different photon energies in the beam.
