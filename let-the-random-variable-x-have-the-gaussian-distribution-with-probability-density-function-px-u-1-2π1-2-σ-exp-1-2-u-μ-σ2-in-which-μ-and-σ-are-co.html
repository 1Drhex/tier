Guest Question.

Let the random variable \(X\) have the Gaussian distribution with probability density function

\(p_{X}(u) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma }\exp (-\frac{1}{2} [\frac{u-\mu }{\sigma } ]^{2} ) \)

in which \( \mu\) and \(\sigma\) are constants. Find general formulas for \( \mu _{X}\) and the central moments \(E[(X – \mu _{X})^{j} ] \).

Admin
Solution

First we note that the probability density function is symmetric about the line \( u = \mu\), so we immediately know that \( \mu _{X} = \mu \) and

\( E[(X-\mu _{X})^{j}]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{(u-\mu)^{j} p_{X}(u)du} \)

If \(j \) is an odd integer, then \((u – \mu)^{j}\) is antisymmotric about \(u = \mu\), so the integral must be zero, giving \(E[(X – \mu _{X})^{j}]=0\) for \( j\) odd. For \(j \) even, \( (u – \mu)^{j} \) is symmetric about \( u – \mu\), so we can use only the integral on one side of this point of symmetry, then double it:

\( E[(X-\mu _{X})^{j}]=2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{(u-\mu)^{j} p_{X}(u)du} \) for \( j\) even

Using the change of variables \( v=(u-\mu )^{2} / (2\sigma ^{2}) \) gives

\( E[(X-\mu _{X})^{j}]=\frac{(2\sigma ^{2})^{j/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}} \int_{0}^{\infty}{v^{(j-1)/2}e^{-v}dv}= \frac{(2\sigma ^{2})^{j/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}} \) for \( j\) even

in terms of the gamma function introduced in . Applying this relationship will require knowledge of the gamma function for arguments that are half-integers. All such terms can be obtained from the fact that \( \Gamma (1/2)= \pi ^{1/2} \) and then using a general recursivo property of the gamma function: \( \Gamma (a+1)=a \Gamma (a) \). Thus,

\( \Gamma (\frac{j+1}{2})= (\frac{j-1}{2}) \Gamma (\frac{j-1}{2})= (\frac{(j-1)(j-3)…(1)}{2^{j/2}})\Gamma (\frac{1}{2} ) \)

or

\( \Gamma (\frac{j+1}{2})= (\frac{(1)(3)…(j-3)(j-1)}{2^{j/2}})\pi ^{1/2} \)

and

\( E[(X-\mu _{X})^{j}]=(1)(3)…(j-3)(j-1)\sigma ^{j} \) for \( j\) even

Thus, \(j=2\) shows us that the variance \( \sigma _{X}^{2}\equiv E[(X-\mu _{X})^{2}] \) is equal to the parameter \(\sigma ^{2}\). Similarly, \(j = 4\) gives \( E[(X-\mu _{X})^{4}]=3\sigma ^{4} \), which shows that \( kurtosis = 3 \)

for a Gaussian random variable. From knowledge of the mean and variance we now know that we can write the probability density function of any Gaussian random variable as

\( p_{X}(u)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma _{X}} exp(-\frac{1}{2}[\frac{u-\mu _{X}}{\sigma _{X}} ]^{2} ) \)

If we let the random variable \(Z \) be the standardized form of \( X \) :

\( Z=\frac{X-\mu _{X}}{\sigma _{X}} \)

then \( \mu _{Z} = 0 \) and \( \sigma _{Z}=1 \) . From we know that this linear combination is also Gaussian, so its probability density function can be written as

\( p_{Z}(w)=\frac{1}{(2\pi )^{1/2}} e^{-w^{2}/2} \)

This is called the unit Gaussian random variable, and any Gaussian random variable can be written in terms of such a unit Gaussian random variable as

\( X=\mu _{X}+\sigma _{X}Z \)
