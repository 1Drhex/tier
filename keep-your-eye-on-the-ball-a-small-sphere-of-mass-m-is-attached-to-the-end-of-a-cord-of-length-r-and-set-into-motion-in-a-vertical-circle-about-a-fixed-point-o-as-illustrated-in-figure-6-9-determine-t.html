Guest Question.

Keep Your Eye on the Ball

A small sphere of mass m is attached to the end of a cord of length R and set into motion in a vertical circle about a fixed point O as illustrated in Figure 6.9. Determine the tangential acceleration of the sphere and the tension in the cord at any instant when the speed of the sphere is v and the cord makes an angle θ with the vertical.

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize Compare the motion of the sphere in Figure 6.9 with that of the child in Figure 6.6a associated with . Both objects travel in a circular path. Unlike the child in , however, the speed of the sphere is not uniform in this example because, at most points along the path, a tangential component of acceleration arises from the gravitational force exerted on the sphere.

Categorize We model the sphere as a particle under a net force and moving in a circular path, but it is not a particle in uniform circular motion. We need to use the techniques discussed in this section on nonuniform circular motion.

Analyze From the force diagram in Figure 6.9, we see that the only forces acting on the sphere are the gravitational force \(\overrightarrow{F}_{g}=m \overrightarrow{g}\) exerted by the Earth and the force \(\overrightarrow{T}\) exerted by the cord. We resolve \(\overrightarrow{F}_{g}\) into a tangential component \(m g \sin \theta\) and a radial component \(m g \cos \theta\).

From the particle under a net force model, apply Newton’s second law to the sphere in the tangential direction:

\(\sum F_{t}=m g \sin \theta=m a_{t}\)

\(a_{t}=g \sin \theta\)

Apply Newton’s second law to the forces acting on the sphere in the radial direction, noting that both \(\overrightarrow{T}\) and \(\overrightarrow{a}_{r}\) are
directed toward O. As noted in Section 4.5, we can use Equation 4.14 for the centripetal acceleration of a particle even when it moves in a circular path in nonuniform motion:

\(a_{c}=\frac{v^{2}}{r}\) (4.14)

\(\sum F_{r}=T-m g \cos \theta=\frac{m v^{2}}{R}\)

\(T=m g\left(\frac{v^{2}}{R g}+\cos \theta\right)\)

Finalize Let us evaluate this result at the top and bottom of the circular path (Fig. 6.9):

\(T_{\mathrm{top}}=m g\left(\frac{v_{\text {top }}^{2}}{R g}-1\right) \quad T_{\mathrm{bot}}=m g\left(\frac{v_{\mathrm{bot}}^{2}}{R g}+1\right)\)

These results have similar mathematical forms as those for the normal forces \(n_{\text {top }}\) and \(n_{\text {bot }}\) on the child in , which is consistent with the normal force on the child playing a similar physical role in as the tension in the string plays in this example. Keep in mind, however, that the normal force \(\overrightarrow{n}\) on the child in is always upward, whereas the force \(\overrightarrow{T}\) in this example changes direction because it must always point inward along the string. Also note that v in the expressions above varies for different positions of the sphere, as indicated by the subscripts, whereas v in is constant.
