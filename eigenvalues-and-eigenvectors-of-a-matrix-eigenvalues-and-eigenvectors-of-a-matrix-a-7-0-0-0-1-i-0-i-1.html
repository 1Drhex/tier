Guest Question.

(Eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a matrix)
Find the eigenvalues and the normalized eigenvectors of the matrix

\(A=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right). \)
Admin
Solution

To find the eigenvalues of A, we simply need to solve the secular equation det(A – aI) = 0:

\(0=\left|\begin{matrix} 7-a & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1-a & -i \\ 0 & i & -1-a \end{matrix} \right| =(7-a)\left[-(1-a)(1+a)+i^2\right]=(7-a)(a^2-2). \) (2.265)

The eigenvalues of A are thus given by

\(a_1=7, a_2=\sqrt{2}, a_3=-\sqrt{2}. \) (2.266)

Let us now calculate the eigenvectors of A. To find the eigenvector corresponding to the first eigenvalue, \(a_1=7,\) we need to solve the matrix equation

\(\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{matrix} \right)=7\left(\begin{matrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{matrix} \right)\Longrightarrow \begin{matrix} 7x & = & 7x \\ y-iz & = & 7y ; \\ iy-z & = & 7z \end{matrix} \) (2.267)

this yields x = 1 (because the eigenvector is normalized) and y = z = 0. So the eigenvector corresponding to \(a_1=7\) is given by the column matrix

\(|a_1〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right). \) (2.268)

This eigenvector is normalized since \(〈a_1|a_1〉 =1.\)

The eigenvector corresponding to the second eigenvalue, \(a_2=\sqrt{2}, \) can be obtained from the matrix equation

\(\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right) \left(\begin{matrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{matrix} \right) =\sqrt{2} \left(\begin{matrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{matrix} \right)\Longrightarrow \begin{matrix} (7-\sqrt{2} )x & = & 0 \\ (1-\sqrt{2} )y-iz & = & 0 ; \\iy-(1+\sqrt{2} )z & = & 0 \end{matrix} \) (2.269)

this yields x = 0 and \(z=i(\sqrt{2}-1 )y. \) So the eigenvector corresponding to \(a_2 =\sqrt{2} \) is given by the column matrix

\(| a_2〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ y \\ i(\sqrt{2}-1 )y \end{matrix} \right). \) (2.270)

The value of the variable y can be obtained from the normalization condition of \(| a_2〉:\)

\(1=〈a_2 | a_2〉= (0 y^* -i(\sqrt{2}-1)y^*)\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ y \\ i(\sqrt{2}-1y ) \end{matrix} \right)=2(2-\sqrt{2}) | y |^2. \) (2.271)

Taking only the positive value of y (a similar calculation can be performed easily if one is interested in the negative value of y), we have \(y=1/\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2} )};\) hence the eigenvector (2.270) becomes

\(| a_2〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})} } \\ \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1 )}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2} )} } \end{matrix} \right\rgroup .\) (2.272)

Following the same procedure that led to (2.272), we can show that the third eigenvector is given by

\(| a_3〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ y \\ -i(1+\sqrt{2} )y \end{matrix} \right); \) (2.273)

its normalization leads to \(y=1/\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2} )} \) (we have considered only the positive value of y); hence

\(| a_3〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})} } \\ -\frac{i(1+\sqrt{2} )}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2} )} } \end{matrix} \right\rgroup .\) (2.274)
