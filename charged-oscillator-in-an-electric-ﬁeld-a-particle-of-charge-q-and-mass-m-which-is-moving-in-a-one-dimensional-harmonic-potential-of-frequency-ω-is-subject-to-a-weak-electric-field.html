Guest Question.

(Charged oscillator in an electric ﬁeld)

A particle of charge q and mass m, which is moving in a one-dimensional harmonic potential of frequency ω, is subject to a weak electric field ε in the x-direction.

(a) Find the exact expression for the energy.
(b) Calculate the energy to first nonzero correction and compare it with the exact result obtained in (a).

Admin
Solution

The interaction between the oscillating charge and the external electric field gives rise to a term \(\hat{H} _p=q\varepsilon\hat{X} \) that needs to be added to the Hamiltonian of the oscillator:

\(\hat{H} =\hat{H} _0+\hat{H} _p=-\frac{\hbar }{2m}\frac{d^2}{dX^2}+\frac{1}{2}m\omega ^2\hat{X} ^2+q\varepsilon\hat{X}. \) (9.25)

(a) First, note that the eigenenergies of this Hamiltonian can be obtained exactly without resorting to any perturbative treatment. A variable change \(\hat{y} =\hat{X}+q\varepsilon/(m\omega ^2) \) leads to

\(\hat{H} =-\frac{\hbar^2 }{2m}\frac{d^2}{dy^2}+\frac{1}{2}m\omega ^2\hat{y} ^2-\frac{q^2\varepsilon^2}{2m\omega ^2}. \) (9.26)

This is the Hamiltonian of a harmonic oscillator from which a constant, \(q^2\varepsilon^2\omega ^2/(2m), \) is subtracted. The exact eigenenergies can thus be easily inferred:

\(E_n=\left(n+\frac{1}{2} \right) \hbar \omega -\frac{q^2\varepsilon ^2}{2m\omega ^2}. \) (9.27)

This simple example allows us to compare the exact and approximate eigenenergies.
(b) Let us now turn to finding the approximate eigenvalues of \(\hat{H}\) by means of perturbation theory. Since the electric field is weak, we can treat \(\hat{H}_p\) as a perturbation.

Note that the first-order correction to the energy, \(E^{(1)}_n=a〈n | \hat{X } | n 〉,\) is zero (since \(〈n | \hat{X} | n 〉=0),\) but the second-order correction is not:

\(E^{(2)}_n=q^2\varepsilon ^2\sum\limits_{m\neq n}{\frac{|〈m|\hat{X}|n 〉|^2}{E^{(0)}_n-E^{(0)}_m}} . \) (9.28)

Since \(E^{(0)}_n= \left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar \omega , \) and using the relations

\(〈n+1 | \hat{X} | n 〉=\sqrt{n+1}\sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2m\omega } }, 〈n-1 | \hat{X} | n 〉=\sqrt{n} \sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2m\omega } }, \) (9.29)

\(E^{(0)}_n-E^{(0)}_{n-1}=\hbar \omega , E^{(0)}_n-E^{(0)}_{n+1}=-\hbar \omega ,\) (9.30)

we can reduce (9.28) to

\(E^{(2)}_n=q^2\varepsilon ^2\left[\frac{|〈n+1 | \hat{X} | n 〉|^2}{E^{(0)}_n-E^{(0)}_{n+1}}+\frac{|〈n-1 | \hat{X} | n 〉|^2}{E^{(0)}_n-E^{(0)}_{n-1}} \right] \)

\(=-\frac{q^2\varepsilon ^2}{2m\omega ^2}; \) (9.31)

hence the energy is given to second order by

\(E_n=E^{(0)}_n+E^{(1)}_{n}+E^{(2)}_{n}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2} \right)\hbar \omega -\frac{q^2\varepsilon ^2}{2m\omega ^2}. \) (9.32)

This agrees fully with the exact energy found in (9.27).
Similarly, using (9.19) \(|\psi ^{(1)}_n〉=\sum\limits_{m\neq n}{\frac{〈\phi _m|\hat{W}|\phi _n 〉}{E^{(0)}_n- E^{(0)}_m}} |\phi _m 〉. \) along with (9.29) and (9.30), we can easily ascertain that \( |\psi ^{(1)} _n 〉 \) is given by

\( |\psi ^{(1)} _n 〉 =\frac{q\varepsilon }{\hbar \omega }\sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2m\omega } } \left\{\sqrt{n}|n-1〉-\sqrt{n+1}|n+1〉 \right\} ;\) (9.33)

hence the state \( |\psi _n 〉\) is given to first order by

\( |\psi _n 〉 =|n〉 +\frac{q\varepsilon }{\hbar \omega }\sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2m\omega } } \left\{\sqrt{n}|n-1〉-\sqrt{n+1}|n+1〉 \right\} ,\) (9.34)

where | n〉 is the exact eigenstate of the nth excited state of a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator.
