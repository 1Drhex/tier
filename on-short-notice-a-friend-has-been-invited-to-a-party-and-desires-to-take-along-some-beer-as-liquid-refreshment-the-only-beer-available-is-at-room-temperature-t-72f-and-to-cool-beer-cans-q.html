Guest Question.

On short notice, a friend has been invited to a party and desires to take along some beer as liquid refreshment. The only beer available is at room temperature (T = 72°F), and to cool beer cans quickly, they are placed in the freezer compartment of a refrigerator (T = 30°F). A typical beer can measures 2-1/2 in. in diameter by 4-3/4 in. tall. What will the beer temperature at the center of the can become after 2 hr in the freezer? What is the beer temperature near the metal of the can? Take the average convection coefficient between the can and the freezer to be 1.7 BTU/(hr · \(ft^{2}\) · °R).

Admin
Solution

The can of beer is placed in an environment where the temperature is low. The can is probably placed so that its bottom is in contact with the freezer shelf; thus, heat is transferred by conduction through the can bottom. Beer in the vicinity of the can metal cools before the beer in the can center does. As the beer near the wall cools, its properties change, and natural convective effects begin to occur. The beer experiences some mixing, which tends to distribute energy more nearly evenly, resulting in a more nearly uniform temperature profile.

 

Assumptions

 

1. Lacking better information, we assume the properties of beer are the same as those of water at 72°F (or thereabout).
2. Properties of beer are constant.
3. Natural convective effects occurring within the beer are negligible, and the beer can be treated as if it were a solid.
4. The convection coefficient is constant and applies to the entire surface of the can.

From Appendix Table C.11 for water at 68°F we fi nd

\(\rho = 62.46 lbm/ft^{3}\) k = 0.345 BTU/(hr · ft · °R)

 

\(c_{p} = 0.9988 BTU/(lbm · °R)\)

 

\(\alpha = 5.54 \times 10^{−3} ft^{2}/hr\)

 

These properties vary within the beer during the time that the temperature falls from 72°F to, say, 32°F. For example, at 32°F, Table C.11 shows the following properties:

 

\(\rho = 62.57 lbm/ft^{3}\) k = 0.319 BTU/(hr · ft · °R)

 

\(c_{p} = 1.0074 BTU/(lbm · °R)\)

 

\(\alpha = 5.07 \times 10^{−3} ft^{2}/hr\)

 

The diffusivity changes by as much as 9%. For the calculations here, it is assumed that the properties at 68°F are representative.

 

Table 4.2, for the short-cylinder problem, indicates that the solution is the product of the infinite-cylinder problem (Figure 4.7) and the infinite-plate problem (Figure 4.6). However, we must determine if the lumped-capacitance formulation applies. We first calculate the Biot number

 

\(Bi=\frac{\bar{h_{c}}L_{c}}{k}=\frac{\bar{h_{c}}V_{s}}{kA_{s}}\)

 

The can volume is

 

\(V_{s}=\frac{\pi D^{2}L}{4}=\frac{\pi \left ( \frac{2.5}{12} \right )^{2}\left ( \frac{4.75}{12} \right )}{4}=0.0135\,ft^{3}\)

 

The surface area of the can is

 

\(A_{s}=\pi DL+2\left ( \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4} \right )=\pi \left ( \frac{2.5}{12} \right )\left ( \frac{4.75}{12} \right )+2\left [ \pi \left ( \frac{2.5}{12} \right )^{2}/4 \right ]=0.327\,ft^{2}\)

 

The characteristic length for the can dimensions is

 

\(L_{c}=\frac{V_{s}}{A_{s}}=0.041\,ft\)

 

The Biot number then becomes

 

\(Bi=\frac{1.7(0.041)}{0.345}=0.203\) (lumped capacitance)

 

A lumped-capacitance analysis is not warranted because Bi > 0.1. The preceding Biot number equation is appropriate for the lumped-capacitance method but not for the chart solutions of Figures 4.6 and 4.7.

 

For the cylinder solution, we find

 

\(Fo=\frac{\alpha t}{R^{2}}=\frac{(5.54\times 10^{-3}ft^{2}/hr)(2\,hr)}{\left [ \left ( \frac{2.5}{2} \right )\left ( \frac{1}{12} \right ) \right ]^{2}ft}=1.02\)

 

and

 

\(Bi=\frac{\bar{h_{c}}R}{k}=\frac{1.7\left ( \frac{2.5}{2} \right )\left ( \frac{1}{12} \right )}{0.345}=0.513\)

 

1/Bi=1.95 (chart solution)

 

From Figure 4.7a at Fo = 1.02 and 1/Bi = 1.95 we read

 

\(\frac{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{cylinder}=0.42\)

 

For the infinite-plate solution

 

\(Bi=\frac{\bar{h_{c}}L}{k}=\frac{1.7\left ( \frac{4.75}{2(12)} \right )}{0.345}=0.98\)

 

\(\frac{1}{Bi}=1.0\)

 

\(Fo=\frac{\alpha t}{L^{2}}=\frac{5.54\times 10^{-3}(2)}{\left [ \frac{4.75}{2(12)} \right ]^{2}}=0.28\)

 

From Figure 4.6a at Fo = 0.28 and 1/Bi = 1.0, we read

 

\(\frac{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{plate}=0.85\)

 

Therefore

 

\(\frac{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{short\,cylinder}=0.42(0.85)=0.357\)

 

Substituting and solving gives

 

\(T_{c}=0.357(72-30)+30=45.0^{\circ}F\)

 

This is the temperature at the center of the can after 2 hr in the freezer.

 

To obtain an estimate of the temperature at the can wall, we refer to Figures 4.6b and 4.7b. For 1/Bi = 1.95 and r/R = 1, we read from Figure 4.7b

 

\(\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{cylinder}=0.77\)

 

From Figure 4.6b, with x/L = 1 and 1/Bi = 1.0

 

\(\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{plate}=0.65\)

 

At the wall then

 

\(\frac{T_{w}-T_{\infty}}{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}\mid_{short\, cylinder}=0.77(0.65)=0.50\)

 

Substituting and solving

 

\(t_{w}=0.50(45.0-30)+30\)

 

or

 

\(T_{w}=37.5^{\circ}F\)

It is prudent to note that there is a diff erence in the semiinfi nite solid graph (Figure 4.12) and the graphs of the other solutions (infi nite slab, infinite cylinder, and sphere); specifi cally, a diff erence in the dimensionless temperature. For the semi-infi nite slab, the vertical axis is of dimensionless temperature at some depth x; namely, (T − Ti)/(T∞ − Ti), in which the reference temperature is Ti. For the other graphs, the vertical axis is of dimensionless center temperature—(Tc − T∞)/(Ti − T∞).
Th e reference temperature is T∞. As mentioned in the preceding paragraph, the reference temperature in the semiinfi nite solid is Ti. If, instead, the graph is produced so that the reference is T∞, the lines do not “spread” out as they do in Figure 4.12, and so they are diffi cult to read and obtain an accurate result. To change the reference temperature in the semi-infi nite solid solution, we merely subtract the dimensionless temperature from 1 to obtain

\(1-\frac{T-T_{{i}}}{T_{\infty}-T_{{i}}}=\frac{T_{\infty}-T_{{i}}-T+T_{{i}}}{T_{\infty}-T_{{i}}}=\frac{T_{\infty}-T}{T_{\infty}-T_{{i}}}=\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{{i}}-T_{\infty}}\)
