Guest Question.

The spherical chicken A spherical chicken\(^{2}\) of radius a at initial temperature \(T_{0}\) is placed into an oven at temperature \(T_{1}\) at time t = 0 (see Fig. 10.3). The boundary conditions are that the oven is at temperature \(T_{1}\) so that \(^{2}\)The methods in this example can also be applied to a spherical nut roast. \(T(a,t)=T_{1},\) (10.32) and the chicken is originally at temperature \(T_{0},\) so that for \(r \lt a\) \(T(r, 0) =T_{0}. \) (10.33) We want to obtain the temperature as a function of time at the centre of the chicken, i.e., T(0, t).

Admin
Solution

We will show how we can transform this to a one-dimensional diffusion equation. This is accomplished using a substitution

\( T(r,t)=T_{1}+\frac{B(r,t)}{r},\) (10.34)

where B(r, t) is now a function of r and t. This substitution is motivated by the solution to the steady–state problem in eqn 10.31 and of course means that we can write B as \(B = r(T − T_{1}).\) Fig. 10.3 Initial condition of a spheri-cal chicken of radius a at initial temper-ature \(T_{0},\) which is placed into an oven at temperature \(T_{1}\) at time t = 0.

\( T=A+\frac{B}{r},\) (10.31)

 

\( \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial B}{\partial t},\) (10.35)

 

\( \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}=-\frac{B}{r^{2}}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial B}{\partial r},\) (10.36)

and hence multiplying eqn 10.36 by \(r^{2}\) we have that

\( r^{2}\frac{\partial T}{\partial r}=-B+r\frac{\partial B}{\partial r},\) (10.37)

and therefore

\( \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left[r^{2}\frac{\partial T}{\partial r} \right]=r\frac{\partial ^{2}B}{\partial r^{2}},\) (10.38)

which means that eqn 10.29 becomes

\( \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=\frac{\kappa }{C}\frac{1}{r^{2}}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2}\frac{\partial T}{\partial r} \right).\) (10.29)

 

\( \frac{\partial B}{\partial t}=D\frac{\partial^{2}B}{\partial r^{2}},\) (10.39)

where \(D=\kappa /C.\) This is a one-dimensional diffusion equation and is therefore much easier to solve than the one with which we started. The new boundary conditions can be rewritten as follows:

(1) Because \(B = r(T − T_{1})\) we have that \(B = 0\) when \(r = 0:\)

\( B(0, t) = 0;\) (10.40)

(2) Because \(T = T_{1}\) at \(r = a\) we have that:

\( B(a, t) = 0;\) (10.41)

3) Because \(T = T_{0}\) at \(t = 0\) we have that (for \(r \lt a\)):

\( B(r, 0) = r(T_{0} − T_{1}).\) (10.42)

We look for wave-like solutions with these boundary conditions and hence are led to try

\( B=\sin (kr)e^{-i\omega t},\) (10.43)

and substituting this into eqn 10.39 yields

\( i\omega =Dk^{2}.\) (10.44)

The relation \(ka = n\pi\) where n is an integer fits the first two boundary conditions and hence

\( i\omega =D\left(\frac{n\pi }{a} \right)^{2},\) (10.45)

and hence our general solution is

\( B(r,t)=\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{A_{n}\sin \left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right)e^{-D\left(\frac{n\pi }{a} \right)^{2}t } }.\) (10.46)

To find \(A_{n},\) we need to match this solution at t = 0 using our third boundary condition. Hence

\( r(T_{0}-T_{1})=\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{A_{n}\sin \left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right) }.\) (10.47)

We multiply both sides by \(\left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right)\) and integrate, so that

\( \int_{0}^{a}{\sin \left(\frac{m\pi r}{a} \right)r(T_{0}-T_{1}) }dr=\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{A_{n}}\int_{0}^{a}{\sin \left(\frac{m\pi r}{a} \right)\sin \left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right) }dr.\) (10.48)

This trick of multiplying both sides by \(\sin(m\pi r/a)\) and integrating is a useful strategy in problems involving Fourier series (the expression in eqn 10.47 is a Fourier series). The trick relies on the fact that the two functions \(\sin(n\pi r/a)\) and \(\sin(m\pi r/a)\) are orthogonal unless \(m = n.\) The right-hand side yields \(A_{m}a/2\) and the left-hand side can be inte-grated by parts. This yields

\( A_{m}=\frac{2a}{m\pi }(T_{1}-T_{0})(-1)^{m},\) (10.49)

and hence by substituting this back into eqn 10.46 we obtain

\( B(r,t)=\frac{2a}{\pi }(T_{1}-T_{0})\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac{(-1)^{n}}{n} }\sin \left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right)e^{-D(n\pi /a)^{2}t}.\) (10.50)

Putting this back into eqn 10.34 shows that the temperature \(T(r, t)\) inside the chicken \((r \leq a)\) behaves as

\(T(r,t)=T_{1}+\frac{2a}{\pi }(T_{1}-T_{0})\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac{(-1)^{n}}{n} \frac{\sin (n\pi r/a)}{r} }e^{-D(n\pi /a)^{2}t}.\) (10.51)

The centre of the chicken has temperature

\( T(0,t)=T_{1}+2(T_{1}-T_{0})\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{(-1)^{n}}e^{-D(n\pi /a)^{2}t},\) (10.52)

which is deduced from eqn 10.51 using the fact that as \(r \rightarrow 0,\)

\( \frac{1}{r}\sin \left(\frac{n\pi r}{a} \right)\rightarrow \frac{n\pi }{a}.\) (10.53)

Fig. 10.4 The sum of the first few terms of eqn 10.52, shown together with T(0, t) evaluated from all terms (thick solid line). The sums of only the first few terms fail near t = 0 and one needs more and more terms to give an accu-rate estimate of the temperatures as t gets closer to 0 (although this is the re-gion where one knows what the temper-ature is anyway!). The expression in eqn 10.52 (see Fig. 10.4) becomes dominated by the first exponential in the sum as time t increases, so that

\( T(0,t)\approx T_{1}-2(T_{1}-T_{0})e^{-D(\pi /a)^{2}t},\) (10.54)

for \(t \gg a^{2}/D\pi ^{2}.\) Analogous behaviour is of course found for a warm sphere cooling in a colder environment. A cooling or warming body thus behaves like a low-pass filter, with the smallest exponent dominating at long times. The smaller the sphere, the shorter the time before it warms or cools, according to a simple exponential law.
