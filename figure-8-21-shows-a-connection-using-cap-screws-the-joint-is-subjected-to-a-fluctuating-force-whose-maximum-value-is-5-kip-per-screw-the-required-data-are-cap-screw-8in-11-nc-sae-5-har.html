Guest Question.

Figure 8–21 shows a connection using cap screws. The joint is subjected to a fluctuating force whose maximum value is 5 kip per screw. The required data are: cap screw,\({5}/{8}\) in-11 NC, SAE ; hardened-steel washer, \(t_w=\frac{1}{16}\) in thick; steel cover plate, \(t_1=\frac {5}{8} \ in \ , \ E_s= 30\) Mpsi ; and cast-iron base, \(t_2=\frac{5}{8} \ in \ , \ E_{ci}=16 \ Mpsi\).

(a) Find \(k_b, k_m, \ and \ C\) using the assumptions given in the caption of Fig. 8–21.
(b) Find all factors of safety and explain what they mean.

(a) For the symbols of Figs. 8–15 and 8–21, \( = t_1+ t_w= 0.6875 \ in \ , l = h + {d}/{2} =1 \ in \ , \ and \ D_2= 1.5d = 0.9375 \) in.
The joint is composed of three frusta; the upper two frusta are steel and the lower one is cast iron.
For the upper frustum: \(t = {l}/{2} = 0.5\) in , \( D = 0.9375\) in , and \(E = 30\) Mpsi.

Using these values in Eq. (8–20)
\(k=\frac{0.57774\pi Ed}{\ln\frac{\left(1.551+D-d\right)\left(D+d\right) }{\left(1.55t+D+d\right)\left(D-d\right) } } \)

\( k_2= 197.43 \ Mlbf/in\).

The same equation yields \( k_3= 32.39 \ Mlbf/in\).

\(\frac{1}{k_m} =\frac{1}{k_1} +\frac{1}{k_2} +\frac{1}{k_3} +…+\frac{1}{K_i} \)
gives \(k_m= 17.40 \ Mlbf/in\).

\(S_p= 85 \) kpsi for an SAE grade 5 cap screw. Using Eq. (8–28),\(n_p=\frac{S_p A_t}{CP + F_i}\)

we obtain the load factor as the yielding factor of safety is
\(n_p=\frac{S_p A_t}{CP + F_i}= \frac{85 \left(0.226\right)}{0.280 \left(5\right) + 14.4}= 1.22\)
This is the traditional factor of safety, which compares the maximum bolt stress to the proof strength.

Using Eq. (8–29),

\(n_L=\frac{S_p A_t− F_i}{CP}\)

 

\(n_L=\frac{S_p A_t− F_i}{CP} = \frac{85 \left(0.226\right) − 14.4}{0.280 \left(5\right)}= 3.44\)
This factor is an indication of the overload on P that can be applied without exceeding the proof strength.
Next, using Eq. (8–30), we have

\(n_0=\frac{F_i}{P \left(1 − C\right)} =\frac{14.4}{5\left(1 − 0.280\right)}= 4.00\)

If the force P gets too large, the joint will separate and the bolt will take the entire load.

This factor guards against that event.
For the remaining factors, refer to Fig. 8–22. This diagram contains the modified Goodman line, the Gerber line, the proof-strength line, and the load line.
The intersection of the load line L with the respective failure lines at points C, D, and E defines a set of strengths Saand Smat each intersection.
Point B represents the stress state \(\sigma_a \ , \ \sigma_m\).Point A is the preload stress \(\sigma_i\).
Therefore the load line begins at A and makes an angle having a unit slope.This angle is 45° only when both stress axes have the same scale.
The factors of safety are found by dividing the distances AC, AD, and AE by the distance AB.
Note that this is the same as dividing Sa for each theory by \(\sigma_a\).
The quantities shown in the caption of Fig. 8–22 are obtained as follows:
Point A
\(\sigma_i=\frac{F_i}{A_t}=\frac{14.4}{0.226}= 63.72 \) kpsi
Point B
\(\sigma_a= \frac{CP}{2At}=\frac{0.280 \left(5\right)}{2\left(0.226\right)}= 3.10 \) kpsi

\(\sigma_m= \sigma_a+ \sigma_i= 3.10 + 63.72 = 66.82 \) kpsi

Point C
This is the modified Goodman criteria. From Table 8–17,

we find \(S_e= 18.6 \) kpsi.
Then, using Eq. (8–45),
\(n_f=\frac{S_e\left(S_{ut}-\sigma_i\right) }{\sigma_a\left(S_{ut}-S_e\right) } \)
the factor of safety is found to be

\(n_f = \frac{S_e \left(S_{ut}− \sigma_i\right)}{\sigma_a\left(S_{ut}+ S_e\right)}=\frac{18.6\left(120 − 63.72\right)}{3.10 \left(120 + 18.6\right)}= 2.44\)

Point D
This is on the proof-strength line where
\(S_m+ S_a= S_p\) (1)
In addition, the horizontal projection of the load line AD is
\(S_m= \sigma_i+ S_a\) (2)
Solving Eqs. (1) and (2) simultaneously results in
\( S_a=\frac{S_P-\sigma_i}{2} =\frac{85-63.72}{2} =10.64 \) kpsi
The factor of safety resulting from this is

 

\(n_p=\frac{S_a}{\sigma_a}=\frac{10.64}{3.10}= 3.43\)

which, of course, is identical to the result previously obtained by using Eq. (8–29).
A similar analysis of a fatigue diagram could have been done using yield strength instead of proof strength.

Though the two strengths are somewhat related, proof strength is a much better and more positive indicator of a fully loaded bolt than is the yield strength.It is also worth remembering that proof-strength values are specified in design codes; yield strengths are not.
We found \(n_f = 2.44\) on the basis of fatigue and the modified Goodman line, and \(n_p= 3.43\) on the basis of proof strength.
Thus the danger of failure is by fatigue, not by overproof loading. These two factors should always be compared to determine where the greatest danger lies.
Point E
For the Gerber criterion, from Eq. (8–46),

\(n_f=\frac{1}{2\sigma_aS_e}\left[S_{ut}\sqrt{S^2_{ut}+4S_e\left(S_e+ \sigma_i\right)}-S^2_{ut}-2 \sigma_i S_e\right]\)

 

\(n_f=\frac{1}{2\sigma_aS_e}\left[S_{ut}\sqrt{S^2_{ut}+4S_e\left(S_e+ \sigma_i\right)}-S^2_{ut}-2\sigma_i S_e\right]\)

 

the safety factor is

\(n_f=\frac{1}{2\sigma_aS_e}\left[S_{ut}\sqrt{S^2_{ut}+4S_e\left(S_e+ \sigma_i\right)}-S^2_{ut}-2\sigma_i S_e\right] \\ =\frac{1}{2 \left(3.10\right) \left(18.6\right)} \left[120 \sqrt{120^2+ 4 \left(18.6\right) \left(18.6 + 63.72\right)} − 120^2− 2 \left(63.72\right) \left(18.6\right)\right] \\ = 3.65\)

which is greater than \( n_p= 3.43\) and contradicts the conclusion earlier that the danger of failure is fatigue. Figure 8–22 clearly shows the conflict where point D lies between points C and E.
Again, the conservative nature of the Goodman criterion explains the discrepancy and the designer must form his or her own concl usion.
