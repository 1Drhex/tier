Guest Question.

A flat-plate-type heater (Fig. 8.23) is to be used to heat air with the hot exhaust gases from a turbine. The required airflow rate is 0.75 kg/s, entering at 290 K; the hot gases are available at a temperature of 1150 K and a mass flow rate of 0.60 kg/s.

Determine the temperature of the air leaving the heat exchanger for the parameters listed below.

\(P_a\) = wetted perimeter on air side, 0.703 m

\(P_g\) = wetted perimeter on gas side, 0.416 m

\(A_g\) = cross-sectional area of gas passage (per passage), \(1.6 \times 10^{-3} m^2\)

\(A_a\) = cross-sectional area of air passage (per passage), \(2.275 \times 10^{-3} m^2\)

A = heat transfer surface area, 2.52 m2

Admin
Solution

Inspection of Fig. 8.23 shows that the unit is of the cross-flow type, with both fluids unmixed. As a first approximation, the end effects will be neglected. The flow systems for the air and gas streams are similar to flow in straight ducts having the following dimensions:

 

\(L_a\) = length of air duct, 0.178 m

 

\(D_{Ha}\) = hydraulic diameter of air duct, \(\frac{4A_a}{P_a} = 0.0129\) m

 

\(L_g\) = length of gas duct, 0.343 m

 

\(D_{Hg}\) = hydraulic diameter of g

as duct \(\frac{4A_g}{P_g} = 0.0154\) m

 

A = heat transfer surface area, \(2.52 m^2\)

 

The heat transfer coefficients can be evaluated from Eq. (6.63) for flow in ducts\( (L_a/ D_{Ha } = 13.8, L_g /D_{Hg} = 22.3)\). A difficulty arises, however, because the temperatures of both fluids vary along the duct. It is therefore necessary to estimate an average bulk temperature and refine the calculations after the outlet and wall temperatures have been found. Selecting the average air-side bulk temperature to be 573 K and the average gas-side bulk temperature to be 973 K, the properties at those temperatures are, from Appendix 2, Table 28 (assuming that the properties of the gas can be approximated by those of air):

\(\mu _{air} = 2.93 \times 10^{-5} N s/m^2\)

 

\(\mu _{gas}=4.085 \times 10^{-5} N s/m^2\)

 

 

\(Pr_{air}\) = 0.71 \( P_{rgas}\) = 0.73

 

\(k_{air}\) = 0.0429 W/m K \(k_{gas}\) = 0.0623 W/m K

 

\(c_{p_{air}}\)= 1047 J/kg K \(c_{p_{gas}}\) = 1101 J/kg K

The mass flow rates per unit area are

 

\(\left(\frac{\dot{m} }{A} \right)_{air}= \frac{(0.75 kg/s)}{(19)(2.275 \times 10^{-3} m^2)} =17.35 kg/m^2 s\)

 

\(\left(\frac{\dot{m} }{A} \right)_{gas}= \frac{(0.60 kg/s)}{(18)(1.600 \times 10^{-3} m^2)} =20.83 kg/m^2 s\)

 

The Reynolds numbers are

 

\(Re_{air}=\frac{(\dot{m}/A )_{air}D_{Ha}}{\mu _{a} }=\frac{(17.35 kg/m^2 s)(0.0129 m)}{(2.93 \times 10^{-5} kg/m s)} = 7640\)

 

\(Re_{gas}=\frac{(\dot{m}/A )_{gas}D_{Hg}}{\mu _{g} }=\frac{(20.83 kg/m^2 s)(0.0154 m)}{(4.085 \times 10^{-5} kg/m s)} = 7850\)

 

Using Eq. (6.63), the average heat transfer coefficients are

\(\overline{Nu}_D=\frac{(f/8)Re_{D}Pr}{K_1+K_2(f/8)^{1/2}(Pr^{2/3}-1)} \hspace{30 pt} \begin{matrix} 0.5 < Pr < 2000 \\ 10^4 < Re_{D} < 5\times 10^{6} \end{matrix} \) (6.63)

\(\overline{h} _{air} =0.023\frac{k_a}{D_{Ha}} Re_{air} ^{0.8}Pr^{0.4}\)

 

\( =0.023\frac{0.0429}{0.0129} (7640)^{0.8}(0.71)^{0.4}\)

 

\( = 85.2 W/m^2 K\)

 

Since \(L_a/D_{Ha} = 13.8\), we must correct this heat transfer coefficient for entrance effects, per Eq. (6.68).

 

\(\overline{Nu}_D = 4.82 + 0.0185 (Re_DPr)^{0.827}\) (6.68)

 

The correction factor is 1.377, so the corrected heat transfer coefficient is\( (1.377)(85.2) = 117 W/m^2 K =\overline{h} _{air}\)

\(\overline{h} _{gas}=(0.023)\frac{0.0623}{0.0154}(7850)^{0.8}(0.73)^{0.4}\)

 

\(= 107.1 W/m^2 K\)

 

Since \(L_g/D_{Hg }= 22.3\), we must correct this heat transfer coefficient for entrance effects, per Eq. (6.69).

 

\(\overline{Nu} _{D} = 3.0Re_D^{0.0833}\) (6.69)

The correction factor is \(1 +6(D_{Hg}/L_g) =1.27\), so the corrected heat transfer coefficient is \((1.27)(107.1) = 136 W/m^2 K= \overline{h} _{gas}\). The thermal resistance of the metal wall is negligible, therefore the overall conductance is

 

\(UA=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{\overline{h}_aA}+\frac{1}{\overline{h}_gA} } =\frac{1}{\frac{1}{(117 W/m^2 K)(2.52 m^2)}+\frac{1}{(136 W/m^2 K)(2.52 m^2)} }\)

 

= 158 W/K

The number of transfer units, based on the gas, which has the smaller heat capacity rate, is

 

\(NTU =\frac{UA}{C_{min}}=\frac{(158 W/K)}{(0.60 kg/s)(1101 J/kg K)}= 0.239\)

 

The heat capacity-rate ratio is

 

\(\frac{C_g}{C_a}= \frac{(0.60)(1101)}{(0.75)(1047)}= 0.841\)

 

and from Fig. 8.21, the effectiveness is approximately 0.13. Finally, the average outlet temperatures of the gas and air are

 

\(T_{gas out}= T{gas in}-\mathscr{E} \Delta T_{max}\)

 

\( = 1150 – 0.13(1150 – 290) = 1038 K\)

 

\(T_{air out}= T_{air in}+ \frac{C_g}{C_a}\mathscr{E}\Delta T_{max}= 290 + (0.841)(0.13)(1150 – 290)\)

 

= 384 K

A check on the average air-side and gas-side bulk temperatures gives values of 337 K and 1094 K. Performing a second iteration with property values based on these temperatures yields values sufficiently close to the assumed values (573 K, 973 K) to make a third approximation unnecessary. To appreciate the usefulness of the approach based on the concept of heat exchanger effectiveness, it is suggested that this same problem be worked out by trial and error, using Eq. (8.17) and the chart in Fig. 8.17.

 

\(q= UA\overline{\Delta T} \) (8.17)
