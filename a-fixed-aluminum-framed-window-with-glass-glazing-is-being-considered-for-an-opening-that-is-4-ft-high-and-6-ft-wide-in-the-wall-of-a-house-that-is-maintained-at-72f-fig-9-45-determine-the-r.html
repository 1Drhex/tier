Guest Question.

A fixed aluminum-framed window with glass glazing is being considered for an opening that is 4 ft high and 6 ft wide in the wall of a house that is maintained at \( 72^{\circ} F\) (Fig. 9-45). Determine the rate of heat loss through the window and the inner surface temperature of the window glass facing the room when the outdoor air temperature is \( 15^{\circ} F\) if the window is selected to be (a) \( \frac{1}{8}\) -in single glazing, (b) double glazing with an air space of \( \frac{1}{2}\) in, and (c) low-e-coated triple glazing with an air space of \( \frac{1}{2}\) in.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION The rate of heat loss through an aluminum framed window and the inner surface temperature are to be determined from the cases of singlepane, double-pane, and low-e triple-pane windows.

Assumptions 1 Steady operating conditions exist. 2 Heat transfer through the window is one-dimensional. 3 Thermal properties of the windows and the heat transfer coefficients are constant.

Properties The U-factors of the windows are given in Table (9-6).

Analysis The rate of heat transfer through the window can be determined from

\(\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}=U_{\text {overall }} A_{\text {window }}\left(T_{i}-T_{o}\right)\)

where \( T_{i} \) and \( T_{0} \) are the indoor and outdoor air temperatures, respectively; \( U_{\text {overall }} \) is the U-factor (the overall heat transfer coefficient) of the window; and \( A_{\text {window }} \) is the window area, which is determined to be

\(A_{\text {window }}=\text { Height } \times \text { Width }=(4 ft )(6 ft )=24 ft ^{2}\)

The U-factors for the three cases can be determined directly from Table 9-6 to be (6.63,3.51), and \( (1.92 W / m ^{2} \cdot K) \), respectively, to be multiplied by the factor (0.176) to convert them to \( Btu/h.ft ^{2} .{ }^{\circ} F \). Also, the inner surface temperature of the window glass can be determined from

\(\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}=h_{i} A_{\text {window }}\left(T_{i}-T_{\text {glass }}\right) \rightarrow T_{\text {glass }}=T_{i}-\frac{\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}}{h_{i} A_{\text {window }}}\)

 

\(\text { where } h_{j} \text { is the heat transfer coefficient on the inner surface of the window, which }\) \( \text { is determined from Table } 9-5 \text { to be }\)

 

\( h_{i}=8.3 W / m ^{2} \cdot K =1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \text { . }\)

 

Then the rate of heat loss and the interior glass temperature for each case are

determined as follows:

(a) Single glazing:

\(\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}=\left(6.63 \times 0.176 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)(72-15)^{\circ} F =1596 Btu / h\)

 

\(T_{\text {glass }}=T_{i}-\frac{\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}}{h_{i} A_{\text {window }}}=72^{\circ} F -\frac{1596 Btu / h }{\left(1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)}=26.5^{\circ} F\)

 

(b) Double glazing \( \left(\frac{1}{2}\right.) \) in air space):

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {window }}=\left(3.51 \times 0.176 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)(72-15)^{\circ} F =845 Btu / h\)

 

\( T_{ glass }=T_{i}-\frac{\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}}{h_{i} A _{\text {window }}}=72^{\circ} F -\frac{845 Btu / h }{\left(1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)}=47.9^{\circ} F\)

 

(c) Triple glazing \( \left(\frac{1}{2}\right.) \) in air space, low-e coated):

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {window }}=\left(1.92 \times 0.176 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2.0} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)(72-15)^{\circ} F =462 Btu / h \)

 

\( T_{\text {glass }}=T_{i}-\frac{\dot{Q}_{\text {window }}}{h_{i} A _{\text {window }}}=72^{\circ} F -\frac{462 Btu / h }{\left(1.46 Btu / h \cdot ft ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\left(24 ft ^{2}\right)}=58.8^{\circ} F\)

 

Therefore, heat loss through the window will be reduced by 47 percent in the case of double glazing and by 71 percent in the case of triple glazing relative to the single-glazing case. Also, in the case of single glazing, the low innerglass surface temperature will cause considerable discomfort in the occupants because of the excessive heat loss from the body by radiation. It is raised from \( 26.5^{\circ} F \), which is below freezing, to \( 47.9^{\circ} F \) in the case of double glazing and to \( 58.8^{\circ} F \) in the case of triple glazing.
