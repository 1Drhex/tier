Guest Question.

A chemical vapor deposition (CVD) process is used to deposit pure silicon thin films on wafers for electronic device applications. One way to deposit pure silicon onto a surface is by the heterogeneous surface reaction:

 

 

\( SiHCl _{3}(g) + H _{2}(g) \rightarrow Si (s) + 3 HCl (g) \)

 

In the present process, 1.0 mole% trichlorosilane (\( SiHCl _{3} \)) vapor (species A) diluted in \( H _{2} \) gas (species B) is fed to the CVD reactor shown in Figure to establish a bulk velocity of 200 cm/s. The trichlorosilane is reduced by the \( H _{2} \) gas to elemental silicon solid (Si), which is deposited as a thin film on a 15-cm by 15-cm wafer. Although the reaction produces HCl gas, it is significantly diluted by the \( H _{2} \) gas, and so the \( H _{2} \) gas can also be considered as the carrier gas. The flow over the silicon wafer is approximated as flow over a flat plate. The reaction process is maintained at 1.0 atm and 1200 K. The reactor contains elaborate safety systems to handle the toxic and flammable gases. At 1200 K, the surface reaction constant for the first-order decomposition reaction with respect to \( SiHCl _{3} \) vapor concentration is \( k_{s} = 0.83 cm / s, \) with the surface reaction rate law defined as \( R_{A, s}^{\prime} = - k_{s} c_{A s} \) based on the studies of Stein. Based on the above information, determine the average rate of solid silicon deposition onto the wafer surface.

Admin
Solution

The trichlorosilane must diffuse through the boundary layer created by flow of the feed gas over the wafer surface. At the wafer surface, the trichlorosilane will decompose to solid Si. The feed gas serves as the source for mass transfer for of \( SiHCl _{3} \), and the reaction at the Si surface serves as the sink. At steady state, the rate of \( SiHCl _{3} \) flux to the Si surface will be balanced by the rate of reaction at the surface:

 

\( N_{A} = k_{c}\left(c_{A, \infty} – c_{A s}\right) \)

 

and

\( N_{A} = – R_{A, s}^{\prime} \)

 

or

\( k_{c} \left(c_{A, \infty} – c_{A s}\right) = k_{s} c_{A s} \)

 

The concentration of trichlorosilane at the wafer surface, \( C_{A s} \), is unknown. Solving for \( C_{A s} \), we obtain

 

\( c_{A s} = c_{A, \infty} \frac{k_{c}}{k_{c} + k_{s}} \)

 

By substation of this relationship into the flux equation, \( c_{A s}\) is eliminated, and the flux equation is now

 

\( N_{A} = c_{A, \infty} \frac{k_{c} k_{s}}{k_{c} + k_{s}} \)

 

It is noted that as \( k_{s} \gg k_{c}, c_{A s} \rightarrow 0, \) and the process becomes convection mass-transfer rate limited. The average convective mass-transfer coefficient for flux of \( SiHCl _{3} \) across the boundary layer, \( k_{c} \), will now be estimated. At 1200 K and 1.0 atm, the gas-phase diffusion coefficient of trichlorosilane in \( H _{2}\left(D_{A B}\right) \) is \( 4.20 cm ^{2} / s, \) as determined by the Fuller–Shettler–Giddings correlation. The kinematic viscosity of the \( H _{2} \) carrier gas \( \left(\nu_{B}\right) \) is \( 11.098 cm ^{2} / s \) (Appendix I). Relevant dimensionless groups—Re, Sc, and Sh—must now be calculated. First, the Reynolds number (Re) is estimated for flow over a flat plate to determine the regime for flow:

 

\( \operatorname{Re}_{L}=\frac{v_{\infty} L}{\nu_{B}} = \frac{(200 cm / s )(15.0 cm )}{11.098 cm ^{2} / s } = 270.3 \)

 

The flow is laminar across the Si wafer, since \( \operatorname{Re}_{L} < 2.0 \times 10^{5} \) The Schmidt (Sc) is

 

\( Sc = \frac{\nu}{D_{A B}} \doteq \frac{\nu_{B}}{D_{A B}} = \frac{11.098 cm ^{2} / s }{4.20 cm ^{2} / s } = 2.64 \)

 

For laminar flow over a flat plate, the average Sherwood number (Sh) is

 

\( Sh = \frac{k_{c} L}{D_{A B}} = 0.664 Re ^{1 / 2} Sc ^{1 / 3} = 0.664 (270.3)^{1 / 2}(2.64)^{1 / 3} = 15.1 \)

 

The value for \( k_{c} \) is obtained from the Sherwood number:

 

\( k_{c}=\frac{\operatorname{Sh} D_{A B}}{L} = \frac{(15.1)\left(4.20 cm ^{2} / s \right)}{(15.0 cm )} = 4.23 cm / sec \)

 

With \( k_{c} \) known, the molar flux of \( SiHCl _{3}\) is determined. The consumption of \( SiHCl _{3}\) to Si deposition relative to the molar flow rate of \( SiHCl _{3}\) in the feed gas is assumed to be small. Therefore, the bulk concentration of \( SiHCl _{3}\) is considered to be constant, and is estimated by

 

\( c_{A, \infty} = y_{A, \infty} c = y_{A, \infty} \frac{P}{R T} = 0.01 \frac{(1.0 atm )}{\left(\frac{82.06 cm ^{3} \cdot atm }{ gmole \cdot K }\right)(1200 K )} = 1.016 \times 10^{-7} gmole / cm ^{3} \)

 

The gas-phase concentration of \( SiHCl _{3}\) at the surface is

 

\( c_{A s} = c_{A, \infty} \frac{k_{c}}{k_{c} + k_{s}} = 1.016 \times 10^{-7} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{3}} \frac{4.23 cm / s }{4.23 cm / s + 0.83 cm / s } = 8.49 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{3}} \)

 

The average flux of \( SiHCl _{3}\) is

 

\( N_{A} = 1.016 \times 10^{-7} gmole / cm ^{3} \frac{(4.23 cm / s )(0.83 cm / s )}{(4.23 cm / s ) + (0.83 cm / s )} = 7.05 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{2} \cdot s } \)

 

The silicon deposition rate, expressed as the rate of Si film thickness vs. time, is readily calculated from the \( SiHCl _{3}\) flux:

 

\( r_{S i} = \frac{N_{A} v_{S i} M_{S i}}{\rho_{S i}} = \frac{\left(7.05 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{2} \cdot s }\right)\left(\frac{1 gmole Si }{1 gmole SiHCl _{3}}\right)\left(\frac{28 g Si }{ gmole Si }\right)\left(\frac{3600 s }{1 h }\right)\left(\frac{10^{4} \mu m }{1.0 cm }\right)}{\left(2.33 \frac{ g Si }{ cm ^{3}}\right)} \)

 

\( = 30.5 \mu m / h \)

 

where \( \rho_{S i} \) is the density of solid silicon, \( M_{S i} \) is the molecular weight of Si, and \( \nu_{ Si }\) is the stoichiometric coefficient for conversion of \( SiHCl _{3}\) to Si. Note that the rate of Si thin film thickness formation is measured at the micron scale \( (\mu m ) \). Since the flux is based on a mean \( k_{c} \) averaged over the whole length of the wafer, the thin film deposition rate also represents an average rate from x = 0 to x = L. However, for flow over a flat plate, the local mass-transfer coefficient \( k_{c, x} \) will decrease proportional to \( x^{1 / 2} \) as x increases from x > 0 to x = L = 15 cm. In contrast, the concentration boundary-layer thickness \( \left(\delta_{ c }\right) \) will increase as x increases. For laminar flow, the working equations are

 

\( S h_{x} = \frac{k_{c, x} x}{D_{A B}} = 0.332 \operatorname{Re}^{1 / 2} S c^{1 / 3} \)

 

\( k_{c, x} = \frac{0.332 D_{A B}}{x}\left(\frac{ v _{\infty} x}{v_{B}}\right)^{1 / 2} S c^{1 / 3} \)

 

\( \delta_{c} = \delta\left(S c^{-1 / 3}\right) = \frac{5 x}{S c^{1 / 3} \sqrt{\operatorname{Re}_{x}}} \)
