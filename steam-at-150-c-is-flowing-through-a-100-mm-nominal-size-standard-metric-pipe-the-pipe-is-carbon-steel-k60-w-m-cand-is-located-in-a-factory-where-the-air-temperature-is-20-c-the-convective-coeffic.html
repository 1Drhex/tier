Guest Question.

Steam at 150 C is flowing through a 100 mm nominal size standard metric pipe. The pipe is carbon steel (k=60 W/m C)and is located in a factory where the air temperature is 20 C. The convective coefficient between the pipe and the room airnis 4 \(W/m^{2}\) C. The pipe is 50 m long and is uninsulated.
(a) What is the rate of heat transfer from the bare pipe to the room air?
(b) Insulation is added to the pipe to reduce the heat transfer. The insulation is 51 mm thick and has a conductivity of0.05 W/m C. What is the new rate of heat transfer?
You may make the following assumptions: Radiation heat transfer from the pipe is negligible. The insulated pipe has the same convective coefficient at its outer surface as the uninsulated pipe. The temperature of the inside surface of the pipe is essentially the same as the steam temperature.

Admin
Solution

The uninsulated pipe is shown in Fig. 3.13.
From an Internet search, the outside diameter of the pipe is 114.3 mm and the inside diameter is 102.26 mm. Therefore,

\(r_{1}\) = 0.05113 m and \(r_{2}\) = 0.05715 m. There are two resistances between the two known temperatures \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{\infty _{0} } \): the resistance of the pipe wall and the convective resistance at the outside surface of the pipe. The circuit for the problem is shown in Fig. 3.14.
From the circuit drawing and the resistance technique, we have

\(q=\frac{\Delta T_{overall} }{\sum{R} } = \frac{T_{1}-T_{\infty _{0} } }{\frac{\ln (r_{2}/r_{1} )}{2\pi KL}+\frac{1}{h_{0}(2\pi r_{2}L ) } } \)………………..(3.59)

Putting values into Eq. (3.59), we have

\(q= \frac{150-20}{\frac{\ln (0.05715/0.05113)}{2\cdot \pi \cdot 60\cdot 50}+\frac{1}{4\cdot (2\cdot \pi \cdot 0.05715\cdot 50)} } = 9332W\)

The insulated pipe is shown in Fig. 3.15. There is an added 51 mm layer of insulation, making
\(r_{3}\)=\(r_{2}\) +0.051 = 0.10815 m. The area at the outer surface of the insulation is now 2π\(r_{3}\)L rather than 2π\(r_{2}\)L.

The added layer of insulation adds a term to the denominator of Eq. (3.59). The new equation is

\(q = \frac{T_{1}-T_{\infty _{0} } }{\frac{\ln (r_{2}/r_{1} )}{2\pi K_{pipe} L}+\frac{\ln(r_{3}/r_{2}) }{2\pi K_{ins} L} +\frac{1}{h_{0}(2\pi r_{3}L ) } } \)…..(3.60)

When values are entered into Eq. (3.60), it is seen that the new heat transfer rate is 2710 W. Addition of insulation hasdecreased the heat transfer from 9332 to 2710 W, a decrease of 71%.
