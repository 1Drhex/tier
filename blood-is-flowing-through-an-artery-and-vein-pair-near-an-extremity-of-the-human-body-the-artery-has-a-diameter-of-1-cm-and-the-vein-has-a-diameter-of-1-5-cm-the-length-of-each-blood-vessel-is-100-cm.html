Guest Question.

Blood is flowing through an artery and vein pair near an extremity of the human body. The artery has a diameter of 1 cm and the vein has a diameter of 1.5 cm. The length of each blood vessel is 100 cm. Blood is flowing through the artery at a mass flow rate of 2 g / s and is flowing through the vein at a mass flow rate of 1.8 g / s. The average temperature of blood in the artery is \( 34^{\circ} C\) and the average temperature of blood in the vein is \( 32^{\circ} C\). The inflow temperature of the artery is \( 36^{\circ} C\) and the external environment temperature is \( 20^{\circ} G\). Determine the overall heat transfer coefficient for blood and the change in temperature with respect to distance for these blood flow conditions.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION Blood in the artery is being cooled, while blood in the vein is being warmed. Blood flows in a counter-current manner and the overall heat transfer coefficient for blood and the change in temperature for these blood flow conditions are to be determined. Assumptions 1 The flow through each blood vessel is fully developed. 2 Properties of blood are constant and equal in the artery and the vein. 3 Constant surface heat flux. 4 The conductances to the environment from the artery and the vein are equal.

Properties The properties of blood are as follows

\( k=0.67 \frac{ W }{ m \cdot K } \)

 

\( c_{p}=3.475 \frac{ kJ }{ kg \cdot K } \)

 

\( \rho=1050 \frac{ kg }{ m ^{3}} \)

 

\( \mu=0.035 \frac{ g }{ s \cdot cm }\)

 

Analysis The schematic of the blood vessels are given in Figure (11-36). The overall heat transfer coefficient can be calculated from

\( \frac{1}{U}=\frac{1}{h_{\text {artery }}}+\frac{1}{h_{\text {vein }}}\)

which is a modification of Eq. (11-5 .)

\(\frac{1}{U} \approx \frac{1}{h_{i}}+\frac{1}{h_{o}}\)

The convection heat transfer coefficients can be determined using the forced convection relationships. Using Eq. (8-5)

\(\operatorname{Re}=\frac{V_{\text {avg }} D}{\nu}=\frac{\rho V_{\text {avg }} D}{\mu}=\frac{\rho D}{\mu}\left(\frac{\dot{m}}{\rho \pi D^{2} / 4}\right)=\frac{4 \dot{m}}{\mu \pi D}\),

the Reynolds number for blood in the artery is

\(Re =\frac{4 \dot{m}}{\mu \pi D}=\frac{4\left(2 \frac{ g }{ s }\right)}{\left(0.035 \frac{ g }{ s \cdot cm }\right)(\pi)(1 cm )}=72.8\)

And the Reynolds number for blood in the vein is

\( Re =\frac{4 \dot{m}}{\mu \pi D}=\frac{4\left(1.8 \frac{ g }{ s }\right)}{\left(0.035 \frac{ g }{ s \cdot cm }\right)(\pi)(1.5 cm )}=43.7\)

The flow is laminar ( Re <2300) in both the artery and vein. Therefore, since the flow is fully-developed, laminar with constant surface heat flux boundary condition, the Nusselt number for each of these flow conditions is 4.36 as given by Eq. (8-60)

\(\text { Circular tube, laminar }\left(\dot{q}_{s}=\text { constant }\right): \quad Nu =\frac{h D}{k}=4.36\)

. The convection heat transfer coefficients can be calculated from

\( h_{\text {artery }}=\frac{k}{D} N u =\frac{0.67 \frac{ W }{ m \cdot K }}{1 cm }(4.36)=292 \frac{ W }{ m ^{2} \cdot K } \)

 

\( h_{\text {vein }}=\frac{k}{D} Nu =\frac{0.67 \frac{ W }{ m \cdot K }}{1.5 cm }(4.36)=195 \frac{ W }{ m ^{2} \cdot K }\)

Therefore, the overall heat transfer coefficient is equal to

\( U=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{h_{\text {artery }}}+\frac{1}{h_{\text {vein }}}}=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{292.12 \frac{ W }{ m ^{2} \cdot K }}+\frac{1}{194.75 \frac{ W }{ m ^{2} \cdot K }}}=116.9 \frac{ W }{ m ^{2} \cdot K }\)

If we solve Eq. 11-44,

\(\dot{m} c_{p} \frac{d T_{A}}{d x}+\left(U A_{s}\right)_{A}\left(T_{A}-T_{V}\right)+\left(U A_{s}\right)_{A}\left(T_{A}-T_{\infty}\right)=0 \quad \text { Artery }\)

 

\(\dot{m} c_{p} \frac{d T_{V}}{d x}+\left(U A_{s}\right)_{A}\left(T_{A}-T_{V}\right)-\left(U A_{s}\right)_{V}\left(T_{V}-T_{\infty}\right)=0 \quad \text { Vein }\)

we get

\(\frac{T_{A}-T_{\infty}}{T_{0}-T_{\infty}}=\frac{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)} \cosh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{L}\right)+\sinh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{L}\right)}{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)} \cosh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)\right)+\sinh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)}\right.} \) \(\frac{T_{V}-T_{\infty}}{T_{0}-T_{\infty}}=\frac{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)} \cosh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{L}\right)-\sinh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{L}\right)}{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)} \cosh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)}\right)+\sinh \left(N_{0} \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{N_{i}}{N_{o}}\right)\right)}\right.}\)

where

\( N_{o}=\frac{\left(U A_{s}\right)_{A} L}{\dot{m} c_{p}} \cong \frac{\left(U A_{s}\right)_{V} L}{\dot{m} c_{p}}=0.00132 \)

 

\( N_{i}=\frac{\left(U A_{s}\right)_{A V} L}{\dot{m} c_{p}}=1.06\)

where (L) is the total length of interest of the blood vessel and the subscripts either denote artery (A) or vein (V) or the combined vascular pair (A V). The solution to Eq. 11-44 follows a similar form as that discussed for the fin equation in Chapter 3 (see Section (3-6 ) and is closely related to the solution for convection from the fin tip. Solving the two preceding equations for our conditions, we get

\(\frac{T_{A}-20^{\circ} C }{36^{\circ} C -20^{\circ} C }\) \(=\frac{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)} \cosh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)+\sinh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)}{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)} \cosh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)\right)+\sinh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)\right)}\right.}\right.}\) \(\frac{T_{v}-20^{\circ} C }{36^{\circ} C -20^{\circ} C }\) \(=\frac{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)} \cosh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)-\sinh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)}\right)\left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)}{\sqrt{1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)} \cosh \left(0.00132 m \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)\right)+\sinh \left(0.00132 \sqrt{\left.1+2\left(\frac{1.06}{0.00132}\right)\right)}\right.}\right.}\)

Carrying out the algebraic operations, we can find an expression for the variation in temperature along the blood vessel length.

\( T_{A}=16^{\circ} C \left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)+20^{\circ} C \)

 

\( T_{V}=15.97^{\circ} C \left(1-\frac{x}{100 cm }\right)+20^{\circ} C\)

Discussion The temperature variation along the length of the blood vessel varies linearly with distance. We have encountered similar solutions for internal fully developed flow under constant surface heat flux boundary conditions (Fig. 8-11) and for heat exchangers with two fluid streams that have the same heat capacity (Fig. 11-13). We made the assumption that the fluid properties were the same in the artery and the vein and would change significantly with temperature. From the two temperature variation equations we can calculate the temperature along the blood vessel length. When (x=0 cm) the temperatures in the artery and vein are \( 36^{\circ} C\) and \( 35.9^{\circ} C\), respectively (remember that the length would be relative to the inlet of the artery). When x=50 cm, the temperatures in the artery and vein are \( 28^{\circ} C\) and \( 27.9^{\circ} C\), respectively. Finally, when x=100 cm, the temperature in the artery and the vein are equal and are \( 20^{\circ} C\), which is the environmental temperature.
