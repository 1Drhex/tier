Guest Question.

In this problem we treat the electron–electron repulsion term in the helium Hamiltonian (Equation 5.38) as a perturbation,

\( \hat{H}=\left\{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla_{1}^{2}-\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{2 e^{2}}{r_{1}}\right\}+\left\{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla_{2}^{2}-\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{2 e^{2}}{r_{2}}\right\}+\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{e^{2}}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} \) (5.38).

\( H^{\prime}=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{e^{2}}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} \).

(This will not be very accurate, because the perturbation is not small, in comparison to the Coulomb attraction of the nucleus …but it’s a start.)

(a) Find the first-order correction to the ground state,

\( \psi_{0}\left( r _{1}, r _{2}\right)=\psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right) \).

(You have already done this calculation, if you worked — only we didn’t call it perturbation theory back then.)

(b) Now treat the first excited state, in which one electron is in the hydrogenic ground state, \( \psi_{100} \), and the other is in the state \( \psi_{200} \).
Actually, there are two such states, depending on whether the electron spins occupy the singlet configuration (parahelium) or the triplet (orthohelium):

\( \psi_{\pm}\left( r _{1}, r _{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) \pm \psi_{200}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right)\right] \) .

Show that

\( E_{\pm}^{1}=\frac{1}{2}(K \pm J) \).

where

\( K \equiv 2 \int \psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) H^{\prime} \psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) d^{3} r _{1} d^{3} r _{2} \).

\( J \equiv 2 \int \psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) H^{\prime} \psi_{200}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right) d^{3} r _{1} d^{3} r _{2} \).

Evaluate these two integrals, put in the actual numbers, and compare your results with Figure 5.2 (the measured energies are -59.2 eV and -58.4 eV).

Solution

(a) The first order correction to the ground state is

\( E_{0}^{1}=\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle=\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} a}\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\frac{a}{r}\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle \).

where \( r=\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right| \). The matrix element evaluates to 5/4; see the solution for for the details.
Therefore

\( E_{0}^{1}=\frac{5}{2} \frac{e^{2}}{8 \pi \epsilon_{0} a}=\frac{5}{2}\left|E_{1}\right| \).

\( \text { where }\left|E_{1}\right|=13.6 eV \).

(b) Now we turn to the degenerate first-excited states. The states are

\( \psi_{\pm}\left( r _{1}, r _{2}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) \pm \psi_{200}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right)\right] \).

Of course we need to worry about whether to use degenerate perturbation theory. It turns out its unnecessary.
The Hamiltonian is invariant under the interchange of \( r _{1} \text { and } r _{2} \) and these two states are even and odd under that interchange so they won’t be mixed by the perturbation. We may therefore compute

\( E_{\pm}^{1}=\left\langle\psi_{\pm}\left|\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r}\right| \psi_{\pm}\right\rangle \).

\( =\frac{1}{2} \iint\left[\psi_{100}^{*}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}^{*}\left( r _{2}\right) \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} \psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right)\right. \) \( \pm \psi_{100}^{*}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}^{*}\left( r _{2}\right) \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} \psi_{200}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right) \) \( \pm \psi_{200}^{*}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}^{*}\left( r _{2}\right) \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} \psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right) \)

\( \left.+\psi_{200}^{*}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}^{*}\left( r _{2}\right) \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} \psi_{200}\left( r _{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left( r _{2}\right)\right] d^{3} r _{1} d^{3} r _{2} \).

The orbitals are real so we can drop the complex conjugation. Additionally, we can interchange \( r _{1} \text { and } r _{2} \) in the third and fourth terms to see that they are equal to the first two integrals and therefore

\( E_{\pm}^{1}=\frac{1}{2}(K \pm J) \).

with the integrals as given in the problem statement.
Now we turn to the business of actually evaluating these two integrals

\( K=2 \int\left|\psi_{200}\left( r _{2}\right)\right|^{2} \underbrace{\int\left|\psi_{100}\left( r _{1}\right)\right|^{2} \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} d r _{1}}_{\equiv V_{1}} d r _{2} \).

where

\( V_{1}=\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} a} \int \frac{8}{\pi a^{3}} e^{-4 r_{1} / a} \frac{a}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} d r _{1} \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{16}{a^{3}} \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-4 r_{1} / a} \underbrace{\int_{0}^{\pi} \frac{a \sin \theta_{1}}{\sqrt{r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}-2 r_{1} r_{2} \cos \theta_{1}}} d \theta_{1}}_{Z_{1}} r_{1}^{2} d r_{1}, \)

having already done the trivial 2 π integral. Next we compute (setting \( u = \cos θ\) )

\( Z_{1}=\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{a}{\sqrt{r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}-2 r_{1} r_{2} u}} d u \).

\( =-\left.\frac{a}{r_{1} r_{2}} \sqrt{r_{1}^{2}+r_{2}^{2}-2 r_{1}, r_{2} u}\right|_{-1} ^{1} \).

\( =\frac{a}{r_{1} r_{2}}\left(r_{1}+r_{2}-\left|r_{1}-r_{2}\right|\right) \).

\( = \begin{cases}2 a / r_{1} & r_{1}>r_{2} \\ 2 a / r_{2} & r_{1}<r_{2}\end{cases} \).

Returning with this result to the expression for \( V_1\) we have

\( V_{1}=2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{32}{a^{3}}\left[\int_{0}^{r_{2}} e^{-4 r_{1} / a} \frac{a}{r_{2}} r_{1}^{2} d r_{1}+\int_{r_{2}}^{\infty} e^{-4 r_{1} / a} a r_{1} d r_{1}\right] \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{32}{a^{3}}\left(\frac{a}{4}\right)^{3}\left[\frac{a}{r_{2}} \int_{0}^{4 r_{2} / a} z^{2} e^{-z} d z+4 \int_{4 r_{2} / a}^{\infty} z e^{-z} d z\right] \).

\( =\left|E_{1}\right|\left\{\frac{a}{r_{2}}\left[2-\left(2+\frac{8 r_{2}}{a}+\left(\frac{4 r_{2}}{a}\right)^{2}\right) e^{-4 r_{2} / a}\right]\right. \)

\( \left.+4\left(1+\frac{4 r_{2}}{a}\right) e^{-4 r_{2} / a}\right\} \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right|\left[\frac{a}{r_{2}}-\left(2+\frac{a}{r_{2}}\right) e^{-4 r_{2} / a}\right] \).

\( \text { Now since } V_{1} \text { and } \psi_{200} \text { depend only on } r_{2}, \text { not } \theta_{2}, \phi_{2} \text { we have } \)

\( K=8 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty}\left|\psi_{200}\left(r_{2}\right)\right|^{2} V_{1}\left(r_{2}\right) r_{2}^{2} d r_{2} \).

\( =8 \pi 2\left|E_{1}\right| \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\pi a^{3}}\left(1-\frac{r_{2}}{a}\right)^{2} e^{-2 r_{2} / a}\left[\frac{a}{r_{2}}-\left(2+\frac{a}{r_{2}}\right) e^{-4 r_{2} / a}\right] r_{2}^{2} d r_{2} \).

\( =\frac{16}{a^{3}}\left|E_{1}\right|\left[\int_{0}^{\infty} a r_{2}\left(1-\frac{r_{2}}{a}\right)^{2} e^{-2 r_{2} / a} d r_{2}\right. \)

\( \left.-\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(2 r_{2}^{2}+a r_{2}\right)\left(1-\frac{r_{2}}{a}\right)^{2} e^{-6 r_{2} / a} d r_{2}\right] \).

Now we’ll perform a variable substitution where \( z=2 r_{2} / a \text { in the first integral and } z=6 r_{2} / a \) in the second integral. We then have

\( K=\frac{16}{a^{3}}\left|E_{1}\right|\left[\int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{a^{2} z}{2}\left(1-\frac{z}{2}\right)^{2} e^{-z} \frac{a}{2} d z\right. \)

\( \left.-\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{18} a^{2} z^{2}+\frac{1}{6} a^{2} z\right)\left(1-\frac{z}{6}\right)^{2} e^{-z} \frac{a}{6} d z\right] \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{16}{9} z-2 z^{2}+\frac{14}{27} z^{3}-\frac{1}{486} z^{4}\right) e^{-z} d z \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right|\left(\frac{16}{9}-4+\frac{42}{9}-\frac{4}{81}\right) \).

and

\( K=\frac{136}{81}\left|E_{1}\right| \).

Next we compute

\( J=2 \int \psi_{200}\left(r_{2}\right) \psi_{100}\left(r_{2}\right) \underbrace{\int \psi_{200}\left(r_{1}\right) \psi_{100}\left(r_{1}\right) \frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} d^{3} r _{1}}_{\equiv V_{2}} d^{3} r _{2} \).

Here

\( V_{2}=\frac{e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} a} \frac{2 \sqrt{2}}{\pi a^{3}} \int\left(1-\frac{r_{1}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{1} / a} \frac{a}{\left| r _{1}- r _{2}\right|} d r _{1} \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{2 \sqrt{2}}{\pi a^{3}} 2 \pi \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(1-\frac{r_{1}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{1} / a} Z_{1}\left(r_{1}\right) r_{1}^{2} d r_{1} \).

where the \( 2 \pi \text { is from the } \phi \text { integral and where the function } Z_{1} \) is the same one that appeared in the computation of K. Then

\( V_{2}=2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{8 \sqrt{2}}{a^{3}}\left[\int_{0}^{r_{2}} \frac{a r_{1}^{2}}{r_{2}}\left(1-\frac{r_{1}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{1} / a} d r_{1}\right. \)

\( \left.+\int_{r_{2}}^{\infty} a r_{1}\left(1-\frac{r_{1}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{1} / a} d r_{1}\right] \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{8 \sqrt{2}}{a^{3}}\left[\int_{0}^{3 r_{2} / a} \frac{a^{3} z^{2}}{9 r_{2}}\left(1-\frac{z}{3}\right) e^{-z} \frac{a}{3} d z\right. \)

\( \left.+\int_{3 r_{2} / a}^{\infty} \frac{a^{2} z}{3}\left(1-\frac{z}{3}\right) e^{-z} \frac{a}{3} d z\right] \).

with the variable change \( z=3 r_{1} / a \text {. Then } \)

\( V_{2}=2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{8 \sqrt{2}}{9}\left[\frac{a}{3 r_{2}} \int_{0}^{3 r_{2} / a}\left(z^{2}-\frac{z^{3}}{3}\right) e^{-z} \frac{a}{3} d z\right. \)

\( \left.+\int_{3 r_{2} / a}^{\infty}\left(z-\frac{z^{2}}{3}\right) e^{-z} \frac{a}{3} d z\right] \).

\( =2\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{8 \sqrt{2}}{9}\left(\frac { a } { 3 r _ { 2 } } \left\{2-\left(2+\frac{6 r_{2}}{a}+\frac{9 r_{2}^{2}}{a^{2}}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a}\right.\right. \) \( \left.-\left[2-\left(2+\frac{6 r_{2}}{a}+\frac{9 r_{2}^{2}}{a^{2}}+\frac{9 r_{2}^{3}}{a^{3}}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a}\right]\right\}+\left(1+\frac{3 r_{1}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a} \)

\( \left.-\frac{1}{3}\left(2+\frac{6 r_{2}}{a}+\frac{9 r_{2}^{2}}{a^{2}}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a}\right) \).

\( =\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{16 \sqrt{2}}{27}\left(1+\frac{3 r_{2}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a} \).

We now plug this back into our expression for J. Once again the angular integration is trivial and

\( J=8 \pi \int \psi_{200}\left(r_{2}\right) \psi_{100}\left(r_{2}\right) V_{2}\left(r_{2}\right) r_{2}^{2} d r_{2} \).

\( =8 \pi\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{16 \sqrt{2}}{27} \frac{2 \sqrt{2}}{\pi a^{3}} \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(1-\frac{r_{2}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a}\left(1+\frac{3 r_{2}}{a}\right) e^{-3 r_{2} / a} r_{2}^{2} d r_{2} \).

\( =\frac{512}{27}\left|E_{1}\right| \frac{1}{a^{3}}\left(\frac{a^{3}}{6}\right) \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(1-\frac{z}{6}\right)\left(1+\frac{z}{2}\right) e^{-z} z^{2} d z \).

\( =\frac{64}{729}\left|E_{1}\right| \int_{0}^{\infty}\left(z^{2}+\frac{z^{3}}{3}-\frac{1}{12} z^{4}\right) e^{-z} d z \).

\( =\frac{64}{729}\left|E_{1}\right|(2+2-2) \).

and

\( J=\frac{128}{729}\left|E_{1}\right| \).

So the energies are

\( E^{0}+\frac{1}{2}(K+J)=4 E_{1}+4 \underbrace{E_{2}}_{E_{1} / 4}-\frac{68}{81} E_{1}-\frac{64}{729} E_{1}=\frac{2969}{729} E_{1}=-55.39 eV \).

for the symmetric spatial state (parahelium) and

\( E^{0}+\frac{1}{2}(K-J)=4 E_{1}+4 \underbrace{E_{2}}_{E_{1} / 4}-\frac{68}{81} E_{1}+\frac{64}{729} E_{1}=\frac{3097}{729} E_{1}=-57.78 eV \).

for the antisymmetric spatial state (orthohelium). Looking at Figure 5.1, this calculation correctly predicts that orthohelium is lower in energy, roughly by the correct amount.
