Guest Question.

The irreversible reaction

\(A+B=A B 10\)

has been studied kinetically, and the rate of formation of product has been found to be well correlated by the following rate equation:

\(r_{\mathrm{AB}}=k C_{\mathrm{B}}^{2} \ldots\) independent of \(C_{\mathrm{A}} 11\)

What reaction mechanism is suggested by this rate expression if the chemistry of the reaction suggests that the intermediate consists of an association of reactant molecules and that a chain reaction does not occur?

Admin
Solution

If this were an elementary reaction, the rate would be given by

\(r_{\mathrm{AB}}=k C_{\mathrm{A}} C_{\mathrm{B}}=k[\mathrm{~A}][\mathrm{B}] 12\)

Since Eqs. 11 and 12 are not of the same type, the reaction evidently is nonelementary. Consequently, let us try various mechanisms and see which gives a rate expression similar in form to the experimentally found expression. We start with simple two-step models, and if these are unsuccessful we will try more complicated three-, four-, or five-step models.

Model 1. Hypothesize a two-step reversible scheme involving the formation of an intermediate substance \(\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*},\) not actually seen and hence thought to be present only in small amounts. Thus,

\(\begin{array}{c}2 \mathrm{~A} \underset{k_{2}}{\stackrel{k_{1}}{\longrightarrow}} \mathrm{A}_{2}^{*} \\\mathrm{~A}_{2}^{*}+\mathrm{B} \frac{k_{3}}{{ }_{k_{4}}} \mathrm{~A}+\mathrm{AB}\end{array} 13\)

which really involves four elementary reactions

\(2 \mathrm{~A} \stackrel{k_{1}}{\longrightarrow} \mathrm{A}_{2}^{*} 14\)

 

\(\begin{array}{c}\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*} \stackrel{k_{2}}{\longrightarrow} 2 \mathrm{~A} 15\\\mathrm{~A}_{2}^{*}+\mathrm{B} \stackrel{k_{3}}{\longrightarrow} \mathrm{A}+\mathrm{AB} 16 \\\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{AB} \stackrel{k_{4}}{\longrightarrow}\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*}+\mathrm{B} 17\end{array}\)

Let the \(k\) values refer to the components disappearing; thus, \(k_{1}\) refers to \(\mathrm{A}, k_{2}\) refers to \(\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*},\) etc.

Now write the expression for the rate of formation of \(\mathrm{AB}\). Since this component is involved in Eqs. 16 and 17 , its overall rate of change is the sum of the individual rates. Thus,

\(r_{\mathrm{AB}}=k_{3}\left[\mathrm{~A}_{2}^{*}\right][\mathrm{B}]-k_{4}[\mathrm{~A}][\mathrm{AB}] 18\)

Because the concentration of intermediate \(\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*}\) is so small and not measurable, the above rate expression cannot be tested in its present form. So, replace \(\left[\mathrm{A}_{2}^{\left(\frac{1}{2}\right]}\right.\) by concentrations that can be measured, such as \([\mathrm{A}],[\mathrm{B}],\) or \([\mathrm{AB}]\). This is done in the following manner. From the four elementary reactions that all involve \(\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*}\) we find

\(r_{\mathrm{A}_{2}}=\frac{1}{2} k_{1}[\mathrm{~A}]^{2}-k_{2}\left[\mathrm{~A}_{2}^{*}\right]-k_{3}\left[\mathrm{~A}_{2}^{*}\right][\mathrm{B}]+k_{4}[\mathrm{~A}][\mathrm{A} \mathrm{B}] 19\)

Because the concentration of \(\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*}\) is always extremely small we may assume that its rate of change is zero or

\(r_{\mathrm{A}_{2}}=0 20\)

This is the steady-state approximation. Combining Eqs. 19 and 20 we then find

\(\left[\mathrm{A}_{2}^{*}\right]=\frac{\frac{1}{2} k_{1}[\mathrm{~A}]^{2}+k_{4}[\mathrm{~A}][\mathrm{AB}]}{k_{2}+k_{3}[\mathrm{~B}]} 21\)

which, when replaced in Eq. 18 , simplifying and cancelling two terms (two terms will always cancel if you are doing it right), gives the rate of formation of \(\mathrm{AB}\) in terms of measurable quantities. Thus,

\(r_{A B}=\frac{\frac{1}{2} k_{1} k_{3}[\mathrm{~A}]^{2}[\mathrm{~B}]-k_{2} k_{4}[\mathrm{~A}][\mathrm{AB}]}{k_{2}+k_{3}[\mathrm{~B}]} 22\)

In searching for a model consistent with observed kinetics we may, if we wish, restrict a more general model by arbitrarily selecting the magnitude of the various rate constants. Since Eq. 22 does not match Eq. 11 , let us see if any of its simplified forms will. Thus, if \(k_{2}\) is very small, this expression reduces to

\(r_{\mathrm{AB}}=\frac{1}{2} k_{1}[\mathrm{~A}]^{2} 23\)

If \(k_{4}\) is very small, \(r_{\text {AB }}\) reduces to

\(r_{A B}=\frac{\left(k_{1} k_{3} / 2 k_{2}\right)[\mathrm{A}]^{2}[\mathrm{~B}]}{1+\left(k_{3} / k_{2}\right)[\mathrm{B}]} 24\)

Neither of these special forms, Eqs. 23 and \(24,\) matches the experimentally found rate, Eq. 11. Thus, the hypothesized mechanism, Eq. 13 , is incorrect, so another needs to be tried.

Model 2. First note that the stoichiometry of Eq. 10 is symmetrical in \(A\) and \(\mathrm{B},\) so just interchange \(\mathrm{A}\) and \(\mathrm{B}\) in Model \(1,\) put \(k_{2}=0\) and we will get \(r_{\mathrm{AB}}=\) \(k[\mathrm{~B}]^{2},\) which is what we want. So the mechanism that will match the secondorder rate equation is.

We are fortunate in this example to have represented our data by a form of equation which happened to match exactly that obtained from the theoretical mechanism. Often a number of different equation types will fit a set of experimental data equally well, especially for somewhat scattered data. Hence, to avoid rejecting the correct mechanism, it is advisable to test the fit of the various theoretically derived equations to the raw data using statistical criteria whenever possible, rather than just matching equation forms.
