Guest Question.

An insulated steam pipe is routed horizontally through an unheated room, then vertically to the ceiling. The pipe is insulated, and the outside diameter of the insulation is 15 cm. The horizontal and vertical lengths of pipe are both 4 m long. The outside-surface temperature of the insulation is \(50^{\circ } C\),and the room temperature is \(5^{\circ } C\).How much heat is lost from the insulation by convection?

Admin
Solution

The steam loses energy as it travels through the pipe, and it is likely that the surface temperature of the insulation does not remain constant. The insulation probably loses a portion of energy by radiation to the surroundings as well as by convection. The insulation surface may not be perfectly cylindrical

 

Assumptions

1. The entire surface of insulation is at \(50^{\circ } C.\)

2. The air has constant properties evaluated at \(\left(50+5\right)/2 =275^{\circ } C or\cong 300K.\)

3. Heat is lost only by convection from the insulation surface.
4. The insulation surface is a cylinder.
5. The system is at steady state.
From Appendix Table D.1, for air at 300 K,

 

\(\rho =1.177kg/m^{3} \) \( k_{f} =0.02624W/\left(m.K\right)\)

 

 

\(c_{p} =1005.7J/\left(kg.K\right)\) \( \alpha =0.22160\times 10^{-4} m^{2} /s\)

 

 

\(\nu =15.68\times 10^{-6} m^{2} /s\) Pr=0.708

 

For air,

\(\beta =1/\left(5+273\right) =0.0036/K\)

The Rayleigh number for the vertical or the horizontal runs of pipe is calculated as

\(Ra_{D} =\frac{g_{x}\beta \left(T_{w}-T_{\infty } \right)D^{3} }{\nu \alpha }=\frac{9.81\left(0.0036\right) \left(50-5\right)\left(15\right) ^{3} }{\left(15.68\times 10^{-6} \right)\left(0.22160\times 10^{-4} \right) } \)

 

or

 

\(Ra_{D}=1.54\times 10^{7} \)

For the horizontal pipe, we use Equation 8.26:

\(\overline{Nu} _{D} =\frac{\overline{h} _{c}D }{k_{f} } =\left\{0.60 +\frac{0.387Ra^{1/6}_{D} }{\left[1+\left\lgroup\frac{0.559}{Pr} \right\rgroup ^{9/16} \right] ^{8/27} }\right\}^2 \)

 

Rearranging and substituting

\(\overline{h} _{c} =\frac{{0.02624} }{0.15 } \left\{0.6 +\frac{0.387\left(1.54\times 10^{7} \right) ^{1/6} }{\left[1+\left\lgroup\frac{0.559}{0.708} \right\rgroup ^{9/16} \right] ^{8/27} }\right\}^2 \)

 

Solving

\(\overline{h} _{c}=5.62W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

 

The heat transferred from the horizontal length of 4 m is

\(q_{hor} =h_{c} A_{s} \left(T_{w}-T_{\infty } \right) =5.62\left(\pi \right) \left(0.15\right) \left(4\right) \left(50-5\right)\)

 

\(q_{hor}=476W\)

 

For the vertical pipe, we use Equation 8.25a:

\(\overline{Nu} _{D} =\frac{\overline{h} _{c}D }{k_{f} }=0.6\left\lgroup Ra_{D}\frac{D}{L} \right\rgroup ^{1/4} \)

 

Rearranging and substituting

\(\overline{h} _{c} =\frac{0.02624}{0.15} \left(0.6\right) \left\lgroup1.54\times 10^{7}\frac{0.15}{4} \right\rgroup ^{1/4}\)

or

\(\overline{h} _{c}=2.89W/\left(m^{2}.K \right)\)

 

The heat loss from the vertical length of 4 m is

\(q_{ver} =2.89\left(\pi \right) \left(0.15\right) \left(4\right) \left(50-5\right) =245W\)

 

The total heat lost by the pipe is \(q_{hor}+q_{ver}\) , or

q=720W
