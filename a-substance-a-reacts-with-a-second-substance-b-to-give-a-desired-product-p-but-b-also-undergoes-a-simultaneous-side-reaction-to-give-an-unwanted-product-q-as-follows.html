Guest Question.

A substance A reacts with a second substance B to give a desired product P, but B also undergoes a simultaneous side reaction to give an unwanted product Q as follows:

A + B \(\rightarrow \) P; rate =\(K_{p}C_{A}C_{B}\)
2B \(\rightarrow \) Q; rate =\(K_{Q}C_{B}^{2}\)

where \(C_{A}\) and \(C_{B}\) are the concentrations of A and B respectively.
A single continuous stirred tank reactor is used for these reactions. A and B are mixed in equimolar proportions such that each has the concentration \(C_{0}\) in the combined stream fed at a volumetric flowrate \(\upsilon \) to the reactor. If the rate constants above are \(K_{P}=K_{Q}\) = k and the total conversion of B is 0.95, that is the concentration of B in the outflow is 0.05\(C_{0}\) , show that the volume of the reactor will be 69 \(\upsilon /KC_{0}\) and that the relative yield of P will be 0.82.
Would a simple tubular reactor give a larger or smaller yield of P than the C.S.T.R.?
What is the essential requirement for a high yield of P? Suggest any alternative modes of contacting the reactants A and B which would give better yields than either a single C.S.T.R. or a simple tubular reactor.

Admin
Solution

A material balance over the tank for material A at steady state gives:

\(\begin{matrix}\upsilon C_{A_{0}} – &\upsilon C_{A} – & K_{P}C_{A}C_{B}V =&0 \\ (in) &(out) &(reaction) &(accumulation) \end{matrix}\)

The fractional conversion of B, \(\beta \) = 0.95. If the fractional conversion of A is \(\alpha \) , noting that \(\alpha < \beta \) since A is consumed in the first reaction only, whereas B is consumed in both, then:

\(C_{A}=C_{A_{0}}(1-\alpha ) \) \(C_{B}=C_{B_{0}}(1-\beta ) \) \(C_{A_{0}}=C_{B_{0}}=C_{0}\)

and hence the material balance is:

\(\upsilon C_{0}-\upsilon C_{0}(1-\alpha )-K_{p}VC_{0}^{2}(1-\alpha )(1-\beta )=0\)

or: \(\alpha =(K_{P}C_{0}V/\upsilon )(1-\alpha )(1-\beta )\) (i)

Similarly, a material balance for material B is:

\(\begin{matrix}\upsilon C_{B_{0}} – &\upsilon C_{B} – & V(K_{P}C_{A}C_{B}+K_{Q}C_{B}^{2}) = &0 \\ (in) &(out) &(reaction) & (accumulation)\end{matrix}\)

or: \(\upsilon C_{0}-\upsilon C_{0}(1-\beta )-VC_{0}^{2}[K_{P}(1-\alpha )(1-\beta )+K_{Q}(1-\beta )^{2}]=0\)

Thus: \(\beta =(C_{0}V/\upsilon )(1-\beta )[K_{P}(1-\alpha )+K_{Q}(1-\beta )]\) (ii)

For the single stirred tank, the relative yield of the desired product, \(\phi \) , is given by the ratio of the rates of reaction, or:

\(\phi=R_{P}/(R_{P}+R_{Q})=(K_{P}C_{A}C_{B})/(K_{P}C_{A}C_{B}+K_{Q}C_{B}^{2}) \)

\((K_{P}C_{A})/(K_{P}C_{A}+K_{Q}C_{B}) \) (iii)

When, as in the problem, the reactant proportions are chosen such that \(C_{A_{0}}=C_{B_{0}}=C_{0} \) then:

\(\phi =\frac{K_{p}C_{0}(1-\alpha )}{K_{P}C_{0}(1-\alpha )+k_{Q}C_{0}(1-\beta )}=\frac{K_{p}(1-\alpha )}{K_{p}(1-\alpha )+K_{Q}(1-\beta )}\) (iv)

For the special case of \(K_{P}=K_{Q}=K\) , the equations become:

\(\alpha =\frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon }(1-\alpha )(1-\beta )\) (v)

\(\beta =\frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon }(1-\beta )[1-\alpha +1-\beta ]\) (vi)

\(\phi =\frac{1-\alpha }{[1-\alpha +1-\beta ]}\) (vii)

Since the fractional conversion of B, \(\beta \) = 0.95, then:

from equation (v): \(\alpha =\left ( \frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon } \right )(1-\alpha )\times 0.05\)

or: \(20\alpha =\left ( \frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon } \right )(1-\alpha )\) (viii)

From equation (vi): 0.95 = \(\left ( \frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon } \right )\times 0.05[1-\alpha +1-0.95]\)

or: \(19=\left ( \frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon } \right )(1.05-\alpha )\) (ix)

Dividing to eliminate the term \(\left ( \frac{KC_{0}V}{\upsilon } \right )\) then:

\((20\alpha /19)=(1-\alpha )/(1.05-\alpha )\)

or: \(\alpha ^{2}-2\alpha +0.95=0\)

Solving, noting that \(\alpha < 0\) : \(\alpha\) = 0.776

From equation (ix): \((KC_{0}V/\upsilon )=(19/(1.05-0.776))=69.4\)

Thus, Volume of the reactor, V = \((69\upsilon /KC_{0})\)

From equation (vii):

Relative yield of P, \(\phi =\frac{(1-0.776)}{(1-0.776+1-0.95)}=0.818\)

The essential requirement for a high relative yield of P can be deduced from the orders of the two competing reactions. With respect to B the undesired reaction to Q has an order of two whilst the desired reaction to P has an order of one and hence the order to Q is greater than the order to P.
Thus the reaction to Q will tend to be suppressed at low concentration of B and the reaction to P will be favoured by high concentration of A.
In a simple tubular reactor, the concentration of both reactants will be high at the inlet and the question is whether the disadvantage of a high concentration of B outweighs the advantage of a high concentration of A. This is nearly always the case because the relative yield, \(\phi =\frac{R_{P}}{R_{P}+R_{Q}}=\frac{K_{P}C_{A}}{K_{P}C_{A}+K_{Q}C_{B}}\) which decreases monotonically with increasing \(C_{B}\) irrespective of the particular values of \(C_{A},K_{P},K_{Q}\) .In the special case where \(K_{P}=K_{Q}\) a simple tubular reactor gives a poor relative yield of the desired product P, as indicated in Figure 1.24.
Alternative modes of contacting which give better yields are also indicated in Figure 1.24,The principle is to use multiple injection points for B along the reactor so that, although concentrations of A are high in the initial stages of the reaction, the concentrations of B are comparatively low.
