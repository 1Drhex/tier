Guest Question.

Model Pile Test (Murthy, 1965). Piles of +15° and +30° batters have been used here to predict the Pt vs. yg and Pt vs. Mmax relationships. The properties of the pile and soil are given below.

\(E l=5.14 \times 10^{4} lb in ^{2}, d=0.75 in , L=30 in ; e=0\) \(\gamma=98 lb / ft ^{3} \text { and } \phi=40^{\circ}\)

For \(\phi=40^{\circ}, C_{\phi}=1.767\left[=3 \times 10^{-5}(1.316)^{\phi}\right]\)

From Eq. (16.29), \(n_{h}^{o}=\frac{150 C_{\phi} \gamma^{1.5} \sqrt{E I d}}{P_{t}}\)

\(n_{h}=\frac{150 C_{\phi} \gamma^{1.5} \sqrt{E I d}}{P_{e}}\) (16.29)

After substituting the known values and simplifying we have

\(n_{h}^{o}=\frac{700}{P_{t}}\)
Admin
Solution

+15° batter pile

From Fig. 16.20 \(n_{h}^{b} / n_{h}^{o}=0.4, n=1.5\)

 

From Eq. (16.14b), \(T_{b}=\left[\frac{E l}{n_{h}^{b}}\right]^{\frac{1}{1.5+4}}=5.33\left[\frac{5.14}{n_{h}^{b}}\right]^{0.1818}\)

 

\(T=\left[\frac{E I}{n_{h}}\right]^{\frac{1}{n+4}}\) (16.14b)

 

Calculations of Deflection \(y _{g}\)

For \(P_{t}=5 lbs , n_{h}^{o}=141 lbs / in ^{3}, n^{b}{ }_{h}=141 \times 0.4=56 lb / in ^{3} \text { and } T_{b}=3.5 in\)

 

\(y_{g}=\frac{2.43 P_{t}^{3} T_{b}^{3}}{5.14 \times 10^{4}}=0.97 \times 10^{-2} in\)

 

Similarly, \(y_{g}\) can be calculated for \(P_{t}=10,15 \text { and } 20 lbs\).

The results are plotted in Fig. 16.21 along with the measured values of \(y_{g}\). There is a close agreement between the two.

 

Calculation of Maximum Moment, \(M_{\max }\)

For \(P_{t}=5 lb , T_{b}=3.5\) in, The equation for M is [Eq. (16.1 1)]

 

\(M=\left[P_{t} T\right] A_{m}+\left[M_{t}\right] B_{m}\) (16.11)

 

\(M=A_{m} P_{t} T_{b}=0.77 P_{t} T_{b}\)

 

where \(A_{m}=0.77(\max )\) from Table 16.2

By substituting and calculating, we have

 

\(M_{(\max )}=13.5 in – lb\)

 

Similarly \(M_{(\max )}\) can be calculated for other loads. The results are plotted in Fig. 16.21 along with the measured values of \(M_{(\max )}\). There is very close agreement between the two.

 

+ 30° Batter Pile

 

From Fig. 16.20, \(n_{h}^{b} / n_{h}^{o}=0.1, \text { and } n=2\); \(T_{b}=\frac{E I}{n_{h}^{b}}^{\frac{1}{2+4}}=4.64 \frac{5.14}{n_{h}^{b}}^{0.1667}\), \(n_{h}^{o}=\frac{700}{P_{t}}\)

 

For \(P_{t}=5 lbs , n^{o}{ }_{h}=141 lbs / in ^{3}, n^{b}{ }_{h}=0.1 \times 141=14.1 lb / in ^{3}, T_{b}=3.93\) in.

 

For \(P_{t}=5 lbs , T_{b}=3.93 in , \text { we have, } y_{g}=1.43 \times 10^{-2} in\)

 

As before, \(M_{(\max )}=0.77 \times 5 \times 3.93=15 \text { in-lb }\).

 

The values of \(y_{g} \text { and } M_{(\max )}\) for other loads can be calculated in the same way. Fig. 16.21 gives \(P_{t} \text { vs. } y_{g} \text { and } P_{t} \text { vs. } M_{(\max )}\) along with measured values. There is close agreement up to about \(P_{t}=10\) Ib, and beyond this load, the measured values are greater than the predicted by about 25 percent which is expected since the soil yields at a load higher than 10 Ib at this batter and there is a plastic flow beyond this load.
