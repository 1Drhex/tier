Guest Question.

A balanced three-phase source of \(400 V\), \(50 Hz\) supplies an unbalanced load through an \(LT\) \(4\)-wire line as in Fig. 8.4-1. Each wire in the line has impedance of \(0.1 +\) \(j0.3 Ω\). (a) Find (i) phase and line voltages at load location and neutral-shift voltage, (ii) wire currents, (iii) power and reactive power delivered to the load, (iv) three-phase apparent power and power factor. (b) Repeat (a) assuming that the neutral wire is a thick conductor with zero impedance. (c) Repeat (a) assuming that the neutral wire is open.

Admin
Solution

(a) We solve the problem by finding out the phasor voltage at load neutral with respect to the ground (earth) node \(G\) by using the \(KCL\) equation at load neutral. The sum of currents flowing away from that node is set to zero.

\(\begin{array}{l} \frac{V-230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}}{10}+\frac{V}{0.1+j 0.3}+\frac{V-230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}}{8+j 6}+\frac{V-230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}}{8-j 6}=0 \\ \text { i.e., } V\left[\frac{1}{10}+\frac{1}{0.1+j 0.3}+\frac{1}{8+j 6}+\frac{1}{8-j 6}\right]=\frac{230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}}{10}+\frac{230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}}{8+j 6}+\frac{230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}}{8-j 6} . \end{array}\)

 

Solving for \(V\), we get, \(V = -2.31-j5.49 = 5.96∠-112.8° V\) \(rms\).

Now, we can find wire currents as

\(\begin{array}{l} I_{\mathrm{R}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}-5.96 \angle-112.8^{\circ}}{10}=23.33 \angle 1.35^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{Y}}=\frac{230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}-5.96 \angle-112.8^{\circ}}{8+j 6}=22.5 \angle-157.06^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{Y}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}-5.96 \angle-112.8^{\circ}}{8+j 6}=23.45 \angle 155.71^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{N}}=I_{\mathrm{R}}+I_{\mathrm{Y}}+I_{\mathrm{B}}=18.83 \angle 175.65^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \end{array}\)

 

The load phase voltages can be determined by multiplying line current phasors by phasor impedance of each phase.

\(\begin{array}{l} V_{\mathrm{RN}}=23.33 \angle 1.35^{\circ} \times(9.9-j 0.3)=231.04 \angle-0.39^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YN}}=22.5 \angle-157.06^{\circ} \times(7.9+j 5.7)=219.18 \angle-121.25^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BN}}=23.45 \angle-155.71^{\circ} \times(7.9+j 6.3)=237 \angle-123.65^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \end{array}\)

 

The line voltages across the load can be determined in terms of the phase voltage phasors now.

\(\begin{array}{l} V_{\mathrm{RY}}=V_{\mathrm{RN}}-V_{\mathrm{YN}}=231.04 \angle-0.39^{\circ}-219.18 \angle-121.25^{\circ}=391.63 \angle 28.33^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YB}}=V_{\mathrm{YN}}-V_{\mathrm{BN}}=219.18 \angle-121.25^{\circ}-237 \angle-123.65^{\circ}=398.32 \angle-90.8^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BR}}=V_{\mathrm{BN}}-V_{\mathrm{RN}}=237 \angle 123.65^{\circ}-231.04 \angle-0.39^{\circ}=400.2 \angle 147.94^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \end{array}\)

 

The active power and reactive power delivered to the load is determined by adding up the corresponding power delivered to each phase of the load.

\(\begin{aligned} P=P_{\mathrm{R}}+P_{\mathrm{Y}}+P_{\mathrm{B}}=& 231.04 \times 23.33 \times \cos \left(-0.39^{\circ}-1.35^{\circ}\right) \\ &+231.04 \times 22.5 \times \cos \left(121.25^{\circ}-\left(-157.06^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ &+237 \times 23.45 \times \cos \left(123.65^{\circ}-\left(-155.71^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ =& 5387.1 \mathrm{~W}+3999 \mathrm{~W}+4345.9 \mathrm{~kW} \\ Q=Q_{\mathrm{R}}+Q_{\mathrm{Y}}+Q_{\mathrm{B}}=& 231.04 \times 23.33 \times \sin \left(-0.39^{\circ}-1.35^{\circ}\right) \\ &+231.04 \times 22.5 \times \sin \left(121.25^{\circ}-\left(-157.06^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ &+237 \times 23.45 \times \sin \left(123.65^{\circ}-\left(-155.71^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ =& 163.25 \mathrm{VAr}+2885.3 \mathrm{VAr}-3465.7 \mathrm{VAr}=-0.744 \mathrm{kVAr} \end{aligned}\)

 

Two different definitions of apparent power and power factor are possible in the case of unbalanced three-phase circuits. The first one is using the total complex power, \(S\), delivered to the load. In this definition, the magnitude of \(S\) is taken as apparent power and ratio of active power to the magnitude of \(S\) is taken as average power factor of the circuit. The apparent power in the load in the circuit in this example then is \((13.732 + 0.7442)°^{.5} = 13.75 kVA\) and power factor is \(0.9985\) lead.

The second definition accepts the sum of apparent powers of load phases as the three-phase apparent power. Power factor is taken as the ratio of active power to apparent power.. The apparent power in the load in the circuit in this example then is \(15.88 kVA\) and power factor is \(0.865\). We cannot specify it as a lead or lag power factor in this case.

The second definition is more appropriate in the sense that power factor and apparent power are expected to give us an indication as to the utilisation efficiency of current in carrying active power. The first method of calculating apparent power and power factor will hide the fact that underutilization of current is taking place when one phase takes leading reactive power and another phase takes lagging reactive power. The phases may consume large reactive power and yet the complex power may come out with only real part or with small imaginary part. Therefore, the definition of apparent power and power factor based on apparent power of individual phases of load is a more meaningful one.

(b) The neutral-shift voltage in this case is zero since the source neutral and load neutral are tied together by a zero impedance link.

The circuit becomes a collection of three single-phase circuits sharing a common point at neutral. Therefore, the line currents can be obtained as

\(\begin{array}{l} I_{\mathrm{R}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}}{10}=23.09 \angle 0^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{Y}}=\frac{230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}}{8+j 6}=23.09 \angle-156.87^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{B}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}}{8-j 6}=23.09 \angle 156.87^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{N}}=I_{\mathrm{R}}+I_{\mathrm{Y}}+I_{\mathrm{B}}=-19.38 \mathrm{Arms} \end{array}\)

 

The load phase voltages can be determined by multiplying line current phasors by phasor impedance of each phase.

\(\begin{aligned} V_{\mathrm{RN}} &=23.09 \angle 0^{\circ} \times(9.9-j 0.3)=228.7 \angle-1.74^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YN}} &=23.09 \angle-156.87^{\circ} \times(7.9+j 5.7)=224.93 \angle-121.06^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BN}} &=23.09 \angle-156.87^{\circ} \times(7.9-j 6.3)=233.31 \angle 118.3^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \end{aligned}\)

 

The line voltages across the load can be determined in terms of the phase voltage phasors now.

\(\begin{aligned} V_{\mathrm{RY}} &=V_{\mathrm{RN}}-V_{\mathrm{YN}}=228.7 \angle-1.74^{\circ}-224.93 \angle-121.06^{\circ}=391.51 \angle 28.32^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YB}} &=V_{\mathrm{YN}}-V_{\mathrm{BN}}=224.93 \angle-121.06^{\circ}-233.31 \angle 118.3^{\circ}=398.15 \angle-90.8^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BR}} &=V_{\mathrm{BN}}-V_{\mathrm{RN}}=233.31 \angle 118.3^{\circ}-228.7 \angle-1.74^{\circ}=400.2 \angle 147.95^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{RY}} &=V_{\mathrm{YB}} V_{\mathrm{BR}}=391.51 \angle 28.32^{\circ}+398.15 \angle-90.8^{\circ}+400.2 \angle 147.95^{\circ} \\ &=344.65+j 185.73-5.56-j 398.11-339.204+j 212.37 \\ & \approx 0 \text { within round-off error } \end{aligned}\)

 

The active power and reactive power delivered to the load are determined by adding up the corresponding power delivered to each phase of the load.

\(\begin{aligned} P=P_{\mathrm{R}}+P_{\mathrm{Y}}+P_{\mathrm{B}}=& 231.04 \times 23.33 \times \cos \left(0^{\circ}-1.74^{\circ}\right) \\ &+231.04 \times 22.5 \times \cos \left(-121.06^{\circ}-\left(-156.87^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ &\left.+237 \times 23.45 \times \cos \left(118.3^{\circ}-156.87^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ =& 5287.2 \mathrm{~W}+4211.9 \mathrm{~W}+4211.9 \mathrm{~W}=13.7 \mathrm{~kW} \\ Q=Q_{\mathrm{R}}+Q_{\mathrm{Y}}+Q_{\mathrm{B}}=& 231.04 \times 23.33 \times \sin \left(0^{\circ}-1.74^{\circ}\right) \\ &+231.04 \times 22.5 \times \sin \left(-121.06^{\circ}-\left(-156.87^{\circ}\right)\right) \\ &+237 \times 23.45 \times \sin \left(118.3^{\circ}-156.87^{\circ}\right) \\ =& 159.94 \mathrm{VAr}+3038.9 \mathrm{VAr}-3358.8 \mathrm{VAr}=-0.48 \mathrm{kVAr} \end{aligned}\)

 

The three-phase apparent power is \(15.86 kVA\) and average power factor is \(0.864.\)

(c) The neutral points are unconnected and hence the system is a three-wire system in this case. The neutral-shift voltage \(V\) is found out from the node equation at the load neutral.

 

\(\begin{array}{c} \frac{V-230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}}{10}+\frac{V-230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}}{8+j 6}+\frac{V-230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}}{8-j 6}=0 \\ \text { i.e., } V\left[\frac{1}{10}+\frac{1}{8+j 6}+\frac{1}{8-j 6}\right]=\frac{230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}}{10}+\frac{230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}}{8+j 6}+\frac{230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}}{8-j 6} \end{array}\)

 

Solving for \(V\), we get, \(V = -74.55 V\) \(rms.\)

Observe the unacceptably large neutral-shift voltage when the source neutral and load neutral are isolated.

Now, we can find wire currents as

\(\begin{array}{l} I_{\mathrm{R}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 0^{\circ}+74.55}{10}=30.545 \angle 0^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{Y}}=\frac{230.9 \angle-120^{\circ}+74.55}{10}=20.414 \angle-138.43^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{B}}=\frac{230.9 \angle 120^{\circ}+74.55}{8-j 6}=20.414 \angle 138.43^{\circ} \mathrm{A} \mathrm{rms} \\ I_{\mathrm{N}}=I_{\mathrm{R}}+I_{\mathrm{Y}}+I_{\mathrm{B}}=0 \mathrm{~A} \mathrm{rms} \end{array}\)

 

The load phase voltages can be determined by multiplying line current phasors by phasor impedance of each phase.

\(\begin{array}{l} V_{\mathrm{RN}}=30.545 \angle 0^{\circ} \times(9.9-j 0.3)=302.53 \angle-1.74^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YN}}=20.414 \angle-138.43^{\circ} \times(7.9+j 5.7)=198.87 \angle-102.62^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BN}}=20.414 \angle 138.43^{\circ} \times(7.9-j 6.3)=206.27 \angle 99.86^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \end{array}\)

 

Observe the over-voltage in \(R\)-phase. The line voltages across the load can be determined in terms of the phase voltage phasors now.

\(\begin{array}{l} V_{\mathrm{RY}}=V_{\mathrm{RN}}-V_{\mathrm{YN}}=302.53 \angle-1.74^{\circ}-198.87 \angle-102.62^{\circ}=392.16 \angle 28.13^{\circ} \mathrm{V} \mathrm{rms} \\ V_{\mathrm{YB}}=V_{\mathrm{YN}}-V_{\mathrm{BN}}=198.87 \angle-102.62^{\circ}-206.27 \angle 99.86^{\circ}=397.37 \angle-91.2^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \\ V_{\mathrm{BR}}=V_{\mathrm{BN}} V_{\mathrm{RN}}=206.27 \angle 99.86^{\circ}-302.53 \angle-1.74^{\circ}=398.94 \angle 147.83^{\circ} \mathrm{Vrms} \end{array}\)

 

The active power and reactive power delivered to the load are determined by adding up the corresponding power delivered to each phase of the load.

\(\begin{array}{l} P=P_{\mathrm{R}}+P_{\mathrm{Y}}+P_{\mathrm{B}}=15.82 \mathrm{~kW} \\ Q=Q_{\mathrm{R}}+Q_{\mathrm{Y}}+Q_{\mathrm{B}}=-0.53 \mathrm{kVAr} \end{array}\)

 

Apparent power is \(17.11 kVA\) and average power factor is \(0.925\).

Note that, in all the cases, the three line voltages add up to zero. The sum of line voltages in any three-phase system is zero since these three voltages are voltages in a loop formed by \(R\) to \(Y, Y\) to \(B\) and \(B\) to \(R\).

This example demonstrated the need for a strong neutral tie in unbalanced four-wire circuits to maintain the phase voltages at load terminal at near-nominal values.
