Guest Question.

Experiments have been conducted using a metallic cylinder 12.7 mm in diameter and 94 mm long. The cylinder is heated internally by an electrical heater and is subjected to a cross flow of air in a low-speed wind tunnel. Under a specific set of operating conditions for which the upstream air velocity and temperature were maintained at \(V=10 m / s\) and 26.2Â°C, respectively, the heater power dissipation was measured to be P = 46 W, while the average cylinder surface temperature was determined to be \(T_{s}=128.4^{\circ} C\).

It is estimated that 15% of the power dissipation is lost through the cumulative effects of surface radiation and conduction through the endpieces. The cumulative uncertainty associated with (i) the air velocity and temperature measurements, (ii) estimating the heat losses by radiation and from the cylinder ends, and (iii) averaging the cylinder surface temperature, which varies axially and circumferentially, renders the experimental value of the convection coefficient accurate to no better than 20%.

1. Determine the convection heat transfer coefficient from the experimental observations.

2. Compare the experimental result with the convection coefficient computed from an appropriate correlation.

Admin
Solution

Known: Operating conditions for a heated cylinder.

Find:

1. Convection coefficient associated with the operating conditions.

2. Convection coefficient from an appropriate correlation.

Assumptions:

1. Steady-state, incompressible flow conditions.

2. Uniform cylinder surface temperature.

Properties: , air \(\left(T_{\infty}=26.2^{\circ} C \approx 300 K \right): v=15.89 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s , k=26.3 \times 10^{-3} W / m \cdot K , \operatorname{Pr}=0.707\). Table A.4, air \(\left(T_{f} \approx 350 K \right): v=20.92 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s , k=30 \times10^{-3} W / m \cdot K , \operatorname{Pr}=0.700\). Table A.4, air \(\left(T_{s}=128.4^{\circ} C =401 K \right): \operatorname{Pr}=0.690\).

Analysis:

1. The convection heat transfer coefficient may be determined from the data by using Newtonâs law of cooling. That is,

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{q}{A\left(T_{s}-T_{\infty}\right)}\)

 

With \(q=0.85 P \text { and } A=\pi D L\), it follows that

 

\(\bar{h}=\frac{0.85 \times 46 W }{\pi \times 0.0127 m \times 0.094 m (128.4-26.2)^{\circ} C }=102 W / m ^{2} \cdot K\)

 

2. Working with the Zukauskas relation, Equation 7.53,

 

\(\begin{gathered}\overline{N u}_{D}=C \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{m} \operatorname{Pr}^{n}\left(\frac{P r}{P r_{s}}\right)^{1 / 4} \\{\left[\begin{array}{l}0.7 \lesssim P r \lesssim 500 \\1 \lesssim R e_{D} \lesssim 10^{6}\end{array}\right]}\end{gathered}\) (7.53)

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=C \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{m} \operatorname{Pr}^{n}\left(\frac{P r}{P r_{s}}\right)^{1 / 4}\)

 

all properties, except \(P r_{s}\), are evaluated at \(T_{\infty}\) Accordingly,

 

\(R e_{D}=\frac{V D}{v}=\frac{10 m / s \times 0.0127 m }{15.89 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s }=7992\)

 

Hence, from Table 7.4, \(C=0.26 \text { and } m=0.6\). Also, since \(\operatorname{Pr}<10, n=0.37\). It follows that

Using the Churchill relation, Equation 7.54,

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62 \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{1 / 2} \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / \operatorname{Pr})^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{R e_{D}}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}\) (7.54)

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62 \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{1 / 2} \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / \operatorname{Pr})^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{R e_{D}}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}\)

 

With all properties evaluated at \(T_{f}, P r=0.70\) and

 

\(R e_{D}=\frac{V D}{v}=\frac{10 m / s \times 0.0127 m }{20.92 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2} / s }=6071\)

 

Hence the Nusselt number and the convection coefficient are

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.3+\frac{0.62(6071)^{1 / 2}(0.70)^{1 / 3}}{\left[1+(0.4 / 0.70)^{2 / 3}\right]^{1 / 4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{6071}{282,000}\right)^{5 / 8}\right]^{4 / 5}=40.6\)

 

\(\bar{h}=\overline{N u}_{D} \frac{k}{D}=40.6 \frac{0.030 W / m \cdot K }{0.0127 m }=96.0 W / m ^{2} \cdot K\)

 

Alternatively, from the Hilpert correlation, Equation 7.52,

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D} \equiv \frac{\bar{h} D}{k}=C R e_{D}^{m} \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}\) (7.52)

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=C \operatorname{Re}_{D}^{m} \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}\)

 

With all properties evaluated at the film temperature, \(R e_{D}=6071 \text { and } \operatorname{Pr}=0.70\). Hence, from Table 7.2, \(C=0.193 \text { and } m=0.618\). The Nusselt number and the convection coefficient are then

 

\(\overline{N u}_{D}=0.193(6071)^{0.618}(0.700)^{1 / 3}=37.3\)

 

\(\bar{h}=\overline{N u}_{D} \frac{k}{D}=37.3 \frac{0.030 W / m \cdot K }{0.0127 m }=88.2 W / m ^{2} \cdot K\)

Comments:

1. Calculations based on the three correlations are within the range of the measured value of the convection heat transfer coefficient, \(\bar{h}=102 \pm 20 W / m ^{2} \cdot K\).

2. Recognize the importance of using the proper temperature when evaluating fluid properties.
