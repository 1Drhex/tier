Guest Question.

In applications involving liquid purification, e.g., water desalination (removal of mineral impurities), and in humidification, thin liquid films are heated from below and evaporation occurs at the liquid-gas interface. This requires a thin liquid film and a solid surface temperature \(T_{s}\), which is not far from the saturation temperature \(T_{lg}\) at one atmosphere (to prevent boiling). This is rendered in Figure. The heat is supplied through the solid substrate as \(q_{k}(W/m^{2})\). This heat flows through the thin liquid film by conduction and then reaches the interface, which is at the liquid-gas phase equilibrium temperature \(T_{lg} = T_{lg}(p = 1 atm)\). The conduction heat flow per unit area (i.e., conduction heat flux) is presented by

\( q_{k}=k_{l}\frac{T_{s}-T_{lg}}{l} \) conduction heat flux through liquid film,

where \(k_{l}\) is the liquid thermal conductivity and l is the liquid film thickness. Under steady state, this heat flows to the surface and results in evaporation. The energy equation for the interface A is the simple form of

\( Q\mid _{A}=\int_{A}{(q.s_{n})}dA =Q+Q_{k}+Q_{u}+Q_{ku}+Q_{r} =-\rho c_{p}V\frac{dT}{dt}+\sum\limits_{i}{\dot{S}_{i} }=\begin{cases}\gt 0 & source\\ \lt 0 & sink\\ =0 & no net heat flow \end{cases} \) , i.e.,

\(-Aq_{k}=\dot{S}_{lg}=-\dot{M}_{lg}\Delta h_{lg}=-A\dot{m}_{lg}\Delta h_{lg} \) surface energy equation.

For a water liquid film with \( k_{l} = 0.68 W/m-K\) , film thickness l = 0.5 mm, temperatures \( T_{s} = 104^{◦}C \) and \( T_{lg} = 100^{◦}C (p = 1 atm, or 1.013 \times 10^{5} Pa)\), and \(\Delta h_{lg} = 2.257 \times 10^{6} J/kg\), determine the evaporation mass flux \(\dot{m}_{lg} \).

Admin
Solution

The rate of heat flow to the interface, per unit area, \(q_{k}\) is given and it results in evaporation of the water liquid film. This would require that the liquid in contact with the solid be slightly superheated [i.e.,\( T_{s} \gt T_{lg}(p)\)], while the liquid at the interface is at the saturated state, i.e., \(T = T_{lg}(p)\). The superheat should not exceed the bubble incipient superheat at which boiling will occur. We assume that the make-up water (shown in Figure) needed for a steady-state evaporation is also at the saturation temperature \(T_{lg}(p)\).

From the energy equation given above we solve for \(\dot{m}_{lg}\) and then substitute the expression for \(q_{k}\). The result is

\( \dot{m}_{lg}=\frac{q_{k}}{\Delta h_{lg}}=\frac{k_{l}(T_{s}-T_{lg})}{\Delta h_{lg}l} \)

Note that for a given \(T_{s} − T_{lg}\), \(\dot{m}_{lg}\) increases as l decreases. Using the numerical values we have

\( \dot{m}_{lg}=\frac{0.68(W/m-^{\circ}C)[104(^{\circ }C)-100(^{\circ }C)]}{2.257\times 10^{6}(J/Kg)\times 10^{-3}(m)}=1.205\times 10^{-3}Kg/m^{2}-s \)

 

\( =1.205Kg/m^{2}-s=4.339 Kg/m^{2}-hr \)
