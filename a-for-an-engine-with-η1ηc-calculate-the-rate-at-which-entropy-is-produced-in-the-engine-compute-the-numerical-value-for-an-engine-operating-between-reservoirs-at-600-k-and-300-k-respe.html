Guest Question.

(a) For an engine with \(η_{1}<η_{C}\) calculate the rate at which entropy is produced in the engine.Compute the numerical value for an engine operating between reservoirs at \(600 K\) and \(300 K\), respectively, and which has an observed efficiency of \(0.30\). The thermal energy current entering the engine is \(1.0 GW\). (b) Show that there is a loss of available energy, and express the rate of loss in terms of the rate of production of entropy. How large is the loss for the engine described in (a) for one day?

Admin
Solution

(a) In an engine which is operating in a steady state, the rate at which entropy is produced must be the difference between the rate at which entropy is emitted and the rate at which it is absorbed. Expressed more formally, according the law of balance of entropy Equ.(4.16) in the steady state \((dS/dt = 0)\):

\(\Pi_{S}=-\left(I_{S, n}+I_{S \rho u t}\right)\)

 

This equation can be transformed using the relations between currents of entropy and energy in heating. If we also use the expression for the efficiency of an ideal Carnot engine, Equ.(4.27), this changes to:

\(\begin{aligned} \Pi_{S} &=-\left(\frac{I_{E, t h i n}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}+\frac{I_{E, h \rho u t}}{T_{\mathrm{L}}}\right)=-\left(-\left(1-\eta_{\mathrm{I}}\right) \frac{1}{T_{\mathrm{L}}}+\frac{1}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\right) I_{E, t h, n} \\ &=\left(\left(1-\eta_{\mathrm{I}}\right)-\frac{T_{\mathrm{L}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\right) \frac{I_{E, t h \cdot n}}{T_{\mathrm{L}}} \end{aligned}\)

 

or

\(\Pi_{S}=\left(\eta_{C}-\eta_{\mathrm{I}}\right) \frac{I_{E, t h, i n}}{T_{\mathrm{L}}}\)

 

If we introduce numerical values we obtain

\(\Pi_{S}=(0.50-0.30) \frac{1.0 \mathrm{GW}}{300 \mathrm{~K}}=0.67 \frac{\mathrm{MW}}{\mathrm{K}}\)

 

The value of \(0.5\) is the Carnot efficiency of the engine operating between \(600 K\) and \(300 K\). The rate at which entropy is absorbed is

\(I_{S, \text { in }}=\frac{1.0 \mathrm{GW}}{600 \mathrm{~K}}=1.7 \frac{\mathrm{MW}}{\mathrm{K}}\)

 

which is \(2.5\) times larger than the rate of production.

(b) The loss of available power is the difference between the power available hypothetically and the actual mechanical power of the engine. According to the definition of the available power in Equ.(4.31), and the Carnot efficiency Equ.(4.27), we have:

\(\left|\mathcal{P}_{a v}\right|-\left|I_{E, \text { mech }}\right|=\left(1-\frac{T_{\mathrm{L}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\right)\left|I_{E \text { th }, \text { in }}\right|-\eta_{\mathrm{I}}\left|I_{E, t h, \text { in }}\right|=\left(\eta_{C}-\eta_{\mathrm{I}}\right)\left|I_{E, t h, n}\right|\)

 

This can be expressed using the result obtained above:

\(\left|\mathcal{P}_{a v}\right|-\left|I_{E \text { mech }}\right|=T_{\mathrm{L}} \Pi_{S}\)

 

This result is important. It demonstrates that the loss of power due to dissipation is directly proportional to the production of entropy. At second glance, this is not so surprising. After all, the non-conservation of entropy leads to the loss in the first place. In one day, the loss is equal to \(300 K\) · \(0.67 MW/K · 86400 s = 1.74 · 10^{13} J\), which is equivalent to \(4.8 · 10^{6} kWh\) of energy. See Section 4.4.3 for an analysis of the loss of power and production of entropy.

\(\frac{d S}{d t}=I_{S, n e t}+\Pi_{S, n e t}\quad\quad\) (4.16)

 

\(\eta_{\mathrm{I}}=\left|I_{E, u s e} / I_{E 1}\right|=\frac{T_{\mathrm{H}}-T_{\mathrm{L}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}} \quad\quad\) (4.27)

 

\(\mathcal{P}_{a v}=\left(T-T_{0}\right) I_{S} \quad\quad\) (4.31)
