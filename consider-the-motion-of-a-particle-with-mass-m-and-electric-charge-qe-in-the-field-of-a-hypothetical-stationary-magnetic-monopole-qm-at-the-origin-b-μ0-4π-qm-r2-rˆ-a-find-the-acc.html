Guest Question.

Consider the motion of a particle with mass m and electric charge \(q_{e}\) in the field of a (hypothetical) stationary magnetic monopole \( q_{m} \) at the origin:

\(B =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{q_{m}}{r^{2}} \hat{ r }\) .

(a) Find the acceleration of \(q_{e}\) , expressing your answer in terms of q, \(q_{m}\) , m, r (the position of the particle), and v (its velocity).

(b) Show that the speed υ = |v| is a constant of the motion.

(c) Show that the vector quantity

\(Q \equiv m( r \times v )-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi} \hat{ r } \)

is a constant of the motion. [Hint: differentiate it with respect to time, and prove—using the equation of motion from (a)—that the derivative is zero.]

(d) Choosing spherical coordinates \((r, \theta, \phi)\) , with the polar (z) axis along Q,

(i) calculate Q ·\(\hat{\phi}\) , and show that θ is a constant of the motion (so \(q_{e}\) moves on the surface of a cone—something Poincaré first discovered in 1896\()^{24}\) ;

(ii) calculate Q ·\(\hat{ r }\) , and show that the magnitude of Q is

\(Q=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi}\left|\frac{q_{e} q_{m}}{\cos \theta}\right|\) ;

(iii) calculate Q ·\(\hat{\theta}\) , show that

\(\frac{d \phi}{d t}=\frac{k}{r^{2}}\) ,

and determine the constant k.

(e) By expressing \(v^{2}\) in spherical coordinates, obtain the equation for the trajectory, in the form

\(\frac{d r}{d \phi}=f(r)\)

(that is: determine the function f (r)).

(f) Solve this equation for r\((\phi)\)

Solution

\(\text { (a) } F =m a =q_{e}( v \times B )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{q_{e} q_{m}}{r^{2}}( v \times \hat{ r }) ; \quad a =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{q_{e} q_{m}}{m r^{3}}( v \times r )\) .

 

\(\text { (b) Because } a \perp v , a \cdot v =0 . \text { But } a \cdot v =\frac{1}{2} \frac{d}{d t}( v \cdot v )=\frac{1}{2} \frac{d}{d t}\left(v^{2}\right)=v \frac{d v}{d t} . \text { So } \frac{d v}{d t}=0 . \quad \text { qed } \)

 

\(\text { (c) } \frac{d Q }{d t}=m( v \times v )+m( r \times a )-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi} \frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{ r }{r}\right)=0+\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi r^{3}}\left[ r \times( v \times r ]-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}\left(\frac{ v }{r}-\frac{ r }{r^{2}} \frac{d r}{d t}\right)\right.\)

 

\(=\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}\left\{\frac{1}{r^{3}}\left[r^{2} v -( r \cdot v ) r \right]-\frac{ v }{r}+\frac{ r }{r^{2}} \frac{d}{d t}(\sqrt{ r \cdot r })\right\}=\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}\left[\frac{ v }{r}-\frac{(\hat{ r } \cdot v )}{r} \hat{ r }-\frac{ v }{r}+\frac{\hat{ r }}{2 r} \frac{2( r \cdot v )}{r}\right]= 0\) .

 

\(\text { (d) (i) } Q \cdot \hat{ \phi }=Q(\hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ \phi })=m( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ \phi }-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}(\hat{ r } \cdot \hat{ \phi }) . \text { But } \hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ \phi }=\hat{ r } \cdot \hat{ \phi }=0, \text { so }( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ \phi }=0 . But\)

 

\(r =r \hat{ r }, \text { and } v =\frac{d l }{d t}=\dot{r} \hat{ r }+r \dot{\theta} \hat{ \theta }+r \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \hat{ \phi }\) (where dots denote di↵erentiation with respect to time), so

\(r \times v =\left|\begin{array}{ccc}\hat{ r } & \hat{ \theta } & \hat{\phi} \\r & 0 & 0 \\\dot{r} & \dot{\theta} & r \sin \theta \dot{\phi}\end{array}\right|=\left(-r^{2} \sin \theta \dot{\phi}\right) \hat{ \theta }+\left(r^{2} \dot{\theta}\right) \hat{ \phi }\) .

Therefore \(( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ \phi }=r^{2} \dot{\theta}=0, \text { so } \theta\) is constant. qed

\(\text { (ii) } Q \cdot \hat{ r }=Q(\hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ r })=m( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ r }-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{\Delta \pi}(\hat{ r } \cdot \hat{ r }) . \operatorname{But} \hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ r }=\cos \theta, \text { and }( r \times v ) \perp r \Rightarrow( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ r }=0, \text { so }\)

 

\(Q \cos \theta=-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}, \text { or } Q=-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi \cos \theta} . \text { And since } \theta \text { is constant, so too is } Q . \text { qed }\)

 

\(\text { (iii) } Q \cdot \hat{ \theta }=Q(\hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ \theta })=m( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ \theta }-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi}(\hat{ r } \cdot \hat{ \theta }) . \text { But } \hat{ z } \cdot \hat{ \theta }=-\sin \theta, \hat{ r } \cdot \hat{ \theta }=0, \text { and }( r \times v ) \cdot \hat{ \theta }=-r^{2} \sin \theta \dot{\phi}\)

 

(from (i)), so \(-Q \sin \theta=-m r^{2} \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \Rightarrow \dot{\phi}=\frac{Q}{m r^{2}}=\frac{k}{r^{2}}, \text { with } k \equiv \frac{Q}{m}=-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m}}{4 \pi m \cos \theta}\) .

 

\(\text { (e) } v^{2}=\dot{r}^{2}+r^{2} \dot{\theta}^{2}+r^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta \dot{\phi}^{2}, \text { but } \dot{\theta}=0 \text { and } \dot{\phi}=\frac{k}{r^{2}}, \text { so } \dot{r}^{2}=v^{2}-r^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta \frac{k^{2}}{r^{4}}=v^{2}-\frac{k^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta}{r^{2}}\) .

 

\(\left(\frac{d r}{d \phi}\right)^{2}=\frac{\dot{r}^{2}}{\dot{\phi}^{2}}=\frac{v^{2}-(k \sin \theta / r)^{2}}{\left(k^{2} / r^{4}\right)}=r^{2}\left[\left(\frac{v r}{k}\right)^{2}-\sin ^{2} \theta\right] ; \frac{d r}{d \phi}=r \sqrt{\left(\frac{v r}{k}\right)^{2}-\sin ^{2} \theta}\) .

 

\(\text { (f) } \int \frac{d r}{r \sqrt{(v r / k)^{2}-\sin ^{2} \theta}}=\int d \phi \Rightarrow \phi-\phi_{0}=\frac{1}{\sin \theta} \sec ^{-1}\left(\frac{v r}{k \sin \theta}\right) ; \sec \left[\left(\phi-\phi_{0}\right) \sin \theta\right]=\frac{v r}{k \sin \theta}, \text { or } \)

 

\(r(\phi)=\frac{A}{\cos \left[\left(\phi-\phi_{0}\right) \sin \theta\right]}, \text { where } A \equiv-\frac{\mu_{0} q_{e} q_{m} \tan \theta}{4 \pi m v} \text {. }\)
