Guest Question.

In a humidification apparatus, liquid water flows in a thin film down the outside of a vertical, circular cylinder. Dry air at 310 K and \( 1.013 \times 10^{5} Pa (1.0 atm ) \) flows at right angles to the 0.076-m-diameter, 1.22-m-long vertically aligned cylinder at a velocity of 4.6 m/s. The liquid film temperature is 290 K. Calculate the rate at which liquid must be supplied to the top of the cylinder if the entire surface of the cylinder is to be used for the evaporating process and no water may drip off from the bottom of the cylinder.

 

Admin
Solution

The liquid film on the outside of the cylinder represents the source for mass transfer, and the air stream flowing normal to the cylinder represents an infinite sink. The properties of the air stream are evaluated at the film-average temperature of 300 K. The properties of air may be obtained from Appendix I, with \( \rho = 1.1769 kg / m ^{3} \) and \( \nu = 1.5689 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \) at 300 K and 1 atm. The Reynolds number is

 

\( \operatorname{Re}_{D} = \frac{v_{\infty} D}{\nu_{\text {air }}} = \frac{(4.6 m / s )(0.076 m )}{1.5689 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s } = 22,283 \)

 

From Appendix J, Table J.1, the diffusion coefficient of water vapor in air at 298 K and 1.0 atm is \( 2.6 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \), which, corrected for temperature, becomes

 

\( D_{A B} = \left(2.60 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \right)\left(\frac{300 K }{298 K }\right)^{3 / 2} = 2.63 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \)

 

The Schmidt number is

 

\( Sc = \frac{\nu_{ air }}{D_{A B}} = \frac{1.5689 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s }{2.63 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s } = 0.60 \)

 

The superficial molar velocity of the air normal to the cylinder is

 

\( G_{M} = \frac{ v _{\infty} \rho_{ air }}{M_{ air }} = \frac{(4.6 m / s )\left(1.1769 kg / m ^{3}\right)}{(29 kg / kg mole )} = 0.187 \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} s } \)

 

Upon substitution of the known values into equation ( \( \frac{k_{G} P(S c)^{0.56}}{G_{M}} = \frac{k_{c}(S c)^{0.56}}{ v _{\infty}} = 0.281\left( Re _{D}\right)^{-0.4}\) ), we can solve for the gas-phase film mass-transfer coefficient:

 

\( k_{G} = \frac{G_{M}}{P S c^{0.56}} 0.281\left(\operatorname{Re}_{D}\right)^{-0.4} \)

 

or

\( k_{G} = \frac{0.281 \left(0.187 kgmole / m ^{2} \cdot s \right)(22,283)^{-0.4}}{\left(1.013 \times 10^{5} Pa \right)(0.60)^{0.56}} = 1.26 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s \cdot Pa } \)

 

The flux of water can be evaluated by

 

\( N_{A} = k_{G}\left(p_{A s} – p_{A, \infty}\right) \)

 

The vapor pressure of water at 290 K is \( 1.73 \times 10^{3} Pa \left(p_{A s} = P_{A}\right) \), and the partial pressure of the dry air \( \left(p_{A \infty}\right) \) is zero, as the surrounding air stream is assumed to be an infinite sink for mass transfer. Consequently

 

\( N_{A} = \left(1.26 \times 10^{-8} \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s \cdot Pa }\right)\left(1.73 \times 10^{3} Pa – 0\right) = 2.18 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s } \)

 

Finally, the mass-feed rate of water for a single cylinder is the product of the flux, and the external surface area of the cylinder is

 

\( W_{A} = N_{A} M_{A}(\pi D L) = \left(2.18 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ kgmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s }\right)\left(\frac{18 kg }{ kgmole }\right)(\pi)(0.076 m )(1.22 m ) \)

 

\( = 1.14 \times 10^{-4} kg / s \)
