Guest Question.

Automotive Fuel Line

An automobile is driving at 40 mph, and it has a fuel economy rating of 28 miles per gallon. The fuel line from the tank to the engine has an inside diameter of \(\frac{3}{8}\)in.(a) Determine the volumetric flow rate of fuel in units of \(ft^3/s\). (b) In the dimensions of in./s, what is the gasoline’s average velocity? (c) What is the Reynolds number for this flow?

Approach
We can use the given information for the automobile’s speed and fuel economy rating to find the volumetric rate at which gasoline is consumed. Then, knowing the cross-sectional area of the fuel line [Equation (5.1)],

\(A=\pi \frac{d^{2} }{4}\) (5.1)

we will apply Equation (6.10)

\(q=Av _{avg}\) (6.10)

to determine the flow’s average velocity. Lastly, we calculate the Reynolds number using Equation (6.6),

\( R{e}=\frac{\rho vl}{\mu } \) (6.6)

where the characteristic length is the fuel line’s diameter. The density and viscosity of gasoline are listed in Table 6.1.

Solution

(a) The volumetric flow rate is the ratio of the vehicle’s speed and the fuel
economy rating:

\(q=\frac{40 mi/h}{28mi/gal}\)

\(=1.429\left(\frac{\cancel{mi}}{h} \right)\left(\frac{gal}{\cancel{\cancel{mi}}} \right) \)

\(=1.429\frac{gal}{h} \)

Converting from a per-hour basis to a per-second basis, this rate is equivalent to

\(q=(1.429\frac{gal}{h})(\frac{1}{3600} \frac{h}{s} ) \)

\(= 3.968 \times 10^{-4}\left(\frac{gal}{\cancel{h}} \right)\left(\frac{\cancel{h}}{s} \right) \)

\(= 3.968 \times 10^{-4}\frac{gal}{h}\)

By applying a factor from Table 6.3,

Table 6.3

\(10^{-3}\) 1 \(3.531×10^{-2}\) 0.2642
\(2.832×10^{-2}\) 28.32 1 7.481
\(3.785×10^{-3}\) 3.785 0.1337 1

we next convert this quantity to the dimensionally consistent basis of \(ft^3\):

\(q=\left(3.968 \times 10^{-4}\frac{gal}{s} \right) \left(0.1337\frac{ft^3/s}{gal/s} \right) \)

\(=5.306 \times 10^{-5}\left(\frac{\cancel{gal}}{\cancel{s}}\right) \left(\frac{ft^3/s}{\cancel{gal/s}} \right)\)

\(= 5.306 \times 10^{-5}\frac{ft^3}{s} \)

(b) The cross-sectional area of the fuel line is

\(A=\frac{\pi }{4} (0.375 in.)^2 \longleftarrow \left[A=\pi \frac{d^{2} }{4}\right] \)

\(= 0.1104 in^2\)

or\( A = 7.670 \times 10^{-4} ft^2\) since 1 ft = 12 in. The average velocity of the gasoline is

\(v _{avg}=\frac{5.306\times 10^{-5}ft^3/s}{7.670\times 10^{-4}ft^2} \longleftarrow \left[q=Av_{avg}\right]\)

\(=6.917 \times 10^{-2}\left(\frac{\cancel{ft^2}.ft}{s} \right)\left(\frac{1}{\cancel{ft^2}}\right) \)

\(=6.917 \times 10^{-2}\frac{ft}{s}\)

or \( v _{avg}= 0.8301 in./s\)

(c) With the fuel line’s diameter being \(d=\frac{3}{8}in.=3.125×10^{-2} ft \), the Reynolds number for this flow is

\(Re=\frac{(1.32slug/ft^3)(6.917\times 10^ {-2}ft/s)(3.125\times 10^{-2}ft)}{6.1\times 10^{-6}slug/(ft.s)} \longleftarrow [R{e}=\frac{\rho vl}{\mu }]\)

\(= 467.8\left(\frac{\cancel{slug}}{\cancel{ft^3}} \right)\left(\frac{\cancel{ft}}{\cancel{s}} \right) (\cancel{ft}) \left(\frac{\cancel{ft}.\cancel{s}}{\cancel{slug}} \right)\)

\(= 467.8\)

Discussion
Since Re< 2000, the flow is expected to be smooth and laminar. A higher fuel economy rating would result in a lower flow rate, velocity, and Reynold’s number, as less fuel is needed to maintain the same vehicle speed.

\(q=5.306\times 10^{-5}\frac{ft^3}{s} \)

\(v_{avg}=0.8301 in./s\)

\(Re = 467.8\)
