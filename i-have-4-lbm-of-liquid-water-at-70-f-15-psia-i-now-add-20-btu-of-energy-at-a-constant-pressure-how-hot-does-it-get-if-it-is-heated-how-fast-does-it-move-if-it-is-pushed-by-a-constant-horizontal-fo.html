Guest Question.

I have 4 lbm of liquid water at 70 F, 15 psia. I now add 20 Btu of energy at a constant pressure. How hot does it get if it is heated? How fast does it move if it is pushed by a constant horizontal force? How high does it go if it is raised straight up?

Admin
Solution

a) Heat at 15 psia.
Energy equation:

\(E _{2}- E _{1}={ }_{1} Q _{2}-{ }_{1} W _{2}={ }_{1} Q _{2}- P \left( V _{2}- V _{1}\right)= H _{2}- H _{1}= m \left( h _{2}- h _{1}\right)\)

 

\(h _{2}= h _{1}+{ }_{1} Q _{2} / m =38.09+20 / 4=43.09 Btu / lbm\)

 

Back interpolate in Table F.7.1: \(T _{2}= 7 5 F\)

(We could also have used \(\Delta T ={ }_{1} Q _{2} / mC =20 /(4 \times 1.00)=5 F\) )

 

b) Push at constant P. It gains kinetic energy.

\(0.5 m V _{2}^{2}={ }_{1} W _{2}\)

 

\(V _{2}=\sqrt{2 { }_{1} W _{2} / m }=\sqrt{2 \times 20 \times 778.17 lbf – f / 4 lbm }\)

 

\(=\sqrt{2 \times 20 \times 778.17 \times 32.174 lbm -( ft / s )^{2} / 4 lbm }= 5 0 0 f t / s\)

 

c) Raised in gravitational field

\(m g Z _{2}={ }_{1} W _{2}\)

 

\(Z _{2}={ }_{1} W _{2} / m g =\frac{20 \times 778.17 lbf – ft }{4 lbm \times 32.174 ft / s ^{2}} \times 32.174 \frac{ lbm – ft / s ^{2}}{ lbf }=3891 ft\)

 

Comment: Notice how fast (500 ft/s) and how high it should be to have the same energy as raising the temperature just 5 degrees. I.e. in most applications we can disregard the kinetic and potential energies unless we have very high V or Z.
