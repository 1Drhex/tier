Guest Question.

Planes \(x=2\) and \(y = -3\), respectively, carry charges \(10nC/m^{2}\) and \(15nC/m^{2}\). If the line \(x=0, z=2\) carries charge \(10\pi nC/m\), calculate E at \((1, 1, -1)\) due to the three charge distributions.

Admin
Solution

Let

\(E=E_{1}+E_{2}+E_{3}\)

where \(E_{1}, E_{2}\), and \(E_{3}\) are, respectively, the contributions to E at point \((1, 1, -1)\) due to the infinite sheet 1, infinite sheet 2, and infinite line 3 as shown in Figure 4.10(a). Applying eqs. (4.26)

\(E=\frac{\rho _{S}}{2\varepsilon _{o}}a_{n}\)

and (4.21)

\(E=\frac{\rho _{L}}{2\pi \varepsilon _{o}\rho } a_{\rho }\)

gives

\(E_{1}=\frac{\rho _{S_{1}}}{2\varepsilon _{o}}\left(-a_{x}\right)=-\frac{10\cdot 10^{-9}}{2\cdot \frac{10^{-9}}{36\pi} }a_{x}=-180\pi a_{x}\)

\(E_{2}=\frac{\rho _{S_{2}}}{2\varepsilon _{o}}a_{y}=\frac{15\cdot 10^{-9}}{2\cdot \frac{10^{-9}}{36\pi} }a_{y}=270\pi a_{y}\)

and

\(E_{3}=\frac{\rho _{L}}{2\pi \varepsilon _{o}\rho } a_{\rho }\)

where \(a_{\rho }\) (not regular \(a_{\rho }\) but with a similar meaning) is a unit vector along LP perpendicular to the line charge and \(\rho \) is the length LP to be determined from Figure 4.10(b).

Figure 4.10(b) results from Figure 4.10(a) if we consider plane \(y=1\) on which \(E_{3} \) lies. From Figure 4.10(b), the distance vector from L to P is

\(R=-3a_{z}+a_{x}\)

\(\rho =\left|R\right|=\sqrt{10}, a_{\rho }=\frac{R }{\left|R\right|} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{10} }a_{x}-\frac{3}{\sqrt{10}}a_{z}\)

Hence

\(E_{3}=\frac{10\pi\cdot 10^{-9}}{2\pi\cdot \frac{10^{-9}}{36\pi} }\cdot \frac{1}{10}\left( a_{x}-3a_{z}\right) =18\pi\left( a_{x}-3a_{z}\right)\)

Thus by adding \(E_{1}\), \(E_{2}\), and \(E_{3}\), we obtain the total field as

\(E=-162\pi a_{x}+270\pi a_{y}-54\pi a_{z}V/m\)

Note that to obtain \(a_{r}, a_{\rho}\), or \(a_{n}\), which we always need for finding F or E, we must go from the charge (at position vector \(r^{'}\)) to the field point (at position vector \(r\)); hence \(a_{r}, a_{\rho}\), or \(a_{n}\) is a unit vector along \(r-r^{'}\). In addition, \(r\) and \(r^{'}\) are defined locally, not globally. Observe this carefully in Figures 4.6 to 4.10.
