Guest Question.

A solid of arbitrary shape is suspended in atmospheric air having a free stream temperature and velocity of \(20^{\circ}C\) and 100 m/s, respectively. The solid has a characteristic length of 1 m, and its surface is maintained at \(80^{\circ}C.\) Under these conditions measurements of the convection heat flux at a particular point \((x^{\ast })\) on the surface and of the temperature in the boundary layer above this point \((x^{\ast },y^{\ast })\) reveal values of \(10^{4}W/m^{2}\) and \(60^{\circ}C,\) respectively. A mass transfer operation is to be effected for a second solid having the same shape but a characteristic length of 2 m. In particular, a thin film of water on the solid is to be evaporated in dry atmospheric air having a free stream velocity of 50 m/s, with the air and the solid both at a temperature of \(50^{\circ}C.\) What are the molar concentration and the species molar flux of the water vapor at the same nondimensional locations where the temperature and heat flux measurements were made in the first case?

Admin
Solution

Known: A boundary layer temperature and heat flux at a location on a solid in an airstream of prescribed temperature and velocity.

Find: Water vapor concentration and flux associated with the same location on a larger surface of the same shape.

Assumptions:
1. Steady-state, two-dimensional, incompressible boundary layer behavior; constant properties.
2. Boundary layer approximations are valid.
3. Negligible viscous dissipation.
4. Mole fraction of water vapor in concentration boundary layer is much less than unity.

Properties: Table A.4, air \((50^{\circ}C): \nu =18.2\times 10^{-6}m^{2}/s, k=28\times 10^{-3} W/m\cdot K, Pr=0.70.\) Table A.6, saturated water vapor \((50^{\circ}C): \rho _{A,sat}=\upsilon _{g}^{-1}=0.082 kg/m^{3}.\) Table A.8, water vapor–air \((50^{\circ}C) D_{AB}\approx 0.26\times 10^{-4} m^{2}/s. \)

Analysis: The desired molar concentration and flux may be determined by invoking the analogy between heat and mass transfer. From Equations 6.47 and 6.51, we know that

 

\(T^{\ast }=f\left ( x^{\ast } , y^{\ast }, Re_{L}, Pr, \frac{dp^{\ast }}{dx^{\ast }}\right )\) (6.47)

 

\(C_{A}^{\ast }=f\left ( x^{\ast }, y^{\ast }, Re_{L}, Sc, \frac{dp^{\ast }}{dx^{\ast }} \right )\) (6.51)

 

\(T^{\ast }\equiv \frac{T-T_{s}}{T_{\infty }-T_{s}}=f\left ( x^{\ast } ,y^{\ast }, Re_{L}, Pr , \frac{dp^{\ast }}{dx^{\ast }}\right )\)

 

and

 

\(C_{A}^{\ast }\equiv \frac{C_{A}-C_{A,s}}{C_{A,\infty }-C_{A,s}}=f\left ( x^{\ast } ,y^{\ast }, Re_{L}, Sc , \frac{dp^{\ast }}{dx^{\ast }}\right )\)

 

For case 1

 

\(\begin{matrix} Re_{L,1}=\frac{V_{1}L_{1}}{\nu }=\frac{100 m/s\times 1 m}{18.2\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s}=5.5\times 10^{6}, & Pr=0.70 \end{matrix}\)

 

while for case 2

 

\(Re_{L,2}=\frac{V_{2}L_{2}}{\nu }=\frac{50 m/s\times 2 m}{18.2\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s}=5.5\times 10^{6}\)

 

\(Sc=\frac{\nu }{D_{AB}}=\frac{18.2\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s}{26\times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s}=0.70\)

 

Since \(Re_{L,1}=Re_{L,2}, Pr=Sc, x_{1}^{\ast }=x_{2}^{\ast },y_{1}^{\ast }=y_{2}^{\ast },\) and the geometries are the same, it follows that the temperature and concentration distributions have the same functional form. Hence

 

\(\frac{C_{A}(x^{\ast },y^{\ast })-C_{A,s}}{C_{A,\infty }-C_{A,s}}=\frac{T(x^{\ast },y^{\ast })-T_{s}}{T_{\infty }-T_{s}}=\frac{60-80}{20-80}=0.33\)

 

or, with \(C_{A,\infty }=0,\)

 

\(C_{A}(x^{\ast }, y^{\ast })=C_{A,s}(1-0.33)=0.67C_{A,s}\)

 

With

 

\(C_{A,s}=C_{A,sat}(50^{\circ}C)=\frac{\rho _{A,sat}}{M_{A}}=\frac{0.082 kg/m^{3}}{18 kg /kmol}=0.0046 kmol/m^{3}\)

 

it follows that

 

\(C_{A}(x^\ast ,y^{\ast })=0.67(0.0046 kmol/m^{3})=0.0031 kmol/m^{3}\)

 

The molar flux may be obtained from Equation 6.8

 

\(N_{A,s}^{"}=h_{m}(C_{A,s}-C_{A,\infty }) \) (6.8)

 

\(N_{A}^{"}(x^{\ast })=h_{m}(C_{A,s}-C_{A,\infty }) \)

 

with \(h_{m}\) evaluated from the analogy. From Equations 6.49 and 6.53 we know that, since \(x_{1}^{\ast }=x_{2}^{\ast },Re_{L,1}=Re_{L,2}\) and Pr = Sc, the corresponding functional forms are equivalent. Hence

 

\(Sh=\frac{h_{m}L_{2}}{D_{AB}}=Nu=\frac{hL_{1}}{k}\)

 

with \(h=q^{"}/(T_{s}-T_{\infty })\) from Newton’s law of cooling,

 

\(h_{m}=\frac{L_{1}}{L_{2}}\times \frac{D_{AB}}{k}\times \frac{{q}''}{T_{s}-T_{\infty }}=\frac{1}{2}\times \frac{0.26\times 10^{-4}m^{2}/s}{0.028 W/m\cdot K}\times \frac{10^{4}W/m^{2}}{(80-20)^{\circ}C}\)

 

\(h_{m}=0.077 m/s\)

 

Hence

 

\(N_{A}^{"}(x^{\ast })=0.077 m/s(0.0046-0.0 ) kmol/m^{3}\)

 

or

 

\(N_{A}^{"}(x^{\ast })=3.54\times 10^{-4}kmol/s\cdot m^{2}\)

 

Comments:
1. Recognize that, since the mole fraction of water vapor in the concentration boundary layer is small, the kinematic viscosity of air \((\nu_{B} )\) may be used to evaluate \(Re_{L,2}.\)

2. Air properties for case 1 are evaluated at the film temperature, \(T_{f}=(T_{s}+T_{\infty })/2=(80^{\circ}C+20^{\circ}C)/2=50^{\circ}C.\)
