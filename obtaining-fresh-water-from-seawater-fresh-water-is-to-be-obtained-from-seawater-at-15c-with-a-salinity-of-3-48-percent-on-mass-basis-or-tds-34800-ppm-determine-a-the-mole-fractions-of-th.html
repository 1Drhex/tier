Guest Question.

Obtaining Fresh Water from Seawater

Fresh water is to be obtained from seawater at 15°C with a salinity of 3.48 percent on mass basis (or TDS = 34,800 ppm). Determine (a) the mole fractions of the water and the salts in the seawater, (b) the minimum work input required to separate 1 kg of seawater completely into pure water and pure salts, (c) the minimum work input required to obtain 1 kg of fresh water from the sea, and (d) the minimum gauge pressure that the seawater must be raised if fresh water is to be obtained by reverse osmosis using semipermeable membranes.

Admin
Solution

Fresh water is to be obtained from seawater. The mole fractions of seawater, the minimum works of separation needed for two limiting cases, and the required pressurization of seawater for reverse osmosis are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The seawater is an ideal solution since it is dilute. 2 The total dissolved solids in water can be treated as table salt (NaCl). 3 The environment temperature is also 15°C.

Properties The molar masses of water and salt are \(M_{w} = 18.0 kg/kmol\) and \(M_{s} = 58.44 kg/kmol\). The gas constant of pure water is \(R_{w} = 0.4615 kJ/kg·K\) . The density of seawater is \(1028 kg/m^3\).

Analysis (a) Noting that the mass fractions of salts and water in seawater are \(\mathrm{mf}_{s}=\) \(0.0348\) and \(\mathrm{mf}_{w}=1-\mathrm{mf}_{s}=0.9652\), the mole fractions are determined from Eqs. 13-4 and 13-5 to be

\(M_{m}=\frac{m_{m}}{N_{m}}=\frac{m_{m}}{\sum m_{i} / M_{i}}=\frac{1}{\sum m_{i} /\left(m_{m} M_{i}\right)}=\frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{\mathrm{mf}_{i}}{M_{i}}}\) (13-4)

\(\mathrm{mf}_{i}=\frac{m_{i}}{m_{m}}=\frac{N_{i} M_{i}}{N_{m} M_{m}}=y_{i} \frac{M_{i}}{M_{m}}\) (13-5)

\(\begin{array}{c}M_{m}=\frac{1}{\sum \frac{\mathrm{mf}_{i}}{M_{i}}}=\frac{1}{\frac{\mathrm{mf}_{s}}{M_{s}}+\frac{\mathrm{mf}_{w}}{M_{w}}}=\frac{1}{\frac{0.0348}{58.44}+\frac{0.9652}{18.0}}=18.44 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{kmol} \\\\y_{w}=\mathrm{mf}_{w} \frac{M_{m}}{M_{w}}=0.9652 \frac{18.44 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{kmol}}{18.0 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{kmol}}=0.9888 \\\\y_{s}=1-y_{w}=1-0.9888=\mathbf{0 . 0 1 1 2}=1.12 \%\end{array}\)

(b) The minimum work input required to separate \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of seawater completely into pure water and pure salts is

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{w}_{\text {min,in }}=&-R_{u} T_{0}\left(y_{A} \ln y_{A}+y_{B} \ln y_{B}\right)=-R_{u} T_{0}\left(y_{w} \ln y_{w}+y_{s} \ln y_{s}\right) \\=&-(8.314 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(288.15 \mathrm{~K})(0.9888 \ln 0.9888+0.0112 \ln 0.0112) \\=& 147.2 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol} \\& w_{\min , \mathrm{in}}=\frac{\bar{w}_{\min , \mathrm{in}}}{M_{m}}=\frac{147.2 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kmol}}{18.44 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{kmol}}=7.98 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \text { seawater }\end{aligned}\)

Therefore, it takes a minimum of \(7.98 \mathrm{~kJ}\) of work input to separate \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of seawater into \(0.0348 \mathrm{~kg}\) of salt and \(0.9652 \mathrm{~kg}\) (nearly \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) ) of fresh water.

(c) The minimum work input required to produce \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of fresh water from seawater is

\(\begin{aligned}w_{\min , \text { in }} &=R_{w} T_{0} \ln \left(1 / y_{w}\right) \\&=(0.4615 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K})(288.15 \mathrm{~K}) \ln (1 / 0.9888) \\&=1.50 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \text { fresh water }\end{aligned}\)

Note that it takes about five times more work to separate \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of seawater completely into fresh water and salt than it does to produce \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of fresh water from a large amount of seawater.

(d) The osmotic pressure in this case is

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta P_{\min } &=\rho_{m} R_{w} T_{0} \ln \left(1 / y_{w}\right) \\&=\left(1028 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(0.4615 \mathrm{kPa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}\right)(288.15 \mathrm{~K}) \ln (1 / 0.9888) \\&=1540 \mathrm{kPa}\end{aligned}\)

which is equal to the minimum gauge pressure to which seawater must be compressed if the fresh water is to be discharged at the local atmospheric pressure. As an alternative to pressurizing, the minimum height above the fresh-water level that the seawater must be raised to produce fresh water is (Fig. \(13-24\) )

\(\Delta z_{\min }=\frac{w_{\min , \mathrm{in}}}{g}=\frac{1.50 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}}{9.81 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}{1 \mathrm{~N}}\right)\left(\frac{1000 \mathrm{~N} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{1 \mathrm{~kJ}}\right)=153 \mathrm{~m}\)

Discussion The minimum separation works determined above also represent the maximum works that can be produced during the reverse process of mixing. Therefore, \(7.98 \mathrm{~kJ}\) of work can be produced when \(0.0348 \mathrm{~kg}\) of salt is mixed with \(0.9652 \mathrm{~kg}\) of water reversibly to produce \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of saline water, and \(1.50 \mathrm{~kJ}\) of work can be produced as \(1 \mathrm{~kg}\) of fresh water is mixed with seawater reversibly. Therefore, the power that can be generated as a river with a flow rate of \(10^{5} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{s}\) mixes reversibly with seawater through semipermeable membranes is (Fig.13-25)

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{W}_{\text {max }, \text { out }} &=\rho V w_{\max , \text { out }}=\left(1000 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(10^{5} \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{s}\right)(1.50 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg})\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{MW}}{10^{3} \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{s}}\right) \\&=1.5 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{MW}\end{aligned}\)

which shows the tremendous amount of power potential wasted as the rivers discharge into the seas.
