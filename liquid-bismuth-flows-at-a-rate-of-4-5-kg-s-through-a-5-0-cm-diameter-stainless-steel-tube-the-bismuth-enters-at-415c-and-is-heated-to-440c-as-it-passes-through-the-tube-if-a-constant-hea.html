Guest Question.

Liquid bismuth flows at a rate of 4.5 kg/s through a 5.0-cm-diameter stainless-steel tube. The bismuth enters at \( 415^{◦} C \) and is heated to \( 440^{◦} C \) as it passes through the tube. If a constant heat flux is maintained along the tube and the tube wall is at a temperature \( 20^{◦} C \) higher than the bismuth bulk temperature, calculate the length of tube required to effect the heat transfer.

Admin
Solution

Because a constant heat flux is maintained, we may use Equation (6-47)

\(Nu =4.82+0.0185\> Pe^{0.827} \hspace{30 pt} [6-47] \)

to calculate the heattransfer coefficient. The properties of bismuth are evaluated at the average bulk temperature of

\((415+440)/2=427.5^{◦} C \)

 

\(μ = 1.34×10^{−3} kg/m· s\quad [3.242 \enspace lb_{m} /h · ft]\hspace{30 pt} \)

 

\(c_{p} = 0.149\enspace kJ/kg · ^{◦} C\enspace [0.0356\enspace Btu/lb_{m} · ^{◦} F] \)

 

\(k = 15.6 \; W/m· ^{◦} C\enspace [9.014 \; Btu/h · ft · ^{◦} F] \)

 

\(Pr = 0.013 \)

The total transfer is calculated from

\(q= \dot{m} c_{p} \Delta T_{b} =(4.5)(149)(440−415)=16.76\enspace kW\enspace [57,186\enspace Btu/h] \hspace{30 pt} [a]\)

We calculate the Reynolds and Peclet numbers as

\(Re_{d}=\frac{dG}{μ}=\frac{(0.05)(4.5)}{[π(0.05)^{2} /4](1.34×10^{−3} )} =85,520 \hspace{30 pt} [b] \)

 

\(Pe=Re\quad Pr =(85,520)(0.013)=1111\)

The heat-transfer coefficient is then calculated from Equation 6-47

\(Nu_{d} =4.82+(0.0185)(1111)^{0.827} =10.93 \hspace{30 pt} [c] \)

 

\(h=\frac{(10.93)(15.6)}{0.05}=3410\; W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C \enspace [600\enspace Btu/h · ft^{2} · ^{◦} F] \)

The total required surface area of the tube may now be computed from

\(q=hA(T_{w} −T_{b} )\hspace{30 pt} [d] \)

where we use the temperature difference of \( 20^{◦} C \);

\(A=\frac{16,760}{(3410)(20)} =0.246 \; m^{2} \; [2.65 \> ft^{2} ] \)

This area in turn can be expressed in terms of the tube length:

\(A=πdL\quad and\quad L=\frac{0.246}{π(0.05)} =1.57\enspace m \enspace [5.15\quad ft] \)
