Guest Question.

Polyauxie in fermentation of hot-water wood extract hydrolysate with E. coli FBWHR

There are 6 substitutable carbon sources in hot-water wood extract hydrolysate, namely glucose, galactose, mannose, rhamnose, xylose and arabinose. The interested products include cell biomass (X) and the extracellular product ethanol (P). Therefore, there are in total 8 species of particular concern in the fermentation of hot-water wood extract hydrolysate with E. coli FBWHR. Based on the experimental observations, E. coli FBWHR differentiates the substrates and exhibits a sequential uptake of these substrates as discovered by Wang and Liu (2014). It appears that glucose is consumed first, and then galactose and arabinose, and finally rhamnose and mannose are consumed almost simultaneously, while xylose is the last substrate to be consumed. After xylose, cells start to consume ethanol, which is the product from the sugar substrates. Based on this observation, derive a selective substrate uptake model to descrivbe the sytem.

Admin
Solution

Based on the observation, the hot-water extract hydrolysate fermentation can be modeled with 5 enzyme species for the E. coli FBWHR: \(E_{1}\) is able to bind with glucose; \(E_{2}\) is able to bind with galactose and arabinose in addition to glucose, \(E_{3}\) is able to bind rhamnose and mannose in addition to those that can be bound by \(E_{2}\), \(E_{4}\) can bind with all the sugar substrate species present in the hot-water wood extract hydrolysate, and \(E_{5}\) can also bind with ethanol. Table E13.3.1 shows the enzymes and their corresponding ranks on binding of substrates.

The selective substrate uptake model can be applied to the hot-water wood extract hydrolysate system by adding the substrate species alike (i.e., binding on the same enzyme together). Table E13.3.1 also includes the substrate species (from 1 to \(N_{ Si }\)) enzyme species i can act on. The specific substrate consumption rate, Eq. (13.100) \(\left[S_{j} \cdot E_{i}\right]=\frac{K_{j} S_{j} E_{i}}{1+\sum_{n=1}^{i} K_{n} S_{n}} \quad \forall j > i\) , is modified to give

\(\mu_{i}=\mu_{\max , i} K_{i} S_{i} \sum_{j=I_{E_{1}}}^{N_{E}} \frac{E_{j}}{1+\sum_{n=1}^{N_{S_{j}}} K_{n} S_{n}}\) (E13.3.1)

Where \(N_{ E }\) is the index of the highest ranking enzyme, \(N_{S j}\) is last (or least ranking) substrate species that the enzyme species j can bind with, \(I _{ Ei }\) is the index of enzyme that can facilitate uptake of the substrate species i. For example,\(I_{E 1}=1, I_{E 2}=2, I_{E 4}=2\), as one can read from Table E13.3.1. The rate of enzyme activation (for all the three species) is given by

\(r_{E_{i}}=\sum_{j=0}^{N_{E}} \frac{k_{j} E_{j}}{1+\sum_{n=1}^{N_{S_{j}}} K_{n} S_{n}} \frac{\sum_{n=N_{S E_{i-1}}+1}^{N_{S E_{i}}} K_{n} S_{n}}{\prod_{m=j+1}^{i}\left(1+\sum_{n=N_{S E_{m-1}}+1}^{N_{S E_{m}}} K_{n} S_{n}\right)}\)\(-\frac{k_{i} E_{i}}{1+\sum_{n=1}^{N_{S i}} K_{n} S_{n}} \sum_{j=1}^{N_{E}} \frac{\sum_{n=N_{S E_{j-1}}+1}^{N_{S E_{j}}} K_{n} S_{n}}{\prod_{m=i+1}^{j}\left(1+\sum_{n=N_{S E_{m-1}}+1}^{N_{SE_{m}}} K_{n} S_{n}\right)}\) (E13.3.2)

where \(N_{ SEi }\) is the last (or lowest ranking) substrate species enzyme i can facilitate uptake, which can be different from the \(N_{ Si }\) or the lowest ranking substrate species enzyme i can bind with.
During batch fermentation, small amount of air is passed through the fermentor to provide oxygen to aid the cell viability.
Ethanol is volatile and ethanol can escape with the air vent during the fermentation. Therefore, the rate of ethanol is given by

\(r_{P}=X \sum_{i=1}^{N_{S}} \mu_{i} Y F_{P / S_{i}}-k_{L} a P-\mu_{P} X\) (E13.3.3)

where \(N_{ S }\) is the total number of substrate species, P is the concentration of ethanol in the fermentation broth, \(\mu_{ P }\) is the specific uptake rate of ethanol that is also defined by Eq. (E13.3.1), and \(k_{ L }\)a is the overall volumetric mass transfer coefficient. The mass transfer coefficient is a function of the aeration rate, stirring rate, temperature, and concentration of solutes in the fermentation broth. In a batch reactor, mass balance leads to

\(\frac{d E_{i}}{d t}=r_{E_{i}}\) (E13.3.4)

\(\frac{d S_{i}}{d t}=r_{S_{i}}=-\mu_{i} X \) (E13.3.5)

\(\frac{d P}{d t}=r_{P}=X \sum_{i=1}^{N_{S}} \mu_{i} Y F_{P / S_{i}}-\mu_{P} X-k_{L} a P\) (E13.3.6)

\(\frac{d X}{d t}=r_{X}=X\left\lgroup \sum_{i=1}^{N_{S}} \mu_{i} Y F_{X / S_{i}}+\mu_{P} Y F_{X / P} \right\rgroup \) (E13.3.7)

The concentrations of the enzymes, substrates, ethanol, and biomass can be determined by solving Eqs. (13.103) \(\frac{d E_{i}}{d t}=r_{E_{i}}\) and (E13.4) \(t_{d}=\frac{\ln 2}{\mu_{n e t}}\) through (E13.7) \(r_{ X }=-k_{ d } X\) , with initial conditions given at time zero. These equations can be integrated by OdexLims in Microsoft Excel.

The simulation results as compared with the experimental data of Wang and Liu (2014) for the fermentation of 20% concentrated sugar maple wood extract hydrolysate are shown in Fig. E13.3.1 and the change of enzyme levels is shown in Fig. E13.3.2.
The corresponding kinetic parameters used for the simulation are shown in Table E13.3.2.

Fig. E13.3.1 shows the concentrations of the substrates and products in the fermentation broth as functions of time for E. coli FBWHR in 20% (v/v) concentrated sugar maple wood-extract hydrolysate. One can observe that the simulation results agree with the experimental data. Fig. E13.3.1A shows that it took over 16 h before xylose consumption started, whereas Fig. E13.3.1B shows that glucose was utilized from time zero. The rest of the monosaccharides (galactose, arabinose, mannose, and rhamnose) were uptaken between 4 and 15 h. Fig. E13.3.1G shows that the ethanol concentration increases with time initially when substrates are abundant, reached a maximum, before it is decreased with time. Fig. E13.3.1H shows tha the cell biomass increased with time throughout of the time duration. Different stages of growth are clearly visible—slow growth before 10 h, fast growth between 10 and 15 h, growth after 30 h was slower than that between 15 and 30 h.

Fig. E13.3.2 shows that the active enzyme levels changes with time—they all increase with time initially, reach maximum, and then decrease with time. Lowest ranking enzyme reaches peak value first, and the highest ranking enzyme reaches peak the last. Enzymes are activated in the sequence of the lowest ranking substrate species they can bind with. Enzymes \(E _{1}\), \(E _{2}\) and \(E _{3}\) are activated almost simultaneously because they all can bind with mannose. The next lower ranking substrate to mannose is xylose, enzyme \(E _{4}\) can bind with xylose, but not enzyme \(E _{5}\). Therefore, enzyme \(E _{4}\) is activated after enzymes \(E _{1}\), \(E _{2}\) and \(E _{3}\).

Table E13.3.2 shows the kinetic parameters employed to generate the curves in Figs. E13.3.1 and E13.3.2. Besides the specific consumption rates (\(\mu_{\text {maxi }}\)), yield factors \(\left(Y F_{ X / Si } \text { and } Y F_{ P / Si }\right)\), binding affinities (\(K_{ i }\)), and enzyme conversion rate constants (\(k_{ i }\)), the lag time (\(\tau_{ L }\)) and volumetric mass transfer coefficient (\(k_{ L } a\)) are also listed. The lag time is the time required before the lowest ranking enzyme \(E _{1}\) started to be activated (from \(E _{0}\)). Owing to the use of unknown substrate(s) or essential nutrients not identified for cells to become functional (to counter the toxic compounds in the medium), a lag time used. The lag time is a function of the toxic compounds (such as phenolic compounds) present in the medium and enzyme activation nutrients (such magnesium ion and vitamins) countering toxic compounds. In this case, the lag time is zero, indicating the enzymes can be activated right after the cells are exposed to the medium.

Example E13.3 shows that cells allocate (or activate) enzymes to facilitate the uptake of substrates based on the preference, abundance and availability of the substrates. The substrates are control agent for cells to activate enzymes in a way similar to controlled gene expression. The highest ranking substrate is the one that is most preferred by the cell, and the lowest ranking substrate is the one that is least preferred substrate cell would consume.
