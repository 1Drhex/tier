Guest Question.

In you showed that the solution to

\( \frac{d f}{d t}=k(t) f(t) \).

(where k(t) is a function of is

\( f(t)=e^{K(t)} f(0), \quad \text { where } \quad K(t) \equiv \int_{0}^{t} k\left(t^{\prime}\right) d t^{\prime} \).

This suggests that the solution to the Schrödinger equation (11.1) might be

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\hat{H} \Psi \) (11.1).

\( \Psi(t)=e^{\hat{G}(t)} \Psi(0), \quad \text { where } \quad \hat{G}(t) \equiv-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) d t^{\prime} \). (11.108).

It doesn’t work, because \( \hat{H}(t) \text { is an operator, not a function, and } \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right) \) does not (in general) commute with \( \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) \).

(a) Try calculating \( i \hbar \partial \Psi / \partial \), using Equation 11.108. Note: as always, the exponentiated operator is to be interpreted as a power series:

\( e^{\hat{G}} \equiv 1+\hat{G}+\frac{1}{2} \hat{G} \hat{G}+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G} \hat{G} \hat{G}+\cdots \).

Show that if \( [\hat{G}, \hat{H}]=0, \text { then } \Psi \) satisfies the Schrödinger equation.

(b) Check that the correct solution in the general case \( ([\hat{G}, \hat{H}] \neq 0) \) is

\( \Psi(t)=\left\{1+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right) \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right) d t_{1}+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right)\left[\int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) d t_{2}\right] d t_{1}\right. \)

\( \left.+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{3} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right)\left[\int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right)\left(\int_{0}^{t_{2}} \hat{H}\left(t_{3}\right) d t_{3}\right) d t_{2}\right] d t_{1}+\cdots\right\} \Psi(0) \). (11.109).

UGLY! Notice that the operators in each term are “time-ordered,” in the sense that the latest \( \hat{H} \) appears at the far left, followed by the next latest, and so on \( \left(t \geq t_{1} \geq t_{2} \geq t_{3} \cdots\right) \). Dyson introduced the time-ordered product of two operators:

\( T \left[\hat{H}\left(t_{i}\right) \hat{H}\left(t_{j}\right)\right] \equiv \begin{cases}\hat{H}\left(t_{i}\right) \hat{H}\left(t_{j}\right), & t_{i} \geq t_{j} \\ \hat{H}\left(t_{j}\right) \hat{H}\left(t_{i}\right), & t_{j} \geq t_{i}\end{cases} \) (11.110).

or, more generally,

\( T \left[\hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right) \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) \cdots \hat{H}\left(t_{n}\right)\right] \equiv \hat{H}\left(t_{j_{i}}\right) \hat{H}\left(t_{j_{2}}\right) \cdots \hat{H}\left(t_{j_{n}}\right) \) (11.111).

\( \text { where } t_{j_{1}} \geq t_{j_{2}} \geq \cdots \geq t_{j_{n}} \).

(c) Show that

\( T [\hat{G} \hat{G}]=-\frac{2}{\hbar^{2}} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right)\left[\int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) d t_{2}\right] d t_{1} \),

and generalize to higher powers of \( \hat{G} . \text { In place of } \hat{G}^{n} \), in equation 11.108, we really want \( T \left[\hat{G}^{n}\right] \):

\( \Psi(t)= T \left[e^{-\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) d t^{\prime}}\right] \Psi(0) \) (11.112).

This is Dyson’s formula; it’s a compact way of writing Equation 11.109, the formal solution to Schrödinger’s equation. Dyson’s formula plays a fundamental role in quantum field theory.

Solution

(a) Using Equation 11.108 we have

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} e^{\hat{G}(t)} \Psi(0) \).

\( =i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[1+\hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\cdots\right] \Psi(0) \).

and noting that \( i \hbar \dot{G}=H, \text { this is } \)

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=i \hbar\left[0+\dot{\hat{G}}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \dot{\hat{G}}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \hat{G}(t) \dot{\hat{G}}(t)+\frac{1}{3 !} \dot{\hat{G}}(t) \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)\right. \)

\( \left.+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G}(t) \dot{\hat{G}}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t) \dot{\hat{G}}(t)+\cdots\right] \Psi(0) \).

\( =\left[\hat{H}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \hat{H}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \hat{G}(t) \hat{H}(t)+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{H}(t) \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)\right. \)

\( \left.+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G}(t) \hat{H}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{3 !} \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t) \hat{H}(t)+\cdots\right] \Psi(0) \).

\( \text { Now if }[\hat{G}, \hat{H}]=0 \text { this becomes } \)

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\hat{H}(t)\left[1+\hat{G}(t)+\frac{1}{2} \hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)+\cdots\right] \Psi(0) \).

\( =\hat{H}(t) e^{\hat{G}(t)} \Psi(0) \).

\( =\hat{H}(t) \Psi(t) \).

so Eq. 11.108 is a solution to the Schrödinger equation if G and H commute which is not typically the case for time-dependent Hamiltonians

(b) Now we try Equation 11.109. Taking the derivative and recalling that

\( \frac{d}{d x} \int_{a}^{x} f(u) d u=f(x) \).

we have

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\left\{0+\hat{H}(t)+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right) \hat{H}(t) \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) d t_{2}\right. \)

\( \left.+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \hat{H}(t) \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right)\left(\int_{0}^{t_{2}} \hat{H}\left(t_{3}\right) d t_{3}\right) d t_{2}\right\} \Psi(0) \).

In this case all the \( \hat{H}(t) \) )’s are on the left so we don’t have to worry about operators commuting.\( t_2\) and \( t_3\) are dummy variables so we can rename them to \( t_1\) and \( t_2\) and we have

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\hat{H}(t)\left\{1+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right) \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right) d t_{1}\right. \)

\( \left.+\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right)\left(\int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) d t_{1}\right) d t_{1}\right\} \Psi(0) \).

which looking back to Equation 11.109 is

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\hat{H}(t) \Psi(t) \).

as required.

(c) We consider

\( T [\hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)]= T \left[\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) d t^{\prime} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime \prime}\right) d t^{\prime \prime}\right] \).

\( =\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \int_{0}^{t} \int_{0}^{t} T \left[\hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime \prime}\right)\right] d t^{\prime} d t^{\prime \prime} \).

\( =\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2}\left[\int_{0}^{t} \int_{0}^{t^{\prime \prime}} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime \prime}\right) \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) d t^{\prime} d t^{\prime \prime}+\int_{0}^{t} \int_{0}^{t^{\prime}} \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime}\right) \hat{H}\left(t^{\prime \prime}\right) d t^{\prime \prime} d t^{\prime}\right] \).

Replacing the variables with \( t^{\prime} \rightarrow t_{2} \text { and } t^{\prime \prime} \rightarrow t_{1} \text { in the first integral and } t^{\prime} \rightarrow t_{1} \text { and } t^{\prime \prime} \rightarrow t_{2} \) in the second integral we have

\( T [\hat{G}(t) \hat{G}(t)]=2\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{2} \int_{0}^{t} \hat{H}\left(t_{1}\right)\left[\int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) d t_{2}\right] d t_{1} \).

the desired result.

The generalization for \( G^{n} \text { is } \)

\( T \left[\hat{G}^{n}\right]=n !\left(-\frac{i}{\hbar}\right)^{n} \int_{0}^{t} H\left(t_{1}\right) \int_{0}^{t_{1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{2}\right) \cdots \int_{0}^{t_{n-1}} \hat{H}\left(t_{n}\right) d t_{n} d t_{n-1} \cdots d t_{2} d t_{1} \).

Note that it is n! (not say, n) because there are n! ways to order the n times \( t_{1}, t_{2}, \ldots, t_{n} \).
