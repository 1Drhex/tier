CONTEXT A long-running challenge in our ability to probe chemical reactions has been inding the minimum collision energy between two reactants necessary for the reaction to take place. A typical sample has molecules spread out over a wide range of kinetic energies, so we normally calculate activation barriers from indirect measurements made over a range of temperatures. An elegant technique to make direct measurements of this threshold involves generating narrow beams of the reactant molecules and sending one or both beams through a velocity selector. A typical velocity selector consists of two or more spinning wheels, with holes drilled in each wheel to let the beam pass when the hole lines up with the beam. By timing the rotation rates of the two wheels, it is possible to delect all the molecules in the beam that are not within a certain velocity window. These experiments are among the most detailed probes of chemical reactions being done, providing extremely sensitive tests of our theoretical understanding of physical chemistry.

PROBLEM In a 1.00 mole sample of \(N_{2} \) at 298 K, calculate the number of molecules that will have speeds v within 0.005 ms-¹ of 10.0 ms-¹.

 

 

Admin
Solution

The probability function \(P_{v}\left( v\right) \) will give us the fraction of molecules at any given speed. We need to plug the mass of \(N_{2} \) and the value of v² into Eq. 1.27.

\(P_{v}\left( v\right) = 4\pi \left(\frac{M}{2\pi RT } \right) ^{3/2}v^{2}e^{-Mv^{2} }/(2RT )\) (1.27)

The mass of \(N_{2} \) is

\((28.0 amu)(1.661\cdot 10^{-27} kg amu^{-1} )= 4.65\cdot 10^{-26} kg\)

The squared velocity v² = 1.00 • 10² m² s-². The thermal energy\(K_{B}T \) is \(4.11\cdot 10^{-21} J\).

\(P_{v}\left( v\right) = 4\pi \left(\frac{m}{2\pi K_{B}T } \right) ^{3/2}v^{2}e^{-mv^{2} /(2K_{B}T )} \)

 

\(=4\pi[\frac{4.65\cdot 10^{-26} kg }{2\pi \left(4.11 \cdot 10^{-21}J \right) } ]^{3/2}\left(10.0 ms^{ -1} \right) ^{2} \)

 

\(\times \exp \left\{-(4.65\cdot 10^{-26}kg )(1.00\cdot 10^{2}m^{2} s^{-2} )/[2(4.11\cdot 10^{-21}J)]\right\} \)

 

\(= 3.03\cdot 10^{-6}s m^{-1} \)

This result is the fraction of \(N_{2} \)molecules at v = 10.0 m s-¹ per “volume element” of speed. To estimate the total number of molecules, we multiply\(P_{v}\left( v\right) \) by the range of allowed values, dv = 0.01 m s-¹(plus or minus 0.005 m s-¹) and by the total number of molecules.

\(N(v)=N P_{v}\left( v\right)dv\)

 

\(= (6.022\cdot 10^{23}mol^{-1} )(3.03\cdot 10^{-6}s m^{-1} )(0.01 m s^{-1} )\)

 

\(= 1.83\cdot 10^{16}\)
