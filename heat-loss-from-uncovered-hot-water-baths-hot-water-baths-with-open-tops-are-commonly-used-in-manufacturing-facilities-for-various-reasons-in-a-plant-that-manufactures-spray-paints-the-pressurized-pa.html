Guest Question.

Heat Loss from Uncovered Hot Water Baths

Hot water baths with open tops are commonly used in manufacturing facilities for various reasons. In a plant that manufactures spray paints, the pressurized paint cans are temperature tested by submerging them in hot water at \( 50^{\circ}C \) in a 40-cm-deep rectangular bath and keeping them there until the cans are heated to \( 50^{\circ}C \) to ensure that the cans can withstand temperatures up to \( 50^{\circ}C \) during transportation and storage (Fig. 14–54). The water bath is 1 m wide and 3.5 m long, and its top surface is open to ambient air to facilitate easy observation for the workers. If the average conditions in the plant are 92 kPa, \( 25^{\circ}C \), and 52 percent relative humidity, determine the rate of heat loss from the top surface of the water bath by (a) radiation, (b) natural convection, and (c) evaporation. Assume the water is well agitated and maintained at a uniform temperature of \( 50^{\circ}C \) at all times by a heater, and take the average temperature of the surrounding surfaces to be \( 20^{\circ}C \).

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION Spray paint cans are temperature tested by submerging them in an uncovered hot water bath. The rates of heat loss from the top surface of the bath by radiation, natural convection, and evaporation are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 The low mass flux conditions exist so that the Chilton–Colburn analogy between heat and mass transfer is applicable since the mass fraction of vapor in the air is low (about 2 percent for saturated air at 300 K). 2 Both air and water vapor at specified conditions are ideal gases (the error involved in this assumption is less than 1 percent). 3 Water is maintained at a uniform temperature of \( 50^{\circ}C \).

Properties Relevant properties for each mode of heat transfer are determined below in respective sections.

Analysis (a) The emissivity of liquid water is given in Table A–18 to be 0.95. Then the radiation heat loss from the water to the surrounding surfaces becomes

\( \dot{Q}_{rad} = \varepsilon A_{s}\sigma \left ( T_{s}^{4} – T_{surr}^{4} \right ) = \)

 

\( \left ( 0.95 \right )\left ( 3.5 m^{2} \right )\left ( 5.67 \times 10^{-8} W/m^{2} \times K^{4} \right )\left [ \left ( 323 K \right )^{4} – \left ( 293 K \right )^{4} \right ] \)

 

\( = 663 W \)

 

(b) The air–water vapor mixture is dilute and thus we can use dry air properties for the mixture at the average temperature of \( \left ( T_{\infty } + T_{s}\right )/2 = \left ( 25 + 50 \right )/2 = 37.5^{\circ}C \). Noting that the total atmospheric pressure is 92/101.3 = 0.9080 atm, the properties of dry air at 37.5°C and 0.908 atm are

\( k = 0.02644 W/m \cdot ^{\circ}C, Pr = 0.7261 \) (independent of pressure)

 

\( \alpha = \left ( 2.311 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \right )/0.9080 = 2.545 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \)

 

\( \nu = \left ( 1.678 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \right )/0.9080 = 1.848 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \)

 

The properties of water at \( 50^{\circ}C \) are

\( h_{fg} = 2383 kJ/kg and P_{\nu } = 12.35 kPa \)

 

The air at the surface is saturated, and thus the vapor pressure at the surface is simply the saturation pressure of water at the surface temperature (12.35 kPa). The vapor pressure of air far from the water surface is determined from

\( P_{\nu ,\infty } = \Phi P_{sat @ T_{\infty }} = \left ( 0.52 \right ) P_{sat @ 25^{\circ}C} \)

 

\( = \left ( 0.52 \right )\left ( 3.17 kPa \right ) = 1.65 kPa \)

 

Treating the water vapor and the air as ideal gases and noting that the total atmospheric pressure is the sum of the vapor and dry air pressures, the densities of the water vapor, dry air, and their mixture at the water–air interface and far from the surface are determined to be

At the surface:

\( \rho _{\nu ,s} = \frac{P_{\nu ,s}}{R_{\nu } T_{S}} = \frac{12.35 kPa}{\left ( 0.4615 kPa \cdot m^{3}/kg \cdot K \right )\left ( 323 K \right )} = 0.0828 kg/m^{3} \)

 

\( \rho _{a ,s} = \frac{P_{a ,s}}{R_{a } T_{S}} = \frac{\left ( 92 – 12.35 \right ) kPa}{\left ( 0.287 kPa \cdot m^{3}/kg \cdot K \right )\left ( 323 K \right )} = 0.8592 kg/m^{3} \)

 

\( \rho _{S} = \rho _{\nu ,s} + \rho _{a ,s} = 0.0828 + 0.8592 = 0.9420 kg/m^{3} \)

 

and

Away from the surface:

\( \rho _{\nu ,\infty } = \frac{P_{\nu ,\infty }}{R_{\nu } T_{\infty }} = \frac{1.65 kPa}{\left ( 0.4615 kPa \cdot m^{3}/kg \cdot K \right )\left ( 298 K \right )} = 0.0120 kg/m^{3} \)

 

\( \rho _{a ,\infty } = \frac{P_{a ,\infty }}{R_{a } T_{\infty }} = \frac{\left ( 92 – 1.65 \right ) kPa}{\left ( 0.287 kPa \cdot m^{3}/kg \cdot K \right )\left ( 298 K \right )} = 1.0564 kg/m^{3} \)

 

\( \rho _{\infty } = \rho _{\nu ,\infty } + \rho _{a ,\infty } = 0.0120 + 1.0564 = 1.0684 kg/m^{3} \)

 

The area of the top surface of the water bath is \( A_{s} = \left ( 3.5 m \right )\left ( 1 m \right ) = 3.5 m^{2} \) and its perimeter is p = 2(3.5 + 1) = 9 m. Therefore, the characteristic length is

\( L_{c} = \frac{A_{s}}{P} = \frac{3.5 m^{2}}{9 m} = 0.3889 m \)

 

Then using densities (instead of temperatures) since the mixture is not homogeneous, the Grashof number is determined to be

\( Gr = \frac{g \left ( \rho _{\infty } – \rho _{S} \right ) L_{c}^{3}}{\rho \nu ^{2}} \)

 

\( = \frac{\left ( 9.81 m/s^{2} \right ) \left ( 1.0684 – 0.9420 kg/m^{3} \right ) \left ( 0.3889 m \right )^{3}}{\left [ \left ( 0.9420 + 1.0684 \right )/2 kg/m^{3} \right ]\left ( 1.848 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \right )^{2}} \)

 

\( = 2.125 \times 10^{8} \)

 

Recognizing that this is a natural convection problem with hot horizontal surface facing up, the Nusselt number and the convection heat transfer coefficients are determined to be

\( Nu = 0.15 \left ( Gr Pr \right )^{1/3} = 0.15 \left (2.125 \times 10^{8} \times 0.7261 \right )^{1/3} = 80.45 \)

 

and

\( h_{conv} = \frac{Nuk}{L} = \frac{\left ( 80.45 \right )\left ( 0.02644 W/m \cdot ^{\circ}C \right )}{0.3889 m} = 5.47 W/m^{2} \cdot ^{\circ}C \)

 

Then the natural convection heat transfer rate becomes

\( \dot{Q}_{conv} = h_{conv} A_{s} \left ( T_{s} – T_{\infty } \right ) \)

 

\( = \left ( 5.47 W/m^{2} \cdot ^{\circ}C \right )\left ( 3.5 m^{2} \right )\left ( 50 – 25 \right )^{\circ}C = 479 W \)

 

Note that the magnitude of natural convection heat transfer is comparable to that of radiation, as expected.

 

(c) Utilizing the analogy between heat and mass convection, the mass transfer coefficient is determined the same way by replacing Pr by Sc. The mass diffusivity of water vapor in air at the average temperature of 310.5 K is determined from Eq. to be

\( D_{AB} = D_{H_{2}O-air} = 1.87 \times 10^{-10} \frac{T^{2.072}}{P} = 1.87 \times 10^{-10} \frac{310.5^{2.072}}{0.908} \)

 

\( = 3.00 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \)

 

The Schmidt number is determined from its definition to be

\( Sc = \frac{\nu }{D_{AB}} = \frac{1.848 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s}{3.00 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s} = 0.616 \)

 

The Sherwood number and the mass transfer coefficients are determined to be

\( Sh = 0.15 \left ( Gr Sc \right )^{1/3} = 0.15 \left ( 2.125 \times 10^{8} \times 0.616 \right )^{1/3} = 76.2 \)

 

and

\( h_{mass} = \frac{ShD_{AB}}{L_{c}} = \frac{\left ( 76.2 \right ) \left ( 3.00 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \right )}{0.3889 m} = 0.00588 m/s \)

 

Then the evaporation rate and the rate of heat transfer by evaporation become

\( \dot{m}_{\nu } = h_{mass} A_{s} \left ( \rho _{\nu , s} – \rho _{\nu , \infty } \right ) \)

 

\( = \left ( 0.00588 m/s \right )\left ( 3.5 m^{2} \right ) \left ( 0.0828 – 0.0120 \right ) kg/m^{3} \)

 

\( = 0.00146 kg/s = 5.25 kg/h \)

 

and

\( \dot{Q}_{evap} = \dot{m}_{\nu } h_{fg} = \left ( 0.00146 kg/s \right )\left ( 2383 kJ/kg \right ) \)

 

\( = 3.479 kW = 3479 W \)

 

which is more than seven times the rate of heat transfer by natural convection. Finally, noting that the direction of heat transfer is always from high to low temperature, all forms of heat transfer determined above are in the same direction, and the total rate of heat loss from the water to the surrounding air and surfaces is

\( \dot{Q}_{total} = \dot{Q}_{rad} + \dot{Q}_{conv} + \dot{Q}_{evap} \)

 

\( = 663 + 479 + 3479 = 4621 W \)

 

Discussion Note that if the water bath is heated electrically, a 4.6 kW resistance heater will be needed just to make up for the heat loss from the top surface. The total heater size will have to be larger to account for the heat losses from the side and bottom surfaces of the bath as well as the heat absorbed by the spray paint cans as they are heated to 50°C. Also note that water needs to be supplied to the bath at a rate of 5.25 kg/h to make up for the water loss by evaporation. Also, in reality, the surface temperature will probably be a little lower than the bulk water temperature, and thus the heat transfer rates will be somewhat lower than indicated here.
