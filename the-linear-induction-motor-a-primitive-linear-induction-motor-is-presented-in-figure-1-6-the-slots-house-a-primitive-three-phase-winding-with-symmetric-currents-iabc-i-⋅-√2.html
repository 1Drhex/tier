The Linear Induction Motor

A primitive linear induction motor is presented in Figure 1.6.

The slots house a primitive three-phase winding with symmetric currents:

\(i_{ABC}=I\cdot \sqrt{2}\cdot \cos \left\lgroup\omega t-(i-1)\cdot \frac{2\pi }{3} \right\rgroup\) (1.74)

They produce a traveling magnetomotive force:

\(F(x ,t)=F_{m}\cdot e^{j\cdot \left\lgroup\omega t-\frac{\pi }{\pmb{τ }}x \right\rgroup }\) (1.75)

The speed \(U_S\) of this traveling mmf is obtained by making the time derivative of its argument zero:

\(U_{s}=2\cdot τ\cdot f;\) \(\omega =2\cdot \pi \cdot f\) (1.76)

Calculate the forces \(F_{x}\) and \(F_{y}\) that are exerted on the conducting half space.

Admin
Solution

′This is a traveling wave field diffusion problem. As long as \(U\neq U_{s}\) the traveling wave A(x, t) produces eddy currents in the conducting half space. Their reaction field in turn reduces them along “y” direction.
Let us consider the traveling mmf in mover coordinates (at speed U):

\(F(x ,t)=F_{m}\cdot e^{j\cdot \left\lgroup s\cdot \omega t-\frac{\pi }{\pmb{τ }}x \right\rgroup }\) (1.77)

with\(S=1-U/U_{s^{\prime } }\) known as slip in electric machinery. We may now consider that there is no motion between primary and secondary, but the magnetic field variables vary at frequency \(S\cdot \omega _{0}.\)Moreover, the source current density \(\bar{J}_{sz}\) in the stator varies along x direction, while that induced in the mover,\(\bar{J}_{z}\), varies with x, y; both are considered only along z direction.
Now we can directly apply Helmholtz equation (1.34):

\(\nabla^2 \bar{\dot{A} }-j\cdot \omega \cdot \sigma \cdot\bar{\dot{A} }=+\mu \cdot \bar{j} _{s}\) (1.34)

\(\frac{\partial ^{2}A_{z}}{\partial x^{2} }+\frac{\partial ^{2}A_{z}}{\partial Y^{2} }-j\cdot S\cdot \omega \cdot \sigma \cdot \bar{A}=-\mu _{0}\cdot J_{sz}(x ,t)= -\mu _{0}\cdot J_{sz} \cdot e^{j\cdot \left\lgroup s\cdot \omega t-\frac{\pi }{\pmb{τ }}x \right\rgroup }\) (1.78)

The vector potential \(A_z\) will vary with x and t as \(J_{sz}(x, t) \) does:

\(A_{z}(x,y,t)= e^{j\cdot \left\lgroup S\cdot \omega t-\frac{\pi }{\tau }\cdot x \right\rgroup }\cdot A_{z}(y)\) (1.79)

A separation of variables was operated. Thus, the time variation can be left aside for the moment:

\(-\left\lgroup \frac{\pi }{\tau } \right\rgroup ^{2}\cdot A_{z}(y)+\frac{\partial A_{z}(y)}{\partial y^{2}}-j\cdot s\cdot \omega \cdot \sigma \cdot \bar{A}_{z}(y)=0\) (1.80)

The characteristic equation is

\(\underline{\gamma ^{2}}-\left\lgroup \left\lgroup \frac{\pi }{\tau } \right\rgroup ^{2} +j\cdot s\cdot \omega \sigma \right\rgroup =0\)(1.81)
\(\underline{\gamma}=\pm sqrt\left\lgroup \left\lgroup \frac{\pi }{\tau } \right\rgroup ^{2} +j\cdot s\cdot \omega \sigma \right\rgroup\)(1.82)

\(A_{z}\)(x, y, t) in the mover is thus

\(A_{z}(x,y,t)=(c_{1}\cdot e^{\underline{\gamma y}}+ c_{2}\cdot e^{\underline{-xy}} )\cdot e^{j\cdot \left\lgroup S\cdot \omega t-\frac{\pi }{\tau }\cdot x \right\rgroup } \) (1.83)

The first boundary condition is simply

\(A_{z}(X,Y,T)_{Y=\infty}=0 \), which means \(C_{1}\)=0 (1.84)

The second one is based on the Ampere’s law, along a contour on the mover surface, which states that

\(\frac{\partial F(x ,t)}{\partial x}=(H_{x})_{y=0}; H_{x}=\frac{B_{x}}{\mu _{0}}\) (1.85)

But as

\(\bar{B}=rot A_{z} \bar{U}_{z} \) (1.86)
\(B_{x}=\frac{\partial A_{z}}{\partial y}; B_{y}=-\frac{\partial A_{z}}{\partial x} \) (1.87)

So

\(\underline{C_{2}}=+j\cdot \frac {\pi }{\tau \cdot \bar{\gamma } }\cdot F_{xm} \) (1.88)

Further on, the current density \( \bar{J}_{z}\) in the mover is

\( \bar{J}_{z}=rot \bar{H}=\frac{\partial H_{y}}{\partial X}-\frac{\partial H_{x}}{\partial y}\) (1.89)

The field distribution in the secondary is thus practically solved. We still have to calculate the force components \(F_{x}\) and \(F_{y}\):

\(F_{x}+j\cdot F_{y}=\iint\int \bar{j}\times \bar{B} dv=L\cdot R_{e}\cdot \left\lgroup -\int\limits_{0}^{2\tau }\int\limits_{0}^{\infty }J_{z}\cdot B_{y} dxdy + \int\limits_{0}^{2\tau }\int\limits_{0}^{\infty }J_{z}\cdot B_{x} dxdy \right\rgroup \) (1.90)

Finally, we obtain the forces per wave length 2τ:

\(\left\langle F_{y(2\tau )}\right\rangle= -\mu _{0}\cdot \frac{I\cdot F^{2}_{xm} }{4}\left\lgroup\frac{(1+S^{\prime 2} )^{\frac{1}{2} }-1 }{(1+S^{\prime 2} )^{\frac{1}{2} }} \right\rgroup ; S^{\prime }= \mu _{0}\cdot \sigma \cdot S\cdot \omega _{1}\cdot \frac{\tau ^{2} }{\pi ^{2} } \) (1.91)
\(\left\langle F_{y(2\tau )}\right\rangle= \mu _{0}\cdot \frac{I\cdot F^{2}_{xm} }{4}\left\lgroup\frac{S^{\prime }}{(1+S^{\prime 2} )^{\frac{1}{2} }} \right\rgroup\) (1.92)

l is the laminated core length along axis z.

It was assumed that the current density in the secondary has only a \(J_{z}\) component like if the secondary conductor would have been short-circuited at ends by a superconductor slab (or that the core is very long (along z direction) with respect to pole pitch τ; not so in reality as seen in forthcoming chapters).

A few remarks are in order in the following:

• At zero slip S, there is no propulsion force \(F_{x}\), neither a repulsive normal force \(F_{y}\) (for S = 0, S′ = 0): this was to be expected as at S = 0 the conducting half space does not experience any magnetic flux change. So, \((J_{z})_{S=0}\) = 0.

• In reality, the primary length is finite and the secondary is longer than the primary; thus, new positions of secondary experience magnetic flux change when they enter or exit the stator mmf zone. Additional currents are induced in the secondary even at zero slip. This is the so-called dynamic longitudinal end effect that reduces, in general, the thrust \(F_{x}\), the efficiency, and the power factor of real LIMs.

• The LIM may be used rather with a fix ac mmf (not traveling) to stir melted metals for better homogeneity (for quality steel, etc.).

• The thrust (\(F_{x}\)) is positive (motoring) for positive slip (S > 0 or U < \(U_{s}\)); \(F_{x}\)< 0 also for S > 1 (\(U_{s}\) > 0 and U < 0)—typical braking.

• Alternatively if the LIM primary is single-phase dc fed and is moving above a conductive sheet at speed U, for U ≠ 0 a repulsive force (\(F_{y}\)) together with a braking force (\(F_{x}\) < 0) are produced as in repulsive levitation systems.
