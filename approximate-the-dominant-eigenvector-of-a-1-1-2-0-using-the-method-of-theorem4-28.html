Guest Question.

Approximate the dominant eigenvector of \(A=\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0\end{array}\right]\) using the method of Theorem 4.28

Theorem 4.28 Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) diagonalizable matrix with dominant eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}\). Then there exists a nonzero vector \(x_{0}\) such that the sequence of vectors \(x_{k}\) defined by

\( \mathbf{x}_{1}=A \mathbf{x}_{0}, \mathrm{x}_{2}=A \mathrm{x}_{1}, \mathrm{x}_{3}=A \mathrm{x}_{2}, \ldots, \mathrm{x}_{k}=A \mathrm{x}_{k-1}, \ldots \)

approaches a dominant eigenvector of \(A\).

Admin
Solution

We will take \(\mathbf{x}_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\ 0\end{array}\right]\) as the initial vector. Then

\( \begin{aligned} &\mathbf{x}_{1}=A \mathbf{x}_{0}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 2 \end{array}\right] \\ &\mathbf{x}_{2}=A \mathbf{x}_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{ll} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 2 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l} 3 \\ 2 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned}\)

We continue in this fashion to obtain the values of \(x_{k}\) in Table \(4.1 .\)

Figure 4.14 shows what is happening geometrically. We know that the eigenspace for the dominant eigenvector will have dimension 1. (Why? See Exercise 46.) Therefore, it is a line through the origin in \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\). The first few iterates \(\mathbf{x}_{k}\) are shown along with the directions they determine. It appears as though the iterates are converging on the line whose direction vector is \(\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\ 1\end{array}\right] .\) To confirm that this is the dominant eigenvector we seek, we need only observe that the ratio \(r_{k}\) of the first to the second component of \(\mathbf{x}_{k}\) gets very close to 1 as \(k\) increases. The second line in the body of Table \(4.1\) gives these values, and you can see clearly that \(r_{k}\) is indeed approaching \(1 .\) We deduce that a dominant eigenvector of \(A\) is \(\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\ 1\end{array}\right]\)

 

Once we have found a dominant eigenvector, how can we find the corresponding dominant eigenvalue? One approach is to observe that if an \(\mathrm{x}_{k}\) is approximately a dominant eigenvector of \(A\) for the dominant eigenvalue \(\lambda_{1}\), then

\(\mathbf{x}_{k+1}=A \mathrm{x}_{k} \approx \lambda_{1} \mathbf{x}_{k}\)

It follows that the ratio \(l_{k}\) of the first component of \(\mathbf{x}_{k+1}\) to that of \(\mathrm{x}_{k}\) will approach \(\lambda_{1}\) as \(k\) increases. Table 4.1 gives the values of \(l_{k}\), and you can see that they are approaching 2 , which is the dominant eigenvalue.
