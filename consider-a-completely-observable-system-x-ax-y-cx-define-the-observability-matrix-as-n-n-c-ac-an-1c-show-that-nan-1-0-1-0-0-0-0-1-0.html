Guest Question.

Consider a completely observable system

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{\mathbf{x}}=\mathbf{A x} \\&y=\mathbf{C x}\end{aligned}\)

Define the observability matrix as \(\mathbf{N}\) :

\(\mathbf{N}=\left[\begin{array}{l:l:l:l}\mathbf{C}^{*} & \mathbf{A}^{*} \mathbf{C}^{*} & \cdots & \left(\mathbf{A}^{*}\right)^{n-1} \mathbf{C}^{*}\end{array}\right]\)

Show that

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}\left(\mathbf{N}^{*}\right)^{-1}=\left[\begin{array}{ccccc}0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\\cdot & \cdot & \cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & \cdot & \cdot & & \cdot \\\cdot & \cdot & \cdot & & \cdot \\0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \\-a_{n} & a_{n-1} & -a_{n-2} & \cdots & -a_{1}\end{array}\right]\) (10.146)

where \(a_{1}, a_{2}, \ldots, a_{n}\) are the coefficients of the characteristic polynomial

\(|s \mathbf{I}-\mathbf{A}|=s^{n}+a_{1} s^{n-1}+\cdots+a_{n-1} s+a_{n}\)
Admin
Solution

Let us consider the case where \(n=3\). Then Equation (10-146) can be written as

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}\left(\mathbf{N}^{*}\right)^{-1}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right]\) (10.147)

 

Equation (10-147) may be rewritten as

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right] \mathbf{N}^ *\) (10.148)

 

We shall show that Equation (10-148) holds true. The left-hand side of Equation (10-148) is

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{c}\mathbf{C} \\\mathbf{C A} \\\mathbf{C A}^{2}\end{array}\right] \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{l}\mathbf{C A} \\\mathbf{C A}^{2} \\\mathbf{C A}^{3}\end{array}\right]\) (10.149)

 

The right-hand side of Equation (10-148) is

\(\begin{aligned}\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right] \mathbf{N}^{*} &=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c}\mathbf{C} \\\mathbf{C A} \\\mathbf{C A}^{2}\end{array}\right] \\\quad\\&=\left[\begin{array}{c}\mathbf{C A} \\ \mathbf{C A}^{2} \\-a_{3} \mathbf{C}-a_{2} \mathbf{C A}-a_{1} \mathbf{C A}^{2}\end{array}\right]\quad\quad\qquad\quad (10.150)\end{aligned}\)

 

The Cayley-Hamilton theorem states that matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) satisfies its own characteristic equation, or

\(\mathbf{A}^{3}+a_{1} \mathbf{A}^{2}+a_{2} \mathbf{A}+a_{3} \mathbf{I}=\mathbf{0}\)

 

Hence,

\(-a_{1} \mathbf{C A}^{2}-a_{2} \mathbf{C A}-a_{3} \mathbf{C}=\mathbf{C A}^{3}\)

 

Thus, the right-hand side of Equation (10-150) becomes the same as the right-hand side of Equation (10-149). Consequently,

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right] \mathbf{N}^{*}\)

 

which is Equation (10-148). This last equation can be modified to

\(\mathbf{N}^{*} \mathbf{A}\left(\mathbf{N}^{*}\right)^{-1}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0 & 1 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1 \\-a_{3} & -a_{2} & -a_{1}\end{array}\right]\)

 

The derivation presented here can be extended to the general case of any positive integer \(n\).
