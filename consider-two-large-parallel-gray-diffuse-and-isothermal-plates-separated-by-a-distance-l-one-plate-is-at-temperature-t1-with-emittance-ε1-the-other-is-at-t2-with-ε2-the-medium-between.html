Guest Question.

Consider two large, parallel, gray-difFuse and isothermal plates, separated by a distance
L. One plate is at temperature \(T_{1}\) with emittance \(ε_{1}\), the other is at \(T_{2}\) with ε2. The medium between the two plates is a gray, absorbing/emitting and linear-anisotropically scattering gas (n = 1) with constant extinction coefficient β and single scattering albedo w. Assuming that radiative equilibrium prevails, determine the radiative heat flux between the two plates using the \(S_{2}\)-approximation.

Admin
Solution

For radiative equilibrium we have, from equation (9.53) \(\triangledown \cdot q_{\eta }=\kappa _{\eta }\left\lgroup 4π I_{b\eta }-\int_{4\pi }^{}{}I_{\eta }d\Omega \right\rgroup =\kappa _{\eta }(4\pi I_{b\eta }-G_{\eta } )\) , \(I_{b} = G/4n\) and q = const; equations (16.18)\(\mu _{i}\frac{dI^{+}_{i} }{d\tau } + I^{+}_{i} = (1-w)I_{b}+\frac{w}{4\pi } (G+A_{1}q\mu _{i})\)
\(-\mu _{i}\frac{dI^{-}_{i} }{d\tau } + I^{-}_{i} = (1-w)I_{b}+\frac{w}{4\pi } (G+A_{1}q\mu _{i}) i=1,2,….,N/2; \mu _{i}\gt 0\) . and (16.19) \(\tau =0 : I^{+}_{i}=J_{1}/\pi =I_{b1} – \frac{1-\epsilon _{1}}{\epsilon _{1}\pi } q_{1}\)
\(\tau =\tau _{L} : I^{-}_{i}=J_{2}/\pi =I_{b2} – \frac{1-\epsilon _{2}}{\epsilon _{2}\pi } q_{q} i=1,2,…,N/2 , \mu _{i}\gt 0.\)become

\(\mu _{1}\frac{dI^{+}_{1} }{d\tau } + I^{+}_{1} = \frac{1}{4\pi } (G+A_{1}w\mu _{1}q),\)
\(-\mu _{1}\frac{dI^{-}_{1} }{d\tau } + I^{-}_{1} = \frac{1}{4\pi } (G-A_{1}w\mu _{1}q),\)
\(\tau =0 : I^{+}_{1}=J_{1}/\pi , \tau =\beta L=\tau _{L}: I^{-}_{1}=J_{2}/\pi\)

For the \(S_{2}\)-approximation we have only a single ordinate direction \(\mu _{1}\) (pointing toward \(\tau _{L}\) for \(I^{+}_{1}\), and toward 0 for\(I^{-}_{1}\)), where \(\mu _{1}\) = 0.57735 for the symmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation, and \(\mu _{1}\) = 0.5 for the nonsymmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation [which satisfies the half-range moment, equation (16.13) \(\int_{\overset{\wedge }{n} \cdot \overset{\wedge }{s}\lt 0}^{}{} \left|\overset{\wedge }{n}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{s}\right| d\Omega =\int_{\overset{\wedge }{n} \cdot \overset{\wedge }{s}\gt 0}^{}{} \overset{\wedge }{n} \overset{\wedge }{s} d\Omega=\pi =\sum\limits_{\overset{\wedge }{n} \cdot \overset{\wedge }{s_{i}}\gt 0}{w^{\prime }_{i} \overset{\wedge }{n} \cdot \overset{\wedge }{s_{i}}}\)]. For the simple \(S_{2}\)-approximation the simultaneous equations (only two in this case) may be separated. We do this here by eliminating \(I^{+}_{1}\) and \(I^{-}_{1}\) in favor of G and q. From equation (16.20) \(q=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{N/2}{w^{\prime }_{i} \mu _{i} }(I^{+}_{i}-I^{-}_{i}) ,\)
\(G=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{N/2}{w^{\prime }_{i} }(I^{+}_{i}+I^{-}_{i}) .\) , with \(w^{\prime }_{i}=2\pi\),

\(G=2\pi (I^{+}_{i}+I^{-}_{i})\)
\(q=2\pi \mu _{1}(I^{+}_{i}-I^{-}_{i})\).

Therefore, adding and subtracting the two differential equations and multiplying by \(2\pi\) leads to

\(\frac{dq}{d\tau } +G=G , or \frac{dq}{d\tau }=0,\)
\(\mu _{1}\frac{dG}{d\tau } +\frac{1}{\mu _{1}}q= A_{1}w\mu_{1}q, or \frac{dG}{d\tau } = – \left(\frac{1}{\mu ^{2}_{1} } – A_{1}w \right) q\)

The first equation is simply a restatement of radiative equilibrium, while the second may be integrated (since q = const), or

\(G=C-\left(\frac{1}{\mu ^{2}_{1} } – A_{1}w\right) q\tau\)

This relation contains two unknown constants (C and q), which must be determined from the boundary conditions, that is,

\(\tau =0 : I^{+}_{1} = \frac{1}{4\pi }\left(G+\frac{q}{\mu _{1}} \right)= J_{1}/\pi ,\)
\(\tau =\tau _{L} : I^{-}_{1} = \frac{1}{4\pi }\left(G+\frac{q}{\mu _{1}} \right)= J_{2}/\pi ,\)

or

\(\tau =0 : 4J_{1}=G+ \frac{q}{\mu _{1}} = C +\frac{q}{\mu _{1}},\)
\(\tau =\tau _{L} : 4J_{2}=G- \frac{q}{\mu _{1}} = C -\left( \frac{1}{\mu^{2} _{1}}-A_{1}w\right)q\tau _{L}- \frac{q}{\mu _{1}}\).

Subtracting, we obtain,

\(\Psi =\frac{q}{J_{1}-J_{2}}=\frac{2\mu _{1}}{1+(1/\mu ^{2}_{1}-A_{1}w) \mu _{1}\tau _{L}/2}\),

from which the radiosities may be eliminated through equation (13.44)\(q_{w}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{n}=\frac{\epsilon _{w}}{1-\epsilon _{w}}(\pi I_{bw}- J_{w})\) . For the symmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation, \(\mu_{1}=0.57735=1/\sqrt{3}\) , and with isotropic scattering,\(A_{1}\) = 0, this expression becomes

\(\Psi _{symmetric}= \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}/2+3\tau _{L}/4 }\)

On the other hand, for the nonsymmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation (\(\mu_{1}=0.5\) ), also with isotropic scattering,

\(\Psi _{symmetric}=\frac{1}{1+\tau _{L}}\).

The \(S_{2}\)-approximation is the same as the two-flux method discussed in Section 14.3, and the nonsymmetric \(S_{2}\)-method is nothing but the Schuster-Schwarzschild approximation. Results from the two \(S_{2}\)– approximations are compared in Table 16.3

It is seen that the accuracy of the \(S_{2}\)-method is roughly equivalent to the one of the \(P_{1}\)-approximation.
The nonsymmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation is superior to the symmetric one, since the symmetric \(S_{2}\) does not satisfy the half-moment condition, equation (16.13), and causes substantial errors in the optically thin limit.
