Guest Question.

A 1.0-kg bar of copper is heated at atmospheric pressure. If its temperature increases from 20°C to 50°C, (a) what is the work done by the copper on the surrounding atmosphere?

Admin
Solution

Because the process is isobaric, we can find the Work done by the copper using Equation 20.11, \(W=P\left(V_{f}-V_{i}\right) . \) We can calculate the change in volume of the copper using Equation 19.6 . Using the average linear expansion coefficient for copper given in Table 19.2, and remembering that \(\beta=3 \alpha, \) we obtain

\(\Delta V =\beta V_{i} \Delta T\) \(=\left[5.1 \times 10^{-5}\left({ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)^{-1}\right]\left(50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}-20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right) V_{i}=1.5 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~V}_{i}\)

The volume \(V_{i}\) is equal to \(m / \rho, \) and Table 15.1 indicates that the density of copper is \(8.92 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\). Hence,

\(\Delta V=\left(1.5 \times 10^{-3}\right)\left(\frac{1.0 \mathrm{~kg}}{8.92 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}}\right)=1.7 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\)

The work done is

\(W =P \Delta V=\left(1.019 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~N} / \mathrm{m}^{2}\right)\left(1.7 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right) \) \(=1.7 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~J}\)

 

(b) What quantity of energy is transferred to the copper oy heat?

Taking the speciﬁc heat of copper from Table 20.1 and using Equation 20.4, we ﬁnd that the energy trans- ferred by heat is

\(Q=m c \Delta T=(1.0 \mathrm{~kg})\left(387 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)\left(30^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\right)=1.2 \times 10^{4} \mathrm{~J}\)

(c) What is the increase in internal energy of the copper?

From the first law of thermodynamics, we have

\(\Delta E_{\text {int }}=Q-W=1.2 \times 10^{4} \mathrm{~J}-1.7 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~J}=1.2 \times 10^{4} \mathrm{~J}\)

Note that almost all of the energy transferred into the system by heat goes into increasing the internal energy. The fraction of energy used to do work on the surrounding atmosphere is only about 10—6! Hence, when analyzing the thermal expan-sion of a solid or a liquid, the small amount of work done by the system is usually ignored.
