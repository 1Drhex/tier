Guest Question.

Solve the following linear system by using the Gaussian elimination procedure:

\(x − 3y + 5z = −9\)
\(2x − y − 3z = 19\)
\(3x + y + 4z = −13\)

Admin
Solution

What is the augmented matrix in this case?
Let \(R_{1}\), \(R_{2}\) and \(R_{3}\) represent rows 1, 2 and 3 respectively. We have

\(\begin{matrix} _{x – 3 y + 5 z = – 9} & _{~~~~~~~~~ROW1} & _{R_{1} } \\ _{2 x – y – 3 z = 19} & _{and~~ROW2} & _{R_{2} } \\ _{3 x + y + 4 z = – 13} & _{~~~~~~~ROW3} & _{R_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{2} & _{- 1} & _{- 3} & _{|} & _{19} \\ _{3} & _{1} & _{4} & _{|} & _{- 13} \end{matrix} \right)\)

Note that each row represents an equation.

How can we find the unknowns \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\)?

The columns in the matrix represent the \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\) coefficients respectively. If we can transform this augmented matrix into

\(\begin{matrix} _{} & _{x} & _{y} & _{z} \\ _{} & _{\downarrow } & _{\downarrow } & _{\downarrow } \end{matrix}\)

\( Final ~row \left(\begin{matrix} _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast } \\ _{0} & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast } \\ _{0} & _{0} & _{A} & _{|} & _{B} \end{matrix} \right) where ~A, ~B ~and ~∗ ~represents ~any ~real ~number\)

then we can find \(z\).

How?
Look at the final row.
What does this represent?

\(\left(0\times x\right) + \left(0\times y\right) + \left(A\times z\right) = B\)

\(Az = B\) which gives \(z = \frac{B}{A}\) provided \(A \neq 0\)

Hence we have a value for \(z = B /A\).

But how do we find the other two unknowns \(x\) and \(y\)?

Now we can use a method called back substitution. Examine the second row of the above matrix:

\(~~~~~~~\begin{matrix} _{} & _{x} & _{y} & _{z}\end{matrix}\)

\(Second ~row \left(\begin{matrix} _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast } \\ _{0} & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast }\\ _{0} & _{0} & _{A} & _{|} & _{B} \end{matrix} \right)\)

By expanding the second row we get an equation in terms of \(y\) and \(z\). From above we already know the value of \(z = B /A\), so we can substitute \(z = B /A\) and obtain \(y\). Similarly from the first row we can find \(x\) by substituting the values of \(y\) and \(z\).

We need to perform row operations on the augmented matrix to transform it from:

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R_{2} } \\ _{R_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{2} & _{- 1} & _{- 3} & _{|} & _{19} \\ _{3} & _{1} & _{4} & _{|} & _{- 13} \end{matrix} \right) to \left(\begin{matrix} _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast } \\ _{0} & _{\ast } & _{\ast } & _{|} & _{\ast } \\ _{0} & _{0} & _{A} & _{|} & _{B} \end{matrix} \right)\)

We need to convert this augmented matrix to an equivalent matrix with zeros in the bottom left hand
corner. That is 0 in place of 2, 3 and 1.

How do we get 0 in place of 2?

Remember, we can multiply an equation by a non-zero constant, and take one equation away from
another. In terms of matrices, this means that we can multiply a row and take one row away from another
because each row represents an equation.

To get 0 in place of 2 we multiple row 1, \(R_{1}\), by 2 and subtract the result from row 2, \(R_{2}\); that is,we carry out the row operation \(R_{2} – 2 R_{1}\):

\(R^{\ast }_{2} = \begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R_{2} } \\ _{R_{3} } \end{matrix} – 2 R_{1} \left(\begin{matrix} _{2 – \begin{matrix} _{1} \\ _{2 \left(1\right) } \\ _{3} \end{matrix} } & _{- 1 – \begin{matrix} _{- 3} \\ _{ \left(2\times \left(- 3\right) \right) } \\ _{1} \end{matrix} } & _{- 3 – \begin{matrix} _{5} \\ _{ \left(2\times 5\right) } \\ _{4} \end{matrix} } & _{\begin{matrix} _{|} \\ _{|} \\ _{|} \end{matrix} } & _{19 – \begin{matrix} _{- 9} \\ _{ \left(2\times \left(- 9\right) \right) } \\ _{- 13} \end{matrix} }\end{matrix} \right)\)

We call the new middle row \(R^{\ast }_{2}\). Completing the arithmetic, the middle row becomes

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\ast }_{2} } \\ _{R_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{0} & _{5} & _{- 13} & _{|} & _{37} \\ _{3} & _{1} & _{4} & _{|} & _{- 13} \end{matrix} \right)\)

Where else do we need a zero?

Need to get a 0 in place of 3 in the bottom row.
How?

We multiply the top row \(R_{1}\) by 3 and subtract the result from the bottom row \(R_{3}\); that is,we carry out the row operation, \(R_{3} – 3 R_{1}\):

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\ast }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\ast }_{3} = R_{3} – 3 R_{1} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{0} & _{5} & _{- 13} & _{|} & _{37} \\ _{3 – 3 \left(1\right) } & _{1 – \left(3\times \left(- 3\right) \right) } & _{4 – \left(3\times 5\right) } & _{|} & _{- 13 – \left(3\times \left(- 9\right) \right) } \end{matrix} \right)\)

We can call \(R^{\ast }_{3}\) the new bottom row of this matrix. Simplifying the arithmetic in the entries gives:

Note that we now only need to convert the 10 into zero in the bottom row.
How do we get a zero in place of 10?

We can only make use of the bottom two rows, \(R^{\ast }_{2}\) and \(R^{\ast }_{3}\).

Why?
Looking at the first column, it is clear that taking any multiple of \(R_{1}\) away from \(R_{3}\) will interfere with the zero that we have just worked to establish.

We execute \(R^{\ast }_{3} – R^{\ast }_{2}\) because

\(10 – \left(2\times 5\right) = 0\) (gives a 0 in place of 10)

Therefore we have

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\ast }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\ast \ast }_{3} = R^{\ast }_{3} – 2 R^{\ast }_{2} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{0} & _{5} & _{- 13} & _{|} & _{37} \\ _{0 – \left(2\times 0\right) } & _{10 – \left(2\times 5\right) } & _{- 11 – \left[2\times \left(- 13\right) \right] } & _{|} & _{14 – \left(2\times 37\right) } \end{matrix} \right)\)

which simplifies to

\(\begin{matrix} _{x} & _{y} & _{z}~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~\end{matrix}\)

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\ast }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\ast \ast }_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{- 3} & _{5} & _{|} & _{- 9} \\ _{0} & _{5} & _{- 13} & _{|} & _{37} \\ _{0} & _{0} & _{15} & _{|} & _{- 60} \end{matrix} \right) ~~~~~~~~~~~~~\left(\dagger \right)\)

From the bottom row \(R^{\ast \ast }_{3}\) we have

\(15 z = – 60\) which gives \(z = – \frac{60}{15} = – 4\)

How do we find the other two unknowns \(x\) and \(y\)?

By expanding the middle row \(R^{\ast }_{2}\) of \(\left(\dagger \right)\) we have:

\(5 y – 13 z = 37\)

We can find \(y\) by substituting \(z = – 4\) into this

\(5 y – 13 \left(- 4\right) = 37\) [Substituting \(z = – 4\)]

\(5 y + 52 = 37\) which implies \(5 y = – 15\) therefore \(y = – \frac{15}{5} = – 3\)

How can we find the last unknown \(x\)?

By expanding the first row \(R_{1}\) of \(\left(\dagger \right)\) we have:

\(x – 3 y + 5 z = – 9\)

Substituting \(y = – 3\) and \(z = – 4\) into this:

\(x – \left(3\times \left(- 3\right) \right) + \left(5\times \left(- 4\right) \right) = – 9\)

\(x + 9 – 20 = – 9\) which gives \(x = 2\)

Hence our solution to the linear system is \(x = 2\), \(y = – 3\) and \(z = – 4\).

Remember, each of the given equations can also be graphically represented by planes in a 3d coordinate system.

The solution is where all three planes (equations) meet. The equations are illustrated in Fig. 1.9.
