Economics of Replacing a Valve with a Turbine

A cryogenic manufacturing facility handles liquid methane at 115 K and 5 MPa at a rate of \(0.280 m^3/s\). A process requires dropping the pressure of liquid methane to 1 MPa, which is done by throttling the liquid methane by passing it through a flow resistance such as a valve. A recently hired engineer proposes to replace the throttling valve with a turbine in order to produce power while dropping the pressure to 1 MPa. Also, determine how much this turbine will save the facility from electricity usage costs per year if the turbine operates continuously (8760 h/yr) and the facility pays $0.075/kWh for electricity.

Solution

Liquid methane is expanded in a turbine to a specified pressure at a specified rate. The maximum power that this turbine can produce and the amount of money it can save per year are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 This is a steady-flow process since there is no change with time at any point and thus \(\Delta m_{\mathrm{CV}}=0, \Delta E_{\mathrm{CV}}=0\), and \(\Delta S_{\mathrm{CV}}=0\). 2 The turbine is adiabatic and thus there is no heat transfer. 3 The process is reversible. 4 Kinetic and potential energies are negligible.

Analysis We take the turbine as the system (Fig. 7-29). This is a control volume since mass crosses the system boundary during the process. We note that there is only one inlet and one exit and thus \(\dot{m}_{1}=\dot{m}_{2}=\dot{m}\).

The preceding assumptions are reasonable since a turbine is normally well insulated and it must involve no irreversibilities for best performance and thus maximum power production. Therefore, the process through the turbine must be reversible adiabatic or isentropic. Then, \(s_{2}=s_{1}\) and

\(\text{State 1:}\quad\quad\left.\begin{array}{l}P_{1}=5 \mathrm{MPa} \\T_{1}=115 \mathrm{~K}\end{array}\right\} \begin{array}{l}h_{1}=232.3 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \\s_{1}=4.9945 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K} \\\rho_{1}=422.15 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\end{array}\)

 

\(\text{State 2:}\quad\quad\left.\begin{array}{l}P_{2}=1 \mathrm{MPa} \\s_{2}=s_{1}\end{array}\right\} h_{2}=222.8 \mathrm{~kJ} /\mathrm{kg}\)

 

Also, the mass flow rate of liquid methane is

\(\dot{m}=\rho_{1} \dot{V}_{1}=\left(422.15 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(0.280 \mathrm{~m}^{3} / \mathrm{s}\right)=118.2 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}\)

Then the power output of the turbine is determined from the rate form of the energy balance to be

\(\begin{aligned}\underbrace{\dot{E}_{\mathrm{in}}-\dot{E}_{\mathrm{out}}}_{\begin{array}{c}\text { Rate of net energy transfer } \\\text { by heat, work, and mass }\end{array}} &=\underbrace{d E_{\mathrm{system}}dt^{\nearrow 0 ( steady )} }_{\begin{array}{r}\text { Rate of change in internal, kinetic, } \\\text { potential, etc., energies }\end{array}}= 0 \\\dot{E}_{\mathrm{in}} &=\dot{E}_{\text {out }} \\\dot{m} h_{1} &\left.=\dot{W}_{\text {out }}+\dot{m} h_{2} \quad \text { (since } \dot{Q}=0, \mathrm{ke} \cong \mathrm{pe} \cong 0\right) \\\dot{W}_{\text {out }} &=\dot{m}\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right) \\&=(118.2 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s})(232.3-222.8) \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{kg} \\&=1123 \mathrm{~kW}\end{aligned}\)

For continuous operation \((365 \times 24=8760 \mathrm{~h})\), the amount of power produced per year is

\(\begin{aligned}\text { Annual power production } &=\dot{W}_{\text {out }} \times \Delta t=(1123 \mathrm{~kW})(8760 \mathrm{~h} / \mathrm{yr}) \\&=0.9837 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{kWh} / \mathrm{yr}\end{aligned}\)

At \(\$ 0.075 / \mathrm{kWh}\), the amount of money this turbine can save the facility is

\(\begin{aligned}\text {Annual money savings} &=\text{(Annual power production)(Unit cost of power)}\\&=\left(0.9837 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{kWh} / \mathrm{yr}\right)(\$ 0.075 / \mathrm{kWh}) \\&=\$ 737,800 / \mathrm{yr}\end{aligned}\)

That is, this turbine can save the facility \(\$ 737,800\) a year by simply taking advantage of the potential that is currently being wasted by a throttling valve, and the engineer who made this observation should be rewarded.

Discussion This example shows the importance of the property entropy since it enabled us to quantify the work potential that is being wasted. In practice, the turbine will not be isentropic, and thus the power produced will be less. The analysis above gave us the upper limit. An actual turbine-generator assembly can utilize about 80 percent of the potential and produce more than \(900 \mathrm{~kW}\) of power while saving the facility more than \(\$ 600,000\) a year.

It can also be shown that the temperature of methane drops to \(113.9 \mathrm{~K}\) (a drop of \(1.1 \mathrm{~K}\) ) during the isentropic expansion process in the turbine instead of remaining constant at \(115 \mathrm{~K}\) as would be the case if methane were assumed to be an incompressible substance. The temperature of methane would rise to \(116.6 \mathrm{~K}\) (a rise of \(1.6 \mathrm{~K}\) ) during the throttling process.
