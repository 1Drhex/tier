After-Tax Replacement Analysis Using the Opportunity Cost Approach

Suppose, in , that the new machine would fall into the same seven-year MACRS class as the old machine. The firm’s after-tax interest rate (MARR) is 10%, and the marginal income tax rate is 40%. Use the opportunity cost approach to decide whether replacement of the old machine is justified now.

Admin
Solution

Given: Financial information for both defender and challenger, \(t_{m}\) = 40%, and i = 10%.
Find: Whether or not to replace the defender now.

  • Option 1: Keep the defender. Table 14.4 shows the worksheet format the company uses to analyze a typical replacement project with the generalized cash-flow approach. Each line is numbered, and a line-by-line description is given.

Lines 1 to 4: If the old machine is kept, the depreciation schedule would be ($3,498, $2,498, and $1,786). Following the half-year convention, it is assumed that the asset will be retired at the end of three years; thus, the depreciation for year three is (0.5)($1,786) = $893. This results in total depreciation in the amount of $14,645 and a remaining book value of $20,000 – $14,645 = $5,355.

Lines 5 to 6: Repair costs in the amount of $5,000 were already incurred before the replacement decision. This is a sunk cost and should not be considered in the analysis. If a repair in the amount of $5,000 is required to keep the defender in serviceable condition, it will show as an expense in year 0. If the old machine is retained for the next three years, the before-tax annual O&M costs are as shown in line 6.

Lines 7 to 10: Recall that the depreciation allowances result in a tax reduction equal to the depreciation amount multiplied by the tax rate. The operating expenses are multiplied by the factor of (1 – the tax rate) to obtain the after-tax O&M. For a situation in which the asset is retained for now, the AEC(10%) is $7,524.

  • Option 2: Replace the defender. Table 14.5 shows an Excel worksheet based on the generalized cash-flow approach. As before, each line is numbered, and a line-by-line description of the table is given.

Line 1: The depreciation schedule, along with the book values for the new machine (seven-year MACRS), is shown. Again, the depreciation amount of $1,312 in year 3 reflects the half-year convention.

Line 2: The purchase price of the new machine, including installation and freight charges (if any), will be the depreciation base for the challenger. The book values are also shown here.

Line 4: With the salvage value estimated at $5,500, we expect a loss ($2,370 = $7,870 – $5,500) on the sale of the new machine at the end of year 3.

Line 6: The O&M costs for the new machine are listed.

Lines 7 to 10: The after-tax calculation for both O&M costs and depreciation tax credit are the same as those in Table 14.4. For a situation in which the asset is replaced with the challenger, the AEC(10%) is $6,723.

Because of the annual difference of $801 in favor of the challenger, the replacement should be made now. Figure 14.14 illustrates the cash flows related to these decision options.
