Bairstow’s Method

Employ Bairstow’s method to determine the roots of the polynomial

\(f_{5}(x) = x^5 − 3.5x^4 + 2.75x^3 + 2.125x^2 − 3.875x + 1.25 \)

Use initial guesses of r = s = −1 and iterate to a level of \(\varepsilon _{s} = 1\)%.

Admin
Solution

Equations (7.32) and (7.36) can be applied to compute

\(\begin{matrix} b_5 = 1&& b_4 =−4.5&& b_3 = 6.25 &&b_2 = 0.375&& b_1 = −10.5 \\ \\ b_0 = 11.375\\ \\ c_5 = 1&& c_4 =−5.5 &&c_3 = 10.75 &&c_2 = −4.875&& c1 = −16.375 \end{matrix} \)

 

\(b-n = a_n \) (7.32a)

\(b_{n−1} = a_{n−1} + rb_n \) (7.32b)

\( \begin{matrix} b_i = a_i + rb_{i+1} + sb_{i+2} && for i = n − 2 to 0 \end{matrix} \) (7.32c)

\(c_n = b_n \) (7.36a)

\(c_{n−1} = b_{n−1} + rc_n \) (7.36b)

\(\begin{matrix} c_i = b_i + rc_{i+1} + sc_{i+2} &&for i = n − 2 to 1 \end{matrix} \) (7.36c)

Thus, the simultaneous equations to solve for \( \Delta r\) and \( \Delta s\) are

\(−4.875\Delta r+10.75\Delta s= 10.5 \)

 

\( −16.375\Delta r-4.875\Delta s= −11.375\)

 

which can be solved for \(\Delta r=0.3558 \) and \(\Delta s = 1.1381\). Therefore, our original guesses can be corrected to

r = −1 + 0.3558 = −0.6442

s = −1 + 1.1381 = 0.1381

and the approximate errors can be evaluated by Eqs. (7.37) and (7.38),

\( \left|\varepsilon_{a,r} \right|=\left|\frac{0.3558}{−0.6442} \right|\) 100% = 55.23% \( \left|\varepsilon_{a,s} \right|=\left|\frac{1.1381}{0.1381 } \right|\) 100% = 824.1%

\(\left|\varepsilon _{a,r}\right|=\left|\frac{\Delta r}{r} \right| \)100% (7.37)

\(\left|\varepsilon _{a,s}\right|=\left|\frac{\Delta s}{s} \right| \)100% (7.38)

Next, the computation is repeated using the revised values for r and s. Applying Eqs. (7.32) and (7.36) yields

\( \begin{matrix}b_5 = 1 && b_4 = −4.1442 &&b_3 = 5.5578&& b_2 = −2.0276&& b_1 = −1.8013 \\ \\ b_0 = 2.1304\\ \\ c_5 = 1 &&c_4 = −4.7884 &&c_3 = 8.7806 &&c_2 = −8.3454 &&c_1 = 4.7874 \end{matrix} \)

Therefore, we must solve

\(−8.3454\Delta r+ 8.7806\Delta s = 1.8013 \)

 

\( 4.7874\Delta r − 8.3454\Delta s = −2.1304 \)

 

for \(\Delta r= 0.1331 \) and \(\Delta s = 0.3316, \) which can be used to correct the root estimates:

r = −0.6442 + 0.1331 = −0.5111 \( \left|\varepsilon _{a,r}\right| \)= 26.0%

s = 0.1381 + 0.3316 = 0.4697 \(\left|\varepsilon _{a,s}\right| \)= 70.6%

The computation can be continued, with the result that after four iterations the method converges on values of \(r=−0.5(\left|\varepsilon _{a,r}\right|= 0.063 \)% ) and \( s=0.5(\left|\varepsilon _{a,s}\right|= 0.040 \)% ) Equation (7.39) can then be employed to evaluate the roots as

\(x=\frac{-0.5\pm \sqrt{(−0.5)^2 + 4(0.5)} }{2} = 0.5, −1.0 \)

\(x=\frac{r\pm \sqrt{r^{2}+4s} }{2} \) (7.39)

At this point, the quotient is the cubic equation

\(f(x) = x^3 − 4x^2 + 5.25x − 2.5 \)

Bairstow’s method can be applied to this polynomial using the results of the previous step, r = −0.5 and s = 0.5, as starting guesses. Five iterations yield estimates of r = 2 and s = −1.249, which can be used to compute

\(x=\frac{2\pm \sqrt{2^2 + 4(−1.249)} }{2}= 1 \pm 0.499i \)

At this point, the quotient is a first-order polynomial that can be directly evaluated by Eq. (7.40) to determine the fifth root: 2.

\( x=-\frac{s}{r} \) (7.40)
