Guest Question.

It has been observed that substrate inhibition occurs in the following enzymatic reaction:

\(E+S \rightarrow P+E\)

a. Show that the rate law for substrate inhibition is consistent with the data shown in the Fig. E7.5.1 for \(r_{ P }\) versus the substrate concentration of S.

b. If this reaction is carried out in a fluidized CSTR that has a working volume of 5000 L, to which the volumetric flow rate is 15 L/min and the feed concentration of the substrate is 60 mmol/L, determine the possible conversion of substrate to the desired product. Since the enzyme is immobilized on the solid particle, assume that there is no loss of the enzyme to the effluent.

c. What would be the effluent substrate concentration if the enzyme loading is 25% lower?

Admin
Solution

For substrate inhibition, the rate expression follows Eq. (7.84) \(r_{P}=r_{\max } \frac{\beta \alpha \frac{S}{K_{S}}+1}{K_{S}+S+\alpha \frac{S^{2}}{K_{S}}} S\) or

\(r_{P}=r_{\max } \frac{\beta \alpha \frac{S}{K_{S}}+1}{K_{S}+S+\alpha \frac{S^{2}}{K_{S}}} S\) (E7.5.1)

with β < 1.

(a) Eq. (E7.5.1) \(r_{P}=r_{\max } \frac{\beta \alpha \frac{S}{K_{S}}+1}{K_{S}+S+\alpha \frac{S^{2}}{K_{S}}} S\) with β < 1 leads to

i. When [S] = 0, \(r_{ P }\) = 0;

ii. When [S] is low, \(r_{ P }\) increases with S;

iii. When [S] is very high, \(r_{ P }\) decreases with increasing [S] because of the \([ S ]^{2}\) term in the denominator;

iv. There is only one maximum (peak) value for \(r_{ P }\) as only [S] in the numerator and [S] and \([ S ]^{2}\) in the denominator.

Therefore, the data or curve shown in Fig. E7.5.1 is consistent with Eq. (E7.5.12) \(r_{P}=r_{\max } \frac{\beta \alpha \frac{S}{K_{S}}+1}{K_{S}+S+\alpha \frac{S^{2}}{K_{S}}} S\) .

(b) Mole balance on the substrate around the CSTR (Fig. E7.5.2) leads to:

\(Q S_{0}-Q S+r_{S} V=0\) (E7.5.2)

From stoichiometry,

\(\frac{r_{S}}{v_{S}}=\frac{r_{P}}{v_{P}}\) (E7.5.3)

Thus

\(r_{P}=-r_{S}=\frac{Q S_{0}-Q S}{V}\) \(=D\left(S_{0}-S\right)\) (E7.5.4)

\(D=Q / V=15 / 5000 min ^{-1}=3 \times 10^{-3} min ^{-1}\)

Therefore, the mole balance Eq (E7.5.4) \(r_{P}=-r_{S}=\frac{Q S_{0}-Q S}{V}\) \(=D\left(S_{0}-S\right)\) is a straight line on the (S, \(r_{ P }\)) plane with a negative slope of −D (−3 × \(10^{-3} min ^{-1}\)) and intercept on the S-axis of \(S_{0}\) = 60 mmol/L. Plot the mole balance line (red line) on to the Fig. E7.5.1 yields Fig. E7.5.3.

The mole balance line intercepts with the consumption rate of substrate (= generation rate of product) three times. Therefore, there are three possible steady state solutions: S = 44, 6.5, and 2.3 mmol/L. Correspondingly, the conversions are given by

\(f_{S}=\frac{S_{0}-S}{S_{0}}=1-\frac{S}{S_{0}}\) (E7.5.5)

The three possible fractional conversions are: 0.267, 0.892, and 0.962.

(c) If the enzyme loading is reduced by 25%, that is, \([E]_{0}\) = 0.75\([E]_{\text {0previous }}\). Based on Eq. (E7.5.12), the reaction rate is going to be reduced by 25%. Therefore, we can rescale the vertical axis of Fig. E7.5.1 (see the scale on the right on Fig. E7.5.4), while plotting the mole balance line, Eq. (E7.5.13) based on the new scale to find the possible steady state solutions. Now that only one steady state solution is obtained. S = 48.5 mmol/L or the conversion \(f_{ S }\) = 0.192.
