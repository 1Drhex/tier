Guest Question.

Two solid 2.00-in.-diameter steel shafts are connected by the gears shown in Figure P6.33. The shaft lengths are \(L_{1}\) = 10 ft and \(L_{2}\) = 18 ft. Assume that the shear modulus of both shafts is G = 12,000 ksi and that the bearings shown allow free rotation of the shafts. If the gear at D is rotated through an angle of 6Â°, what is the maximum shear stress in each shaft?

Admin
Solution

Torque relationships: The torque in shaft (2) is equal to the torque on gear D.

\(\Sigma M_{x}=T_{D}-T_{2}=0 \quad \therefore T_{2}=T_{D}\)

Accordingly, the torque applied to gear C is equal to \(T_{C}=T_{2}=T_{D}\). The torque on gear B is found with the gear ratio:

\(T_{B}=-T_{C} \frac{54 \text { teeth }}{30 \text { teeth }}=-1.8 T_{C}\)

Shaft (1) has the same torque as gear B; therefore, \(T_{1}=-1.8 T_{C}\), and thus:

\(T_{1}=-1.8 T_{2}\)

Torque-twist relationships: The angles of twist in each shaft can be expressed by

\(\phi_{1}=\frac{T_{1} L_{1}}{J_{1} G_{1}} \quad \text { and } \quad \phi_{2}=\frac{T_{2} L_{2}}{J_{2} G_{2}}\)

Rotation angles of gears: The angle of twist in shaft (1) can be expressed in terms of the rotation angles of gears A and B:

\(\phi_{1}=\phi_{B}-\phi_{A}\)

The angle of twist in shaft (2) can be expressed in terms of the rotation angles of gears C and D:

\(\phi_{2}=\phi_{D}-\phi_{C}\)

Additionally, as gear B rotates, gear C also rotates but it rotates in the opposite direction. The magnitude of the rotation is dictated by the gear ratio:

\(\phi_{C}=-\phi_{B} \frac{54 \text { teeth }}{30 \text { teeth }}\)

Combine all of these relationships to develop an expression for the rotation angle at D. Set this expression equal to the 6Â° rotation angle specified for gear D.

\(\phi_{D}=\phi_{2}+\phi_{C}=\phi_{2}-\phi_{B} \frac{54 \text { teeth }}{30 \text { teeth }}=\phi_{2}-\left(\phi_{1}+\phi_{A}\right) \frac{54 \text { teeth }}{30 \text { teeth }}=\phi_{2}-\phi_{1} \frac{54 \text { teeth }}{30 \text { teeth }}=6^{\circ}\)

Substitute the torque-twist relationships and the torque equilibrium relationships:

\(\phi_{D}=\frac{T_{2} L_{2}}{J_{2} G_{2}}-1.8 \frac{T_{1} L_{1}}{J_{1} G_{1}}=6^{\circ}\left(\frac{\pi}{180^{\circ}}\right)=0.104720 rad\)

Simplify and solve for \(T_{2}\), recognizing that the polar moments of inertia and the shear moduli are the same for both shafts:

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{T_{2} L_{2}}{J_{2} G_{2}}-1.8 \frac{\left(-1.8 T_{2}\right) L_{1}}{J_{1} G_{1}} &=0.104720 rad \\\frac{T_{2}}{J G}\left[L_{2}+1.8^{2} L_{1}\right] &=0.104720 rad \\T_{2} &=\frac{(0.104720 rad ) J G}{L_{2}+1.8^{2} L_{1}}\end{aligned}\)

Calculate the polar moment of inertia of a solid 2.0-in.-diameter shaft:

\(J=\frac{\pi}{32}(2.00 in .)^{4}=1.570796 in. ^{4}\)

and substitute this value into the previous expression to obtain \(T_{2}\):

\(T_{2}=\frac{(0.104720 rad )\left(1.570796 in .^{4}\right)(12,000,000 psi )}{\left[(18 ft )+1.8^{2}(10 ft )\right](12 in. / ft )}=3,263.758 lb \text {-in. }\)

Accordingly, the torque in shaft (1) is:

\(T_{1}=-1.8 T_{2}=-1.8(3,263.758 lb \text {-in. })=-5,874.764 lb \text {-in. }\)

Maximum shear stress in shafts (1) and (2): Note that only the magnitude of the shear stress is desired; therefore, the negative sign for \(T_{1}\) can be omitted from the following calculation.

\(\begin{aligned}&\tau_{1}=\frac{T_{1} c_{1}}{J_{1}}=\frac{(5,874.764 lb – in .)(2.0 in. / 2)}{1.570796 in .^{4}}=3,739.991 psi =3,740 psi \\&\tau_{2}=\frac{T_{2} c_{2}}{J_{2}}=\frac{(3,263.758 lb – in .)(2.00 in. / 2)}{1.570796 in .^{4}}=2,077.773 psi =2,080 psi\end{aligned}\)
