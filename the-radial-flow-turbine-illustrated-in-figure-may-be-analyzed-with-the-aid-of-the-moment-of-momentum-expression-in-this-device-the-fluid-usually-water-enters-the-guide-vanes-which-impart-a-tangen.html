Guest Question.

The radial-flow turbine illustrated in Figure may be analyzed with the aid of the moment-of-momentum expression. In this device, the fluid (usually water) enters the guide vanes, which impart a tangential velocity and hence angular momentum to the fluid before it enters the revolving runner, which reduces the angular momentum of the fluid while delivering torque to the runner.

Admin
Solution

The control volume to be used is illustrated below in Figure. The outer boundary of the control volume is at radius \(r_{1}\), and the inner boundary is at \( r_{2}\).The width of the runner is h.

We will use equation (\( \sum M = \iint_{c.s.}^{} \left ( r \times v \right )\rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) dA + \frac{\partial }{\partial t} \int \int \int_{c.v.}^{} \left ( r \times v \right ) \rho dV \)) in order to determine the torque. For steady flow, this equation becomes

 

\( \sum M = \iint_{c.s.}^{} \left ( r \times v \right )\rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) d A \)

 

Evaluating each term separately, we have, for the external moment of the runner on the fluid,

\( \sum M = M_{fluid} e_{z} = – T e_{z} \)

 

where T is the shaft torque. The surface integral requires the evaluation of the vector product \( \left ( r \times v \right ) \) at the outer boundary \( r_{1} \) and at the inner boundary \( r_{2}. \) If we express the velocity of the water in polar coordinates \( \upsilon = \upsilon_{r} e_{r} + \upsilon_{\theta } e_{\theta } \), so that
\( \left ( r \times v \right ) = r e_{r} \times \left ( \upsilon_{r} e_{r} + \upsilon_{\theta } e_{\theta } \right ) = r \upsilon_{\theta } e_{z}. \) Thus, the surface integral, assuming uniform velocity distribution, is given by

\( \iint_{c.s.}^{} \left ( r \times v \right ) \rho \left ( v \cdot n \right ) d A = \left \{ r_{1} \upsilon_{\theta_{1}} \rho \left ( – \upsilon_{r_{1}} \right ) 2 \pi r_{1} h + r_{2} \upsilon_{ \theta_{2}} \rho \upsilon_{ r_{2}} 2 \pi r_{2} h \right \} e_{z} \)

 

The general result is

\( – T e_{z} = \left ( – \rho \upsilon_{r_{1}} \upsilon_{\theta} 2 \pi r_{1}^{2} h + \rho \upsilon_{r_{2}} \upsilon_{\theta_{2}} 2 \pi r_{2}^{2} h \right ) e_{z} \)

 

The law of conservation of mass may be used

\( \rho \upsilon_{r_{1}} 2 \pi r_{1} h = \dot{m} = \rho \upsilon_{r_{2}} 2 \pi r_{2} h \)

 

so that the torque is given by

\( T = \dot{m} \left ( r_{1} \upsilon_{\theta_{1}} – r_{2} \upsilon_{\theta_{2}} \right ) \)

 

The velocity at \(r_{1} \) is seen from Figures to be determined by the flow rate and the guide vane angle a. The velocity at \(r_{2} \), however, requires knowledge of flow conditions on the runner.
The velocity at \(r_{2} \) may be determined by the following analysis. In Figure, the flow conditions at the outlet of the runner are sketched. The velocity of the water \(\upsilon_{2} \) is the vector sum of the velocity with respect to the runner \( {\upsilon}'_{2} \) and the runner velocity \( r_{2} \omega. \)

The velocity \( \upsilon_{\theta_{2}} \), the tangential velocity of the water leaving the runner, is given by

\( \upsilon_{\theta_{2}} = r_{2} \omega – {\upsilon}'_{0} \sin \beta \)

 

where \( \beta \) is the blade angle as shown. The fluid is assumed to flow in the same direction as the blade. The radial component of the flow may be determined from conservation of mass

\( \upsilon_{r_{2}} = {\upsilon}'_{2} \cos \beta = \frac{\dot{m}}{2 \pi \rho r_{2} h} \)

Thus,

\( T = \dot{m} \left ( r_{1} \upsilon_{\theta_{1}} – r_{2} \left [ r_{2} \omega – \frac{\dot{m} \tan \beta }{2 \pi \rho r_{2} h} \right ] \right ) \)

 

In practice, the guide vanes are adjustable to make the relative velocity at the runner entrance tangent to the blades.
