Guest Question.

The angles of rotation are selected as generalized coordinates for the double pendulum, whose bars have equal mass m. Derive Hamiltonian equations of motion.

Admin
Solution

This system is sufficiently simple that we could skip some steps, but we will follow the formal procedures in order to get a good picture of what operations are required for formulating Hamiltonâ€™s equations for a complicated system. We begin the analysis by expressing the Lagrangian in terms of the generalized coordinates and velocities. Bar AB is in pure rotation about end A, and bar BC is in general motion. We obtain the velocity of the center of mass G of bar BC by differentiating its position, which gives

\(\bar{v}_{G}=\left(L \dot{\theta}_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+\frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\right) \bar{i}+\left(L \dot{\theta}_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+\frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \bar{j}, \)

where \(\bar{i}\) is to the right and \(\bar{j}\) is upward. The kinetic energy is

\(\begin{aligned}T=& \frac{1}{2} I_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m \bar{v}_{G} \cdot \bar{v}_{G}+\frac{1}{2} I_{G} \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2} \\\\=& \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}\right) \dot{\theta}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m\left[L^{2} \dot{\theta}_{1}^{2}+\frac{L^{2}}{4} \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2}\right.\\\\&\left.+L^{2} \dot{\theta}_{1} \dot{\theta}_{2}\left(\cos \theta_{1} \cos \theta_{2}+\sin \theta_{1} \sin \theta_{2}\right)\right]+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{12} m L^{2}\right) \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2} \\\\=& \frac{1}{2} m L^{2}\left[\frac{4}{3} \dot{\theta}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{3} \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2}+\dot{\theta}_{1} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right].\end{aligned} \)

The elevation of pin \(A\) is a convenient datum for gravitational potential energy. The corresponding Lagrangian is

\(\begin{aligned}\mathcal{L}=& \frac{1}{2} m L^{2}\left[\frac{4}{3} \dot{\theta}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{3} \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2}+\dot{\theta}_{1} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right] \\\\&+m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta_{1}+m g\left(L \cos \theta_{1}+\frac{L}{2} \cos \theta_{2}\right).\end{aligned} (1) \)

The next step is to form the generalized momenta. The kinetic energy has only quadratic terms in the generalized velocities, so \(\{N\}=\{0\}\) and \(T_{0}=0 .\) The easiest way to identify \([M]\) is to form the \(p_{j}\) variables by differentiating \(\mathcal{L}\) and then compare the result with Eq. (9.2.9). Differentiation yields

\(\begin{aligned}p_{j} &=\frac{\partial}{\partial \dot{q}_{j}}\left[\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} M_{k n}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{q}_{k} \dot{q}_{n}+\sum_{k=1}^{N} N_{k}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{q}_{k}+T_{0}\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right] \\\\&=\sum_{k=1}^{N} M_{k j}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{q}_{k}+N_{j}\left(q_{i}, t\right), \quad j=1,2, \ldots, N.\end{aligned} (9.2.9) \)

 

\(\begin{array}{l}p_{1}=\frac{\partial \mathscr{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_{1}}=m L^{2}\left[\frac{4}{3} \dot{\theta}_{1}+\frac{1}{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right], \\\\p_{2}=\frac{\partial \mathscr{L}}{\partial \dot{\theta}_{2}}=m L^{2}\left[\frac{1}{3} \dot{\theta}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \dot{\theta}_{1} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right] .\end{array} \)

Matching this to \(\{p\}=[M]\{\dot{q}\}+\{N\}\) leads to

\([M]=m L^{2}\left[\begin{array}{cc}\frac{4}{3} & \frac{1}{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right) \\\\\frac{1}{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right) & \frac{1}{3}\end{array}\right]. (2) \)

The inverse of the inertia matrix is

\([M]^{-1}=\frac{1}{m L^{2} \Delta}\left[\begin{array}{cc}12 & -18 \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right) \\\\-18 \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right) & 48\end{array}\right], (3) \)

where

\(\Delta=16-9 \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)^{2} . (4)\)

From this, we form \(\mathcal{H}\) by following Eq. (9.2.13), which yields

\(\begin{aligned} \mathcal{H}=& \frac{1}{2}\{p\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\{p\}-\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\{p\} \\\\ &-\frac{1}{2}\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}-T_{0}\left(q_{i}, t\right)+V\left(q_{i}, t\right). \end{aligned} (9.2.13) \)

\(\begin{aligned}\mathcal{H}=& \frac{1}{2}\left[\begin{array}{ll}p_{1} & p_{2}\end{array}\right][M]^{-1}\left\{\begin{array}{c}p_{1} \\\\p_{2}\end{array}\right\}+V \\\\=& \frac{1}{2 m L^{2} \Delta}\left[12 p_{1}^{2}+48 p_{2}^{2}-36 p_{1} p_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right] \\\\&-m g \frac{L}{2}\left(3 \cos \theta_{1}+\cos \theta_{2}\right).\end{aligned} (5)\)

All applied forces are conservative forces, and the generalized coordinates are unconstrained, so the generalized forces are zero.

We obtain the first set of Hamilton’s equations by differentiating \(\mathcal{H}\) with respect to the generalized momenta:

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{\theta}_{1}=\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p_{1}}=\frac{1}{m L^{2} \Delta}\left[12 p_{1}-18 p_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right],\\\dot{\theta}_{2}=\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p_{2}}=\frac{1}{m L^{2} \Delta}\left[48 p_{2}-18 p_{1} \cos \left(\theta_{1}-\theta_{2}\right)\right].\end{aligned} (6) \)

Differentiation of \(\mathcal{H}\) with respect to the generalized coordinates leads to the second portion of Hamilton’s equations. The generalized coordinates appear in the inertial terms only as \(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\), which simplifies the operations. Thus,

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{p}_{1}=-\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial \theta_{1}}=& \frac{1}{2 m L^{2}}\left\{\frac{1}{\Delta^{2}} \frac{d \Delta}{d \theta_{1}}\left[12 p_{1}^{2}+48 p_{2}^{2}-36 p_{1} p_{2} \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right]\right\} \\\\&+\frac{1}{2 m L^{2} \Delta}\left[-36 \sin \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right]-\frac{3}{2} m g L \sin \theta_{1} \\\\=&-\frac{18 \sin \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)}{m L^{2} \Delta^{2}}\left[\left(6 p_{1}^{2}+24 p_{2}^{2}\right) \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right.\\\\&\left.-p_{1} p_{2}\left(16+9 \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)^{2}\right)\right]-\frac{3}{2} m g L \sin \theta_{1},\end{aligned} (7) \)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{p}_{2}=-\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial \theta_{2}}=& \frac{18 \sin \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)}{m L^{2} \Delta^{2}}\left[\left(6 p_{1}^{2}+24 p_{2}^{2}\right) \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)\right.\\\\&\left.-p_{1} p_{2}\left(16+9 \cos \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)^{2}\right)\right]-\frac{1}{2} m g L \sin \theta_{2}.\end{aligned} \)

Given that this system is relatively uncomplicated, it is clear that forming Hamilton’s equations in more engineering-oriented systems is likely to be a tedious process, unless one uses symbolic mathematical software.
