Guest Question.

The axial-flow pump in Fig. 6-12a has an impeller with blades having a mean radius of \(r_m = 80 mm\). Determine the average torque T that must be applied to the impeller, in order to create a flow of water through the pump of\( 0.1 m^3/s \) while the impeller rotates at \(\omega = 120 rad/s.\) The open cross-sectional area through the impeller is\( 0,025 m^2\). The flow of water is delivered onto each blade along the axis of the pump, and it exits the blade with a fangential component of velocity of 5 m/s, as shown in Fig. 6-12b.

Admin
Solution

Fluid Description. Flow through the pump is cyclic unsteady flow, but on a time average basis it may be considered mean steady flow Here we will consider water is an ideal fluid, where \(\rho= 1000 kg/in^3 \).
Since we are looking for the forque developed by the pump on the shall, we must apply the angular momentum equation.

Control Volume and Free-Body Diagram. As in the previous example, we will consider a fixed control volume that includes the impeller and the water surrounding yet slightly removed from it, Fig. 6-12a. The forces acting on its free-body diagram, Fig. 6-12c, include the pressure of the water acting on the entrance and exit control surfaces, and the torque Ton the impeller shaft. The weight of the water and blades, along with the pressure distribution around the rim of the closed control surfaces, is not shown since they produce no torque about the shaft.

Continuity Equation. The axial flow through each open controle surface is maintained since their areas are equal. That is

 

\(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{cv} V\rho dV +\int_{cs}\rho V\cdot dA =0\)

 

\(0 – \rho V_{a1}A + V_{a2}A =0\)

 

\(V_{a1} = V_{a2}\)

Therefore, flow in the axial direction is constant, and so Q = V.

\(Q V_aA; 0.1 m^3/s = V_a(0.025 m^2)\)

 

\(V_a = 4 m/s\)

Angular Momentum. Applying the angular momentum equation about the axis of the shaft, for steady flow we have

\( \sum{M}_A = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{cv}(r \times V)\rho dV + \int_{cs}(r \times V)\rho V\cdot dA\)

\(T = 0 + \int_{cs} r_mV_{t}\rho V_adA \) (1)

Here the vector cross product is replaced by the moment of the Tangential componen V, of the water’s velocity V. Only this component produces a moment about the axis of the shaft, Fig. 6-12b. Also, notice that the flow through the open control surfaces is determined only from the axial component of V, that is \(\rho V . dA = \rho V_a dA.\) Therefore, integrating over the area, we get

\(T = r_m(V_t)_2(\rho V_aA) + r_m(V_t)_1( -\rho V_aA)\)(2)

The flow through the open control surfaces, onto and off a blade, is shown in Fig. 6-12b. Here it can be seen that there is no initial tangential component of velocity. \(V_{t1} 0 \), because the flow is delivered to the blade in the axial direction at the rate of \( V_{a} = 4 m/s. \) At the top of the blade, the impeller has given the water a velocity \( V_2 = V_a + (V_t)_2 \), but as stated, only the tangential component creates angular momentum. Substituting the data into Eq.2, we therefore have

\(T = (0,08 m) (5 m/s)[(1000 kg/m)(4 m/s)(0.025 m^2)] – 0 = 40Nm\)
