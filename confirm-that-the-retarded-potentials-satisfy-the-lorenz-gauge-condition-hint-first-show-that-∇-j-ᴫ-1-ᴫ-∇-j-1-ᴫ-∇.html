Guest Question.

Confirm that the retarded potentials satisfy the Lorenz gauge condition. [Hint: First show that

\(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}(\nabla \cdot J )+\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left(\nabla^{\prime} \cdot J \right)-\nabla^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)\).

where ∇ denotes derivatives with respect to r, and \(\nabla^{\prime}\) denotes derivatives with respect to \(r ^{\prime} .\) Next, noting that \(J \left( r ^{\prime}, t-ᴫ / c\right)\) depends on \(r ^{\prime}\) both explicitly and through ᴫ, whereas it depends on r only through ᴫ, confirm that

\(\nabla \cdot J =-\frac{1}{c} \dot{ J } \cdot(\nabla ᴫ), \quad \nabla^{\prime} \cdot J =-\dot{\rho}-\frac{1}{ c } \dot{ J } \cdot\left( \nabla ^{\prime} ᴫ \right)\).

Use this to calculate the divergence of A (Eq. 10.26).]

\(V( r , t)=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho\left( r ^{\prime}, t_{r}\right)}{ᴫ} d \tau^{\prime}, \quad A ( r , t)=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( r ^{\prime}, t_{r}\right)}{ᴫ} d \tau^{\prime}\) (10.26)

Admin
Solution

From the product rule:

\(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}(\nabla \cdot J )+ J \cdot\left(\nabla \frac{1}{ᴫ}\right), \quad \nabla^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left(\nabla^{\prime} \cdot J \right)+ J \cdot\left(\nabla^{\prime} \frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)\).

But \(\nabla \frac{1}{ᴫ}=-\nabla^{\prime} \frac{1}{ᴫ}, \text { since } ᴫ = r – r ^{\prime}\) . So

\(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}( \nabla \cdot J )- J \cdot\left( \nabla ^{\prime} \frac{1}{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}( \nabla \cdot J )+\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left( \nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J \right)-\nabla^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)\).

But

\(\nabla \cdot J =\frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial J_{y}}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial J_{z}}{\partial z}=\frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial J_{y}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial J_{z}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial z}\),

and

\(\frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial x}=-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial x}, \quad \frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial y}=-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial y}, \quad \frac{\partial t_{r}}{\partial z}=-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial z}\),

so

\(\nabla \cdot J =-\frac{1}{c}\left[\frac{\partial J_{x}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial J_{y}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial J_{z}}{\partial t_{r}} \frac{\partial ᴫ}{\partial z}\right]=-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial J }{\partial t_{r}} \cdot(\nabla ᴫ\).

Similarly,

\(\nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J =-\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial J }{\partial t_{r}} \cdot\left( \nabla ^{\prime} ᴫ \right)\).

[The first term arises when we differentiate with respect to the explicit \(r ^{\prime}\), and use the continuity equation.] thus

\(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left[-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial J }{\partial t_{r}} \cdot\left(\nabla^{\prime} ᴫ\right)\right]+\frac{1}{ᴫ}\left[-\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}-\frac{1}{c} \frac{\partial J }{\partial t_{r}} \cdot\left(\nabla^{\prime} ᴫ\right)\right]-\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)=-\frac{1}{ᴫ} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}-\nabla^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right)\)

(the other two terms cancel, since \(\nabla ᴫ=- \nabla ^{\prime} ᴫ \) ). Therefore:

\(\nabla \cdot A =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi}\left[-\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int^{л{ }} \frac{\rho}{ᴫ} d \tau-\int \nabla ^{\prime} \cdot\left(\frac{ J }{ᴫ}\right) d \tau\right]=-\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left[\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho}{ᴫ} d \tau\right]-\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \oint \frac{ J }{ᴫ}\) · da .

The last term is over the suface at “infinity”, where J = 0, so it’s zero. Therefore \(\nabla \cdot A =-\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}\).
