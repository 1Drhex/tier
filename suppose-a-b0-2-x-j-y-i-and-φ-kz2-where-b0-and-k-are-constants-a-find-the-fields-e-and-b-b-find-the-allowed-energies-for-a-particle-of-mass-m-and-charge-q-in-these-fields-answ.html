Guest Question.

Suppose

\( A =\frac{B_{0}}{2}(x \hat{\jmath}-y \hat{\imath}), \quad \text { and } \quad \varphi=K z^{2} \).

where \( B_0 \) and K are constants.

(a) Find the fields E and B.

(b) Find the allowed energies, for a particle of mass m and charge q, in these fields. Answer:

\( E\left(n_{1}, n_{2}\right)=\left(n_{1}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{1}+\left(n_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{2}, \quad\left(n_{1}, n_{2}=0,1,2, \ldots\right) \), (4.195).

\( \text { where } \omega_{1} \equiv q B_{0} / m \text { and } \omega_{2} \equiv \sqrt{2 q K / m} \). Comment: In two dimensions (x and y , with K = 0 ) this is the quantum analog to cyclotron motion; \( ω_1 \) is the classical cyclotron frequency, and \( ω_2 \) s zero. The allowed energies, \( \left(n_{1}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{1} \), are called Landau Levels.

Admin
Solution

(a)

\( E =- \nabla \varphi=-2 K z \hat{k} . \quad B = \nabla \times A =\left|\begin{array}{ccc} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \partial / \partial x & \partial / \partial y & \partial / \partial z \\ -B_{0} y / 2 & B_{0} x / 2 & 0 \end{array}\right|=B_{0} \hat{k} \).

(b) For time-independent potentials Eq. 4.191 separates in the usual way:

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\left[\frac{1}{2 m}(-i \hbar \nabla-q A )^{2}+q \varphi\right] \Psi \) (4.191).

\( \frac{1}{2 m}(-i \hbar \nabla-q A ) \cdot(-i \hbar \nabla -q A ) \psi+q \varphi \psi=E \psi \), or

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla^{2} \psi+\frac{i q \hbar}{2 m}[ \nabla \cdot( A \psi)+ A \cdot( \nabla \psi)]+\frac{q^{2}}{2 m} A^{2} \psi+q \varphi \psi=E \psi . \quad \text { But } \quad \nabla \cdot( A \psi)=( \nabla \cdot A ) \psi+ A \cdot( \nabla \psi) \), so

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla^{2} \psi+\frac{i q \hbar}{2 m}[2 A \cdot( \nabla \psi)+( \nabla \cdot A ) \psi]+\left(\frac{q^{2}}{2 m} A^{2}+q \varphi\right) \psi=E \psi \).

This is the time-independent Schrödinger equation for electrodynamics. In the present case

\( \nabla \cdot A =0, \quad A \cdot( \nabla \psi)=\frac{B_{0}}{2}\left(x \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y}-y \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}\right), \quad A^{2}=\frac{B_{0}^{2}}{4}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right), \quad \varphi=K z^{2} \).

But \( L_{z}=-i \hbar\left(x \frac{\partial}{\partial y}-y \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\right), \quad \text { so }-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla^{2} \psi-\frac{q B_{0}}{2 m} L_{z} \psi+\left[\frac{q^{2} B_{0}^{2}}{8 m}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)+q K z^{2}\right] \psi=E \psi \).

Since \( L_z \) commutes with H, we may as well pick simultaneous eigenfunctions of both: \( L_{z} \psi=\bar{m} \hbar \psi \) where \( \bar{m}=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots \) (with the overbar to distinguish the magnetic quantum number from the mass). Then

\( \left[-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \nabla^{2}+\frac{\left(q B_{0}\right)^{2}}{8 m}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)+q K z^{2}\right] \psi=\left(E+\frac{q B_{0} \hbar}{2 m} \bar{m}\right) \psi \).

Now let \( \omega_{1} \equiv q B_{0} / m \), \( \omega_{2} \equiv \sqrt{2 K q / m} \), and use cylindrical coordinates (r,Φ,z) (you can also do it in Cartesian coordinates):

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial^{2} \psi}{\partial \phi^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} \psi}{\partial z^{2}}\right]+\left[\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{2}^{2} z^{2}\right] \psi=\left(E+\frac{1}{2} \bar{m} \hbar \omega_{1}\right) \psi \).

\( \text { But } L_{z}=-i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}, \text { so } \frac{\partial^{2} \psi}{\partial \phi^{2}}=-\frac{1}{\hbar^{2}} L_{z}^{2} \psi=-\frac{1}{\hbar^{2}} \bar{m}^{2} \hbar^{2} \psi=-\bar{m}^{2} \psi \). Use separation of variables: \( \psi(r, \phi, z)= R(r) \Phi(\phi) Z(z) \):

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left[\Phi Z \frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d R}{d r}\right)-\frac{\bar{m}^{2}}{r^{2}} R \Phi Z+R \Phi \frac{d^{2} Z}{d z^{2}}\right]+\left(\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2} r^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{2}^{2} z^{2}\right) R \Phi Z=\left(E+\frac{1}{2} \bar{m} \hbar \omega_{1}\right) R \Phi Z \).

Divide by RΦZ and collect terms:

\( \left\{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left[\frac{1}{r R} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d R}{d r}\right)-\frac{\bar{m}^{2}}{r^{2}}\right]+\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2} r^{2}\right\}+\left\{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \frac{1}{Z} \frac{d^{2} Z}{d z^{2}}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{2}^{2} z^{2}\right\}=\left(E+\frac{1}{2} \bar{m} \hbar \omega_{1}\right) \).

The first term depends only on r, the second only on z, so they’re both constants; call them\( E_r \) and \( E_z \):

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d R}{d r}\right)-\frac{\bar{m}^{2}}{r^{2}} R\right]+\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2} r^{2} R=E_{r} R ; \quad-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \frac{d^{2} Z}{d z^{2}}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{2}^{2} z^{2} Z=E_{z} Z ; \quad E=E_{r}+E_{z}-\frac{1}{2} \bar{m} \hbar \omega_{1} \).

The z equation is a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator, and we can read off immediately that \( E_z =\left(n_{2}+1 / 2\right) \hbar \omega_{2}, \text { with } n_{2}=0,1,2, \ldots \).

The r equation is actually a two-dimensional harmonic oscillator; to get \( E_{r}, \operatorname{let} u(r) \equiv \sqrt{r} R \), and follow the method of Sections 4.1.3 and 4.2.1:

\( R=\frac{u}{\sqrt{r}}, \quad \frac{d R}{d r}=\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{r}}-\frac{u}{2 r^{3 / 2}}, \quad r \frac{d R}{d r}=\sqrt{r} u^{\prime}-\frac{u}{2 \sqrt{r}}, \quad \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d R}{d r}\right)=\sqrt{r} u^{\prime \prime}+\frac{u}{4 r^{3 / 2}} \).

\( \frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d R}{d r}\right)=\frac{u^{\prime \prime}}{\sqrt{r}}+\frac{u}{4 r^{5 / 2}} ; \quad-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left(\frac{u^{\prime \prime}}{\sqrt{r}}+\frac{1}{4} \frac{u}{r^{2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}-\frac{\bar{m}^{2}}{r^{2}} \frac{u}{\sqrt{r}}\right)+\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2} r^{2} \frac{u}{\sqrt{r}}=E_{r} \frac{u}{\sqrt{r}} \).

\( -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m}\left[\frac{d^{2} u}{d r^{2}}+\left(\frac{1}{4}-\bar{m}^{2}\right) \frac{u}{r^{2}}\right]+\frac{1}{8} m \omega_{1}^{2} r^{2} u=E_{r} u \).

This is identical to the equation we shall encounter in (the three- dimentional harmonic oscillator), only with \( \omega \rightarrow \omega_{1} / 2, E \rightarrow E_{r}, \text { and } \ell(\ell+1) \rightarrow \bar{m}^{2}-1 / 4 \), which is to say \( \ell^{2}+\ell+1 / 4=\bar{m}^{2} \), or \( (\ell+1 / 2)^{2}=\bar{m}^{2}, \text { or } \ell=|\bar{m}|-1 / 2 \). [Our present equation depends only on \( \bar{m}^{2} \), and hence is the same for either sign, but the solution to assumes \( \ell+1 / 2 \geq 0 \) (else u is not normalizable), so we need |m| here.] Quoting that solution:

\( E=\left(j_{\max }+\ell+3 / 2\right) \hbar \omega \rightarrow E_{r}=\left(j_{\max }+|\bar{m}|+1\right) \hbar \omega_{1} / 2, \quad \text { where } \quad j_{\max }=0,2,4, \ldots \).

\( E=\left(j_{\max }+|\bar{m}|+1\right) \hbar \omega_{1} / 2+\left(n_{2}+1 / 2\right) \hbar \omega_{2}-\bar{m} \hbar \omega_{1} / 2=\left(n_{1}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{1}+\left(n_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{2} \).

\( \text { where } \left.n_{1}=0,1,2, \ldots \text { (if } \bar{m} \geq 0 \text {, then } n_{1}=j_{\max } / 2 \text {; if } \bar{m}<0 \text {, then } n_{1}=j_{\max } / 2-\bar{m}\right) \).

[A more elegant solution runs as follows: Let

\( P \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\left(p_{x}+p_{y}\right)-\frac{q B_{0}}{2}(x-y)\right], \quad Q \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left[\frac{1}{q B_{0}}\left(p_{x}-p_{y}\right)+\frac{1}{2}(x+y)\right] \).

Show (a) P and Q satisfy the canonical commutation relations (Eq. 2.52): \( [Q, P]=i \hbar \), and (b) the Hamiltonian (square brackets in Eq. 4.191) can be written as

\( [x, \hat{p}]=i \hbar \) (2.52).

\( i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t}=\left[\frac{1}{2 m}(-i \hbar \nabla-q A )^{2}+q \varphi\right] \Psi \) (4.191).

\( H=\left(\frac{1}{2 m} P^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{1}^{2} Q^{2}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2 m} p_{z}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega_{2}^{2} z^{2}\right) \).

The problem is therefore mathematically identical to two independent harmonic oscillators, with the same mass, and frequencies \( \omega_{1} \text { and } \omega_{2} \); the total energy is just the sum: \( \left(n_{1}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{1}+\left(n_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega_{2} \).]
