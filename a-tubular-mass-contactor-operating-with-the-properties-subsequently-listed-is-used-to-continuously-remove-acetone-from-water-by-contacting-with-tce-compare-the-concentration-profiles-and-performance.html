Guest Question.

A tubular mass contactor operating with the properties subsequently listed is used to continuously remove acetone from water by contacting with TCE. Compare the concentration profiles and performance of a tubular mass contactor operating in cocurrent and countercurrent operation.
Data: The flow rates of water (II) and TCE (I) are 100 L/min. The droplet size and contactor volume are such that the contact area is 50\( m^{2}\). Further, \(K_{m} = 3.3 × 10^{−5} m/s\). The equilibrium relationship is M=2 at the concentrations of interest (see Figure 4.2).

Admin
Solution

A Level IV analysis for cocurrent operation is given in Eqs. (4.4.7) and (4.4.8). This analysis can be readily calculated to yield the concentration profiles shown in Figure E4.4(a).

\(C_{A}^{I}=\frac{M}{\lambda +M} (C_{AF}^{I}+\lambda C_{AF}^{II})+\frac{\lambda }{\lambda +M}(C_{AF}^{I}+M C_{AF}^{II})e^{-\beta Z}\) (4.4.7)

\(C_{A}^{II}=\frac{1}{\lambda +M} (C_{AF}^{I}+\lambda C_{AF}^{II})-\frac{1}{\lambda +M}(C_{AF}^{I}+M C_{AF}^{II})e^{-\beta Z} \) (4.4.8)
Notice that the concentration profiles look similar to the temperature profiles observed in the cocurrent heat exchanger, shown in Figure E3.5. However, these concentration profiles cross in the contactor. This is a consequence of the distribution coefficient M = 2. To make a clearer analogy to heat transfer, the results are plotted as\( C^{I}_{A}\) and \(2C^{II}_{ A}\) in Figure E4.4(b). The driving force for mass transfer at any position (z/L) in the contactor is\( (C^{I}_{A}− MC^{II}_{A}),\) which is the vertical difference between the curves. As seen, the driving force for mass transfer approaches zero near the exit of the contactor.
Countercurrent operation, given by Eq. (4.4.14) and the Level I balance, results in the concentration profiles given inFigure E4.5(a). Note that the direction of stream I is held constant in this example, whereas process stream II is reversed in direction.
The concentration profiles are similar to those observed in the countercurrent heat exchanger, Figure E3.5. The driving force for mass transfer is illustrated by the plots of\( C^{I}_{A} \)and \(2C^{II}_{A}\) in Figure E4.5(b).

\(C_{A}^{I}=M\frac{\lambda C_{AF}^{II}-e^{-\beta L}C_{AF}^{I} }{\lambda -M e^{-\beta L}} +\lambda(\frac{C_{AF}^{I}-MC_{AF}^{II}}{\lambda -M e^{-\beta L}} )e^{-\beta Z} \) (4.4.14)
Comparison of the performance of the cocurrent and countercurrent mass contactors illustrates that, for a given contactor size,flowrates, and area for mass transfer,the countercurrent operation yields a larger average driving force for mass transfer and, hence, better performance. The amount of acetone in the exit water stream is calculated to be
