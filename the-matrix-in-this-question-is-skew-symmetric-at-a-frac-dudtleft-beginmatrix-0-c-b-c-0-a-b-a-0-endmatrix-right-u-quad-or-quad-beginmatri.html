Guest Question.

The matrix in this question is skew-symmetric (\({A}^{T} = -A\)):

\(\frac {du}{dt}=\left[ \begin{matrix} 0 & c & -b \\ -c & 0 & a \\ b & -a & 0 \end{matrix} \right] u \quad\) or \(\quad \begin{matrix} { u }^{ \prime }_{1}= c{u}_{2} - b{u}_{3} \\{ u }^{ \prime }_{2}= a{u}_{3} - c{u}_{1} \\{ u }^{ \prime }_{3}= b{u}_{1} - a{u}_{2} \end{matrix}\).

(a) The derivative of \({ \left\| u(t) \right\| }^{ 2 } = {u}^{2}_{1} + {u}^{2}_{2} + {u}^{2}_{3}\) is \(2{u}_{1}{ u }^{ \prime }_{1} + 2{u}_{2}{ u }^{ \prime }_{2} + 2{u}_{3}{ u }^{ \prime }_{3}\). Substitute \({ u }^{ \prime }_{1}, { u }^{ \prime }_{2}, { u }^{ \prime }_{3}\) to get zero. Then \({ \left\| u(t) \right\| }^{ 2 }\) stays equal to \({ \left\| u(0) \right\| }^{ 2 }\).

(b) When A is skew-symmetric, \(Q = {e}^{At}\) is orthogonal. Prove \({Q}^{T} = {e}^{-At}\) from the series for \(Q = {e}^{At}\). Then \({Q}^{T}Q = I\).

Admin
Solution

When A is skew-symmetric,

\(\left\| u(t) \right\|=\left\|{e}^{At}u(0) \right\|\) is \(\left\|u(0) \right\|\) .

So \({e}^{At}\) is orthogonal.
