Guest Question.

The desired final orientation of the hand of a Cartesian-Euler robot is given. Find the necessary Euler angles.

\( ^{R} T_{H}=\left [ \begin{matrix} n_{x} & o_{x} & a_{x} & p_{x} \\ n_{y} & o_{y} & a_{y} & p_{y} \\ n_{z} & o_{z} & a_{z} & p_{z} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} -0.167 & -0.773 & -0.612 & 7 \\ 0.932 & 0.078 & -0.354 & 2 \\ 0.321 & -0.630 & 0.707 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ]\)
Admin
Solution

From these equations, we find:

\( \phi = ATAN2 (a_{y} ,a_{x}) = ATAN2 (−0.354, −0.612) =30^{\circ } or 210^{\circ }\)

 

Realizing that both the sines and cosines of \(30^{\circ }\) and \(210^{\circ }\) can be used for the remainder,

\(\psi = ATAN2 (−n_{x}S \phi+ n_{y}C \phi, −o_{x}S \phi + o_{y}C \phi) = (0.891 , 0.454) =63^{\circ } or 243^{\circ }\)

 

\(\theta = ATAN2 (a_{x}Cϕ + a_{y}S\phi , a_{z})=ATAN2 (− 0.707 , 0.707 )= −45^{\circ } or 135^{\circ }\)

Unlike , for a robot of Euler + cylindrical configuration, it will not be easy to find the angles because there are two rotations about the a-axis. The angles have to be decoupled individually. In this case, it is probably better to use the Denavit-Hartenberg technique presented in Section 2.12.
