Guest Question.

Most of us have poked a campfire or seat by a fireplace at night and watched the sparks fly up. They glow a bright red, which indicates that they are emitting blackbody radiation from a source with a surface temperature of 1000–1200°C. You have also noticed that their color does not change until they suddenly disappear. The time for this reaction varies, but they seem to glow for a few sec and then suddenly disappear.

a. Estimate the temperature of a campfire spark.
b. How long does it take to consume a fiber bundle of \(\phi\)0.2mm × 1mm pine wood fiber (particle)?

Admin
Solution

Let us construct mass and energy balances on the particle, which we assume to be a cylinder of dry pine wood particle of radius R. The reaction is

\(X +\frac{\gamma_{ DR }}{4} O _{2} \rightarrow CO _{2}+\frac{v_{ H/C }}{2} H _{2} O\) (E16.1.1)

where X stands for the pine wood biomass (normalized by carbon), and \(ν _{ H / C }\) is the mole fraction of hydrogen over carbon in the biomass. The elemental composition of dry pine wood can be found in weight percent as C:51.8%, H: 6.3 %, O: 41.3% and N: 0.1%. The lower heating value of dry pine wood is given by \(-\Delta H_{ R }=21.03 MJ / kg\).

Based on the elemental composition of pinewood, one can find the Roels’ formula for pinewood as \(CH _{1.4493} O _{0.5985} N _{0.1655}\). The lower heating value can be converted to the heat of reaction for (E16.1.1) as

\(-\Delta H_{R}=1 kg \times \frac{21.03 MJ / kg }{0.518 \div 12.011 kmol – C }=487.6 k J / mol – C\) (E16.1.2)

The degree of reduction for pine wood is given by

\(\gamma_{ DR }=4 v_{ C }+v_{ H }-2 v_{ C }=4+1.4493-2 \times 0.5985=4.2523\) (E16.1.3)

Thus, the heat of reaction for (E16.1.1) can be determined as

\(-\Delta H_{R}=\frac{487.6 kJ / mol – C }{4.2523 mol -\text { electron } / mol – C }=458.7 kJ / mol – O _{2}\) (E16.1.4)

We do not know, nor do we need, the exact kinetics of the reaction explicitly. It is a surface reaction between the biomass (carbon and hydrogen) on the surface of the particle and \(O _{2}\) from the air.

A mass balance on \(O _{2}\) around the particle yields

\(-r_{O_{2}}^{\prime \prime} A\left\{\begin{array}{l}<br /> =-\frac{\gamma_{D R}}{4} r_{X}^{\prime \prime} A=-\frac{\gamma_{D R}}{4} \frac{d n_{X}}{d t}=-\frac{\gamma_{D R}}{4} \frac{2 \pi R L \rho_{X}}{M_{X}} \frac{d R}{d t} \\<br /> =\bar{N}_{O_{2}} A=2 \pi R L k_{c}\left(C_{O_{2} b}-C_{O_{2}}\right)<br /> \end{array}\right.\) (E16.1.5)

where \(r^{\prime \prime}\) is the rate of reaction based on the surface area. We have simply equated the rate of reaction of \( O _{2}\) at its surface with the loss of moles of biomass particle, and the rate of mass transfer of \( O _{2}\) to the surface.

An energy balance on the particle yields

Heat released due to reaction = heat lost by conduction + heat lost by radiation

\(\left(-r_{O_{2}}^{\prime \prime} A\right)\left(-\Delta H_{R}\right)=h A\left(T_{s}-T_{a}\right)+A \varepsilon \sigma\left(T_{s}^{4}-T_{a}^{4}\right)\) (E16.1.6)

where all terms are familiar except the radiation term, with ε the emissivity of the surface, σ the Stefan-Boltzmann radiation constant, \(T_{ s }\), the carbon particle temperature, and \(T_{ a }\) the ambient air temperature (300 K or whatever it was that night).

Combining Eqs. (E16.1.6) and (E16.1.5), one can obtain

\(h\left(T_{s}-T_{a}\right)+\varepsilon \sigma\left(T_{s}^{4}-T_{a}^{4}\right)=k_{c}\left(C_{O_{2} b}-C_{O_{2}}\right)\left(-\Delta H_{R}\right) \approx\left(-\Delta H_{R}\right) k_{c} C_{O_{2} b}\) (E16.1.7)

where we have assumed that \(C_{ O _{2}}<< C _{ O _{2} b }\), meaning that the oxidation of the carbon particle is mass transfer limited.
Now we need \(k_{ c }\) and h, the mass and heat transfer coefficients around a cylinder. These come from Sherwood and Nusselt numbers, respectively, for flow around a cylinder,

\(N u=3.66+\frac{0.085 \operatorname{Re} \operatorname{Pr} \frac{2 R}{L}}{1+0.047\left\lgroup \operatorname{Re} P r \frac{2 R}{L} \right\rgroup ^{\frac{2}{3}}}\) (E16.1.8)

\(S h=3.66+1.077\left\lgroup \operatorname{RePr} \frac{2 R}{L} \right\rgroup ^{\frac{1}{3}}\) (E16.1.9)

Since the fluid (air) is nearly stagnant, Sh = Nu = 3.66. Therefore, we have

\(S h=\frac{k_{c} 2 R}{D_{O_{2}-A i r}}=3.66=\frac{h 2 R}{k_{T}}=N u\) (E16.1.10)

where \(D_{ O _{2}- Air }\), is the diffusivity of O2 in air at atmospheric pressure, \(D_{ O _{2}}=0.1 cm ^{2} / s\), and \(k_{ T }\) is the thermal conductivity of air at atmospheric pressure, \(k_{ T }\) = 0.026 W/(m·K). As the wood particles burns, and the surface carbonize and thus one can assume black body radiation, that is, \( \varepsilon \approx 1 \text { and } \sigma=5.6703 \times 10^{-8} Wm ^{-2} K ^{-4} \).

We can now solve our problem by substituting the parameters into Eq. (E16.1.7).

\(3.66 \frac{k_{T}}{2 R}\left(T_{s}-T_{a}\right)+\varepsilon \sigma\left(T_{s}^{4}-T_{a}^{4}\right)=\left(-\Delta H_{R}\right) \times 3.66 \frac{D_{ O _{2}- Air }}{2 R} C_{O_{2} b}\) (E16.1.11)

or

\(3.66 \frac{0.026}{0.2 \times 10^{-3}}\left(T_{s}-300\right)+5.6703 \times 10^{-8}\left(T_{s}^{4}-300^{4}\right)=458.7 \times 10^{3} \times 3.66 \frac{0.1 \times 10^{-4}}{0.2 \times 10^{-3}} \times \frac{0.21}{22.4 \times 10^{-3}}\) (E16.1.12)

Which can be solved to give

\(T_{ s } \approx 1441 K =1168^{\circ} C\)

which is in good agreement with the observed temperature of a spark (bright yellow). In this calculation we used for the concentration of \(O _{2}\) in air the approximation

\(C_{O_{2} b}=\frac{0.21 mol }{22.4 L }\) (E16.1.13)

Finally we return to the mass balance to calculate the time needed to burn completely the particle. Eq. (E16.1.5) can be reduced to

\(-\frac{\gamma_{D R}}{4} \frac{2 \pi R L \rho_{X}}{M_{X}} \frac{d R}{d t}=2 \pi R L k_{c}\left(C_{O_{2} b}-C_{O_{2}}\right)\) (E16.1.14)

or

\(-\frac{\gamma_{D R}}{4} \frac{\rho_{X}}{M_{X}} \frac{d R}{d t}=3.66 \frac{D_{ O _{2}-\text { Air }}}{2 R} C_{O_{2} b}\) (E16.1.15)

Separation of variables (R and t) gives

\(\frac{\rho_{X} \gamma_{D R}}{14.64 M_{X} D_{ O _{2}- Air } C_{O_{2} b}} 2 R d R=d t\) (E16.1.16)

This equation can be integrated to yield

\(t=\frac{\rho_{X} \gamma_{D R} R_{0}^{2}}{14.64 M_{X} D_{ O _{2}- Air } C_{O_{2} b}}\) (E16.1.17)

Inserting the numbers (\(\rho_{ X }=550 kg / m ^{3}, D_{ O _{2} \text { -Air }}=0.1 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2} / s , \text { and } M_{ x }\) = 12.011 + 1.4493 × 1.0079 + 0.5985 × 15.9994 + 0.1655 × 14.01 = 25.366 g/mol), we have

\(t=\frac{4.2523 \times 550 \times\left(0.1 \times 10^{-3}\right)^{2}}{14.64 \times 25.366 \times 0.1 \times 10^{-4} \times 0.21 / 22.4} s =0.672 s\) (E16.1.18)

which is a reasonable time for a spark to disappear.
