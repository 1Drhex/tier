Guest Question.

The data obtained in the tank described in need to be further analyzed so that the effect of air flow and tank geometry can be predicted for any tank-type contactor. The Level III analysis and experiment produced two values for \(k_{m}a/V^{I}\), where\(V^{I}\) is the total volume of the mass contactor. We now need to estimate a so we can predict performance for other mass contactors.

Admin
Solution

The power input to the plume, Eq. (7.2.8), is

power input=\(q^{II}_{AVG}g\rho ^{I}h_{L}\) (7.2.8)

power=\((3.9\times 10^{-3}m^{3}/s)(9.8 m/s^{2})(1000 kg/m^{3})(6m)\)=230 W

The plume diameter and plume volume, Eq. (7.2.9), are

\(d^{2}=0.5 D^{2}\) (7.2.9)

\(d^{2}=0.5(1.4)^{2}\)

=0.98m

The plume liquid volume is

\(V=\frac{\pi (0.98)^{2}}{4}\times 6\)\(=4.5 m^{3}\)

The power per unit mass ε is

\(\epsilon =\frac{230W}{(4.5 m^{3})(1000kg/m^{3})}\)\( =0.05 \frac{W}{kg}\)

Because this is an air–water system the bubble diameter can be estimated from Eq. (7.2.11):

\(d_{B}=(0.26\times 10^{-2})\epsilon ^{-0.4}\) (7.2.11)

\(d_{B}=0.26\times 10^{-2}(0.05) ^{-0.4}\)

=0.0087 m(0.34 in.).

The liquid velocity in the plume is estimated with Eq. (7.2.13):

\(V^{I}=0.79d^{-2/3}(gq^{II})^{1/3}h_{L}^{1/3}\) (7.2.13)

\(V^{I}=0.79(0.98)^{-2/3}(9.8\times 3.9\times 10^{-3})^{1/3}6^{1/3}\)

= 0.47 m/s.

The bubble rise velocity for air–water, Eq. (7.2.14), is

\(u_{r}=(\frac{2\sigma }{\rho ^{I}d_{B}}+\frac{gd_{B}}{2} )^{1/2}\) (7.2.14)

\(u_{r}=[\frac{1.45\times 10^{-3}}{0.0087}+4.9(0.0087) ]^{1/2}\)\(=0.23 \frac{m}{s}\)

The holdup, Eq. (7.2.15), is

\(\phi =\frac{q^{II}}{\pi D^{2}(V^{I}+u_{r})}[\frac{P_{0}}{P_{s}+\rho ^{I}g(\frac{h_{L}}{2} )} ]\) (7.2.15)

\(\phi =\frac{3.9\times 10^{-3}}{\frac{\pi (0.98)^{2}}{4} (0.47+0.23)}[\frac{1\times 10^{5}+1000(9.8)6}{ 1\times 10^{5}+1000(9.8)3} ]\)

=0.008.

The interfacial area per unit volume of liquid in the plume \(a_{v}\) is

\(a_{V}=\frac{6\phi }{d_{B}} \) \(=\frac{6\times 0.008}{0.0087} \) \(=5.51 m^{-1} \)

For the tank, the total interfacial area is that of bubbles in the plume because there are no bubbles in the liquid that flow down in the annular region surrounding the plume:

a = 5.51 × 4.5 = \(24.8 m^{2}\).

Our experiment produced

\(\frac{K_{m}a}{V^{I}}\)=0.0007

because

\(V^{I}=\frac{\pi }{4}(1.4)^{2}\times 6\)

\(=9.28m^{3}\)

\(K_{m}=\frac{0.0007\times 9.28}{24.8}\)

\(=2.6\times 10^{-4}m/s\)

Predicting km from Eq. (7.2.16) yields

\(K_{m}=\sqrt{\frac{4}{\pi }D\frac{u_{r}}{d_{B}}}\) (7.2.16)

\(K_{m}=\sqrt{\frac{4}{\pi }\frac{D}{d_{B}}u_{r} }\)

\(K_{m}=4.79\times 10^{-5}/(\frac{u_{r}}{d_{B}} )^{1/2}\)

\(=4.79\times 10^{-5}/(\frac{0.23}{0.0087} )^{1/2}\)

\(=2.46\times 10^{-4}m/s\)

This is rather good agreement with the experiment.
If we increase our gas flow rate, \(q^{II}\), to 16.9 ×\( 10^{−3} m^{3}/s\), the power per unit now becomes 0.22 W/kg and the bubble size becomes 0.0047 m. The superficial liquid velocity is now 0.78 m/s, and the bubble rise velocity is 0.23 m/s. Holdup is increased to 0.027, giving an interfacial area per unit volume of 34.5 m−1 in the plume and a total area of 155 m2. For the same Km value, Eq. (4.3.15) shows that we can increase the oxygen concentration at 10 minutes from 4.8 × \(10^{−3} kg/m^{3}\) to 10.8 × \(10^{−3} kg/m^{3}\):

\(C_{A}^{I}(t)=MC_{AF}^{II}+(C_{A,i}^{II}-MC_{AF}^{II})e^{-\beta t}\) (4.3.15)

\(C_{A}^{I}=11.5+9.8e^{\frac{-K_{m}at}{V^{I}} }\)

\({\frac{K_{m}at}{V^{I}} }\)\(=\frac{2.6\times 10^{-4}(m/s)\times 155(m^{2})\times 10(min)\times 60(s/min)=2.6}{9.28(m^{3})}\)

\(C_{A}^{I}\)=11.5-0.7\(=10.8\times 10^{-3}kg/m^{3}\)
