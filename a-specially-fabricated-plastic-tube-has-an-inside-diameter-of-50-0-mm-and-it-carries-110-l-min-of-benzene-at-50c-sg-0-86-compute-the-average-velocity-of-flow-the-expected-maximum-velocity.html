Guest Question.

A specially fabricated plastic tube has an inside diameter of 50.0 mm and it carries 110 L/min of benzene at 50°C (sg = 0.86). Compute the average velocity of flow, the expected maximum velocity of flow, and several points on the velocity profile. Plot the velocity versus the distance from the tube wall and show where the average velocity occurs.

Admin
Solution

Given the following data:

Q = 110 L/min

D = 50.0 mm = 0.050 m

Benzene at 50°C (sg = 0.86)

In order to apply Eqs. (9–3) and (9–4), we need to compute the Reynolds number and then find the friction factor for the plastic tube.

\( U = \left[1+1.43\sqrt{f}+2.15\sqrt{f}\log_{10}\left(y/r_o\right) \right] \) (9–3)

\( U{max}=\nu \left(1+1.34\sqrt{f} \right) \) (9–4)

For the benzene:

\( \rho = sg \times \rho_W = \left(0.86\right) \left(1000kg/m^3\right) = 860kg/m^3\)

From Appendix D, the dynamic viscosity is: \( \eta = 4.2 \times 10^{-4}Pa·s\)

The average velocity of flow is:

\( v=Q/A\)

\( Q = 110L/min\left[\frac{1m^3/s}{60000L/min} \right]= 1.83 \times 10^{-3}m/s \)

\( A = \pi D^2/4=\pi\left(0.050m\right)^2/4 = 1.963\times10^{-3}m^2 \)

Then the average velocity is:

\( v = Q/A = \left(1.83 \times 10^{-3} m/s\right)/\left(1.963 \times 10^{-3}m^2\right) =0.932m/s \)

Now compute the Reynolds number, \(N_R = \nu D\rho/\eta\)

\( N_R = \frac{\left(0.932\right)\left(0.050\right)\left(860\right) }{4.2 \times 10^{-4}} = 9.54 \times 10^4 \) (turbulent)

We now need to compute the relative roughness, \( D/\epsilon \) From Table 8.2, we find \( \epsilon = 3.0 \times 10^{-7}m \).

TABLE 8.2 Pipe roughness—design values

Material Roughness \(\epsilon\) (m) Roughness \(\epsilon\) (ft)
Glass Smooth Smooth
Plastic \(3.0 \times 10^{-7}\) \(1.0 \times 10^{-6}\)
Drawn tubing; copper, brass, steel \(1.5 \times 10^{-6}\) \(5.0 \times 10^{-6}\)
Steel, commercial or welded \(4.6 \times 10^{-5}\) \(1.5 \times 10^{-4}\)
Galvanized iron \(1.5 \times 10^{-4}\) \(5.0 \times 10^{-4}\)

Ductile iron—uncoated \(2.4 \times 10^{-4}\) \(8.0 \times 10^{-4}\)
Concrete, well made \(1.2 \times 10^{-4}\) \(4.0 \times 10^{-4}\)
Riveted steel \(1.8 \times 10^{-3}\) \(6.0 \times 10^{-3}\)

Then

\( D/\epsilon = 0.050/3.0 \times 10^{-7} = 1.667 \times 10^5\)

From Moody’s diagram, we find f = 0.018,

Now, from Eq. (9–4), we see that the maximum velocity of flow is

\( U_{max} = v \left(1+1.43 \sqrt{f} \right) \) (9–4)

\( U_{max} = v \left(1+1.43 \sqrt{f} \right) = \left(0.932 m/s\right)\left(1+1.43\sqrt{0.018} \right) \)

\( U_{max} = 1.11m/s\) at the center of the tube

Equation (9–3) can be used to determine the points on the velocity profile. We know that the velocity equals zero at the tube wall (y = 0). Also, the rate of change of velocity with position is greater near the wall than near the center of the tube. Therefore, increments of 0.5 mm will be used from y = 0.5 to y = 2.5 mm. Then, increments of 2.5 mm will be used up to y = 10 mm. Finally, increments of 5.0 mm will provide sufficient definition of the profile near the center of the tube. At y = 1.0 mm and \( r_o = 25mm . \)

\( U = v\left[1+1.43\sqrt{f}+2.15\sqrt{f} \log_{10}\left(y/r_o\right) \right] \)

\( U = \left(0.932m/s\right) \left[1+1.43\sqrt{0.018}+2.15\sqrt{0.018} \log_{10}\left(1/25\right) \right] \)

U = 0.735 m/s

Figure 9.7 is the plot of y versus velocity in the form in which the velocity profile is normally shown. Because the plot is symmetrical, only one-half of the profile is shown. Note that the position of the average velocity on this chart is at approximately y = 5.4 mm from the tube wall, about 22 percent of the radius.
