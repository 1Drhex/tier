Guest Question.

Maneuvering a space satellite
Hydrazine has been studies extensively for use in monopropellant thrusters for space flights of long duration. Thrusters are used for altitude control of communication satellites. Here the decomposition of hydrazine over a packed bed of alumina-supported iridium catalyst is of interest (Smith and Solomon, 1982). In a proposed study, a 5% hydrazine in 95% helium mixture is to be passed over a packed bed of cylindrical particles 2.54 mm in diameter and 5.08 mm in length at a gas-phase superficial velocity of 10 m/s and a temperature of 750 K. The kinematic viscosity of helium at this temperature is 4.5 × \(10^{-4} m ^{2} / s\). The hydrazine decomposition reaction is believed to be external mass transfer-limited under these conditions. If the packed bed is 0.0508 m in length, what conversion can be expected? The bed porosity is 35% and the diffusivity of hydrazine in helium is 6.9 × \(10^{-5} m ^{2} / s\) at 298 K. Assume isothermal operation.

Admin
Solution

Hydrazine (A) decomposition reaction is represented by

\(N _{2} H _{4} \rightarrow N _{2}+2 H _{2}\)

which is highly exothermic and thus suitable as rock fuel. The reaction is carried out isothermally in a packed bed reactor. As a first approximation, we assume the reactor can be characterized as a plug-flow reactor or PFR (i.e., neglect the back-mixing caused by dispersion in the bed. A schematic of the reactor is shown in Fig. E17.2.1.

Mole balance of A (hydrazine) in the differential volume (between z and z + dz) leads to

\(A_{c} \cup C_{A \mid z}-A_{c} U C_{A \mid z+d z}+r_{A} A_{c} d z=0\), (E17.2.1)

where \(A_{c}\) is the cross-sectional area of the bed and U is the superficial flow velocity. Dividing Eq. (17.2.1) by \(A_{c}\)dz and letting dz → 0 and assuming U and \(A_{c}\)are constant, we obtain

\(-U \frac{d C_{A}}{d z}+r_{A}=0\). (E17.2.2)

The reaction occurs only on the catalyst surface with catalyst not moving:

\(\left(-r_{A}\right) A_{c} d z=k_{c}\left(C_{A}-C_{A S}\right) a_{c} A_{c} d z\), (E17.2.3)

where \(C_{A S}\) is the concentration of A on the catalyst surface at axial location z and \(a_{c}\) is the specific surface area of the particles in the bed,

\(a_{c}=\frac{\text { total surface ara of the particles }}{\text { bed volume }}\) \(=\frac{\text { total surface ara of the particles }}{\text { total volume of the particles }} \times \frac{\text { total volume of the particles }}{\text { bed volume }}\) (E17.2.4)

\(=\frac{2 \times \frac{1}{4} \pi d_{c}^{2}+\pi d_{c} h_{c}}{\frac{1}{4} \pi d_{c}^{2} h_{c}}(1-\varepsilon)=\frac{2 d_{c}+4 h_{c}}{d_{c} h_{c}}(1-\varepsilon)\).

Here \(d_{c}\) is the diameter of the cylindrical particle, \(h_{c}\) is the length of the cylindrical particle, and ε is the bed porosity.
Since mass transfer is limiting, the concentration of A on the catalyst surface is very small as compared with the bulk concentration.

\(C_{ AS } \approx 0\) (E17.2.5)

Substituting Eqs. (E17.2.3) and (E17.2.5) into Eq. (E17.2.2), we obtain

\(-U \frac{d C_{A}}{d z}+k_{c} a_{c} C_{A}=0\). (E17.2.6)

Integrating with the limit \(C_{A}=C_{A 0}\) at z = 0, we obtain

\(\frac{C_{A}}{C_{A 0}}=\exp \left\lgroup -\frac{k_{c} a_{c}}{U} z \right\rgroup\). (E17.2.7)

The conversion of A at the end of the reactor is given by

\(f_{A e}=\frac{C_{A 0}-C_{A}}{C_{A 0}}=1-\exp \left\lgroup -\frac{k_{c} a_{c}}{U} L \right\rgroup\). (E17.2.8)

Now we need to evaluate the mass transfer coefficient kc. Correlations can be found in references, for example, Perry’s Chemical Engineers’ Handbook. In this case, we can also use Eq. (17.4) \(a_{c}=\frac{S_{p}}{V}\).

The particle diameter may be computed via volume average, that is

\(d_{p}=\left\lgroup \frac{6}{\pi} \times \frac{1}{4} \pi d_{c}^{2} h_{c} \right\rgroup ^{\frac{1}{3}}=\left\lgroup \frac{3}{2} d_{c}^{2} h_{c} \right\rgroup ^{\frac{1}{3}}=3.663 \times 10^{-3} m\).

The Reynolds number

\(R e=\frac{d_{p} \rho U}{\mu_{f}}=\frac{d_{p} U}{v_{f}}=\frac{3.663 \times 10^{-3} \times 10}{4.5 \times 10^{-4}}=81.407\)

The diffusivity needs to be corrected for temperature effects:

\(D_{A B}=D_{A B 0}\left\lgroup \frac{T}{T_{0}} \right\rgroup ^{1.75}=6.9 \times 10^{-5}\left\lgroup \frac{750}{298} \right\rgroup ^{1.75} m ^{2} / s =3.47 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2} / s\).

The Schmidt number

\(S C=\frac{\mu_{f}}{\rho D_{A B}}=\frac{v_{f}}{D_{A B}}=\frac{4.5 \times 10^{-4}}{3.47 \times 10^{-4}}=1.297\).

From Eq. (17.4), we obtain

\(k_{c}=\frac{D_{A B}}{d_{p}} S h=\frac{0.4548 S c^{1 / 3} R e^{0.5931}}{\varepsilon d_{p}} D_{A B}\)

 

\(=3.47 \times 10^{-4} \frac{0.4548 \times 1.297^{1 / 3} \times 81.4^{0.5931}}{0.35 \times 3.663 \times 10^{-3}} m / s\)

= 1.824m/s.

The specific surface area from Eq. (E17.2.4):

\(a_{c}=\frac{2 d_{c}+4 h_{c}}{d_{c} h_{c}}(1-\varepsilon)=\frac{2 \times 2.54+4 \times 5.08}{2.54 \times 5.08} \times(1-0.35) mm ^{-1} \partial\)

 

\(=1.280 \times 10^{3} m ^{-1} \text { . }\)

From Eq. (17.2.8),

\(f_{A e}=1-\exp \left\lgroup -\frac{k_{c} a_{c}}{U} L \right\rgroup =1-\exp \left\lgroup -\frac{1.824 \times 1.280 \times 10^{3}}{10} \times 0.0508 \right\rgroup \)

\(=1-7.06 \times 10^{-6} \approx 1\).

That is, near complete conversion.
