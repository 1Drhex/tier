Guest Question.

Consider an isolated system consisting of two simple subsystems labelled 1 and 2 separated by a diathermal, permeable and fixed wall of area A (Fig. 3.5). Both subsystems contain the same electrically charged substance. In view of , the thermal power \(P^{(21)}_ Q\) and the chemical power \(P^{(21)}_ C\) exerted by subsystem 2 on subsystem 1 and the rate \(Ω^{(21)}\) of substance entering subsystem 1 are given by the coupled system of equations,

\(\binom{P^{(21)}_Q+P^{(21)}_C}{\Omega ^{(21)}} =\binom{L_{QQ} L_{QN}}{L_{NQ}L_{NN}} \binom{\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} }{\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} }\).

1. Show that the entropy production rate can be written as a positive quadratic form, i.e.

\(ΠS = x^T L x >0\) where \(x \neq 0\).

Deduce from this inequality that det (L) > 0 and Tr (L) > 0. Show that the eigenvalues \(λ_1\) and \(λ_2\) of matrix L are positive.
2. When the wall is impermeable and diathermal, show that the system of coupled equations predicts the existence of the ‘discrete’ Fourier law (3.16) and express the thermal conductivity κ in terms of the empirical coefficients.

\(P^{(21)}_{Q} =\kappa \frac{A}{\ell } \Bigl(T_2(S_2)-T_1(S_1)\Bigr)\).

3. When the temperature T is uniform and the system contains a neutral substance, show that the coupled system of equations predicts the existence of a ‘discrete’ Fick law (3.45) and express the diffusion coefficient F in terms of the empirical coefficients.

\(\dot{N_1} =F\frac{A}{\ell } \Bigl(\mu _2(S_2,N_2)-\mu _1(S_1,N_1)\Bigr)\).

4. When the temperature difference between the two subsystems is kept constant, i.e. \(T_1 = T − ΔT/2 and T_2 = T + ΔT/2 (ΔT \ll T)\), and when the substance consists of electrons of temperature-independent chemical potential \(μ_e\) and electrochemical potentials \(\bar{\mu_1 } = μ_e − q_e Δ\varphi /2\) and \(\bar{\mu_2 } = μ_e + q_e Δ\varphi /2\), show that in the stationary regime \((\dot{N_1} = 0)\), the electrostatic potential difference \(Δ\varphi \) is given by,

\(Δ\varphi=-εΔT \).

The existence of this potential difference is called the Seebeck effect. Find an expression for the Seebeck coefficient ε in terms of the empirical coefficients, in the limit where \(μ_e \ll L_{NQ}/L_{NN}\).
5. When the electrochemical potential depends on temperature, i.e. \(\bar{\mu_1 } = μ − qΔ\varphi/2 −sΔT/2\) and \(\bar{\mu_2 } = μ + qΔ\varphi/2 + sΔT/2\) where μ is the temperature-independent chemical potential and s = −∂μ/∂T the molar entropy of the substance, find under the condition \(\dot{N_1} = 0\) an expression for the Seebeck coefficient ε in terms of s, in the
limit where \(μ \ll Ts\) and \(L_{NQ}/L_{NN}\ll Ts\).

Solution

1. Since the wall is fixed, the volume of subsystem 1 is constant, i.e. \(\dot{V_1}\) , and the mechanical power exerted by subsystem 2 on subsystem 1 vanishes, i.e. \(P^{(21)}_ W = 0\). Thus, the internal energy variation rate is equal to the sum of the thermal power and the chemical power exerted by subsystem 2 on subsystem 1, i.e. \(\dot{U_1} = P^{(21)}_ Q + P^{(21)}_C\) . Expression (3.50) for the entropy variation rate \(\dot{S}\) of the isolated system, where the chemical potentials are replaced by the electrochemical potentials, is equal to the entropy production rate,

\(\dot{S} =\Bigl(\frac{1}{T_1(S_1,V_1,N_1)}-\frac{1}{T_2(S_2,V_2,N_2)} \Bigr)\dot{U_1}(S_1,V_1,N_1) +\Bigl(\frac{p_1(S_1,V_1,N_1)}{T_1(S_1,V_1,N_1)}-\frac{p_2(S_2,V_2,N_2)}{T_2(S_2,V_2,N_2)} \Bigr)\dot{V_1}-\Bigl(\frac{μ_1(S_1,V_1,N_1)}{T_1(S_1,V_1,N_1)}-\frac{μ_2(S_2,V_2,N_2)}{T_2(S_2,V_2,N_2)} \Bigr)\dot{N_1}\).

\(Π_s=\biggl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} \biggr)\Bigl(P^{(21)}_{Q}+P^{(21)}_{C} \Bigr)+\biggl(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \biggr)\Omega ^{(21)}>0\).

This can be expressed as a scalar product, i.e.

\(Π_s=\biggl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2},\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \biggr).\binom{P^{(21)}_{Q} }{\Omega ^{(21)}} >0\).

Using the coupled system of equations, the entropy production rate can be written as a
positive definite quadratic form,

\(Π_s=\biggl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2},\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \biggr)\binom{L_{QQ} L_{QN}}{L_{NQ} L_{NN}} \binom{\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} }{\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} }>0\).

Defining vector x and matrix L as,

\(x=\binom{\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} }{\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} }>0\). and \(L=\binom{L_{QQ} L_{QN}}{L_{NQ} L_{NN}}\).

the positive definite quadratic form is written as,

\(ΠS = x^T L x > 0\).

where the exponent T denotes the transpose obtained by transforming rows into columns and vice versa. In order to determine the determinant and the trace of the positive definite matrix L, we express this matrix using an invertible 2 × 2 matrix,

\(M=\binom{M_{11} M_{12}}{M_{21} M_{22}}\).

in the following manner [85],

\(x^T L x = x^T M^T M x = (M x)^T · (M x) =\left\|Mx\right\| ^2>0\) \(∀ x \neq 0\).

Then, we show that the determinant of the matrix L is positive, i.e.

\(det (L) = det(M^TM)= det (M)^2 > 0\).

Writing the positive definite matrix \( L = M^T M\) explicitly in components yields,

\(\binom{L_{QQ} L_{QN}}{L_{NQ} L_{NN}}=\binom{M^2_{11}+M^2_{21} M_{11}M_{12}+M_{21}M_{22}}{M_{11}M_{12}+M_{21}M_{22} M^2_{12}+M^2_{22}}\).

Thus, the empirical coefficients satisfy the following conditions,

\(L_{QQ} = M^2_{11} + M^2_{21} > 0\).

\(L_{NN} = M^2_{12} + M^2_{22} > 0\).

\(L_{QN} = L_{NQ} = M_{11} M_{12} + M_{21} M_{22}\).

Hence, the trace of the matrix L is positive, i.e.

\(Tr (L) = L_{QQ} + L_{NN} > 0\).

Since the determinant is the product of the eigenvalues and the trace is the sum of the
eigenvalues, we find,

\(det (L) = λ_1 λ_2 >0 Tr(L) = λ_1 + λ_2 > 0\).

This implies that the eigenvalues are positive, i.e. \(λ_1 > 0 and λ_2 > 0\).

2. For a system consisting of an impermeable diathermal wall, i.e. \(P^{(21)}_C = 0\), the amount of matter is constant in each subsystem, i.e. \(Ω^{(21)} = 0\). Thus, the system of coupled equations reduces to,

\(P^{(21)}_Q=L_{QQ}\Bigl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} \Bigr)+L_{QN}\Bigl(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \Bigr)\).

\(0=L_{NQ}\Bigl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} \Bigr)+L_{NN}\Bigl(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \Bigr)\).

The second equation implies that,

\(\Bigl(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2}-\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} \Bigr)=-\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}\Bigl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} \Bigr)\).

Substituting this relation into the first equation yields the equation,

\(P^{(21)}_Q=\frac{L_{QQ}L_{NN}-L_{QN}L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}\Bigl(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2} \Bigr)\).

This corresponds to the ‘discrete’ Fourier law (3.16),

\(P^{(21)}_{Q} =\kappa \frac{A}{\ell } \Bigl(T_2(S_2)-T_1(S_1)\Bigr)\).

\(P^{(21)}_{Q} =\kappa \frac{A}{\ell } (T_2-T_1)\).

where the thermal conductivity κ is expressed in terms of the empirical coefficients of
the wall area A and characteristic length \(\ell\) as,

\(\kappa =\frac{\ell }{A}\biggl(\frac{L_{QQ}L_{NN}-L_{QN}L_{NQ}}{T_1T_2L_{NN}} \biggr)=\frac{\ell }{A} \frac{det(L)}{T_1T_2L_{NN}} >0\).

3. For a system at uniform temperature T, i.e. \(T_1 = T_2 = T\),consisting of an electrically neutral substance, i.e. \(\bar{\mu _1} = μ_1 and \bar{\mu _2} = μ_2\), the second equation of the system of coupled equations reduces to,

\(Ω^{(21)}=\dot{N_1}=\frac{L_{NN} }{T}(μ_2-μ_1)\).

which corresponds to the ‘discrete’ Fick law (3.45),

\(\dot{N_1} =F\frac{A}{\ell } (\mu _2-\mu _1)\).

where the diffusion coefficient F is expressed in terms of the empirical coefficients of the wall area A and characteristic length \(\ell\) as,

\(F=\frac{\ell}{A }\frac{L_{NN}}{T}>0\).

4. The series expansion of \(1/T_1\) and \(1/T_2\) to first order in ΔT/T is given by,

\(\frac{1}{T_1 }=\frac{1}{T }\biggl(1-\frac{\Delta T}{2T} \biggr)^{-1}=\frac{1}{T }\biggl(1+\frac{\Delta T}{2T} \biggr)+O\biggl(\frac{\Delta T^2}{T} \biggr)\).

\(\frac{1}{T_2 }=\frac{1}{T }\biggl(1+\frac{\Delta T}{2T} \biggr)^{-1}=\frac{1}{T }\biggl(1-\frac{\Delta T}{2T} \biggr)+O\biggl(\frac{\Delta T^2}{T} \biggr)\).

Thus, to first order in ΔT/T,

\(\frac{1}{T_1 }-\frac{1}{T_2 }=\frac{ΔT}{T^2 }\) and \(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2} -\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1}=-\mu _e\frac{\Delta T}{T^2} +q_e\frac{\Delta \varphi }{T}\).

Substituting these relations into the equation of point 2 yields the equation,

\(-μ_e\frac{ΔT}{T}+q_e Δ\varphi =-\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}} \frac{ΔT}{T}\).

that describes the Seebeck effect,

\(Δ\varphi=-\frac{1}{q_eT}\Bigl(\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}-\mu _e \Bigr)ΔT ≡−εΔT\).

where in the limit \(μ_e \ll L_{NQ}/L_{NN}\) the Seebeck coefficient ε is expressed in terms of the empirical coefficients as,

\(ε =\frac{1}{q_eT}\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}\).

5. To first order in ΔT/T,

\(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2}=\frac{ΔT}{T^2}\) and \(\frac{\bar{\mu _2} }{T_2} -\frac{\bar{\mu _1} }{T_1} =-μ\frac{ΔT}{T^2}+q\frac{Δ\varphi }{T}+s\frac{ΔT }{T}\).

Substituting these relations into the equation of point 2 yields the equation,

\(-μ\frac{ΔT}{T}+q Δ\varphi +sΔT=-\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}\frac{ΔT}{T}\).

that describes the Seebeck effect,

\(Δ\varphi=-\frac{1}{qT}\biggl(\frac{L_{NQ}}{L_{NN}}-\mu +Ts \biggr)\Delta T\).

where in the limit \(μ \ll T s and L_{NQ}/L_{NN} \ll T s\) the Seebeck coefficient ε is expressed in terms of the coefficient s as,

\(ε =\frac{s}{q}\).

and represents the thermogalvanic effect (§ 8.13). This effect is used in thermogalvanic cells [86, 87, 88].
