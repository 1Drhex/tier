Guest Question.

A long-radius nozzle of diameter 6 cm is used to meter air flow in a 10-cm-diameter pipe. Upstream conditions are \(p_1\) = 200 kPa and \(T_1\) = 100°C. If the pressure drop through the nozzle is 60 kPa, estimate the flow rate in \(m^3\)/s.

Admin
Solution

• Assumptions: The pressure drops 30 percent, so we need the compressibility factor Y, and Eq. (6.117) is applicable to this problem.

\(\dot{m} = C_d Y A_t \sqrt{\frac{2 \rho_1 (p_1 – p_2)}{1 -\beta^4}} where \beta = \frac{d}{D}\) (6.117)

• Approach: Find \(\rho_1 and C_d\) and apply Eq. (6.117) with β = 6/10 = 0.6.

• Property values: Given \(p_1 and T_1, \rho_1 = p_1/RT_1 = (200,000)/[287(100 + 273)] = 1.87 kg/m^3\). The downstream pressure is \(p_2\) = 200 – 60 = 140 kPa, hence \(p_2/p_1\) = 0.7. At 100°C, from Table A.2, the viscosity of air is 2.17E-5 kg/m-s

• Solution steps: Initially apply Eq. (6.117) by guessing, from Fig. 6.42, that \(C_d\) ≈ 0.98. From Fig. 6.45, for a nozzle with \(p_2/p_1\) = 0.7 and β = 0.6, read Y ≈ 0.80. Then

\(\dot{m} = C_d YA_t \sqrt{\frac{2 \rho_1(p_1 – p_2)}{1 – \beta^4}} \approx (0.98)(0.80)\frac{\pi}{4}(0.06 m)^2 \sqrt{\frac{2(1.87 kg/m^3)(60,000 Pa)}{1 – (0.6)}} \approx 1.13 \frac{kg}{s}\)

Now estimate \(Re_d\), putting it in the convenient mass flow form:

\(Re_d = \frac{\rho Vd}{\mu} = \frac{4 \dot{m}}{\pi \mu d} = \frac{4(1.13 kg/s)}{\pi (2.17 E-5 kg/m-s)(0.06 m)} \approx 1.11E6\)

Returning to Fig. 6.42, we could read a slightly better \(C_d\) ≈ 0.99. Thus our final estimate is

\(\dot{m}\) ≈ 1.14 kg/s

• Comments: Figure 6.45 is not just a “chart” for engineers to use casually. It is based on the compressible flow theory of Chap. 9. There, we may reassign this example as a theory.
