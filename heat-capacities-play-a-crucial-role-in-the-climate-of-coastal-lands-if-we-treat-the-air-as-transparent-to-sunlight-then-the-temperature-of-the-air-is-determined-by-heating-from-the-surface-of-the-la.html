Guest Question.

CONTENT Heat capacities play a crucial role in the climate of coastal lands. If we treat the air as transparent to sunlight, then the temperature of the air is determined by heating from the surface of the land or water beneath it. But the land and the sea have vastly diferent heat capacities, and they warm and cool at diferent rates. As a result, when the sun rises or sets, temperature diferences rapidly develop between the marine and land air, with the warm air expanding and rising. his generates breezes that circulate the air in coastal areas and keep overall temperature changes relatively mild.

PROBLEM The speciic heat capacities of rock, sandy soils, and clays range between roughly 0.75 J g-¹ K-¹ and 0.90 J g-¹ K-¹, and the densities are roughly 1.0 -1.8 g cm-³. When sunlight falls on soil, the heat generated penetrates to a depth of roughly 10. cm, whereas the same sunlight warms seawater to a depth of about 10 m. Calculate the heat capacity per square centimeter of surface for the land and for the water that are warmed by the sun. Estimate the speciic heat capacity and density of seawater to be the same as pure water (c = 4.184 J g-¹ K-¹ and \(\rho _{m,water} = 1.00 cm^{-3}\), and for soil use\(c_{soil}= 0.80 J g^{-1}K^{-1} \) and a density\(\rho _{m,soil} = 1.5 g cm^{-3}\) .

Admin
Solution

We multiply the speciic heat capacities by the density to get a heat capacity per unit volume, and then multiply this by the depth to get the heat capacity per unit area. Unit analysis is helpful here to
make sure that you are multiplying properly:

\(\frac{C_{water} }{A} = (4.184 J g^{-1} K^{-1} (1.00 g cm^{-3} ) (1000 cm) = 4.2\cdot 10^{3} J K^{-1}cm^{-2}\)

 

\(\frac{C_{land} }{A} = (0.80 J g^{-1} K^{-1} (1.5 g cm^{-3} ) (10 cm) = 16 J K^{-1}m^{-2}\)

the heat capacity per unit area of water is greater than that for the land by a factor of roughly \(4.2\cdot 10^{3} /16=260\).
EXTEND Land warms and cools much more quickly than water, both because it has a much lower heat capacity and because the sunlight warms a much thinner layer of the soil than seawater. As a result, at sunrise the land warms more quickly than the water, and the air above the land expands and rises, creating a breeze from the denser and colder air over the water. Over the course of the day, the water gradually warms up so the air temperatures over land and water become more similar. At sunset, the land cools quickly as the sunlight decreases, and the air above may become cooler than the sea air, causing a land breeze to blow out to sea. However, the land breeze is usually weaker than the morning sea breeze, partly because sea air at the same temperature is denser than the land air, since it extends to a lower altitude. These morning and evening breezes account for much of the distinctive ambiance of coastal regions.
