Guest Question.

Let \(\Omega\) be the region in the upper half of the \(x y\)-plane bounded by the parabolas \(y^{2}=\) \(9-9 x\) and \(y^{2}=9+9 x\) and by the \(x\)-axis. Compute \(\iint_{\Omega}(x+y) d x\) dy by making the change of variables \(x=u^{2}-v^{2}, y=3 u v\).

Admin
Solution

We saw in Example 1 that this mapping takes straight lines in the \(u v\)-plane into parabolas in the \(x y\)-plane. For example, if \(y^{2}=9-9 x\), then \(y^{2}=9 u^{2} v^{2}\) \(=9-9 x\), or \(u^{2} v^{2}=1-x=1-u^{2}+v^{2}\), or \(u^{2}+u^{2} v^{2}=1+v^{2}=u^{2}\left(1+v^{2}\right)\), and \(u^{2}=1\), so \(u=\pm 1 .\) Similarly, if \(y^{2}=9+9 x\), then \(v=\pm 1 .\) Since, in \(\Omega, y \geq 0\), we must have \(u v \geq 0\), so that \(u\) and \(v\) have the same sign. We will choose \(u=v=1\) for reasons to be made clear shortly. Note also that if \(u=0\), then \(y=0\) and \(x=-v^{2}\) \(\leq 0\), so that the positive \(v\)-axis in the \(u v\)-plane is mapped into the negative \(x\)-axis in the \(x y\)-plane. Similarly, the positive \(u\)-axis in the \(u v\)-plane is mapped into the positive \(x\)-axis in the \(x y\)-plane. The situation is sketched in Figure 3. The reason we chose \(u=v=1\) is that moving around \(\Sigma\) in the positive direction corresponds to moving around \(\Omega\) in the positive direction. [Try it: Take the path \(v=0\) (the \(u\)-axis) to \(u=1\) to \(v=1\) to \(u=0 .]\) Thus the integral around the “parabolic” region given in the problem can be reduced to an integral around a square. Also, as we computed in Example 2,

\(\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)}=6\left(u^{2}+v^{2}\right)\)

Finally, \(x+y=u^{2}+3 u v-v^{2}\), so

\(\begin{aligned}\iint_{\Omega}(x+y) d x d y &=6 \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1}\left(u^{2}+3 u v-v^{2}\right)\left(u^{2}+v^{2}\right) d u d v \\&=6 \int_{0}^{1} \int_{0}^{1}\left(u^{4}+3 u^{3} v+3 u v^{3}-v^{4}\right) d u d v=\frac{9}{2}\end{aligned}\)
