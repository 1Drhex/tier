Guest Question.

U-Bolt’s Dimensional Changes

For the 10-mm-diameter steel U-bolt in , determine the(a) strain, (b) change in length, and (c) change in diameter of the bolt’s 325-mm-long straight section. Use the rule-of-thumb value E = 210 GPa for the elastic modulus, and take the Poisson’s ratio as \(ν \)= 0.3. (See Figure 5.14.)

Approach
The stress in the U-bolt’s straight section was previously found in to be \( σ= 5.093 × 10^7\) Pa. We will calculate the strain, change in length, and change in diameter by applying Equations (5.5), (5.3), and (5.8) to parts (a), (b), and (c), respectively.

σ = \( E \varepsilon \) (5.5)

\(\varepsilon =\frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (5.3)

\(\Delta d= -ν d\frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (5.8)

Admin
Solution

(a) The strain in the straight section is

\(\varepsilon =\frac{5.093\times 10^7Pa}{210\times 10^9 Pa} \longleftarrow \left[\varepsilon =\frac{\sigma }{E}\right]\)
\(=2.425\times 10^{-4}\frac{\cancel{Pa}}{\cancel{Pa}}= 2.425\times 10^{-4}\)

Because this value is a small dimensionless number, we will write it as the percentage \(\varepsilon = 0.02425%.\)

(b) The change in the U-bolt’s length (the elongation) is

\(\Delta L=(2.425 × 10^{-4})(0.325 m) \longleftarrow \left[\Delta L =\varepsilon L\right]\)

\(= 7.882 × 10^{-5} m\)

We convert this numerical value to the derived SI unit of microns following the definition in Table 3.2:

Table 3.2 Certain Derived Units in the SI

Quantity SI Derived Unit Abbreviation Definition
Length micrometer or micron \(\mu m\) \(1\mu m = 10^{-6} m\)
Volume liter L \(1 L = 0.001 m^{3}\)
Force newton N \(1 N = 1 (kg . m)/s^{2}\)
Torque, or moment of a force newton-meter N . m —
Pressure or stress pascal Pa \(1 Pa = 1 N/m^{2}\)
Energy, work, or heat joule J 1 J = 1 N . m
Power watt W 1 W = 1 J/s
Temperature degree Celsius °C °C = K – 273.15
Although a change in temperature of 1 Kelvin equals a change of 1 degree Celsius, numerical values are converted using the formula.

\(\Delta L=(7.882 × 10^{-5}m)(10^6\frac{\mu m}{m} )\)
\(= 78.82(\cancel{m})(\frac{\mu m}{\cancel{m}} )\)
\( = 78.82 \mu m\)

Here the SI prefix “micro” represents the factor of one-millionth.

(c) The diameter change is even smaller than the U-bolt’s elongation:

\(\Delta d=-(0.3)(0.01 m)(\frac{7.882\times 10^{-5}m}{0.325m} ) \longleftarrow \left[\Delta d=-\nu d\frac{\Delta L}{L} \right] \)
\(= -7.276\times 10^{-7}(m)(\frac{ \cancel{m}}{\cancel{m}} )\)
\( = -7.276\times 10^{-7} m\)

To suppress the large negative power-of-ten exponent, we apply the “nano” prefix from Table 3.3:

\( \Delta d = (-7.276 \times 10^{-7} m)(10^9\frac{nm}{m} )\)
\(= – 727.6(\cancel{m})(\frac{nm}{\cancel{m}} )= -727.6 nm\)

Discussion
The U-bolt’s elongation is small indeed; this is expected because the U-bolt is made of steel. The elongation is approximately the same as the diameter of a human hair or slightly larger than the 632.8 nm wavelength of light in a helium–neon laser. The bolt’s diameter therefore contracts by an amount only somewhat more than one wavelength of light. Even though the bolt carries a load of 4 kN (approximately 900 lb), the changes in its dimensions are imperceptible to the eye and would require specialized equipment to measure.
