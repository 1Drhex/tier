Calculation of Fugacity Using the van der Waals Equation

Determine an expression for the fugacity of a pure gas from the van der Waals equation of state.

Admin
Solution

We begin with the van der Waals equation:

 

\(P=\frac{R T}{v_{i}-b}-\frac{a}{v_{i}^{2}}\) (E7.2A)

 

At constant temperature, we can differentiate Equation (E7.2A) to get:

 

\(d P=\left[\frac{-R T}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)^{2}}+\frac{2 a}{v_{i}^{3}}\right] d v_{i}\) (E7.2B)

 

Plugging Equation (E7.2B) into Equation (7.8) yields:

 

\(g_{i}-g_{i}^{o}=\int_{P_{ low }}^{P} v_{i} d P=R T \ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P_{ low }}\right]\) (7.8)

 

\(\int_{\frac{R T}{P_{\text {low }}}}^{v_{i}}\left[\frac{-v_{i} R T}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)^{2}}+\frac{2 a}{v_{i}^{2}}\right] d v_{i}=R T \ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P_{\text {low }}}\right]\) (E7.2C)

 

To integrate the first term, it is convenient to decompose the quotient using partial fractions:

 

\(\frac{v_{i}}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)^{2}}=\frac{1}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}+\frac{b}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)^{2}}\)

 

Inserting this expression in Equation (E7.2C), integrating, and dividing by RT, we get:

 

\(\ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P_{\text {low }}}\right]=-\ln \left[\frac{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}{\frac{R T}{P_{\text {low }}}-b}\right]+b\left[\frac{1}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}-\frac{1}{\frac{R T}{P_{\text {low }}}-b}\right]-\frac{2 a}{R T}\left[\frac{1}{v_{i}}-\frac{1}{\frac{R T}{P_{\text {low }}}}\right]\)

 

However, since \(R T / P_{\text {low }}>>b\), we can simplify the denominators in the two terms above:

 

\(\ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P_{\text {low }}}\right]=-\ln \left[\frac{\left(v_{i}-b\right) P_{\text {low }}}{R T}\right]+b\left[\frac{1}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}-\frac{P_{\text {low }}}{R T}\right]-\frac{2 a}{R T}\left[\frac{1}{v_{i}}-\frac{P_{\text {low }}}{R T}\right]\)

 

Adding \(\ln \left(P_{\text {low }}\right)\) to each side and then letting \(P_{\text {low }} \rightarrow 0\), we get:

 

\(\ln \left[f_{i}^{v}\right]=-\ln \left[\frac{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}{R T}\right]+\frac{b}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}-\frac{2 a}{R T v_{i}}\)

 

Finally, subtracting ln(P) from both sides gives:

 

\(\ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P}\right]=\ln \left[\varphi_{i}^{v}\right]=-\ln \left[\frac{\left(v_{i}-b\right) P}{R T}\right]+\frac{b}{\left(v_{i}-b\right)}-\frac{2 a}{R T v_{i}}\)

 

This result for the fugacity coeffi cient can be interpreted through our physical understanding of the van der Waals parameters, a and b. If a and b are negligible, we get \(\varphi_{i}^{v}=1\left(\ln \varphi_{i}^{v}=0\right)\), which is true for an ideal gas, by defi nition. If attractive interactions dominate, the parameter a will most infl uence the system behavior. The third term on the right-hand side will cause \(\varphi_{i}^{v}<1\left(\ln \varphi_{i}^{v}<0\right)\). Conversely, if size is more important (repulsive interactions), and the parameter b determines the behavior, then \(\varphi_{i}^{v}>1\left(\ln \varphi_{i}^{v}>0\right)\).

 

ALTERNATIVE APPROACH We have an easier time, mathematically, if we use the virial form of the van der Waals equation truncated to the second term. The van der Waals equation written in virial form becomes:

 

\(P V=n_{T}\left[R T+\left(b-\frac{a}{R T}\right) P+\text { terms in } P^{2}, P^{3}, \ldots\right]\)

 

Recall that for moderate pressures, we can truncate the above expression at P. Solving for \(v_{i}=\left( V / n_{T}\right)\) and substituting into Equation (7.8):

 

\(\int_{P_{\text {low }}}^{P}\left[\frac{R T}{P}+\left(b-\frac{a}{R T}\right)\right] d P=R T \ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P_{\text {low }}}\right]\)

 

Integrating, rearranging (note that the terms in reference pressure cancel), and setting \(P_{\text {low }}\) to zero:

 

\(\left(b-\frac{a}{R T}\right) P=R T \ln \left[\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P}\right]=R T \ln \varphi_{i}\)

 

So for pure i, we get:

 

\(\varphi_{i}^{v}=\frac{f_{i}^{v}}{P}=\exp \left\{\left(b-\frac{a}{R T}\right) \frac{P}{R T}\right\}\) (E7.2D)

 

The van der Waals equation is not as accurate as more modern cubic equations of state. However, as inspection of Equation (E7.2D) shows, it provides a simple relation between the fugacity coefficient and molecular parameters. For an ideal gas \(a=b=0\), so the fugacity coefficient is 1, as we expect. Again if attractive forces are greater than repulsive forces, \(b<a / R T \text { and } \varphi_{i}^{v}<1\); whereas when repulsive forces dominate, \(b>a / R T \text { and } \varphi_{i}^{v}>1\). As the temperature increases, attractive forces become less important relative to repulsive forces.
