Guest Question.

Two very large gray planes are placed parallel to each other. The top plane 1 is at 1200 K and has an emissivity of 0.85. The bottom plane 2 is at a temperature of 800 K and has an emissivity of 0.65. The gap between the planes is 1.5 m and is filled with a gray gas at 1050 K with an absorption coefficient of \(0.074 m^{âˆ’1}\). Determine the heat transfer at each of the boundaries. Also, determine the net heat transfer to the gas. What would be the heat transfer across the gap in the absence of the gas?

Admin
Solution

This problem consists of two surfaces (number of surface zones is (2) with an enclosed isothermal gas volume (number of gas zones is (1). Since all the radiation leaving surface zone 1 reaches surface zone 2, the angle factor area product is the area itself. We may base the calculations on a per unit area basis.

Surface 2: \(T_{1}=800K\) \(\varepsilon _{1}=0.65\)
Gas: \(T_{g}=1050K\) \(k=0.074m^{-1}\)

wehave \(E_3(0.1998) = 0.352\). The gas emissivity and transmittivity are given by

\(\varepsilon _{g}=1-2E_{3}(0.1998)=1-2\times 0.352=0.296\)

\(t_{g}=2E_{3}(0.1998)=2\times 0.352=0.704\)

Step 3 Emissive powers: The black body emissive powers are calculated as

\(\begin{matrix} E_{b1}=\sigma T_{1}^4=5.67\times 10^{-8}\times 1200^4=117573.1W/m^2 \\ E_{b2}=\sigma T_{2}^4=5.67\times 10^{-8}\times 800^4=23224.3W/m^2 \\ E_{bg}=\sigma T_{g}^4=5.67\times 10^{-8}\times 1050^4=68919.2W/m^2 \end{matrix}\)

Step 4 Radiosity formulation: For surface 1, we have

\(J_{1}=\varepsilon _{1}E_{b1}+\left(1-\varepsilon _{1}\right)\left(\varepsilon _{g}E_{bg}+t_{g}J_{2}\right)\)

\(=0.85\times 117573.1+\left(1-0.85\right)\left(0.296 \times 68919.2+0.70J_{2}\right) =102997.1+0.1056J_{2}\)

For surface 2, we have

\(J_{2}=\varepsilon _{2}E_{b2}+\left(1-\varepsilon _{2}\right)\left(\varepsilon _{g}E_{bg}+t_{g}J_{1}\right)\)

\(=0.65\times 23224.3+\left(1-0.65\right)\left(0.296 \times 68919.2+0.704J_{1}\right) =22235.8+0.2464J_{2}\)

The two radiosity equations maybewritten in the form ofamatrix equation

\(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.1056 \\ -0.2464 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} J_{1} \\ J_{2} \end{Bmatrix}= \begin{Bmatrix} 102997.1 \\ 22235.8 \end{Bmatrix}\)

Using Kramerâ€™s rule, the solution to the above equations is

\(\begin{matrix} J_{1}=108159.5W/m^2, & J_{2}=48886.3W/m^2 \end{matrix}\)

Step 5 Heat fluxes are calculated now. q1, the heat flux at the hot surface, is

\(q_{1}=\frac{\varepsilon _{1}}{1-\varepsilon _{1}}\left(E_{b1}-J_{1}\right)=\frac{0.85}{1-0.85}(117573.1-108159.5)=53343.7W/m^2\)

q2, the heat flux at the cold surface, is

\(q_{2}=\frac{\varepsilon _{2}}{1-\varepsilon _{2}}\left(E_{b2}-J_{2}\right)=\frac{0.65}{1-0.65}(23224.3-48886.3)=-47658W/m^2\)

The heat transfer to the gas qg may be determined by energy balance.

\(q_{g}=-q_{1}-q_{2}=-53343.7+47658=5685.7W/m^2\)

It is seen that heat leaving the hot wall is transferred partly to the gas and partly to the cold wall.

Step 6 Results in the absence of gas: If the gap between the slabs is evacuated, the heat transfer may be calculated using a simple two-surface enclosure analysis presented earlier. The heat transfer across the enclosure is given by Eq. 10.42: \(q_{1-2}=\frac{\varepsilon_{1}\varepsilon _{2}\sigma \left(T_{1}^4-T_{2}^4\right) }{1-(1-\varepsilon _{1})(1-\varepsilon _{2})}=\frac{\sigma \left(T_{1}^4-T_{2}^4\right) }{\left(\frac{1}{\varepsilon _{1}}+\frac{1}{\varepsilon _{2}}-1\right) }\) and hence

\(q_{1-2}=\frac{\sigma \left(1200^4-800^4\right) }{\left(\frac{1}{0.85}+\frac{1}{0.65}-1 \right) }=55016.1W/m^2\)

The heat transfer across the gap is more in the case of an evacuated enclosure as compared to the gas-filled enclosure.
