Emissivity of a Surface and Emissive Power

The spectral emissivity function of an opaque surface at 800 K is approximated as (Fig. 11–30)

\( \varepsilon _{\lambda } = \left\{\begin{matrix} \varepsilon _{1 } = 0.3, & 0 \leq \lambda \leq 3 \mu m\\\varepsilon _{2 } = 0.8, & 3 \mu m \leq \lambda < 7 \mu m\\\varepsilon _{3 } = 0.1,& 7 \mu m \leq \lambda < \infty \end{matrix}\right. \)

 

Determine the average emissivity of the surface and its emissive power.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION The variation of emissivity of a surface at a specified temperature with wavelength is given. The average emissivity of the surface and its emissive power are to be determined.

Analysis The variation of the emissivity of the surface with wavelength is given as a step function. Therefore, the average emissivity of the surface can be determined from Eq. \(\varepsilon \left ( T \right ) = \frac{E \left ( T \right )}{E_{b}\left ( T \right )} = \frac{\int_{0}^{\infty } \varepsilon _{\lambda } \left ( \lambda ,T \right ) E_{b\lambda } \left ( \lambda ,T \right ) d\lambda }{\sigma T^{4}} \) by breaking the integral into three parts,

\( \varepsilon \left ( T \right ) = \frac{\varepsilon _{1} \int_{0}^{\lambda _{1}} E_{b\lambda } d\lambda }{\sigma T^{4}} + \frac{\varepsilon _{2} \int_{\lambda _{1}}^{\lambda _{2}} E_{b\lambda } d\lambda }{\sigma T^{4}} + \frac{\varepsilon _{3} \int_{\lambda _{2}}^{\infty } E_{b\lambda } d\lambda }{\sigma T^{4}} \)

 

\( = \varepsilon_{1} f_{0 – \lambda _{1}}\left ( T \right ) + \varepsilon_{2} f_{\lambda _{1} – \lambda _{2}}\left ( T \right ) + \varepsilon_{3} f_{\lambda _{2} – \infty }\left ( T \right ) \)

 

\( = \varepsilon_{1} f_{ \lambda _{1}} + \varepsilon_{2} \left ( f_{\lambda _{2}} – f_{\lambda _{1}} \right ) + \varepsilon_{3} \left ( 1 – f_{\lambda _{2}} \right ) \)

 

where \( f_{\lambda _{1}} \) and \( f_{\lambda _{2}} \) are blackbody radiation functions corresponding to \( \lambda _{1} T \) and \( \lambda _{2} T \). These functions are determined from Table 11–2 to be

\( \lambda _{1} T = \left ( 3 \mu m \right )\left ( 800 K \right ) = 2400 \mu m \cdot K \rightarrow f_{\lambda _{1}} = 0.140256 \)

 

\( \lambda _{2} T = \left ( 7 \mu m \right )\left ( 800 K \right ) = 5600 \mu m \cdot K \rightarrow f_{\lambda _{2}} = 0.701046 \)

 

Note that \( f_{0 – \lambda _{1}} = f_{\lambda _{1}} – f_{0} = f_{\lambda _{1}} \), since \( f_{0} = 0 \), and \( f_{ \lambda _{2} – \infty } = f_{\infty } – f_{\lambda _{2}} = 1 – f_{\lambda _{2}} \), since \( f_{\infty } = 1 \), Substituting,

\( \varepsilon = 0.3 \times 0.140256 + 0.8 \left ( 0.701046 – 0.140256 \right ) + \)

 

\( 0.1 \left ( 1 – 0.701046 \right ) = 0.521 \)

 

That is, the surface will emit as much radiation energy at 800 K as a gray surface having a constant emissivity of \( \varepsilon = 0.521 \). The emissive power of the surface is

\( E = \varepsilon \sigma T^{4} = 0.521 \left ( 5.67 \times 10^{-8} W/m^{2} \cdot K^{4} \right )\left ( 800 K \right )^{4}\)

 

\( = 12,100 W/m^{2} \)

 

Discussion Note that the surface emits 12.1 kJ of radiation energy per second per \(m^{2} \) area of the surface.
