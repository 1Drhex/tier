Guest Question.

Let \( \vec{X} \) be a general Gaussian random vector, for which \( p_{ \vec{X} } (\vec{u}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi )^{n/2} \left|K\right|^{1/2} } \exp (-\frac{1}{2} (\vec{u} -\vec{\mu })^{T} K^{-1}(\vec{u} -\vec{\mu }) ) \)

in which \( \vec{\mu } \) is a vector of constants; \( K \) is a square, symmetric, positive definite matrix of constants; and \( K^{-1} \) and \( \left|K\right| \) denote the inverse and determinant, respectively, of \( K \) . Show that \( \vec{\mu } \) and \( K \) give the mean and covariance of \( \vec{X } \) ; that is, \( \vec{\mu } _{X} = \vec{\mu } \) and \( K_{XX}=K \) .

Admin
Solution

A convenient way to do this is to let \( D \) be a matrix with columns that are the unit length, orthogonal eigenvectors of K, meaning that \( KD= D \Lambda \) , in which \( \Lambda \) is a diagonal matrix containing the eigenvalues of \( K \). Then \( D^{T}D= I \) and \( D^{T}KD= \Lambda \). Manipulating these expressions also shows that \( D^{-1}=D^{T}, D^{T} K^{-1} D = \Lambda ^{-1} \) , and \( \left|K\right| = \left|\Lambda \right| / \left|D \right|^{2} = \left|\Lambda \right| = \Lambda _{11} … \Lambda _{nn} \). Now consider a new random vector \( \vec{X} \) defined as \( \vec{Y} = D^T (\vec{X} – \vec{\mu}) \) . Using Eq. 2.41 then gives

\( p_{\vec{Y}}(\vec{u})=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}(\Lambda _{11}… \Lambda _{nn})^{1/2}} exp (-\frac{1}{2} \vec{v}^{T}D^{T}K^{-1}D\vec{v} )\)

Using the fact that \( D^{T}K^{-1}D = \Lambda ^{-1} \) is diagonal allows this to be expanded as

\( p_{\vec{Y}}(\vec{v})=\frac{\exp [(-\Lambda _{11}^{-1}v_{1}^{2} -,…,-\Lambda _{nn}^{-1}v_{n}^{2})/2]}{(2\pi)^{n/2}(\Lambda _{11}… \Lambda _{nn})^{1/2}} = \prod\limits_{j=1}^{n}{\frac{e^{-\Lambda _{jj}^{-1}v_{j}^{2}/2}}{(2\pi \Lambda _{jj})^{1/2}} }\)

The fact that \( p_{\vec{Y}}(\vec{v}) \) is a product of individual functions of \(v_{j} \) terms indicates that the \( Y_{j} \) terms are independent with

\( p_{Y_{j}}(v_{j})=\frac{e^{-\Lambda _{jj}^{-1}v_{j}^{2}/2}}{(2\pi \Lambda _{jj})^{1/2}}\)

Noting that the \( (j, j) \) component of \( \Lambda ^{-1} \) is \( \Lambda _{jj}^{-1} = 1/\Lambda _{jj} \) , and comparing \( p_{Y_{j}}(v_{j}) \) with the standard form for a scalar Gaussian random variable, as in , shows that

\( E(Y_{j})=0 \), \( \sigma _{Y_{j}}^{2}=\Lambda _{jj}\)

Thus, we can say that the mean vector and covariance matrix for \( \vec{Y} \) are \( \vec{\mu } _{Y}=\vec{0} \) and \( K_{YY} = E(\vec{Y} \vec{Y}^{T})\) . The independence of the \( Y_{j} \) components, along with their known variance values, gives \( K_{YY} = \Lambda \) . Next we note that the definition of \( \vec{Y} \) and the fact that \( D^{-1}=D^{T} \) imply that \(\vec{X} =\vec{\mu }+ D \vec{Y }\), so

\( \vec{\mu }_{X}=\vec{\mu }\) , \( K_{XX}\equiv E((\vec{X} -\vec{\mu})(\vec{X} -\vec{\mu})^{T})=DK_{YY}D^{T}=D\Lambda D^{T}=K \)

Thus, the desired result is obtained without evaluating any expectation integrals involving the jointly Gaussian probability density function.
