Guest Question.

The tower crane shown in Fig. 6-46a is used to lift construction materials. The counterweight A weighs 31,000 N, the motor M weighs 4500 N, the weight of the boom AB is 36,000 N and can be considered acting at point G, and the weight of the tower is 23,000 N and can be considered as acting at its midpoint.
(a) If the tower is lifting a 9000-N load, calculate and plot the reaction forces on the feet at D and E as a function of the distance x at which the weight is being lifted (4 ≤x ≤36 m).
(b) It is desired that the reaction forces at the feet D and E always be greater than 4500 N to ensure that the tower is never in danger of tipping over. Plot \(W_{max}\) , the maximum load that can be lifted as a function of the distance x (4 ≤x ≤36 m)

Admin
Solution

The free-body diagram of the crane is shown in Fig. 6-46b. There is nothing trying to push the crane in the horizontal direction. Therefore, no horizontal reaction is needed on the footpads to prevent horizontal motion, and the reaction forces on the footpads are shown as a pair of vertical forces. This leaves only two equations of equilibrium relating the four variables D, E, W, and x. Writing force equilibrium in the y-direction and moment equilibrium about point H, which is midway between the two footpads (where x = 0), gives

\(+\uparrow \sum{F_y}=0: \quad\quad D+E-W-94,500=0 \) (a)

 

\(+\circlearrowleft \sum{M_H}=0: \quad\quad 3E-3D-Wx+31,000(5)+4500(3)-36,000(6)=0 \) (b)

Solving Eqs. (a) and (b) yields

\(D=39,333.3+\frac{W(3-x)}{6}N \) (c)

 

\(E=55,166.5+\frac{W(x+3)}{6}N \) (d)

(a) If W = 9000 N, Eqs. (c) and (d) give

\(D=43,833.3-1500xN\) (e)

 

\(E=59,666.6+1500xN\) (f)

The force E increases linearly with x and the force D decreases linearly with x. This result is shown on the graph of Fig. 6-46c. Note that the force D = 0 and the crane will tip if

\(x\geq \frac{43,833.3}{1500}=29.22m \)

(b) If D = 4500, Eq. (c) gives the maximum weight that can be lifted

\(W_{max}=\frac{209,000}{x-3}N \) (g)

The maximum weight decreases rapidly from about 105 kN when x = 5 m to about 6 kN when x = 36 m. This result is also shown on the graph of Fig. 6-46c.
