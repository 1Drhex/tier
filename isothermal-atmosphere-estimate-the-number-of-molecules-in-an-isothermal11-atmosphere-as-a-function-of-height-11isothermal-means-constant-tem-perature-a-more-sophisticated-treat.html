Guest Question.

Isothermal atmosphere Estimate the number of molecules in an isothermal\(^{11}\) atmosphere as a function of height. \(^{11}\)“Isothermal” means constant tem-perature. A more sophisticated treat-ment of the atmosphere is postponed until Section 12.4; see also Chapter 37.

Admin
Solution

This is our first attempt at modelling the atmosphere, where we make the rather naive assumption that the temperature of the atmosphere is constant. Consider a molecule in an ideal gas at temperature T in the presence of gravity. The probability \(P(z)\) of the molecule of mass m being at height z is given by

\( P(z) \alpha e^{-mgz/k_{B}T},\) (4.20)

because its potential energy is mgz. Hence, the number density\(^{12}\) of molecules \(n(z)\) at height \(z,\) which will be proportional to the probability function \(P(z)\) of finding a molecule at height \(z,\) is given by \(^{12}\)Number density means number per unit volume.

\( n(z)=n(0)e^{-mgz/k_{B}T}.\) (4.21)

This result (plotted in Fig. 4.11) agrees with a more pedestrian deriva-tion, which goes as follows: consider a layer of gas between height \(z\) and \(z+dz.\) There are \(n dz\) molecules per unit area in this layer, and therefore they exert a pressure (force per unit area)

\( dp=-n dz\cdot mg\) (4.22)

downwards (because each molecule has weight mg). We note in passing that eqn 4.22 can be rearranged using \(\rho =nm\) to show that

\( dp=-\rho g dz,\) (4.23)

which is known as the hydrostatic equation. Using the ideal gas law (in the form derived in Chapter 6), which is \(p = nk_{B}T,\) we have that

\( \frac{dn}{n}=-\frac{mg}{k_{B}T}dz,\) (4.24)

which is a simple differential equation yielding

\( \ln n(z)-\ln n(0)=-\frac{mg}{k_{B}T}z,\) (4.25)

so that, again, we have

\( n(z)=n(0)e^{-mgz/k_{B}T}.\) (4.26)

Our prediction is that the number density falls off exponentially with height, but the reality is somewhat different. Our assumption of constant T is at fault (the temperature falls as the altitude increases, at least initially) and we will return to this problem in Section 12.4, and also in Chapter 37.
