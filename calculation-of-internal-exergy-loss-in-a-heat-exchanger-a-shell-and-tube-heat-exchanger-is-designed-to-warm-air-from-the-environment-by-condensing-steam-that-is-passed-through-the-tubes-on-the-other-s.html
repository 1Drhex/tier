Calculation of Internal Exergy Loss in a Heat Exchanger

A shell and tube heat exchanger is designed to warm air from the environment by condensing steam that is passed through the tubes on the other side. The maximum air flow is 30 kg/min, and the air is inlet at the temperature and pressure of the environment, 285 K and 1 bar, respectively. The steam fl ows through the system at a pressure of 10 bar and a flow rate of 3 kg/min. The quality of the steam at the inlet is 0.9, and at the exit is 0.2.

(a) Calculate the lost work during this process using the “Exergy Method” by calculating the difference in exthalpy between the initial and fi nal states.

(b) Calculate the lost work during this process using the entropy change of the universe, i.e., Equation (3.41).

\(-w_{l}=T_{0} \Delta s_{\text {univ }}\) (3.41)

Admin
Solution

ENERGY CONSERVATION

A schematic of the process is shown in Figure E3.18.

 

First we need to find the outlet temperature of the air. To do this we perform an energy balance:

 

\(0=\dot{n}_{\text {air }}\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)+\dot{m}_{\text {water }}\left(\hat{h}_{4}-\hat{h}_{3}\right)\) (E3.18A)

 

Where,

 

\(n_{\text {air }}=\frac{\dot{m}_{\text {air }}}{M W_{\text {air }}}=\frac{30\left[\frac{ kg }{\min }\right]}{0.029\left[\frac{ kg }{ mol }\right]}=1034\left[\frac{ mol }{\min }\right]\) (E3.18B)

 

The enthalpy change for air is given by:

 

\(h_{2}-h_{1}=\int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}} c_{P, a i r} d T=R \int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}}\left[A+B T+D T^{-2}\right] d T\)

 

\(=R\left[A\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)+\frac{B}{2}\left(T_{2}^{2}-T_{1}^{2}\right)-D\left(\frac{1}{T_{2}}-\frac{1}{T_{1}}\right)\right]\) (E3.18C)

 

Where the values for air can be found in Appendix A.2:

 

\(A=3.355 ; \quad B=0.575 \times 10^{-3} ; \quad \text { and } D=-0.016 \times 10^{5}\)

 

For water we look up the values of enthalpy for saturated steam at 10 bar (1 MPa) in the steam tables (Appendix B.2):

 

\(\hat{h}_{v}=2778.1\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg }\right] \text { and } \hat{h}_{l}=762.79\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg }\right]\)

 

Using the definition of quality:

 

\(\hat{h}_{3}=x \hat{h}_{v}+(1-x) \hat{h}_{l}=0.9 \times 2778.1+0.1 \times 762.79=2576.6\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg }\right]\)

 

Similarly,

 

\(\hat{h}_{4}=x \hat{h}_{v}+(1-x) \hat{h}_{l}=0.2 \times 2778.1+0.8 \times 762.79=1165.9\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg }\right]\)

 

Substitution of these values for specifi c enthalpy gives:

 

\(\dot{m}_{\text {water }}\left(\hat{h}_{4}-\hat{h}_{3}\right)=-42,300\left[\frac{ kJ }{\min }\right]\) (E3.18D)

 

Substitution of Equations (E3.18B), (E3.18C), and (E3.18D) into Equation (E3.18A) and solving for \(T_{2}\) implicitly gives:

 

\(T_{2}=423.8 K\)

 

(a) Calculation using the exergy method:

First we fi nd the change in exthalpy in the air stream. Here we use the extensive form of exthalpy, expressed in a rate form:

 

\(\dot{B}_{f, 2}-\dot{B}_{f, 1}=\dot{n}_{\text {air }}\left[\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{2}-s_{1}\right)\right]\)

 

\(s_{2}-s_{1}=\int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}} \frac{c_{P, \text { air }}}{T} d T-\ln \frac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}=R \int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}}\left[\frac{A}{T}+B+D T^{-3}\right] d T\)

 

\(=R\left[A \ln \frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}+B\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{T_{2}^{2}}-\frac{1}{T_{1}^{2}}\right)\right]_{255 K}^{423.8 K }=11.7\left[\frac{ J }{ mol K }\right]\)

 

so,

 

\(\dot{B}_{f, 2}-\dot{B}_{f, 1}=1034\left[\frac{ mol }{\min }\right]\left[4090\left[\frac{ J }{ mol }\right]-285[ K ] \times 11.7\left[\frac{ J }{ mol K }\right]\right]=787\left[\frac{ kJ }{\min }\right]\)

 

For the exthalpy change of steam, we have:

 

\(\hat{b}_{f, 4}-\hat{b}_{f, 3} \equiv\left(\hat{h}_{4}-\hat{h}_{3}\right)-T_{0}\left(\hat{s}_{4}-\hat{s}_{3}\right)\)

 

From the steam tables:

 

\(\hat{s}_{v}=6.5864\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg K }\right] \text { and } \hat{s}_{l}=2.1386\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg K }\right]\)

 

So,

 

\(\hat{s}_{3}=x \hat{s}_{v}+(1-x) \hat{s}_{l}=0.9 \times 6.5864+0.1 \times 2.1386=6.142\left[\frac{ kJ }{\operatorname{kg} K }\right]\)

 

and,

 

\(\hat{s}_{4}=x \hat{s}_{v}+(1-x) \hat{s}_{l}=0.2 \times 6.5864+0.8 \times 2.1386=3.028\left[\frac{ kJ }{\operatorname{kg} K }\right]\)

 

So,

 

\(\hat{b}_{f, 4}-\hat{b}_{f, 3}=(1165.9-2576.6)-T_{0}(3.028-6.142)=-523.4\left[\frac{ kJ }{ kg }\right]\)

 

And,

 

\(\dot{B}_{f, 4}-\dot{B}_{f, 3}=\dot{m}_{\text {water }}\left(\hat{b}_{4}-\hat{b}_{3}\right)=-1570\left[\frac{ kJ }{\min }\right]\)

 

Finally, for the entire heat exchanger, we sum the total exthalpy change:

 

\(\dot{W}_{l}=\Delta \dot{B}_{f}=\left(\dot{B}_{f, 2}-\dot{B}_{f, 1}\right)+\left(\dot{B}_{f, 4}-\dot{B}_{f, 3}\right)=-783\left[\frac{ kJ }{\min }\right]\)

 

Thus, we lose 783 kJ of useful work for every minute the heat exchanger operates! This value represents the internal exergy loss in this process. Said another way, 783 kJ of work is lost because heat is transferred nonreversibly due to the temperature difference. If we could place a Carnot engine between the steam and air, we could generate 783 kJ/min of power.

 

(b) Calculation using the change in entropy of the universe:

Since there is no heat transfer to the surroundings, we can calculate the change in the entropy of the universe by the extensive entropy differences in each stream during the process:

 

\(\Delta \dot{S}_{\text {univ }}=\dot{n}_{\text {air }}\left(s_{2}-s_{1}\right)+\dot{m}_{\text {water }}\left(\hat{s}_{4}-\hat{s}_{3}\right)\)

 

Inserting values from above, we get:

 

\(\Delta \dot{S}_{\text {univ }}=1034\left[\frac{ mol }{\min }\right] \times 11.7\left[\frac{ J }{ mol K }\right]+3\left[\frac{ kg }{\min }\right] \times(3.028-6.142)\left[\frac{ J }{ mol K }\right]\)

 

\(=2749\left[\frac{ J }{\min K }\right]\)

 

We can then calculate the lost work based on Equation (3.41):

 

\(\dot{W}_{l}=-T_{0} \Delta \dot{S}_{\text {univ }}=-285[ K ] \times 2749\left[\frac{ J }{\min K }\right]=-783\left[\frac{ kJ }{\min }\right]\)

 

This value is identical to that calculated in part A.
