Guest Question.

The 6061-T6 aluminum shaft shown in Fig. 5–27 has a cross-sectional area in the shape of an equilateral triangle. Determine the largest torque T that can be applied to the end of the shaft if the allowable shear stress is \(\tau_{allow} = 8 ksi\) and the angle of twist at its end is restricted to \(\phi_{allow} = 0.02 rad\). How much torque can be applied to a shaft of circular cross section made from the same amount of material?

Admin
Solution

By inspection, the resultant internal torque at any cross section along the shaft’s axis is also T . Using the formulas for \(\tau_{max}\) and \(\phi\) in Table 5–1 , we require

\(\tau _{allow}=\frac{20T}{a^3}; \quad\quad 8(10^3)lb/in^2=\frac{20T}{(1.5in.)^3} \quad\quad T=1350lb\cdot in. \)

Also,

\(\phi _{allow}=\frac{46T}{a^4G_{al}}; \quad\quad 0.02rad=\frac{46T(4ft)(12in./ft)}{(1.5in.)^4[3.7(10^6)lb/in^2]} \quad\quad T=170lb\cdot in. \)

By comparison, the torque is limited due to the angle of twist.
Circular Cross Section. If the same amount of aluminum is to be used in making the same length of shaft having a circular cross section, then the radius of the cross section can be calculated. We have

\(A_{circle}=A_{triangle}; \quad\quad \pi c^2=\frac{1}{2}(1.5in.)(1.5\sin 60^\circ ) \quad\quad c=0.557 in. \)

The limitations of stress and angle of twist then require

\(\tau _{allow}=\frac{Tc}{J}; \quad\quad 8(10^3)lb/in^2=\frac{T(0.557in.)}{(\pi /2)(0.557in.)^4} \quad\quad T=2170lb\cdot in.\)

 

\(\phi _{allow}=\frac{TL}{JG_{al}}; \quad\quad 0.02rad=\frac{T(4ft)(12in./ft)}{(\pi /2)(0.557in.)^4[3.7(10^6)lb/in^2]} \quad\quad T=233lb\cdot in.\)

Again, the angle of twist limits the applied torque.
NOTE: Comparing this result (233 lb · in.) with that given above (170 lb · in.), it is seen that a shaft of circular cross section can support 37% more torque than the one having a triangular cross section.
