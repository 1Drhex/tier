Guest Question.

The 20-cm-diameter (= d) tank of Figure 5.15 contains chloroform, and it is being drained by the attached piping system. The pipe itself is made of 3-nominal schedule 40 commercial steel and it is 25 m long. Neglecting minor losses, determine the variation of velocity and height with time if the height h is allowed to vary from an initial value of 2 m to 1.2 m.

Admin
Solution

We begin by determining properties from the appropriate tables:
chloroform \(ρ = 1 470 kg/m^3\) \(µ = 0.53 \times 10^{-3} N·s/m^2\)
[App. Table B.1]
3-nom sch 40 D = 7.792 cm \(A = 47.69 cm^2\) [App. Table D.1]
commercial steel ε = 0.004 6 cm [Table 3.1]
With the tank diameter of 0.2 m, we calculate
\(A_1 =\frac{ πd^2}{4} =\frac{ π(0.2)^2}{4} = 0.031 42 m^2\)
The area ratio then is
\(\left\lgroup\frac{ A_2}{A_1}\right\rgroup^2= \left\lgroup\frac{ 47.69 \times 10^{-4}}{0.031 42}\right\rgroup^2= 0.023\)
Equation 5.43 becomes

\(Δh=-\frac{A_2}{A_1}VΔt\) (5.43)

\(∆h = –\left\lgroup\frac{ 47.69 \times 10^{-4}}{0.031 42} \right\rgroup V∆t\)
or
∆h = – 0.151 8V∆t (i)
Substituting known quantities into Equation 5.39, we get

\(V = \left[\frac{2g h}{1 – (A_2/A_1)^2 + fL/D + ∑ K} \right]^{1/2}\) (5.39)
\(V = \left[\frac{2(9.81)h}{1 – 0.023 + f(25)/0.077 92 + 0} \right] ^{1/2}\)
or
\(V =\left\lgroup\frac{19.62h}{320.8f + 0.977}\right\rgroup^{1/2}\) (ii)
The Reynolds number in terms of velocity is
\(Re =\frac{ ρVD}{µg_c} =\frac{ 1.47(1 000)V(0.077 92)}{0.53 \times 10^{-3}}\)
\(Re = 2.16 \times 10^5 V\)
Also
\(\frac{ε}{D} =\frac{ 0.004 6}{7.792} = 0.000 6\)
We begin the calculations by letting h = 2 m, as given in the problem statement. Thus,
1st step: h = 2 m, then from Equation ii,
\(V = \left\lgroup\frac{19.62(2)}{320.8f + 0.977}\right\rgroup^{1/2}\)
Assume f = 0.018 V = 2.4 m/s \(Re = 5.2 \times 10^5\) f = 0.018 close enough
Next, we substitute into Equation i:
∆h = – 0.151 8V∆t = – 0.151 8(2.4) ∆t
Before proceeding, we must select a value for ∆t. Without rigorous proof, we select a value for ∆t that will result in at least 10 time intervals. Selecting
∆t is sometimes a matter of trial and error. Suppose for example, that we select ∆t = 1 second. Then we find ∆h = – 0.151 8(2.4)(1) = – 0.36 m. For our second step, the value of h we use is the previous value plus ∆h: h = 2 – 0.36 = 1.64 m. By the second or third step, the value of h will have reached the 1.2 m given in the problem statement, so ∆t = 1 s is too large. A better selection is ∆t = 0.2 s. Thus, we have

∆h = – 0.151 8(2.4)(0.2) = – 0.073
Then h = 2 – 0.073 = 1.93 m;

and 2nd step: h = 1.93 m
Now we proceed by using Equation ii to find velocity V and Equation i to find ∆h. We continue in this way until the desired ∆h is reached. The solution is provided in Table 5.6. The steps are easily completed using a spreadsheet 2nd step: h = 1.93 m
