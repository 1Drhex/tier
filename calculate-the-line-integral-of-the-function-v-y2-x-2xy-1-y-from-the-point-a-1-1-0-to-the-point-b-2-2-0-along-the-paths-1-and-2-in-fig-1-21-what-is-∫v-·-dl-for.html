Guest Question.

Calculate the line integral of the function\( v = y^{2}\hat{x} + 2x(y + 1) \hat{y}\) from the point a = (1, 1, 0) to the point b = (2, 2, 0), along the paths (1) and (2) in Fig. 1.21. What is \(\oint{v.dI} \)for the loop that goes from a to b along (1) and returns to a along (2)?

Admin
Solution

As always, \(dI=dx\hat{x}+dy\hat{y}+dz\hat{z}.\) Path (1) consists of two parts. Along the“horizontal” segment, dy = dz = 0, so

\((i)dI=dx\hat{x}, y= 1, v·dI = y^{2}dx=dx, so\int{v · dl} =\int_{1}^{2}{dx}=1\)

On the “vertical” stretch, dx = dz = 0, so

\((i)dI=dy\hat{y}, x=2, v·dI = 2x(y+1)dy=4(y+1)dy, so\\ \int{v · dl} =4\int_{1}^{2}{(y+1)dy}=10.\)

By path (1), then,

\(\int_{a}^{b}{v.dI}=1+10=11 \)

Meanwhile, on path (2) x = y, dx = dy, and dz = 0, so

\(dI=dx\hat{x}+dy\hat{y}, v.dI=x^{2}dx+2x(x+1)dx=(3x^{2}+2x)dx,\)

and

\(\int_{a}^{b}{v.dI}=\int_{1}^{2}{(3x^{2}+2x)dx}=(x^{3}+x^{2})\mid ^{2}_{1}=10. \)

(The strategy here is to get everything in terms of one variable; I could just as well have eliminated x in favor of y.)

For the loop that goes out (1) and back (2), then,

\(\oint{v.dI}=11-10=1 \)
