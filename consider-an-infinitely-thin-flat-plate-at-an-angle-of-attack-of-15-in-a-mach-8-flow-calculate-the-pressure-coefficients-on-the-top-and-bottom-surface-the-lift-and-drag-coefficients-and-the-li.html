Guest Question.

Consider an infinitely thin flat plate at an angle of attack of 15° in a Mach 8 flow. Calculate the pressure coefficients on the top and bottom surface, the lift and drag coefficients, and the lift-to-drag ratio using (a) exact shock-expansion theory, and (b) newtonian theory. Compare the results.

Admin
Solution

(a) Using the diagram in Figure 9.35 showing a flat plate at angle of attack, and following the shock-expansion technique given in , we have for the upper surface, for \(M_{1}=8 \text { and } v_{1}=95.62^{\circ}\)

\(h_{1}+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2}=h_{2}+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2} (9.11)\)

\(v_{2}=v_{1}+\theta=95.62+15=110.62^{\circ}\)

From Appendix C, interpolating between entries,

\(M_{2}=14.32\)

From Appendix A, for \(M_{1}=8, p_{0_{1}} / p_{1}=0.9763 \times 10^{4}\), and for \(M_{2}=14.32, p_{0_{2}} / p_{2}= 0.4808 \times 10^{6} . \text { Since } p_{0_{1}}=p_{0_{2}}\)

\(\frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}=\frac{p_{0_{1}}}{p_{1}} / \frac{p_{0_{2}}}{p_{2}}=\frac{0.9763 \times 10^{4}}{0.4808 \times 10^{6}}=0.0203\)

The pressure coefficient is given by Equation (11.22), and the freestream static pressure in Figure 9.28 is denoted by \(p_{1}\) . Hence

\(C_{p}=\frac{2}{\gamma M_{\infty}^{2}}\left(\frac{p}{p_{\infty}}-1\right)(11.22)\)

\(C_{p_{2}}=\frac{2}{\gamma M_{1}^{2}}\left(\frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}-1\right)=\frac{2}{(1.4)(8)^{2}}(0.0203-1)=-0.0219\)

To obtain the pressure coefficient on the bottom surface from the oblique shock theory,we have from the θ-β-M for \(M_{1}=8 \text { and } \theta=15^{\circ}, \beta=21^{\circ}:\)

\(M_{n, 1}=M_{1} \sin \beta=8 \sin 21^{\circ}=2.87\)

Interpolating from Appendix B, for \(M_{n, 1}=2.87, p_{3} / p_{1}=9.443\). Hence the pressure coefficient on the bottom surface is

\(C_{p_{3}}=\frac{2}{\gamma M_{1}^{2}}\left(\frac{p_{3}}{p_{2}}-1\right)=\frac{2}{(1.4)(8)^{2}}(9.443-1)=0.1885\)

The lift coefficient can be obtained from the pressure coefficients via Equations (1.15),(1.16), and (1.18).

\(c_{n}=\frac{1}{c}\left[\int_{0}^{c}\left(C_{p, l}-C_{p, u}\right) d x+\int_{0}^{c}\left(c_{f, u} \frac{d y_{u}}{d x}+c_{f, l} \frac{d y_{l}}{d x}\right) d x\right] (1.15)\)

\(c_{a}=\frac{1}{c}\left[\int_{0}^{c}\left(C_{p, u} \frac{d y_{u}}{d x}-C_{p, l} \frac{d y_{l}}{d x}\right) d x+\int_{0}^{c}\left(c_{f, u}+c_{f, l}\right) d x\right] (1.16)\)

\(c_{l}=c_{n} \cos \alpha-c_{a} \sin \alpha (1.18)\)

\(c_{n}=\frac{1}{c} \int_{0}^{c}\left(C_{p, \ell}-C_{p, u}\right) d x=C_{p_{3}}-C_{p_{2}}=0.1885-(-0.0219)=0.2104\)

The axial force on the plate is zero, because the pressure acts only perpendicular to the plate. On a formal basis, dy/dx in Equation (1.16) is zero for a flat plate. Hence, from Equation (1.18),

\(c_{\ell}=c_{n} \cos \alpha=0.2104 \cos 15^{\circ}=0.2032\)

From Equation (1.19),

\(c_{d}=c_{n} \sin \alpha+c_{a} \cos \alpha (1.19)\)

\(c_{d}=c_{n} \sin \alpha=0.2104 \sin 15^{\circ}=0.0545\)

Hence, \(\frac{L}{D}=\frac{c_{\ell}}{c_{d}}=\frac{0.2032}{0.0545}=3.73\)

(b) From newtonian theory, the pressure coefficient is given by Equation (14.4), where θ ≡ α. This is the pressure coefficient on the lower surface, hence

\(C_{p}=2 \sin ^{2} \theta (14.4)\)

\(C_{p 3}=2 \sin ^{2} \alpha=2 \sin ^{2} 15^{\circ}=0.134\)

From Equation (14.9), we have for the upper surface

\(C_{p, u}=0 (14.9)\)

\(C_{p_{2}}=0\)

Hence, \(c_{\ell}=\left(C_{p_{3}}-C_{p_{2}}\right) \cos \alpha=0.134 \cos 15^{\circ}=0.1294\)

and \(c_{d}=\left(C_{p_{3}}-C_{p_{2}}\right) \sin \alpha=0.134 \sin 15^{\circ}=0.03468\)

and \(\frac{L}{D}=\frac{c_{\ell}}{c_{d}}=\frac{0.1294}{0.3468}=3.73\)
