Guest Question.

Design a state-variable feedback controller to yield a 20.8% overshoot and a settling time of 4 seconds for a plant,

\(G(s) =\frac{(s + 4)}{(s + 1)(s + 2)(s + 5)}\) (12:43)

that is represented in cascade form as shown in Figure 12.9.

Admin
Solution

First find the state equations and the controllability matrix. The state equations written from Figure 12.9 are

\(\mathbf{\dot{z}} = \mathbf{A_{z}z +B_{z}u} = \begin{bmatrix} -5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{z} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u\) (12:44a)

\(y = \mathbf{C_{z}z} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{z}\) (12:44b)

from which the controllability matrix is evaluated as

\(\mathbf{C_{Mz}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{B_{z}} & \mathbf{A_{z}B_{z}} & \mathbf{A_{z}^{2}B_{z}} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & -3 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) (12:45)

Since the determinant of \(\mathbf{C_{Mz}}\) is -1, the system is controllable.
We now convert the system to phase variables by first finding the characteristic equation and using this equation to write the phase-variable form. The characteristic equation, \(det(s\mathbf{I – A_{z}})\), is

\(det(s\mathbf{I – A_{z}}) = s^{3} + 8s^{2} + 17s + 10 = 0\) (12:46)

Using the coefficients of Eq. (12.46) and our knowledge of the phase-variable form, we write the phase-variable representation of the system as

\(\mathbf{\dot{x}} = \mathbf{A_{x}x +B_{x}u} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ -10 & -17 & -8 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} u\) (12:47a)

\(y = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\mathbf{x}\) (12:47b)

The output equation was written using the coefficients of the numerator of Eq. (12.43), since the transfer function must be the same for the two representations. The controllability matrix, \(\mathbf{C_{Mx}}\) , for the phase-variable system is

\(\mathbf{C_{Mx}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{B_{x}} & \mathbf{A_{x}B_{x}} & \mathbf{A_{x}^{2}B_{x}} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & -8 \\ 1 & -8 & 47 \end{bmatrix}\) (12:48)

Using Eq.\(\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{C_{Mz}} \mathbf{C_{Mx}}^{-1}\) (12.39), we can now calculate the transformation matrix between the two systems as

\(\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{C_{Mz}} \mathbf{C_{Mx}}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 5 & 1 & 0 \\ 10 & 7 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) (12:49)

We now design the controller using the phase-variable representation and then use Eq. (12.49) to transform the design back to the original representation. For a 20.8% overshoot and a settling time of 4 seconds, a factor of the characteristic equation of the designed closed-loop system is \(s^{2} + 2s + 5\). Since the closed-loop zero will be at s = – 4, we choose the third closed-loop pole to cancel the closed-loop zero. Hence, the total characteristic equation of the desired closed-loop system is

\(D(s) = (s + 4)(s^{2} + 2s + 5) = s^{3} + 6s^{2} +13s + 20 = 0\) (12:50)

The state equations for the phase-variable form with state-variable feedback are

\(\mathbf{\dot{x}} = \mathbf{(A_{x}-B_{x}K_{x})x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ -(10+ k_{1_{x}}) & -(17+ k_{2_{x}}) & -(8+ k_{3_{x}}) \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}\) (12:51a)

\(y = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}\) (12:51b)

The characteristic equation for Eqs. (12.51) is

\(det(s\mathbf{I – (A_{x} – B_{x} K_{x})}) = s^{3} + (8 + k_{3_{x}})s^{2} + (17+ k_{2_{x}})s + (10 + k_{1_{x}}) = 0\) (12:52)

Comparing Eq. (12.50) with (12.52), we see that

\(\mathbf{K_{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} k_{1_{x}} & k_{2_{x}} & k_{3_{x}} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & -4 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\) (12:53)

Using Eqs.\(\mathbf{K_{z}} = \mathbf{K_{x}P^{-1}}\) (12.42) and (12.49), we can transform the controller back to the original system as

\(\mathbf{K_{z}} = \mathbf{K_{x}P^{-1}} \begin{bmatrix} -20 & 10 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\) (12:54)

The final closed-loop system with state-variable feedback is shown in Figure 12.10, with the input applied as shown.

Let us now verify our design. The state equations for the designed system shown in Figure 12.10 with input r are

\(\mathbf{\dot{z}} = \mathbf{(A_{z}- B_{z}K_{z}) z +B_{z}r} = \begin{bmatrix} -5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 20 & -10 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{z} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} r\) (12:55a)

\(y = \mathbf{C_{z}z} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{z}\) (12:55b)

Using Eq. (3.73) to find the closed-loop transfer function, we obtain

\(T(s)=\frac{(s + 4)}{s^{3} + 6s^{2} + 13s + 20} = \frac{1}{s^{2} + 2s + 5}\) (12:56)

The requirements for our design have been met.

If we design controller gains using MATLAB, we do not have to convert to phase-variable form. MATLAB will give us the controller gains for any state-space representation we input.
