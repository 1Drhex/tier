After-Tax Replacement Analysis under the Infinite Planning Horizon

Recall , in which the Advanced Electrical Insulator Company is considering replacing a broken inspection machine. Let’s assume the following additional data:

  • The old machine has been fully depreciated, so it has zero book value. The machine could be used for another six years.
  • The new machine falls into the five-year MACRS property class and will be depreciated accordingly.

The marginal income tax rate is 40%, and the MARR is 12% after taxes. Find the useful life for each option presented in , and decide whether the defender should be replaced now or later.

Admin
Solution

Given: Financial data as provided in , depreciation schedules for both the defender and the challenger, i = 12%, and \(t_{m}\)= 40%.
Find: When to replace the defender.

Economic service life:

  • Defender. The defender is fully depreciated, so that all salvage values can be treated as ordinary gains and taxed at 40%. The after-tax market values are thus as shown in cells B21 through B27 in Table 14.9.

If the company retains the inspection machine, it is in effect deciding to overhaul the machine and invest the machine’s current market value (after taxes) in that alternative. Although the company will make no physical cash flow transaction, it is withholding the market value of the inspection machine (the opportunity cost) from the investment. The after-tax O&M costs are as shown in cells C21 through C27 in Table 14.9.

Using the current year’s market value as the investment required to retain the defender, we obtain the AEC as a function of holding period as shown in Table 14.9, indicating that the remaining useful life of the defender is two years in the absence of future challengers. The overhaul (repair) cost of $1,200 in year 0 can be treated as a deductible operating expense for tax purposes as long as it does not add value to the property. (Any repair or improvement expenses that increase the value of the property must be capitalized by depreciating them over the estimated service life.)

  • Challenger. Because the challenger will be depreciated over its tax life, we must determine the book value of the asset at the end of each period to compute the aftertax salvage value. This is done in rows 4 through 15 in Table 14.10. With the aftertax O&M values computed in rows 17 through 28 in that table, we are now ready to find the economic service life of the challenger by generating AEC value entries. These calculations are summarized in rows 30 through 41 in Table 14.10. The economic life of the challenger is five years, with an AEC(12%) value of $3,541.

Optimal time to replace the defender:

Since the AEC value for the defender’s remaining useful life (two years) is $3,030, which is less than $3,541, the decision will be to keep the defender for now. Of course, the defender’s remaining useful life of two years does not imply that the defender should actually be kept for two years before the company switches to the challenger. The reason for this is that the defender’s remaining useful life of two years was calculated without considering what type of challenger would be available in the future.

When a challenger’s financial data is available, we need to enumerate all replacement timing possibilities.

The possible replacement cash patterns associated with each of these alternatives are shown in Figure 14.16, assuming that the cost and efficiency of the current challenger remain unchanged in future years. From the figure, we observe that on an annual basis the cash-flows after the remaining physical life of the defender are the same.

Instead of using the marginal analysis in , we will use PW analysis, which requires an evaluation of infinite cash-flow streams. (The result is the same under both analyses.) Immediate replacement of the defender by the challenger is equivalent to computing the PW for an infinite cash-flow stream of -$3,541. If we use the capitalized equivalent-worth approach of Chapter 5 (CE(i) = A/i), we obtain the following results.

  • n = 0:

\(PW\left(12\%\right)_{n_{0} = 0}\) = (1/0.12)($3, 541)
= $29,508

Suppose we retain the old machine n more years and then replace it with the new one. Now we will compute \(PW\left(i\right)_{n_{0} = n}\) .

  • n = 1:

\(PW\left(12\%\right)_{n_{0} = 1}\) = $3,116(P/F, 12%, 1) + $29,508(P/F, 12%, 1)
= $29,129

  • n = 2:

\(PW\left(12\%\right)_{n_{0} = 2}\) = $3,030(P/A, 12%, 2) + $29,508(P/F, 12%, 2)
= $28,644

  • n = 3:

\(PW\left(12\%\right)_{n_{0} = 3}\) = $3,206(P/A, 12%, 3) + $29,508(P/F, 12%, 3)
= $28,703

  • n = 4:

\(PW\left(12\%\right)_{n_{0} = 4}\) = $3,449(P/A, 12%, 4) + $29,508(P/F, 12%, 4)
= $29,228

and so forth.

The present worth of $28,644 represents the net cost associated with retaining the defender for two years, replacing it with the challenger, and then replacing the challenger every five years for an indefinite period.

Comments: In our example, it happens to be that we are keeping the defender over its economic service life, but we should not expect to reach the same conclusion all the time. There are many situations where keeping the defender for a couple of more years beyond its economic service life would be more economical.
