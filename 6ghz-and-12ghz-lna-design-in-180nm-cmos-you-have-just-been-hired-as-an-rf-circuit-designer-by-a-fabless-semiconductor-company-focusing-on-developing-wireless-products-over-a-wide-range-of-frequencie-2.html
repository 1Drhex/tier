6GHz and 12GHz LNA design in 180nm CMOS You have just been hired as an RF circuit designer by a fabless semiconductor company focusing on developing wireless products over a wide range of frequencies. Your boss has assigned you the task of designing a n-MOS cascode LNA (like the one in Figure \( 7.5(\mathrm{~d})\) with a center frequency of \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) in a \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) RF CMOS process. While this may not be the most advanced technology, it has adequate performance for frequencies up to \(20 \mathrm{GHz}\) and is very cost effective. At the same time, there is a pressing need to design a \(12 \mathrm{GHz} \) LNA for a new satellite receiver, which also falls among your design responsibilities. You have the following data for the \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) RF CMOS process (Table 7.1).
\(C_{d b}^{\prime}, C_{s b}^{\prime}, C_{g s}^{\prime}, C_{g d}^{\prime}\), and \(g_{m e f f}^{\prime}\) represent the device capacitances and transcoductance per micron of gate width. Note that \(g_{m e f f}^{\prime} g_{o}^{\prime}, C_{g s}^{\prime}\), and \(C_{d b}^{\prime}\) already include the impact of \(R_{s}\). Assume that \(k_{1}=0.5\). Furthermore, assume that \(X_{S O P T}\) is equal to \(1.15 \omega_{T} / \omega g_{m e f f}\) and that the load inductor has a Q of 10 , irrespective of frequency and that the output of the LNA is terminated on a matched load.

Determine the transistor sizes and bias current, the required inductor values, and estimate the gain of the \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) CMOS cascode LNA.

Solution

Let us start with the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) LNA. From device simulations, we find that, in this\(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) process, the best finger width for minimum noise is \(W_{f}=2 \mu \mathrm{m}\). We can calculate \(g_{m}^{\prime}{ }_{m}=g_{m e f}^{\prime}\left(1-R_{s}^{\prime} \times\right. \left.g_{\text {meff }}^{\prime}\right)=0.434 \mathrm{mS} / \mu \mathrm{m}\). The first step is to size the transistors in the cascode such that \(R_{\text {SOPT }}\)= 50Ω. At \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\), we determine the gate width \(W_{G}=N_{f} \times W_{f}\).

Fixing the gate width also sets the input capacitance, which can be used to determine the gat inductance needed to cancel out the imaginary part of the input impedance
\(L_{G}+L_{S}=\frac{\omega_{T}}{(2 \pi f)^{2} W g_{m e f f}^{\prime}}=\frac{6.28 \times 35 \times 10^{9}}{\left(6.28 \times 6 \times 10^{9}\right)^{2} \times 142 \mu \mathrm{m} \times 0.4 \frac{\mathrm{mS}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=2.72 \mathrm{nH}\) .
Additionally, the degeneration inductance is a function of the cascode \(f_{T}\). The inductanc required to achieve a \(50 \Omega\) input impedance match in the \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) design is calculated as

\(L_{S}=\frac{Z_{0}-R_{g}-R_{s}}{2 \pi f_{T}}=\frac{50-75 / 77-100 / 77}{6.28 \times 35 \times 10^{9}}=217 \mathrm{pH}\)
which leaves \(L_{G}=2.72 \mathrm{nH}-217 \mathrm{pH}=2.5 \mathrm{nH}\).
One option for the output network is to select the output inductor, \(L_{D}\), such that it resonates with the transistor output capacitance at \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\). This output capacitance can be calculated as a function of the device size.
\(C_{O U T}=\left(C_{d b}^{\prime}+C_{g d}^{\prime}\right) W=(1.1+0.4) \frac{\mathrm{fF}}{\mu \mathrm{m}} \times 142 \mu \mathrm{m}=213 \mathrm{fF}\)
At \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\), the load inductor becomes
\(L_{D}=\frac{1}{(2 \pi \mathrm{f})^{2} \mathrm{C}_{\text {OUT }}}=\frac{1}{\left(6.28 \times 6 \times 10^{9}\right)^{2} \times 213 \mathrm{fF}}=3.3 \mathrm{nH}\) .
If Q=10, then the equivalent parallel loss resistance of the inductor becomes
\(R_{P L}=2 \pi f_{0} Q L_{D}=6.28 \times 6 \times 10^{9} \times 10 \times 3.3 \times 10^{-9}=1244 \Omega\) .
However, we must also consider the loading due to the output resistance of the cascode stage itself, \(r_{o u t}\), which is approximated by \(g_{m} / g_{o}^{2}=1760 \Omega\) for a \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) MOSFET cascode with a transconductance of \(56.8 \mathrm{mS}\). The output impedance then becomes \(R_{P L} / / r_{\text {out }}=728 \Omega\). The resulting maximum power gain is
\(|G|=\frac{f_{T}^{2}}{f_{0}^{2}} \frac{R_{p}}{4 Z_{0}}=\frac{5.833^{2} \times 728}{200}=126=21 \mathrm{~dB}\) .
We can apply the same methodology to scale the design to \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\). As the frequency has doubled, the gate width required for noise matching is half that of the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) design, or \(71 \mu \mathrm{m}\). Since the unit finger width is \(2 \mu \mathrm{m}\), we will round the total gate width up to \(72 \mu \mathrm{m}\) and 36 fingers. It follows that the current consumption, transconductance, and capacitances will also scale by a factor of 2 \(I_{D S}=J_{O P T} W=0.15 \frac{\mathrm{mA}}{\mu \mathrm{m}} \times 72 \mu \mathrm{m}=10.8 \mathrm{~mA} \text { and } g_{\text {meff }}=0.4 \mathrm{mS} \times 72=28.8 \mathrm{mS}\) .
The total inductance required to tune out the input capacitance is now
\(L_{G}+L_{S}=\frac{\omega_{T}}{(2 \pi f)^{2} W g_{\text {meff }}^{\prime}}=\frac{6.28 \times 35 \times 10^{9}}{\left(6.28 \times 12 \times 10^{9}\right)^{2} \times 72 \mu \mathrm{m} \times 0.4 \frac{\mathrm{mS}}{\mu \mathrm{m}}}=1.46 \mathrm{nH}\)
nearly half the value in the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) design. The degeneration inductance depends only on the cascode \(f_{T}\), and hence should remain unchanged from the previous design \((217 \mathrm{pH})\). The input inductance for the \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) LNA becomes
\(L_{G}=1.46 \mathrm{nH}-217 \mathrm{pH}=1.24 \mathrm{nH}\) .

Now, after accounting for the output resistance of the amplifier (\(3472 \Omega\) ), the maximum power gain of the \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) LNA is calculated as
\(|G|=\frac{f_{T}^{2}}{f_{0}^{2}} \frac{R_{P}}{4 Z_{0}}=\frac{2.91^{2} \times 907}{200}=38.4=15.8 \mathrm{~dB}\) .
This value is almost \(6 \mathrm{~dB}\) lower than that of the \(6 \mathrm{GHz} \mathrm{LNA}\) and reflects the decreasing available gain of the transistor at higher frequencies. The key parameters of the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) and \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) designs are summarized in Table 7.2.

In the previous design examples, we have ignored the parasitic resistances of \(L_{G}\) and \(L_{S}\) since they are not known before the inductor values are found and the inductor layouts are designed. In practice, especially at frequencies below \(10 \mathrm{GHz}\), because of the relatively large inductor values, the series resistances \(R_{L G}\) and \(R_{L S}\) are comparable or larger than \(R_{g}\) and \(R_{s}\). This leads to design iterations and larger transistors sizes than originally calculated.

Indeed, \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) and \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) CMOS cascode LNAs based on these design results were fabricated in a \(180 \mathrm{~nm}\) RF-CMOS process and using the generic schematic from Figure 7.8 [6]. In each case, the LNAs were optimized for best possible noise performance. The optimized transistor widths were \(160 \mu \mathrm{m}\) and \(80 \mu \mathrm{m}\) for the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) and \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) designs, respectively. \(L_{G}\) was \(2.32 \mathrm{nH}\) for the \(6 \mathrm{GHz}\) LNA, and \(1.1 \mathrm{nH}\) for the \(12 \mathrm{GHz}\) design. In both cases, \(L_{S}\) was \(350 \mathrm{pH}\). Excepting \(L_{S}\), these values are within 10 % of the hand analysis, verifying that simple design equations can be applied to CMOS LNA designs which can easily be scaled in frequency.
