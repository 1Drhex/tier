Guest Question.

Consider the infinite quarter-space\(\tau _{x} >0 , \tau _{z} >0\) bounded by isothermal black surfaces at \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) as shown in Fig. 15-3. Develop the boundary conditions for the \(P_{1}\)-approximation at both surfaces (i.e., \(\tau _{x} =0 and \tau _{z} =0\)).

Admin
Solution

For the \(P_{1}\)-approximation equation (15.1) \(I(r,\overset{\wedge }{s} ) = \sum\limits_{l=0}^{\infty }{} \sum\limits_{m=-l}^{l}{} I^{m}_{l} (r)Y^{m}_{l} (\overset{\wedge}{s})\)reduces to

\(I(\tau _{x},\tau _{z},\theta ,\psi )=I^{0}_{0}(\tau _{x},\tau _{z}) +I^{-1}_{1} (\tau _{x},\tau _{z}) \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }e^{-i\psi } P^{1}_{1} (\cos \theta ) + I^{0}_{i}(\tau _{x},\tau _{z})P^{0}_{1} (\cos \theta ) – I^{1}_{1} (\tau _{x},\tau _{z})\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } e^{+i\psi }P^{1}_{1} (\cos \theta )\)

For this two-dimensional problem it is convenient to measure polar angle θ from the \(\tau _{z}\)-axis, and azimuthal angle ψ in the\(\tau _{x}-\tau _{y}\)-plane from the \(\tau _{x}\)-axis. Then I(ψ) = I(-ψ) and, with \(P^{0}_{1} (\cos \theta ) = \cos \theta\),\(P^{1}_{1} (\cos \theta ) = \sin \theta\), and \(e^{\pm i\psi } =\cos \psi \pm i\sin \psi\),

\(I(\tau _{x},\tau _{z},\theta ,\psi )=I^{0}_{0} + I^{0}_{1}\cos \theta + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }(I^{-1}_{1}-I^{1}_{1} ) \cos \psi \sin \theta -\frac{i}{\sqrt{2} } (I^{-1}_{1}+ I^{1}_{1} ) \sin \psi \sin \theta\)
\(= I^{0}_{0} + I^{0}_{1}\cos \theta +I^{*}_{1} ) \cos \psi \sin \theta ,\)

since the last term must vanish owing to symmetry. Therefore, equation (15.22) \(\int_{\overset{\wedge }{n}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{s} \gt 0}^{}{} I(r_{w}, \overset{\wedge }{s} ) Y^{m}_{2i-1} ( \overset{\wedge }{s})d\Omega = \int_{\overset{\wedge }{n}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{s} \gt 0}^{}{} I_{w}( \overset{\wedge }{s} ) Y^{m}_{2i-1} ( \overset{\wedge }{s})d\Omega, i= 1,2,…,\frac{1}{2}(N+1) ,all relevant m.\) is able to provide two boundary conditions everywhere on the surface (i = 1 and m = 0,1), while we need only one (as to be developed in the next section). Thus, following the discussion of equation (15.22), we introduce local direction coordinate systems on the surfaces and satisfy equation (15.22) only for m = 0. For the bottom surface, \(\tau _{z}=0\), the problem is simple since the surface normal is parallel to the r.-axis, from which the polar angle is measured. Thus,

\(\int_{\psi =0}^{2\pi }{} \int_{\theta =0}^{\pi /2}{} (I^{0}_{0} + I^{0}_{1}\cos \theta +I^{*}_{1} \cos \psi \sin \theta)\cos \theta \sin \theta d\theta d\psi =\int_{0}^{2\pi }{} \int_{0}^{\pi /2}{} I_{b1}\cos \theta \sin \theta d\theta d\psi ,\)

or

\(I^{0}_{0}(\tau _{x},0 )+\frac{2}{3} I^{0}_{1}(\tau _{x} ,0)= I_{b1}\)

At the vertical surface\((\tau _{x}=0)P^{0}_{1}=\cos \theta ^{\prime }\), where \(\theta ^{\prime } \) is the angle between a direction vector and the surface normal \(\overset{\wedge} {n} = \overset{\wedge }{i}\). Thus, with\(\cos \theta ^{\prime }= \overset{\wedge }{s}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{i}\) and \(\overset{\wedge }{s} = \sin \theta (\cos \psi \overset{\wedge }{i}+\sin \psi \overset{\wedge }{j}) + \cos \theta \overset{\wedge }{k}\), it follows that \(\cos \theta ^{\prime }= \sin \theta \cos \psi\) and

\(\int_{\psi =-\pi /2}^{\pi/2 }{} \int_{\theta =0}^{\pi }{} (I^{0}_{0} + I^{0}_{1}\cos \theta +I^{*}_{1} \cos \psi \sin \theta)\sin \theta \cos \psi \sin \theta d\theta d\psi =\pi \left(I^{0}_{0}+\frac{2}{3}I^{*}_{1} \right) =\pi I_{b2} ,\)

or

\(I^{0}_{0}(0,\tau _{z} )+\frac{2}{3} I^{*}_{1}(0,\tau _{z})= I_{b2}\)

We shall see in the next section that \(I^{0}_{0}\) is directly proportional to incident radiation, while \(I^{0}_{1}\) and \(I^{*}_{1}\) are proportional to radiative heat flux into the \(\tau _{y}\) and \(\tau _{x}\)-directions, respectively.

Davison [3] stated that for low-order approximations Marshak’s boundary conditions would give superior results, but that for high-order approximations Mark’s boundary conditions should be more accurate. However, subsequent numerical work by Pellaud [ 16] and Schmidt and Gelbard [17] showed Marshak’s boundary condition leads to more accurate results, even in high-order approximations.
