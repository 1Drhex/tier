Guest Question.

Reconsider , but assume that the water vapor~air mixture temperature varies linearly between 400K and 800K over its path of 10cm. How does this affect the total band absorptance for the 6.3 μm band?

Admin
Solution

We may express the temperature variation as T = 400 K(\((1+s^{\prime }/s) , where s^{\prime }\) is distance along path s , and the density variation as

\(\rho _{a}=\rho _{600}\frac{600K}{T}=6\rho _{600}\frac{T_{0}}{T}=\frac{\frac{3}{2}\rho _{600} }{1+s^{\prime }/s}.\)

thus,

\(X=\int_{0}^{s}{}\rho _{a}ds^{\prime }=6\rho _{600}\int_{0}^{s}{}\left(\frac{T_{0}}{T} \right)ds^{\prime }=\frac{3}{2}\rho _{600^{s}}\int_{0}^{1}{}\frac{d\xi }{1+\xi }=\frac{3}{2}X_{600}\ln 2=1.040X_{600}=5.702g/m^{2}.\)

The path-averaged band strength becomes

\(\widetilde{\alpha }=\frac{1}{X}\int_{0}^{s}{} \alpha \rho _{a}ds=\frac{1}{X}\alpha _{0}X =\alpha _{0}=41.2cm^{-1}/(g/m^{2}),\)

since the 6.3 μm band is a fundamental band and a is independent of temperature. For the averaged \(\widetilde{w}\) we get, from \(w=w_{0}\sqrt{T/T_{0}}=w_{0}\sqrt{4}\sqrt{1+s^{\prime }/s}\) ,

\(\widetilde{w}=\frac{1}{\widetilde{\alpha }X}\int_{0}^{s}{} w\alpha \rho _{a}ds^{\prime }=\frac{6w_{0\rho _{600}}}{X}\int_{0}^{s}{}\sqrt{\frac{T}{T_{0}} }\frac{T_{0}}{T}ds^{\prime }=\frac{3w_{0}\rho _{600}s}{X}\int_{0}^{1}{}\frac{d\xi }{\sqrt{1+\xi } }\)

\(=\frac{3w_{0}X_{600}}{X}\times 2\sqrt{1+\xi }|^{1}_{0}=6(\sqrt{2}-1 )\frac{X_{600}}{X}w_{0}=\frac{6(\sqrt{2}-1 )}{\frac{3}{2}\ln 2 }\times 56.4 cm^{-1}\)

\(=134.8 cm^{-1}.\)

And, finally, the overlap parameter is obtained from

\(\widetilde{\beta }=\frac{1}{\widetilde{w\alpha }X } \int_{0}^{s}{}\beta w\alpha \rho _{a}ds^{\prime }=\frac{6\rho _{600}}{\widetilde{w}X }\int_{0}^{s}{}\left(\gamma _{0}P_{e}\frac{\gamma }{\gamma _{0}} \right)\left\lgroup w_{0}\sqrt{\frac{T}{T_{0}} } \right\rgroup \left(\frac{T_{0}}{T} \right) ds^{\prime }\)

\(=6\gamma _{0}P_{e}\frac{w_{0}}{\widetilde{w} }\frac{X_{600}}{X}\int_{0}^{1}{}\frac{\gamma }{\gamma _{0}}\sqrt{\frac{T_{0}}{T} }d\xi ^{\prime }\) .

Inspection of Fig. 10-18 reveals that the integrand varies between \(0.59\sqrt{4}\simeq 0.30\) (at 400 K), to \(0.66\sqrt{6}\simeq 0.27\) (at 600 K), back to \(0.80\sqrt{8}\simeq 0.29\) (at 500 K); i.e., the integrand is relatively constant.

Keeping in mind the inherent inaccuracies of the wide band model, the integral may be approximated by using an average value of 0.28. Then

\(\widetilde{\beta }\simeq 0.28\times 6\gamma _{0}P_{e}\frac{w_{0}}{\widetilde{w} }\frac{X_{600}}{X}=\frac{0.28\times 6\gamma _{0}P_{e}}{6(\sqrt{2}-1 )} =\frac{0.28\times 0.09427\times 3.4515}{\sqrt{2}-1 }=0.220.\)

The effective optical thickness at the band center is now

\(\tau _{0}=\widetilde{\alpha }X/\widetilde{w}=41.2\times 5.702/134.8=1.743\)

Again we are in the square root regime and

\(A^{*}=2\sqrt{\tau _{0}\widetilde{\beta } }-\widetilde{\beta }=2\sqrt{1.743\times 0.220}-0.220 = 1.018 and A=137 cm^{-1}\) .

Thus, although the temperature varied considerably over the path (by a factor of two) values for α, β, and w changed only slightly, and the final value for the band absorptance changed by less than 3%. In view of the accuracy of the wide band correlation, the assumption of an isothermal gas can often lead to satisfactory results. This has been corroborated by Felske and Tien [102], who suggested a linear average for temperature, and a second independent linear average for density (as opposed to density evaluated at average temperature). They found negligible discrepancy for a large number of nonisothermal examples.
