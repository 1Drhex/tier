Guest Question.

When high-temperature gas covers a cross tube bundle as shown in Fig. 2.10, what is the mean beam length of the cross tube bundle?

Admin
Solution

The flue gas volume between the per-unit length of the bundle is:

\(V= (2D)^{2}-2.\frac{\pi D^{2}}{4}= \left(4-\frac{\pi }{2} \right)D^{2} \)

The heating area corresponding to the flue gas volume between the per-unit length of the bundle is:

\(A= 4.\frac{1}{4}\pi D+\pi D=2\pi D\)

So the mean bean length of the cross tube bundle can be calculated as follows:

\(s_{b}= 4\frac{V}{A}=4\frac{\left(4 – \frac{\pi }{2} \right)D^{2}}{2\pi D} = 1.546D\) (2.57)

Eq. (2.58) \(s= 3.6\frac{V}{A}\) is applicable for accurate calculation of any geometry even when an exact geometric shape is not available.
In most cases, the effective radiation layer thickness is required to calculate the radiation heat transfer between two surfaces filled with an absorbing medium. The effective radiation layer thickness between surface \(A_{k}\) and surface \(A_{j}\) is:

\(s_{kj}= \frac{1}{A_{k}\phi _{kj}}\int_{A_{j}}^{}{}\int_{A_{k}}^{}{\frac{cos\beta_{j}cos\beta_{k}}{\pi r} } dA_{k}dA_{j}\) (2.59)

A few details are particularly relevant here. First, shape factor \(\varphi_{k,j}\) can be calculated when there is no absorbing medium, then \(s_{k,j}\) can be obtained by solving Eq.(2.59) \(s_{kj}= \frac{1}{A_{k}\phi _{kj}}\int_{A_{j}}^{}{}\int_{A_{k}}^{}{\frac{cos\beta_{j}cos\beta_{k}}{\pi r} } dA_{k}dA_{j}\). Because \(\alpha_{k,j}\) is given by \(\alpha = 1- e^{-Ks}\), \(A_{k}\varphi_{k,j}\alpha_{k,j}\) can be obtained. Finally, heat transfer quantity can be determined according to Eq. (2.37) \(Q_{kj}=(A_{j}\varphi_{jk}\tau _{jk})E_{j}+(A_{j}\varphi_{jk}\alpha _{jk})E_{bm}\).
