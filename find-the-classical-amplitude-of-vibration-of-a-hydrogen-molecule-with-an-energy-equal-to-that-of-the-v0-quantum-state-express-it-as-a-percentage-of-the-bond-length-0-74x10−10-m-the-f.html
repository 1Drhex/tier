Guest Question.

Find the classical amplitude of vibration of a hydrogen molecule with an energy equal to that of the v=0 quantum state. Express it as a percentage of the bond length, \(0.74×10^{−10} m\). The force constant k is equal to \(576Nm^{−1}\) and the reduced mass is equal to \(8.363×10^{−28} kg\) (half the mass of a hydrogen nucleus).

Admin

the frequency of vibration is

\(ν=(4401.2 cm^{−1})(2.9979 × 10^{10} cm s^{−1})=1.319 × 10^{14} s^{−1}\)

For the v=0 state,

\(E_{1}=\frac{1}{2}hv=\frac{1}{2}(6.6261 × 10^{−34}J s)(1.319 × 10^{14}s^{−1})=4.371 × 10^{−20} J\)

The amplitude of oscillation corresponds to

\(\mathscr{V}_{max}=\frac{1}{2} kx^{2}_{t}=E\)

where \(x_{t}\) is the value of x at the turning point.

\(x_{t}=\frac{\sqrt{2E} }{k} =\Bigl(\frac{2(4.371 × 10^{−20} J)}{576Nm^{−1}} \Bigr)^{1/2}=1.23×10^{−11 }m\)

This value is 16.6% of the bond length.
