Guest Question.

Consider the following potential well (vedi Fig. 2.3):

\(V(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}0, & \text { if }|x|>a \\-V_{0}, & \text { if }|x|<a\end{array}\right.\)

Determine the negative eigenvalues of the energy and the corresponding eigenfunctions.

Admin
Solution

In the two regions \(|x|>a\), to the right and to left of the well, we are in the presence, as in the case \(E<V_{0}\) for the potential step (see problem 2.2 ), of an equation of the kind in (2.6) which has the general solution

\(\psi(x)=c_{1} \mathrm{e}^{x x}+c_{2} \mathrm{e}^{-\chi x}, \quad \text { where } \quad \chi^{2}=-\frac{2 m E}{\hbar^{2}}>0\)

To avoid divergences at infinity of the wave function, in the left region, we will have to consider only the first of the two terms and, in the right region, only the second one. The wave function in the various regions has the form:

\(\psi(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}c_{1} \mathrm{e}^{x x}, & \text { if } x<-a \\A \cos k^{\prime} x+B \sin k^{\prime} x, & \text { if }|x|<a \\c_{2} \mathrm{e}^{-\chi x}, & \text { if } x>a\end{array}\right.\)

where, in the central region, we have chosen as a general solution a combination of real functions (sines and cosines), instead of the complex exponentials of (2.16) . By imposing the continuity conditions of the wave function and its derivative, the following equation system is obtained:

\(\begin{aligned}c_{1} \mathrm{e}^{-\chi a} &=A \cos k^{\prime} a-B \sin k^{\prime} a \\c_{1} \chi \mathrm{e}^{-\chi a} &=A k^{\prime} \sin k^{\prime} a+B k^{\prime} \cos k^{\prime} a \\c_{2} \mathrm{e}^{-\chi a} &=A \cos k^{\prime} a+B \sin k^{\prime} a \\-c_{2} \chi \mathrm{e}^{-\chi a} &=-A k^{\prime} \sin k^{\prime} a+B k^{\prime} \cos k^{\prime} a\end{aligned}\)

We derive \(c_{1}\) and \(c_{2}\) from the first and third equations and replace their values in the second and fourth ones; we get two equations that allow us to calculate A and B :

\(\begin{array}{l}\chi=k^{\prime} \frac{A \sin k^{\prime} a-B \cos k^{\prime} a}{A \cos k^{\prime} a+B \sin k^{\prime} a} \\\chi=k^{\prime} \frac{A \sin k^{\prime} a+B \cos k^{\prime} a}{A \cos k^{\prime} a-B \sin k^{\prime} a}\end{array}\)

If we equate the right members, we find that, in order for the two equations to be compatible, it must occur that

\(A B=0\)

We can therefore distinguish two types of solution,

– Case \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{0}\). From the system, we see that \(c_{1}=c_{2}\) and the solution in the central region is \(\psi(x)=A \cos k^{\prime} x\) . Therefore, considering the whole real axis, the solution is an even function of x.

-Case \(\mathrm {A}=0\). From the system, we see that \(c_{1}=-c_{2}\) and the solution in the central region is \(\psi(x)=B \sin k^{\prime} x \). Therefore, in this case, the solution is an odd function of x.

Even eigenfunctions. Imposing \(B=0\) in the Eqs. (2.19), we obtain

\(\chi=k^{\prime} \tan k^{\prime} a\)

which, taking into account the relationship between \(\chi\) and \(k^{\prime}\)

\(\chi^{2}=\frac{-2 m E}{\hbar^{2}}=\frac{2 m V_{0}}{\hbar^{2}}-k^{\prime 2}\)

can be rewritten as

\(\frac{\sqrt{\lambda^{2}-z^{2}}}{z}=\tan z\)

where

\(\lambda^{2}=\frac{2 m V_{0} a^{2}}{\hbar^{2}}\)

and \(\quad z=k^{\prime} a\)

are both positive quantities. Energy E, in terms of the new variable z, is given by

\(E=\frac{\hbar^{2} z^{2}}{2 m a^{2}}-V_{0}\)

As (2.22) is a transcendental equation, we can only qualitatively study the solutions using a graph in which we plot the first and second members as a function of the variable z. The z values of the intersection points between the two curves allow us to determine the eigenvalues of the energy through the relationship (2.21) . In Fig. 2.4 we show the two members of Eq. (2.22) versus z . Function \(\frac{\sqrt{\lambda^{2}-z^{2}}}{z}\) has been drawn for various values of the parameter \(\lambda\) . This function intersects the z -axis at the point \(z_{\max }=\lambda=\sqrt{\frac{2 m V_{0} a^{2}}{\hbar^{2}}}\) . We note that

as \(\lambda\) increases, the number of intersections between the two curves, and therefore the number of the energy eigenvalues, increases, but, although \(\lambda\) is small, there is always at least one;

\(\bullet\) in the limit \(\lambda \rightarrow \infty, i.e., V_{0} \rightarrow \infty\), the intersections move towards

\(z_{n}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \pi=(2 n+1) \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text { with } \quad n=0,1, \ldots\)

recovering the results obtained for the infinite potential well when n is odd (taking into account the replacement 2 a \(\rightarrow L ).\)

Odd eigenfunctions. Imposing, instead, A=0 in Eq. (2.19), we obtain the equation

\(\chi=-k^{\prime} \cot k^{\prime} a=k^{\prime} \tan \left(k^{\prime} a+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\)

which, using the variables already defined, becomes

\(\frac{\sqrt{\lambda^{2}-z^{2}}}{z}=\tan \left(z+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\)

The solutions can be found, again, graphically, as we can see in Fig. 2.5 . We note that

for \(\lambda<\pi / 2\), we have no odd eigenfunction;

\(\bullet\) as \(\lambda\) increases, the number of intersections, and therefore the number of the energy eigenvalues, increases; in the limit \(\lambda \rightarrow \infty, i.e., V_{0} \rightarrow \infty\), the intersections move towards

\(z_{n}=n \pi=(2 n) \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text { with } \quad n=1,2, \ldots\)

recovering the results obtained for the infinite potential well when n is even. being antisymmetric, these solutions are zero in the origin, the same situation that one would have if the potential was given by

Fig. 2.5 Square well: graphical search for the odd eigenfunctions energy levels

\(V(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}\infty, & \text { if } x<0 \\-V_{0}, & \text { if } 0<x<a\end{array}\right.\)

So, this potential has energy eigenfunctions coinciding with the odd ones of the square well.

Looking at both figures, starting from \(\lambda=0\) and increasing \(\lambda\), which is proportional to \(V_{0} a^{2}\), we first get an energy eigenvalue corresponding to an even eigenfunction, followed by the emergences of a second eigenvalue corresponding to an odd eigenfunction, and so on, alternating even solutions and odd solutions. This is a general behavior originating from the symmetry property of the potential. If

\(\lambda \in\left[(n-1) \frac{\pi}{2}, n \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\)

we have n energy levels and the eigenfunction corresponding to the n -th eigenvalue has parity \((-1)^{(n+1)}\)

We also note that, in each of the two cases (B=0 and A=0 ), the coefficients \(c_{1}\) and \(c_{2}\) are proportional to the other surviving coefficient (A or B, respectively ) This residual coefficient can be fixed, up to the usual phase, by the normalization condition. Regarding the spectrum, we can say that within this range of energies, it is discrete, while the eigenfunctions go to zero exponentially at infinity; they are the quantum corresponding to the classical orbits limited to a region of space, i.e., bound states.

As we said, there is always a bound state. This result applies to any potential well \(\bar{V}(x)\) of arbitrary form. In fact, it is always possible to bound \(\bar{V}(x)\) above by a square potential V(x); setting \(\psi_{0}(x)\) as the energy eigenfunction of the ground state of V(x) it results that

\(\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\frac{p^{2}}{2 M}+\bar{V}(x)\right|\psi_{0}\right\rangle<\left\langle\psi_{0}\left|\frac{p^{2}}{2 m}+V(x)\right| \psi_{0}\right\rangle<0\)

and, in order to obtain an operator that has a negative expectation value, there must be at least one negative eigenvalue, that is a bound state
