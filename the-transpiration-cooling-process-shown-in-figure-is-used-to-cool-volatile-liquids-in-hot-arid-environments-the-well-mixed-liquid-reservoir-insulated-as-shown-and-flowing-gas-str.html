Guest Question.

The “transpiration cooling” process shown in Figure is used to cool volatile liquids in hot, arid environments. The well-mixed liquid reservoir, insulated as shown, and flowing gas streamare separated by a thin, semipermeable barrier that allows vapor, but not the liquid, to pass through it. At liquid temperature (T), the volatile liquid exerts a vapor pressure \( P_{A} \), and a small amount of liquid vaporizes, using heat transferred from the hot flowing gas stream to the cooler liquid reservoir to drive the vaporization process. In the present process, the reservoir is loaded with liquid water, and a flowing air stream at \( 47^{\circ} C (320 K ) \) and 1.0 atm is contacted with the device. The gas stream has a heat-transfer coefficient of \( h = 20 W / m ^{2} \cdot K \) and a water vapor partial pressure of 0.010 atm. It is assumed that the semipermeable barrier offers no resistance to heat or mass transfer, so that \( T = T_{s} \). Determine the lowest steady-state water temperature allowed by these process conditions, and the flux of water vapor to the gas stream.

Admin
Solution

An energy balance must be performed on the process. Let species A represent water vapor and species B represent air. At steady state, the heat transferred by convection across the gas stream boundary layer to the liquid is balanced by energy needed to drive the mass transfer of vapor across the boundary layer. The energy balance is described by

\( k_{c}\left(c_{A s} – c_{A, \infty}\right) S \Delta H_{v, A} M_{A} = \operatorname{Sh} \left(T_{\infty} – T_{s}\right) \)

 

where S is the surface area of the semi-permeable barrier surface, and \( \Delta H_{v, A} \) is the latent heat of vaporization of the liquid at temperature \( T_{s}. \) To determine the water temperature \( T_{s} \), it is first noted that the Chilton-Colburn analogy for heat and mass transfer, equation (\( \frac{h}{\rho v_{\infty} c_{p}} (\operatorname{Pr})^{2 / 3} = \frac{k_{c}}{v_{\infty}} ( Sc )^{2 / 3} \)), can also be expressed as

 

\( h = k_{c} C_{p, B} \rho_{B} \left(\frac{ Sc }{\operatorname{Pr}}\right)^{2 / 3} \)

 

This form of the Chilton–Colburn analogy is combined with the energy balance to yield

\( \left(c_{A s} – c_{A, \infty}\right) \Delta H_{v, A} M_{A} = C_{p, B} \rho_{B}\left(\frac{S c}{\operatorname{Pr}}\right)^{2 / 3}\left(T_{\infty} – T_{s}\right) \)

 

Since the mass-transfer coefficient \( k_{c} \) and heat-transfer coefficient h share the same flow field in the gas stream, the bulk gas velocity \( \left(v_{\infty}\right) \) is not present. It is also noted that the water vapor composition at the surface \( \left(c_{A s}\right) \) is determined by the vapor pressure of the water, which is a function of \( T_{s} \). Since the vapor pressure of water can be correlated to temperature through the Antoine equation:

 

\( c_{A s}\left(T_{s}\right) = P_{A} \frac{\left(T_{s}\right)}{R T} \quad \text { with } \quad P_{A}\left(T_{s}\right) = 10^{\left(a – \frac{b}{T_{s} + c}\right)} \)

 

with literature values of the Antoine constants for water given as \( a = 8.10765, b = 1750.286, c = 235.00, \) with \( P_{A}\) in units of mmHg and \( T_{s} \) in units of \( ^{\circ} C \). Finally, if it is assumed that the gas stream properties can be taken at the bulk gas stream temperature of \( T_{\infty} \) then the solution is implicit in \( T_{s} \), and can be determined by the root of the equation once all of the other thermophysical properties are known, as detailed below. At 320 K, from Appendix I, for air, \( \rho_{B} = 1.1032 kg / m ^{3}, C_{p, B} = 1007.3 J / kg \cdot K, \nu_{B} = 1.7577 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \) and \( Pr = 0.703, \) At 320 K, for \( H _{2} O , \Delta H_{v, A} = 2390 kJ / kg . \) From Appendix J, the diffusion coefficient of \( H _{2} O \) vapor in air is \( D_{A B} = 0.26 cm ^{2} / s \) at 298 K and 1.0 atm, which scales to \( 0.29 cm ^{2} / s \left(2.9 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s \right) \) at 320 K.

Consequently,

\( S c = \frac{\nu_{B}}{D_{A B}} = \frac{1.7577 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s }{2.9 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s } = 0.606 \)

 

and

\( c_{A, \infty} = \frac{p_{A}}{R T} = \frac{(0.010 atm )}{\left(8.206 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ m ^{3} \cdot atm }{ gmole \cdot K }\right)(320 K )} = 0.381 \frac{ gmole }{ m ^{3}} \)

 

Finally, insertion of all known parameters into the energy balance yields

\( \left(c_{A s}\left(T_{s}\right) – \frac{3.81 \times 10^{-4} kgmole }{ m ^{3}}\right)\left(\frac{2390 \times 10^{3} J }{ kg } \frac{18 kg }{ kgmole }\right) \)

 

\( = \left(\frac{1007.3 J }{ kg \cdot K }\right)\left(\frac{1.1032 kg }{ m ^{3}}\right)\left(\frac{0.606}{0.703}\right)^{2 / 3}\left(320 K – T_{s}\right) \)

 

Solving this equation gives \( T_{s} = 20.7^{\circ} C \) which is obtained from the root of the nonlinear equation. With the steady-state liquid temperature \( T_{s} \) now known, the flux of water vapor can be estimated. At \( T_{s} = 20.7^{\circ} C, \) the water vapor pressure \( \left(P_{A}\right) \) is 0.024 atm, and so the concentration of water vapor at the surface \( \left(c_{A s}\right) \) is \( 1.00 gmole / m ^{3}. \) The mass-transfer coefficient \( \left(k_{c}\right) \) is scaled the heat-transfer coefficient (h) using the Chilton–Colburn analogy:

\( k_{c} = \frac{h}{C_{p, B} \rho_{B}}\left(\frac{P r}{S c}\right)^{2 / 3} = \frac{\left(20 J / s \cdot m ^{2} \cdot K \right)}{\left(\frac{1007.3 J }{ kg \cdot K }\right)\left(\frac{1.1032 kg }{ m ^{3}}\right)}\left(\frac{0.703}{0.606}\right)^{2 / 3} = 0.020 m / s \)

 

Finally, the flux of water vapor is determined by

\( N_{A} = k_{c}\left(c_{A s} – c_{A, \infty}\right) = \left(0.020 \frac{ m }{ s }\right)(1.00 – 0.381)\left(\frac{ gmole }{ m ^{3}}\right) = 0.0122 \frac{ gmole }{ m ^{2} \cdot s } \)

 

In Figure, a small flow of makeup water is added to the process to account for the evaporative loss.
