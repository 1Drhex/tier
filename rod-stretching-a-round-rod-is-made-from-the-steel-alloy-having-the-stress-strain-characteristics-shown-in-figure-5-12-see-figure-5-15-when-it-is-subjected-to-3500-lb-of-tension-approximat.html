Guest Question.

Rod Stretching

A round rod is made from the steel alloy having the stress–strain characteristics shown in Figure 5.12. (See Figure 5.15.) When it is subjected to 3500 lb of tension (approximately equal to the weight of an automobile sedan), calculate (a) the stress and strain in the rod, (b) the amount that it stretches, (c) its change in diameter, and (d) its stiffness. (e) If the force was only 1000 lb, by what amount would the rod have stretched? Use the value v =0.3 for the Poisson’s ratio.

First calculate the stress by applying Equation (5.2). Then, calculate the strain, change in length, change in diameter, and stiffness by applying Equations (5.5), (5.3), (5.8), and (5.7) respectively.

 

\(\sigma=\frac{F}{A}\) (Equation 5.2)

 

\(\sigma=E \varepsilon\) (Equation 5.5)

 

\(\varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (equation 5.3)

 

\(\Delta d=-v d \frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (Equation 5.8)

 

\(k=\frac{E A}{L}\) (Equation 5.7)

Admin
Solution

(a) The rod’s cross-sectional area is

 

\(\begin{aligned}A &=\frac{\pi(0.5 in .)^{2}}{4} \leftarrow\left[A=\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}\right] \\&=0.1963 in ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

and the tensile stress is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma &=\frac{3500 lb }{0.1963 in ^{2}} \leftarrow\left[\sigma=\frac{F}{A}\right] \\&=1.783 \times 10^{4} \frac{ lb }{ in ^{2}} \\&=1.783 \times 10^{4} psi\end{aligned}\)

 

The USCS dimension ksi is next used to represent the factor of 1000 psi:

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma &=\left(1.783 \times 10^{4} psi \right)\left(10^{-3} \frac{ ksi }{ psi }\right) \\&=17.83( psi )\left(\frac{ ksi }{ psi }\right) \\&=17.83 ksi\end{aligned}\)

 

The strain within the rod is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}\varepsilon &=\frac{1.783 \times 10^{4} psi }{30.6 \times 10^{6} psi } \leftarrow\left[\varepsilon=\frac{\sigma}{E}\right] \\&=5.825 \times 10^{-4}\left(\frac{ psi }{ psi }\right) \\&=5.825 \times 10^{-4}\end{aligned}\)

 

or 0.05825%. Because stress and elastic modulus have the same units, their dimensions cancel when strain is calculated by using Equation (5.5).

 

\(\sigma=E \varepsilon\) (Equation 5.5)

 

(b) The 3500 lb load elongates the rod by

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta L &=\left(5.825 \times 10^{-4}\right)(12 \text { in. }) \quad \leftarrow[\Delta L=\varepsilon L] \\&=6.990 \times 10^{-3} \text { in }\end{aligned}\)

 

(c) Using ν =0.3 for steel, the diameter changes by the amount

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta d &=-(0.3)(0.5 \text { in. })\left(\frac{6.990 \times 10^{-3} \text { in. }}{12 \text { in. }}\right) \leftarrow\left[\Delta d=-v d \frac{\Delta d}{L}\right] \\&=-8.738 \times 10^{-5} \text { (in.) }\left(\frac{\text { in }}{\text { in }}\right) \\&=-8.738 \times 10^{-5} \text { in. }\end{aligned}\)

 

and the negative sign convention indicates that the diameter contracts.

 

(d) The rod’s stiffness is determined from the material’s elastic modulus, cross-sectional area, and length:

 

\(k=\frac{\left(30.6 \times 10^{6} psi \right)\left(0.196 in ^{2}\right)}{12 in } \leftarrow\left[k=\frac{E A}{L}\right]\) \(\begin{aligned}&=5.007 \times 10^{5}\left(\frac{ lb }{ in ^{2}}\right)\left( in ^{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{ in }\right) \\&=5.007 \times 10^{5} \frac{ lb }{ in }\end{aligned}\)

 

In canceling the dimensions, we have expanded the stress unit psi as \(lb / in ^{2}\).

 

(e) With a force of only 1000 lb, the rod would stretch by only

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta L &=\frac{1000 lb }{5.007 \times 10^{5} lb / in .} \leftarrow\left[\Delta L=\frac{F}{k}\right] \\&=1.997 \times 10^{-3}( lb )\left(\frac{ in .}{ lb }\right) \\&=1.997 \times 10^{-3} in .\end{aligned}\)

 

Discussion

In the USCS, the derived unit mil (Table 3.5) is equivalent to onethousandth of an inch, and it is convenient for representing small length and diameter changes. A sheet of writing paper is only 3–4 mils thick, and so the rod stretches by about the thickness of two sheets of standard paper, which is expected with the given load and choice of steel for the rod. The diameter contracts by a far smaller amount, less than 0.10 mil.

To place that small change in perspective, if the rod’s initial diameter had been measured to five significant digits as 0.50000 in., the diameter after extension would be 0.49991 in. Indeed, measuring a change at the fifth decimal place requires sensitive and well-calibrated instrumentation.

σ = 17.83 ksi

ε = 0.05825%

ΔL (at 3500 lb) = 6.990 mils

Δd = −0.08738 mil

k = 5.007 × \(10^5\) lb/in.

ΔL (at 1000 lb) = 1.997 mils
