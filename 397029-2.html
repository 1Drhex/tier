Guest Question.

Two beams support a uniformly distributed load of w = 40 kN/m, as shown in Figure P11.72/73. Beam (1) is supported by a fixed support at A and by a simply supported beam (2) at D. In the unloaded condition, beam (1) touches but exerts no force on beam (2). Beam (1) consists of a W310 × 60 shape that has a length of \(L_{1}\) = 3 m and an elastic modulus of \(E_{1}\) = 200 GPa. Beam (2) is a timber beam that is 150-mm wide and 300-mm deep. The elastic modulus of the timber beam is \(E_{2}\) = 12 GPa and its length is \(L_{2}\) = 4 m.
Determine
(a) the reactions that act on beam (1) at A, and
(b) the reaction on beam (2) at C.

Admin
Solution

Case 1—Cantilever Beam with Uniformly Distributed Load
Remove beam (2) and consider cantilever beam (1) subjected to a uniformly distributed load of w = 30 kN/m. The deflection of this beam must be determined at point D. From Appendix C, the deflection of beam (1) at D can be expressed by:

\(v_{D}=-\frac{w L_{1}^{4}}{8 E_{1} I_{1}}\) (a)

Case 2—Cantilever Beam with Upward Concentrated Load at D
Beam (2) exerts an upward reaction force on the beam (1) at D. Consider beam (1) subjected to this upward reaction force \(D_{y}\). From Appendix C, the deflection of the cantilever beam at the point of application of the concentrated load can be written as:

\(v_{D}=-\frac{P L_{1}^{3}}{3 E_{1} I_{1}}=-\frac{\left(-D_{y}\right) L_{1}^{3}}{3 E_{1} I_{1}}=\frac{D_{y} L_{1}^{3}}{3 E_{1} I_{1}}\) (b)

Case 3—Simply Supported Beam with Downward Concentrated Load at Midspan
Beam (2) supplies an upward force to beam (1) at D. Conversely, beam (1) exerts an equal magnitude force on beam (2), causing it to deflect downward. The downward deflection of beam (2) that is produced by reaction force \(D_{y}\) is given by:

\(v_{D}=-\frac{D_{y} L_{2}^{3}}{48 E_{2} I_{2}}\) (c)

Compatibility Equation
The sum of the downward deflection of beam (1) due to the concentrated load [Equation (a)] and the upward deflection produced by the reaction force supplied by beam (2) [Equation (b)] must equal the downward deflection of beam (2) [Equation (c)]. These three equations for the deflection at D are combined in a compatibility equation:

\(-\frac{w L_{1}^{4}}{8 E_{1} I_{1}}+\frac{D_{y} L_{1}^{3}}{3 E_{1} I_{1}}=-\frac{D_{y} L_{2}^{3}}{48 E_{2} I_{2}}\) (d)

The only unknown term in this equation is the reaction force \(D_{y}\). Rearrange this equation to obtain:

\(D_{y}\left[\frac{L_{1}^{3}}{3 E_{1} I_{1}}+\frac{L_{2}^{3}}{48 E_{2} I_{2}}\right]=\frac{w L_{1}^{4}}{8 E_{1} I_{1}}\) (e)

Before beginning the calculation, pay special attention to the distinction between those properties that apply to beam (1) (i.e., \(L_{1}, I_{1}, \text { and } E_{1}\)) and those that apply to beam (2) (i.e., \(L_{2}, I_{2}, \text { and } E_{2} \)). For instance, the flexural stiffness term EI appears in each term, but EI for beam (2) is much different than EI for beam (1).

Calculate the reaction force exerted on beam (1) using the following values:

Steel Beam (1) Properties Timber Beam (2) Properties

\(\begin{aligned}&w=40 kN / m \\&L_{1}=3 m \\&I_{1}=128 \times 10^{6} mm ^{4} \\&\text { (from Appendix } B \text { for } W 310 \times 60) \\&E_{1}=200 \times 10^{3} MPa \\&E_{1} I_{1}=25,600 kN – m ^{2}\end{aligned}\) \(\begin{aligned}&L_{2}=4 m\\&I_{2}=\frac{(150 mm )(300 mm )^{3}}{12}=337.5 \times 10^{6} mm ^{4}\\&E_{2}=12 \times 10^{3} MPa\\&E_{2} I_{2}=4,050 kN – m ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

Substitute these values into Equation (e) and compute \(D_{y}=23.2385 kN\).

Equilibrium Equations
(a) Reactions at A. The reactions at A on beam (1) can be calculated from:

\(\begin{aligned}\Sigma M_{A}=M_{A} &-w L_{1}\left(\frac{L_{1}}{2}\right)+D_{y} L_{1}=0 \\\therefore M_{A} &=(40 kN / m )(3 m )\left(\frac{3 m }{2}\right)-(23.2385 kN )(3 m ) \\&=110.285 kN – m =110.3 kN – m ( ccw )\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Sigma F_{y} &=A_{y}-w L_{1}+D_{y}=0 \\& \therefore A_{y}=(40 kN / m )(3 m )-23.2385 kN =96.762 kN =96.8 kN \uparrow\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) Reaction at C. The reaction at C on beam (2) can now be calculated from:

\(\begin{aligned}&\Sigma M_{E}=C_{y} L_{2}-D_{y}\left(\frac{L_{2}}{2}\right)=0 \\&\therefore C_{y}=\frac{D_{y}}{2}=\frac{23.2385 kN }{2}=11.619 kN =11.62 kN \uparrow\end{aligned}\)
