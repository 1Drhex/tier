Guest Question.

In Section 10.2, we discussed white paint on a roof as a selective solar absorber. Consider now a barn roof under a sunlit sky. The solar radiation on the plane of the roof is 600 W/m², the air temperature is 35°C, and a light breeze produces a convective heat transfer coefficient of \(\overline{h}\) = 8 W/m²K. The sky temperature is 18°C. Find the temperature of the roof if it is painted with white acrylic paint, and find it again if painted with a non-selective black paint having ε = 0.9.

Admin
Solution

Heat loss from the roof to the inside of the barn will lower the roof temperature. Since we don’t have enough information to evaluate that loss, we can make an upper bound on the roof temperature by assuming that no heat is transferred to the interior. Then, an energy balance on the roof must account for radiation absorbed from the sun and the sky and for heat lost by convection and reradiation:

 

\(\alpha _{solar}q_{solar}+\varepsilon _{IR}\sigma T^4_{SKY}=\overline{h}(T_{roof}-T_{air})+\varepsilon _{IR}\sigma T^4_{roof}\)

 

Rearranging and substituting the given numbers,

 

\(8[T_{roof}-(273+35)]+\varepsilon _{IR}(5.67\times 10^{-8})[T^4_{roof}-(273+18)^4]=\alpha _{solar}(600)\)

 

For the non-selective black paint, \(α_{solar} = ε_{IR}\) = 0.90. Solving by iteration, we find

 

\(T_{roof}=338 K = 65° C\)

 

For white acrylic paint, from Table 10.4, \(α_{solar} = 0.26 and ε_{IR} = 0.90\). We find

 

\(T_{roof}=312 K = 39° C\)

 

The white painted roof is only a few degrees warmer than the air.
