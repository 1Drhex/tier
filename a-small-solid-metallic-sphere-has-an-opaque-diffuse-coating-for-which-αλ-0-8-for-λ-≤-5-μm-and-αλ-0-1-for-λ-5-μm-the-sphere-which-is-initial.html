Guest Question.

A small, solid metallic sphere has an opaque, diffuse coating for which \(\alpha_{\lambda}=0.8\) for \(\lambda \leq 5 \mu m \text { and } \alpha_{\lambda}=0.1 \text { for } \lambda>5 \mu m\). The sphere, which is initially at a uniform temperature of 300 K, is inserted into a large furnace whose walls are at 1200 K. Determine the total, hemispherical absorptivity and emissivity of the coating for the initial condition and for the final, steadystate condition.

Admin
Solution

Known: Small metallic sphere with spectrally selective absorptivity, initially at \(T_{s}=300 K\), is inserted into a large furnace at \(T_{f}=1200 K\).

Find:
1. Total, hemispherical absorptivity and emissivity of sphere coating for the initial condition.
2. Values of α and ε after sphere has been in furnace a long time.

Assumptions:
1. Coating is opaque and diffuse.

2. Since furnace surface is much larger than that of sphere, irradiation approximates emission from a blackbody at \(T_{f}\).

Analysis:
1. From Equation 12.52 the total, hemispherical absorptivity is

 

\(\alpha=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \alpha_{\lambda}(\lambda) G_{\lambda}(\lambda) d \lambda}{\int_{0}^{\infty} G_{\lambda}(\lambda) d \lambda}\) (12.52)

 

\(\text { or, with } G_{\lambda}=E_{\lambda, b}\left(T_{f}\right)=E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 1200 K ) \text { , }\)

 

\(\alpha=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \alpha_{\lambda}(\lambda) E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 1200 K ) d \lambda}{E_{b}(1200 K )}\)

Hence

\(\alpha=\alpha_{\lambda, 1} \frac{\int_{0}^{\lambda_{1}} E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 1200 K ) d \lambda}{E_{b}(1200 K )}+\alpha_{\lambda, 2} \frac{\int_{\lambda_{1}}^{\infty} E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 1200 K ) d \lambda}{E_{b}(1200 K )}\)

or

\(\alpha=\alpha_{\lambda, 1} F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}+\alpha_{\lambda, 2}\left[1-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}\right]\)

\(\lambda_{1} T_{f}=5 \mu m \times 1200 K =6000 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}=0.738\)

Hence

\(\alpha=0.8 \times 0.738+0.1(1-0.738)=0.62\)

 

The total, hemispherical emissivity follows from Equation 12.43.

 

\(\varepsilon(T)=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \varepsilon_{\lambda}(\lambda, T) E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, T) d \lambda}{E_{b}(T)}\) (12.43)

 

\(\varepsilon\left(T_{s}\right)=\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \varepsilon_{\lambda} E_{\lambda, b}\left(\lambda, T_{s}\right) d \lambda}{E_{b}\left(T_{s}\right)}\)

 

Since the surface is diffuse, \(\varepsilon_{\lambda}=\alpha_{\lambda}\) and it follows that

 

\(\varepsilon=\alpha_{\lambda, 1} \frac{\int_{0}^{\lambda_{1}} E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 300 K ) d \lambda}{E_{b}(300 K )}+\alpha_{\lambda, 2} \frac{\int_{\lambda_{1}}^{\infty} E_{\lambda, b}(\lambda, 300 K ) d \lambda}{E_{b}(300 K )}\)

or,

\(\varepsilon=\alpha_{\lambda, 1} F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}+\alpha_{\lambda, 2}\left[1-F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}\right]\)

From Table 12.2,

\(\lambda_{1} T_{s}=5 \mu m \times 300 K =1500 \mu m \cdot K : \quad F_{\left(0 \rightarrow \lambda_{1}\right)}=0.014\)

Hence

\(\varepsilon=0.8 \times 0.014+0.1(1-0.014)=0.11\)

 

2. Because the spectral characteristics of the coating and the furnace temperature remain fixed, there is no change in the value of \(\alpha\) with increasing time. However, as \(T_{s}\) increases with time, the value of \(\varepsilon\) will change. After a sufficiently long time, \(T_{s}=T_{f}\), and \(\varepsilon=\alpha(\varepsilon=0.62)\).

 

Comments:
1. The equilibrium condition that eventually exists \(\left(T_{s}=T_{f}\right)\) corresponds precisely to the condition for which Kirchhoff’s law was derived. Hence \(\alpha\) must equal \(\varepsilon\).

2. Approximating the sphere as a lumped capacitance and neglecting convection, an energy balance for a control volume about the sphere yields

 

\(\dot{E}_{\text {in }}-\dot{E}_{\text {out }}=\dot{E}_{\text {st }}\)

 

\((\alpha G) A_{s}-\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{s}^{4}\right) A_{s}=m c_{p} \frac{d T_{s}}{d t}\)

 

The differential equation could be solved to determine \(T(t) \text { for } t>0\), and the variation in \(\varepsilon\) that occurs with increasing time would have to be included in the solution.
