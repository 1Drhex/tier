Guest Question.

Consider beam 6 of Table A–9, which is a simply supported beam having a concentrated load F not in the center. Develop the deflection equations using singularity functions.

Admin
Solution

First, write the load intensity equation from the free-body diagram,

 

\(q=R_1\left\langle x\right\rangle^{-1} -F\left\langle x-a\right\rangle ^{-1} +R_2\left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^{-1} \)

Integrating Eq. (1) twice results in

\(V=R_1\left\langle x\right\rangle ^0-F\left\langle x-a\right\rangle ^0+R_2\left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^0\)

 

\(M=R_1\left\langle x\right\rangle ^1-F\left\langle x-a\right\rangle ^1+R_2\left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^1\)

 

Recall that as long as the q equation is complete, integration constants are unnecessary for V and M; therefore, they are not included up to this point.

From statics, setting \(V = M = 0\) for x slightly greater than l yields \(R_1= {Fb}/{l} \ and \ R_2= {Fa}/{l}\). Thus Eq. (3) becomes

 

\(M=\frac{Fb}{l} \left\langle x\right\rangle ^1-F\left\langle x-a\right\rangle ^{-1} +\frac{Fa}{l} \left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^1\)

 

Integrating Eqs. (4–12)

 

\(\frac{M}{El} =\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \)

 

and (4–13)

 

\(\theta =\frac{dy}{dx} \)

 

as indefinite integrals gives

 

\(EI\frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{FB}{2l}\left\langle x\right\rangle^2 -\frac{F}{2} \left\langle x-a\right\rangle^2+\frac{Fa}{2l} \left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^2+C_1\)

 

\(EIy =\frac{FB}{6l}\left\langle x\right\rangle^3 -\frac{F}{6} \left\langle x-a\right\rangle^3+\frac{Fa}{6l} \left\langle x-l\right\rangle ^3+C_1x+C_2\)

 

Note that the first singularity term in both equations always exists, so \(\left\langle x\right\rangle ^2=x^2 \ and \left\langle x\right\rangle ^3=x^3\). Also, the last singularity term in both equations does not exist until
\(x = l\) , where it is zero, and since there is no beam for \(x\gt 1 \) we can drop the last term.
Thus

 

\(EI\frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{FB}{2l} x^2 -\frac{F}{2} \left\langle x-a\right\rangle^2+C_1\)

 

\(EIy =\frac{FB}{6l}x^3 -\frac{F}{6} \left\langle x-a\right\rangle^3+C_1x+C_2\)

 

The constants of integration \(C_1 \ and \ C_2\) are evaluated by using the two boundary conditions \(y = 0 \ at \ x = 0 \ and \ y = 0 \ at \ x = l\). The first condition, substituted into Eq. (5),gives \(C_2= 0 \ (recall \ that \left\langle 0 − a\right\rangle ^3= 0)\).

The second condition, substituted into Eq. (5), yields

\(0=\frac{Fb}{6l} l^3-\frac{F}{6} \left(l-a\right) ^3+C_1l=\frac{Fbl^2}{6} -\frac{Fb^3}{6}+C_1l \)

 

Solving for \(C_1\) gives

\(C_1=-\frac{Fb}{6l} \left(l^2-b^2\right) \)

 

Finally, substituting \( C_1 \ and \ C_2 \) in Eq. (5) and simplifying produces

 

\(y=\frac{F}{6EIl} \left[bx\left(x^2+b^2-l^2\right)-l\left\langle x-a\right\rangle ^3 \right] \)

Comparing Eq. (6) with the two deflection equations for beam 6 in Table A–9, we note that the use of singularity functions enables us to express the deflection equation with a single equation.
