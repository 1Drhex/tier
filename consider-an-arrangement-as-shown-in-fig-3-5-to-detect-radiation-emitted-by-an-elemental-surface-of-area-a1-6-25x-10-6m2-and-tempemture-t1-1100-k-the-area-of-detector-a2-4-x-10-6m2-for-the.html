Guest Question.

Consider an arrangement as shown in Fig. 3.5 to detect radiation emitted by an elemental surface of area \(A_{1}=6.25 \times 10^{-6} m ^{2}\) and tempemture \(T_{1}=1100 K\). The area of detector \(A_{2}=4 \times 10^{-6} m^{2}\). For the mdiation emitted by \(A_{1} \text { at } \theta=0\) (normal direction) at a distance of L=0.4 m, the detector measures a mdiant power of \(1.5 \times 10^{-6} W\). Determine the directional total emissivity of \(A_{1} \text { at } \theta=0\). Now the detector is moved horizontally to position b such that \(\theta=45^{\circ}\). For this position, the detector measures a mdiant power of \(1.46 \times 10^{-7} W\). Can we comment on whether the surface 1 is a diffuse emitter?

Admin
Solution

\(E_{b}=\sigma T^{4}=5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times(1100)^{4}\) (3.72)

\(E_{b}=83014.5 W / m ^{2}\) (3.73)

\(I_{b}=\frac{\sigma T^{4}}{\pi}=26432 W / m ^{2} sr\) (3.74)

\(d w=\frac{A_{2} \cos \theta}{R^{2}}=\frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{0.4^{2}}=2.5 \times 10^{-5} sr\) (3.75)

\(Q=1.5 \times 10^{-6} W\) (3.76)

\(Q=A \epsilon I_{b} d \omega\) (3.77)

\(1.5 \times 10^{-6}=6.25 \times 10^{-6} \times \epsilon \times 26432 \times 2.5 \times 10^{-5}\) (3.78)

\(\epsilon=0.36\) (3.79)

 

The normal emissivity = 0.36. This is one possible way by which we can measure emissivity. If we have a vacuum arrangement with a detector and we are able to eliminate the effects of conduction and convection, it is possible to get emissivity. The more difficult part is when the detector is moved horizontally. So cos(0) now changes to cosθ. The distance, L also changes. Now because the detector is at an angle and the distance also changes, expectedly, the radiant power intercepted by \(A _{2}\) has to go down.

\(R^{\prime}=\frac{L}{\cos \theta_{2}}=\sqrt{2} L=0.566 m\) (3.80)

\(d \omega^{\prime}=\frac{A_{2} \cos \theta_{2}}{R^{2}}=8.84 \times 10^{-6} sr\) (3.81)

\(Q^{\prime}=1.46 \times 10^{-7} W\) (3.82)

\(Q^{\prime}=A_{1} \cos \theta_{1} d \omega^{\prime} I_{b} \epsilon_{\theta=45}^{\prime}\) (3.83)

\(1.46 \times 10^{-7}=4 \times 10^{-6} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \times 8.84 \times 10^{-6} \times \epsilon^{\prime} \times 26432\) (3.84)

\(\epsilon_{\theta=45}^{\prime}=0.142\) (3.85)

 

Suppose we had gotten an answer of 0.36, we could have conjectured that there is a possibility that it is a diffuse surface, as with results from just two angles, we cannot decide for sure. But we now know for sure that it is not a diffuse surface as even for two angles, the values differ. So the emitter \(A _{1}\) is NOT a diffuse surface.
