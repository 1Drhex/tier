Guest Question.

The pump in Fig. 5-27 discharges water at 4000 gal/h. The pressure at A is 20 psi, whereas the exit pressure in the pipe at B is 60 psi. The pipe filter causes the internal energy of the water to increase by 400 ft-lb/slug at its exit due to frictional heating, while there is a heat conduction loss from the water of 20 ft-lb/s. Determine the horsepower that is developed by the pump.

Admin
Solution

Fluid Description. We have steady flow into and out of the pump. The water is considered incompressible, but viscous frictional losses occur. \(\gamma_w = 62.4 lb/ft \)

Control Volume. The fixed control volume contains the water within the pump, filter, and pipe extensions. Since there is 7.48 gal in 1 ft of water, the volumetric and mass flows are 0-4000 gal

\(Q = (\frac{400 gal}{h} )(\frac{1 h}{3600 s} )(\frac{1ft^3}{7.48 gal} ) =0.1485 ft^3/s \)

and

\(\dot{m} = \rho Q = (\frac{62. 4 Ib/ft^3}{32.2 ft/s^2} ) (0.2485 ft^3/s) = 0.2879 slug/s\)

Therefore, the velocities at A (in) and B(out) are

\( Q=V_AA_A; 0.1485 ft^3/s = V_A[\pi (\frac{1.5 }{12} ft)^2]; V_A = 3.026 ft/s\)

 

\( Q=V_BA_B; 0.1485 ft^3/s = V_B[\pi (\frac{0.5 }{12} ft)^2]; V_B = 27.235 ft/s\)

Energy Equation. Since there is heat conduction loss, m is negative; that is, the heat flows kut. Also, there is no elevation change in the flow from A to B. For this problem we will apply Eq.5-11.

\( dot{Q}_{ {in }}-\dot{W}_{ {turb }}+\dot{W}_{ {pump }}=\left[\left(\frac{p_{B}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{B}^{2}}{2}+g z_{B}+u_{B}\right)\right.\\ \left.-\left(\frac{p_{A}}{\rho}+\frac{V_{A}^{2}}{2}+g z_{A}+u_{A}\right)\right] \dot{m} \\ -20 {ft} \cdot {lb} / {s}-0+\dot{W}_{ {pump }} \\ \)

 

\(=\left[\left(\frac{60 {lb} / {in}^{2}(12 {in} \cdot / {ft})^{2}}{62.4 {lb} / {ft}^{3} / 32.2 {ft} / {s}^{2}}+\frac{(27.235 {ft} / {s})^{2}}{2}+0+400 {ft} \cdot {lb} / { slug }\right)\right. \left.-\left(\frac{20 {lb} / {in}^{2}(12 {in} . / {ft})^{2}}{62.4 {lb} / {ft}^{3} / 32.2 {ft} / {s}^{2}}+\frac{(3.026 {ft} / {s})^{2}}{2}+0+0\right)\right](0.2879 {slug} / {s}) \)

 

\(\dot{W}_{ {pump }}=\left(\frac{1096,2 {ft} \cdot {lb}}{{s}}\right)\left(\frac{1 {hp}}{550 {ft} \cdot {lb} / {s}}\right)=1.99 {hp}\)

The positive result indicates that indeed energy is added to the water within the control volume using the pump
