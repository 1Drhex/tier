Guest Question.

Figure 7.16a shows a thin-walled tube that is part of an oil cooler. Engine oil enters the tube at the left end at temperature 50ºC with a ﬂow rate of 0.2 kg/min. The tube is surrounded by air ﬂowing at a constant temperature of 15ºC. The thermal properties of the oil are as follows:

Thermal conductivity: \(\ K_{x} =0.156 {W}/{(m-ºC)} \)

Speciﬁc heat: c = 0.523 W-hr/(kg-ºC)

The convection coefﬁcient between the thin wall and the ﬂowing air is h = 300 W/(m2-ºC). The tube wall thickness is such that conduction effects in the wall are to be neglected; that is, the wall temperature is constant through its thickness and the same as the temperature of the oil in contact with the wall at any position along the length of the tube. Using four two-node ﬁnite elements, obtain an approximate solution for the temperature distribution along the length of the tube and determine the heat removal rate via convection.

Admin
Solution

The ﬁnite element model is shown schematically in Figure 7.16b, using equal length elements L = 25 cm = 0.025 m. The cross-sectional area is \(\ A=({\pi }/{4})({20}/{1000^{2} })= \)

 

\(\ 3.14(10^{-4} )m^{2} \) . And the peripheral dimension (circumference) of each element is \(\ P=\pi ({20}/{1000})=6.28(10^{-2} )m \) . The stiffness matrix for each element (note that all elements are identical) is computed via Equation 7.69 as follows:

\(\left[K^{(e)} \right] =\frac{K_{x}A }{L}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] +\frac{hPL}{6}\left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ] +\frac{\dot{m}c }{2} \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ]=\left[K^{(e)}_{c} \right] +\left[K^{(e)}_{h} \right]+\left[K^{(e)}_{\dot{m} } \right]\) (7.69)

\(\ \left[K^{(e)}_{c} \right] =\frac{K_{x}A }{L}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ]=\frac{0.156(3.14)(10^{-4} )}{0.025}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 1.9594 & -1.9594 \\ -1.9594 & 1.9594 \end{matrix} \right ] (10^{-3} ) \)

 

\(\ \left[K^{(e)}_{h} \right] =\frac{hPL}{6} \left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ] =\frac{300(6.28)(10^{-2} )(0.025)}{6}\left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ]=\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.157 & 0.0785 \\ 0.0785 & 0.157 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

 

\(\ \left[K^{(e)}_{\dot{m} } \right] =\frac{\dot{m}c }{2}\left [ \begin{matrix} -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] =\frac{(0.2)(60)(0.523)}{2} \left [ \begin{matrix} -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ]=\left [ \begin{matrix} -3.138 & 3.138 \\ -3.138 & 3.138 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

 

\(\ \left[k^{(e)} \right] =\left [ \begin{matrix} -2.9810 & 3.2165 \\ -3.0595 & 3.2950 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

At this point, note that the mass transport effects dominate the stiffness matrix and we anticipate that very little heat is dissipated, as most of the heat is carried away with the ﬂow. Also observe that, owing to the relative magnitudes, the conduction effects have been neglected.

Assembling the global stiffness matrix via the now familiar procedure, we obtain

\(\ \left[K\right] =\left [ \begin{matrix} −2.9810 & 3.2165 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & −3.0595 & 3.2950 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

The convection-driven forcing function for each element per Equation 7.18 is

\(\ \left\{f^{(e)}_{h} \right\}=hPT_{a}\left \{ \begin{matrix} \int\limits_{0}^{1}{N_{1}dx } \\ \int\limits_{0}^{1}{N_{2}dx } \end{matrix} \right \} =\frac{hPT_{a}L }{2}\left \{ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right \} \) (7.18)

 

\(\ \left\{f^{(e)}_{h} \right\} =\frac{hPT_{a}L }{2}\left \{ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right \} =\frac{300(6.28)(10^{-2} )(15)(0.025)}{2} \left \{ \begin{matrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 3.5325 \\ 3.5325 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

As there is no internal heat generation, the per-element contribution of Equation 7.16 is zero.

\(\ \left\{f^{(Q)}_{h} \right\} =A\left \{ \begin{matrix} \int\limits_{0}^{L}{QN_{1}dx } \\ \int\limits_{0}^{L}{QN_{2}dx } \end{matrix} \right \} \) (7.16)

Finally, we must examine the boundary conditions. At node 1, the temperature is speciﬁed but the heat ﬂux \(\ q_{1} =F_{1} \) is unknown; at node 5 (the exit), the ﬂux is also unknown. Unlike previous examples, where a convection boundary condition existed, here we assume that the heat removed at node 5 is strictly a result of mass transport. Physically, this means we deﬁne the problem such that heat transfer ends at node 5 and the heat remaining in the ﬂow at this node (the exit) is carried away to some other process. Consequently, we do not consider either a conduction or convection boundary condition at node 5. Instead, we compute the temperature at node 5 then the heat removed at this node via the mass transport relation. In terms of the ﬁnite element model, this means

that we do not consider the heat ﬂow through node 5 as an unknown (reaction force). With this in mind, we assemble the global force vector from the element force vectors to obtain

\(\ \left\{F\right\} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 3.5325+F_{1} \\ 7.065 \\ 7.065 \\ 7.065 \\ 3.5325 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

The assembled system (global) equations are then

\(\ \left[K\right]\left[T\right]=\left [ \begin{matrix} −2.9801 & 3.2165 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & −3.05950.314 & 0.314 & 3.2165 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & −3.0595 & 3.2950 \end{matrix} \right ]\left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{1} \\ T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \\ T_{5} \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 3.5325+F_{1} \\ 7.065 \\ 7.065 \\ 7.065 \\ 3.5325 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

Applying the known condition at node 1, T = 50ºC, the reduced system equations become

\(\ \left [ \begin{matrix} 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 & 0 \\ −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 & 0 \\ 0 & −3.0595 & 0.314 & 3.2165 \\ 0 & 0 & −3.0595 & 3.2950 \end{matrix} \right ] \left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \\ T_{5} \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 160.04 \\ 7.065 \\ 7.065 \\ 3.5325 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

which yields the solution for the nodal temperatures as

\(\ \left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \\ T_{5} \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 47.448 \\ 45.124 \\ 42.923 \\ 40.928 \end{matrix} \right \} ºC \)

As conduction effects have been seen to be negligible, the input rate is computed as

\(\ q_{in}=q_{m1}=\dot{m}cT_{1}=0.2(60)(0.523)(50)=3138 W \)

while, at node 5, the output rate is

\(\ q_{m5}=\dot{m}cT_{5}=0.2(60)(0.523)(40.928) = 2568.6W \)

The results show that only about 18 percent of input heat is removed, so the cooler is not very efﬁcient.
