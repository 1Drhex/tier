Guest Question.

Evaluating a coupling energy
A beam of electrons of number density N travels in the z-direction with linear momentum \(k\hbar \) at a perpendicular distance b from a neutron (Fig. 13.12). Calculate the energy of interaction between the neutron magnetic moment and the electron beam.

Method This is a one-dimensional problem, so dτ = dz. The flux density of a particle beam was calculated in Section 2.5, and it may readily be converted into a current density by multiplication by −e. For normalization, suppose that the beam lies in the range \(-\frac{1}{2}L\lt z\lt \frac{1}{2} L\), and let \(L\rightarrow \infty \) at the end of the calculation. The number density of electrons is related to their actual number by \(N=N_{e} /L\). Note from Fig. 13.12 that only the x-component of \(r\times k\) is non-zero.

Admin
Solution

The flux density is \(N_{e} k\hbar \left|A\right| ^{2} /m_{e} \); for the normalization in a region of length \(L,\left|A\right| ^{2} =1/L\). The current density is therefore

\(j_{z} =-\frac{eN_{e}k\hbar }{m_{e}L } =-\frac{Nek\hbar }{m_{e} } \)

Then, by making use of the relation \(k\times r=-ir\sin\theta \) (see Fig. 13.12), we find

\(E^{(1)} =-\left(\frac{Nek\hbar \gamma _{N}\mu _{0} }{4\pi m_{e} } \right) I_{x} \int{\frac{\sin\theta }{r^{2}}dz } \)

To evaluate the integral we write \(\sin\theta =b/r\) and \(r=(b^{2}+z^{2} )^{1/2} \), which implies that

\(E^{(1)} =-\left(\frac{Nek\hbar \gamma _{N}\mu _{0} }{4\pi m_{e} } \right) I_{x}b \int_{-L/2}^{L/2}{(b^{2}+z^{2} )dz } =-\left(\frac{Nek\hbar \gamma _{N}\mu _{0} }{4\pi m_{e} } \right)\frac{I_{x}b}{b^{2} }\frac{L}{(b^{2}+\frac{1}{4}L^{2} )^{1/2} } \)

(To evaluate the integral we have used a standard form.) Finally, we take the limit L→∞; the last factor becomes 2 and the final result is

\(E^{(1)} =-\left(\frac{Nek\hbar \gamma _{N}\mu _{0} }{2\pi m_{e} b} \right) I_{x}\)

Comment If the energy of interaction is written as \(E^{(1)} =-\gamma _{N} I\cdot B\), then we can interpret the interaction as arising between the magnetic moment of the neutron and a field of induction B in the x-direction, where

\(B =\frac{Nek\hbar \mu _{0} }{2\pi m_{e} b} \)

A neutron has been used in setting up the calculation to avoid the effects of charge on the path of the electron beam.
