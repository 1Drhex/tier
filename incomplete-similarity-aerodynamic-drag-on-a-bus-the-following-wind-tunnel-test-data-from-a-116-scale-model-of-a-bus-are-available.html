INCOMPLETE SIMILARITY: AERODYNAMIC DRAG ON A BUS

The following wind tunnel test data from a 1:16 scale model of a bus are available:

Air Speed (m/s) 18.0 21.8 26.0 30.1 35.0 38.5 40.9 44.1 46.7
Drag Force (N) 3.10 4.4 6.1 8.0 10.7 12.9 14.7 16.9 18.9

Using the properties of standard air, calculate and plot the dimensionless aerodynamic drag coefficient,

\(C_{D}=\frac{F_{D}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} A}\)

versus Reynolds number \(R e=\rho V w / \mu\), where \(w\) is model width. Find the minimum test speed above which \(C_D\) remains constant. Estimate the aerodynamic drag force and power requirement for the prototype vehicle at \(100 \mathrm{~km} /\mathrm{hr}\). (The width and frontal area of the prototype are \(8 \mathrm{ft}\) and \(84 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\), respectively.)

 

Admin
Solution

Given: Data from a wind tunnel test of a model bus. Prototype dimensions are width of \(8 \mathrm{ft}\) and frontal area of \(84 \mathrm{ft}^{2}\). Model scale is 1: 16. Standard air is the test fluid.
Find:

(a) Aerodynamic drag coefficient, \(C_{D}=F_{D} / \frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} A\), versus Reynolds number, \(\operatorname{Re}=\rho V w / \mu ;\) plot.
(b) Speed above which \(C_{D}\) is constant.
(c) Estimated aerodynamic drag force and power required for the full-scale vehicle at \(100 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{hr}\).

Solution:
The model width is

\(w_{m}=\frac{1}{16} w_{p}=\frac{1}{16} \times 8 \mathrm{ft} \times 0.3048 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{ft}}=0.152 \mathrm{~m}\)

The model area is

\(A_{m}=\left(\frac{1}{16}\right)^{2} A_{p}=\left(\frac{1}{16}\right)^{2} \times 84 \mathrm{ft}^{2} \times(0.305)^{2} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{ft}^{2}}=0.0305 \mathrm{~m}^{2}\)

The aerodynamic drag coefficient may be calculated as

\(\begin{aligned}C_{D} &=\frac{F_{D}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2} A} \\&=2 \times F_{D}(\mathrm{~N}) \times \frac{\mathrm{m}^{3}}{1.23 \mathrm{~kg}} \times \frac{\mathrm{s}^{2}}{(V)^{2} \mathrm{~m}^{2}} \times \frac{1}{0.0305 \mathrm{~m}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}} \\C_{D} &=\frac{53.3 F_{D}(\mathrm{~N})}{[V(\mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})]^{2}}\end{aligned}\)

The Reynolds number may be calculated as

\(\begin{aligned}R e &=\frac{\rho V w}{\mu}=\frac{V w}{\nu}=V \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}} \times 0.152 \mathrm{~m} \times \frac{\mathrm{s}}{1.46 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{~m}^{2}} \\R e &=1.04 \times 10^{4} V(\mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})\end{aligned}\)

The calculated values are plotted in the following figure:

 

\(\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad C_{D_m}\text{ versus} \quad Re_m}\)

 

 

The plot shows that the model drag coefficient becomes constant at \(C_{D m} \approx 0.46\) above \(R e_{m}=4 \times 10^{5}\), which corresponds to an air speed of approximately \(40 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\). Since the drag coefficient is independent of Reynolds number above \(\operatorname{Re} \approx 4 \times 10^{5}\), then for the prototype vehicle \(\left(\operatorname{Re} \approx 4.5 \times 10^{6}\right), C_{D} \approx 0.46 .\) The drag force on the full-scale vehicle is

\(\begin{aligned}F_{D_{p}} &=C_{D} \frac{1}{2} \rho V_{p}^{2} A_{p} \\&=\frac{0.46}{2} \times 1.23 \frac{\mathrm{kg}}{\mathrm{m}^{3}}\left(100 \frac{\mathrm{km}}{\mathrm{hr}} \times 1000 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{km}} \times \frac{\mathrm{hr}}{3600 \mathrm{~s}}\right)^{2} \times 84 \mathrm{ft}^{2} \times(0.305)^{2} \frac{\mathrm{m}^{2}}{\mathrm{ft}^{2}} \times \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{s}^{2}}{\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \\F_{D_{p}} &=1.71 \mathrm{kN} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad F_{D p}}\end{aligned}\)

 

The corresponding power required to overcome aerodynamic drag is

\(\begin{aligned}\mathscr{P}_{p}=& F_{D_{p}} V_{p} \\=& 1.71 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~N} \times 100 \frac{\mathrm{km}}{\mathrm{hr}} \times 1000 \frac{\mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{km}} \\& \times \frac{\mathrm{hr}}{3600 \mathrm{~s}} \times \frac{\mathrm{W} \cdot \mathrm{s}}{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{m}} \\\mathscr{P}_{p}=& 47.5 \mathrm{~kW}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad \mathscr{P}_{p}}\end{aligned}\)

 

This problem illustrates a common phenomenon in aerodynamics: Above a certain minimum Reynolds number the drag coefficient of an object usually approaches a constant—that is, becomes independent of the Reynolds
number.

Hence, in these situations we do not have to match the Reynolds numbers of the model and prototype in order for them to have the same drag coefficient—a considerable advantage. However, the SAE Recommended Practices [8] suggests \(Re ≥ 2 × 10^6\) for truck and bus testing.
