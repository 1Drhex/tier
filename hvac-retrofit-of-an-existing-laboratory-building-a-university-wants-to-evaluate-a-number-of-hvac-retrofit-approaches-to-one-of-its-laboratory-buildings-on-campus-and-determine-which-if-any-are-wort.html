HVAC Retrofit of an Existing Laboratory Building

A university wants to evaluate a number of HVAC retrofit approaches to one of its laboratory buildings on campus and determine which, if any, are worth implanting. The building contains 71,000 gross square feet of laboratory and office space. Of that area, roughly 38,000 square feet are assignable. Three design options are considered:

• Option A: Variable-air-volume (VAV) distribution system for non-laboratory areas
• Option B: VAV distribution system for laboratory areas
• Option C: Combination of Option A and Option B

Costs for each design option are summarized in Table 6.2.

Solution

Given: Financial data as summarized in Table 6.2, MARR = 7%, and N = 30 years.
Find: Which design option to choose.

For each option, we calculate both the capital cost and equivalent annual energy savings over the base case. Since typical operating and maintenance costs are already included in the annual utility costs, we are basically looking at whether or not each option provides sufficient energy savings to cover the capital costs.

• Option A:

\(CR\left(7\%\right) _{A}\) = ($500,000 – 0$)(A/P, 7%, 30) + (0.07)($0)
= $40,293
Energy savings = $245,186 per year
AE(7%) = $245,186 – $40,293 = $204,893

• Option B:

\(CR\left(7\%\right) _{B}\) = ($650,000 – 0$)(A/P, 7%, 30) + (0.07)($0)
= $52,381
Energy savings = $75,370 per year
AE(7%) = $75,370 – $52,381 = $22,989

• Option C:

\(CR\left(7\%\right) _{C}\) = ($875,000 – 0$)(A/P, 7%, 30) + (0.07)($0)
= $70,513
Energy savings = $378,364 per year
AE(7%) = $378,364 – $70,513 = $307,851

The analysis shows that Option C has the highest first cost but also the most energy savings, resulting in the maximum annual net savings. Therefore, over the long run, Option C comes out the most cost effective.

Comments: Many practitioners may be tempted to pick Option A with the notion that it has the shortest payback period of 2.04 years, whereas Option C has a 2.31 per year payback period. Therefore, Option A could be seen as the best choice, balancing first-cost impacts with energy savings. But clearly, this violates the very principle of LCCA—do not just compare different design options based on the speed of recovery of the initial investment.
