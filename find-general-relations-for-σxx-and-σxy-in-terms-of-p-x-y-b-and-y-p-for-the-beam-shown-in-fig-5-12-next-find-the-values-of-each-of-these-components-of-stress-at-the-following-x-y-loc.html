Guest Question.

Find general relations for \(\sigma _{xx}\) and \(\sigma _{xy}\) in terms of P, x, y, b, and \(\bar{y}_{p}\) for the beam shown in Fig. 5.12. Next find the values of each of these components of stress at the following (x, y) locations: \((0, h/2), (0, 0), (L/2, h/2), (L/2, 0), (L, h/2), (L, 0), (L, –h/2).\)

Admin
Solution

From the free-body diagram of the whole structure, we see that

\( \sum{F_{x}}=0\rightarrow R_{x}=0,\)

 

\( \sum{F_{y}}=0\rightarrow R_{y}=P,\)

 

\( \sum{M_{z}})_{A}=0\rightarrow -M_{A}-PL=0,\)

 

\( M_{A}=-PL.\)

Next, let us find the internal forces and moments. From a free-body diagram of the parts, we obtain FIGURE 5.12 Similar to Fig. 5.11 except for a cantilever beam having a rectangular cross section and subjected to an applied transverse load P at the end. Beam Bending and Column Buckling

\( \sum{F_{x}}=0\rightarrow f=0,\)

 

\( \sum{F_{y}}=0\rightarrow V=P,\)

 

\( \sum{M_{z}})_{A}=0\rightarrow PL-Vx+M_{z}=0,\)

 

\( M_{z}=Vx-PL=P(x-L).\)

From Eq. (5.23), therefore,

\( \sigma _{xx}=-\frac{M_{z}(x)y}{I_{zz}}.\) (5.23)

 

 

\( \sigma _{xx}=-\frac{M_{z}y}{I_{zz}}=-\frac{P(x-L)y}{I_{zz}} \forall x,y,z,\)

where

\( I_{zz}=\int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{}\int_{-h/2}^{h/2}{y^{2}dydz}=\frac{1}{12}bh^{3}.\)

From Eq. (5.30),

\( \sigma _{xy})_{ave}=\left(\frac{V(x)Q(y)}{I_{zz}b} \right),\) (5.30)

 

 

\( \sigma _{xy})_{ave}=\frac{VQ}{I_{zz}b}=\frac{PQ}{I_{zz}b},\)

where

\( Q=\int{ydA_{p}}=\int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{}\int_{y}^{h/2}{y dy dz}=\int_{-b/2}^{b/2}{\left(\frac{h^{2}}{8}-\frac{y^{2}}{2} \right)dz }=\frac{b}{2}\left(\frac{h^{2}}{4}-y^{2} \right).\)

Hence,

\(\sigma _{zz}=\frac{12P(x-L)y}{bh^{3}}\) and \(\sigma _{xy}=\frac{12P\left[b(h^{2}/4-y^{2})/2\right] }{b^{2}h^{3}}.\)

Now, we can compute \(\sigma _{xx}\) and \(\sigma _{xy}\) at various points of interest p. For example, for the points indicated, let the point \((x, y)=(0, h/2); (0, 0); (L/2, h/2); (L/2,0); (L, h/2); (L, 0); (L, -h/2).\)

Whereas we have determined the values of the components \(\sigma _{xx}\) and \(\sigma _{xy}\) at the indicated points, we emphasize that \(\sigma _{xx}\) and \(\sigma _{xy}\) can (should) be found at all (x, y). Indeed, at each (x, y), we should also compute the maximum normal stress \(\sigma ^{\prime }_{xx})_{\max }\) if we expect failure to occur due to normal stresses (e.g., in brittle materials) or the maximum shear stress \(\sigma ^{\prime }_{xy})_{\max }\) if we expect failure to occur in shear (e.g., in ductile materials). Because the principal and maximum shear stresses both depend on \(\sigma _{xx}\) and \(\sigma _{xy},\) it is clearly more challenging to identify the point in the structure where an absolute maximum stress exists. For this reason and given the availability of computers and color graphics, many simply create color contour plots of the principal or maximum shear stresses to aid failure analysis.
