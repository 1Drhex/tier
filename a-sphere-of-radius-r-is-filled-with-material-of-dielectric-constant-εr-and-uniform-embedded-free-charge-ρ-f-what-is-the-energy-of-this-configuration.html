Guest Question.

A sphere of radius R is filled with material of dielectric constant \(\epsilon_{r}\) and uniform embedded free charge \(\rho f \). What is the energy of this configuration?

Admin
Solution

From Gauss’s law (in the form of Eq. 4.23), the displacement is

\(\oint{D}.da=Q_{fenc}, \) (4.23)

\(\pmb{D}(r)=\begin{cases} \frac{\rho f}{3}\pmb{r} (r\lt R),\\\frac{\rho f}{3}\frac{R^{3}}{r^{2}}\hat{\pmb{r}} (r\gt R).\end{cases} \)

So the electric field is

\(E(r)=\begin{cases} \frac{\rho f}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\pmb{r} (r\lt R),\\\frac{\rho f}{3\epsilon _{0}}\frac{R^{3}}{r^{2}}\hat{\pmb{r}} (r\gt R).\end{cases} \)

The purely electrostatic energy (Eq. 4.55) is

\(W=\frac{\epsilon _{0}}{2}\int{E^{2}d\tau } \) (4.55)

\(W_1=\frac{\epsilon _{0}}{2}\left[\left(\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}} \right)^{2}\int_{0}^{R}{r^{2}4\pi r^{2}dr+\left(\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}} \right)^{2}R^{6} }\int_{R}^{\infty }{\frac{1}{r^{4}} }4\pi r^{2}dr \right]=\frac{2\pi}{9\epsilon _{0}}\rho ^{2}_{f}R^{5}\left(\frac{1}{5\epsilon ^{2}_{r}}+1 \right) . \)

But the total energy (Eq. 4.58) is

\(W=\frac{1}{2}\int{D.Ed\tau }, \) (4.58)

\(W_{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left[\left(\frac{\rho_{f}}{3} \right) \left(\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}} \right)\int_{0}^{R}{r^{2}4\pi r^{2}dr+\left(\frac{\rho _{f}R^{3}}{3} \right)\left(\frac{\rho _{f}R^{3}}{3\epsilon _{0}} \right) }\int_{R}^{\infty }{\frac{1}{r^{4}} }4\pi r^{2}dr \right]=\frac{2\pi}{9\epsilon _{0}}\rho ^{2}_{f}R^{5}\left(\frac{1}{5\epsilon _{r}}+1 \right) .\)

 

Notice that \(W_{1} < W_{2}\)—that’s because \(W_{1}\) does not include the energy involved in stretching the molecules.
Let’s check that \(W_{2}\) is the work done on the free charge in assembling the system. We start with the (uncharged, unpolarized) dielectric sphere, and bring in the free charge in infinitesimal installments (dq), filling out the sphere layer by layer. When we have reached radius \(\acute{r}\), the electric field is

\(E(r)=\begin{cases} \frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\pmb{r} (r\lt \acute{r}), \\ \frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\frac{\acute{r}^{3}}{r^{2}}\hat{\pmb{r}} (\acute{r}\lt r\gt R),\\ \frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\frac{\acute{r}^{3}}{r^{2}}\hat{\pmb{r}} (r\gt R).\end{cases} \)

The work required to bring the next dq in from infinity to \(\acute{r}\) is

\(dW=-dq\left[\int_{\infty }^{R}{E.dI}+\int_{R}^{\acute{r}}{E.dI} \right] \\=-dq\left[\frac{\rho _{f}\acute{r}^{3}}{3\epsilon_{0}}\int_{\infty }^{R}{\frac{1}{r^{2}} }dr+\frac{\rho _{f}\acute{r}^{3}}{3\epsilon_{0}\epsilon _{r}}\int_{R}^{\acute{r}}{\frac{1}{r^{2}} dr} \right]\\=\frac{\rho _{f}\acute{r}^{3}}{3\epsilon_{0}}\left[\frac{1}{R}+\frac{1}{\epsilon _{r}}\left(\frac{1}{\acute{r}}-\frac{1}{R} \right) \right] dq. \)

This increases the radius ( \(\acute{r}\)):

\(dq=\rho _{f}4\pi\acute{r}^{2}d\acute{r},\)

so the total work done, in going from \(\acute{r}=0 to \acute{r}=R\), is

\(W=\frac{4\pi\rho ^{2}_{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}}\left[\frac{1}{R}\left(1-\frac{1}{\epsilon _{r}} \right)\int_{0}^{R}{\acute{r}^{5}d\acute{r}}+\frac{1}{\epsilon _{r}}\int_{0}^{R}{\acute{r}^{4}d\acute{r}} \right] \\=\frac{2\pi}{9\epsilon _{0}}\rho ^{2}_{f}R^{5}\left(\frac{1}{5\epsilon _{r}}+1 \right)=W_{2}.\)√

 

Evidently the energy “stored in the springs” is

\(W_{spring}=W_{2}-W_{1}=\frac{2\pi}{45\epsilon _{0}\epsilon ^{2}_{r}}\rho ^{2}_{f}R^{5}(\epsilon _{r}-1). \)

I would like to confirm this in an explicit model. Picture the dielectric as a collection of tiny proto-dipoles, each consisting of +q and −q attached to a spring of constant k and equilibrium length 0, so in the absence of any field the positive and negative ends coincide. One end of each dipole is nailed in position (like the nuclei in a solid), but the other end is free to move in response to any imposed field. Let \(dτ\) be the volume assigned to each proto-dipole (the dipole itself may occupy only a small portion of this space).
With the field turned on, the electric force on the free end is balanced by the spring force;\(^{19}\) the charges separate by a distance \(d: qE = kd. \) In our case

\(E=\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\pmb{r}. \)

The resulting dipole moment is \(p = qd\), and the polarization is\( P = p/dτ,\) so

\(k=\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}d^{2}}P rd\tau . \)

The energy of this particular spring is

\(dW_{spring}=\frac{1}{2}kd^{2} =\frac{\rho _{f}}{6\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}P rd\tau ,\)

and hence the total is
\(W_{spring}=\frac{\rho _{f}}{6\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\int{} P rd\tau .\)

Now

\(\pmb{P} = \epsilon _{0}χ_e\pmb{E} = \epsilon _{0}χ_e\frac{\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\pmb{r}=\frac{(\epsilon _{r}-1)\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{\epsilon r}}\pmb{r}, \)

so

\(W_{spring}=\frac{\rho _{f}}{6\epsilon _{0}\epsilon _{r}}\frac{(\epsilon _{r}-1)\rho _{f}}{3\epsilon _{r}}4\pi\int_{0}^{R}{r^{4}dr}=\frac{2\pi}{45 \epsilon _{0}\epsilon ^{2}_{r}}\rho ^{2}_{f}R^{5}(\epsilon _{r}-1) , \)

and it works out perfectly.
