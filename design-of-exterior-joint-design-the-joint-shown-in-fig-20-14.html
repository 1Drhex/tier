Guest Question.

Design of exterior joint. Design the joint shown in Fig. 20.14.

Admin
Solution

As discussed in Chapter 18, a joint must be detailed so that the beam and column bars do not interfere with each other and so that placement and consolidation of the concrete are practical. Bar placement is shown in Fig. 20.14.

Development of the spandrel beam flexural steel within the joint is checked based on the requirement that the column dimension be at least 20 times the bar diameter of the largest bars. This requirement is met for the No. 10 (No. 32) bars used as top reinforcement.

\(20 \times 1.27 = 25.4 in. \lt 36 in.\)

The flexural steel in the normal beam must be anchored within the core of the column based on Eq. (20.28), but not less than \(8d_b\) or 6 in. For the No. 9 (No. 29) top bars, Eq. (20.26) controls

\(\frac{A_{sh}}{sb_c} = 0.2 k_f k_n \frac{P_u}{f_{yt} A_{ch}}\) (20.26)

\(ℓ_{dh} = \frac{f_y d_b}{65 λ \sqrt{f_c^\prime}}\) (20.28)

\(ℓ_{dh} = \frac{60,000 \times 1.128}{65 \times 1.0 \sqrt{4000}} = 16.5\) in.

The same holds true for the No. 8 (No. 25) bottom bars, which must also be anchored in tension (ACI Code 18.8.2) because lateral loading will subject the beam to both positive and negative bending moments at the exterior joint.

\(ℓ_{dh} =\frac{60,000 \times 1.0}{65 \times 1.0 \sqrt{4000}} = 14.6\) in.

Since \(3.25ℓ_{dh}\) is not available for the top bars and \(2.5ℓ_{dh}\) is not available for the bottom bars, all flexural steel from the normal beam must be anchored using hooks, not straight reinforcement, extended to the far face of the column core, as shown in Fig. 20.14b.

To check the shear strength of the joint, the shear forces acting on the joint must be calculated based on a stress of \(1.25f_y\) in the flexural reinforcement. By inspection, shear in the plane of the spandrel beam will control.

The tensile force in the negative reinforcement, including the bars within the effective width of the flange, is
\(T_1 = 1.25 \times 7.59 \times 60 = 569\) kips

For an effective depth of 33.4 in. () and a depth of stress block \(a = 569/(0.85 \times 4 \times 27) = 6.20\) in., the moment due to negative bending is

\(M^- =\frac{569}{12}\left(33.4-\frac{6.20}{2} \right)=1437\) ft-kips

For positive bending on the other side of the column,
\(T_2 = 1.25 \times 5.00 \times 60 = 375\) kips

\(a = \frac{375}{0.85 \times 4 \times 54} = 2.04\) in.

\(M^+ =\frac{375}{12}\left(33.4-\frac{2.04}{2} \right) = 1012\) ft-kips

The column shear corresponding to the sum of the moments \(M^+\) and \(M^-\) and based on the free body of the column between assumed midheight inflection points, as shown in Fig. 20.16a, is \(V_{col}\) = (1437 + 1012)/12 = 204 kips. The shear forces acting on the joint are shown in Fig. 20.16b, and the factored joint shear is

\(V_u = T_1 + T_2 − V_{col} = 569 + 375 − 204 = 740\) kips

For a joint confined on three faces with an effective cross-sectional area \(A_j = 36 \times 36 = 1296 in^2\), the nominal and design capacities of the joint are

\(V_n = 15 λ \sqrt{f_c^\prime} A_j = \frac{15 \times 1.0 \sqrt{4000} \times 1296}{1000} = 1229\) kips

\(ϕV_n = 0.85 \times 1229 = 1045\) kips

Since \(ϕV_n \gt V_u\), the joint is satisfactory for shear.

Because the joint is not confined on all four sides, the transverse reinforcement in the column must be continued, unchanged, through the joint.
