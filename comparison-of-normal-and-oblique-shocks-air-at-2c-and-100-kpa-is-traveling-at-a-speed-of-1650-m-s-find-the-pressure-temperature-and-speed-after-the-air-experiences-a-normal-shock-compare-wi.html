COMPARISON OF NORMAL AND OBLIQUE SHOCKS

Air at -2°C and 100 kPa is traveling at a speed of 1650 m/s. Find the pressure, temperature, and speed after the air experiences a normal shock. Compare with the pressure, temperature, and speed (and find the deflection angle θ) if the air instead experiences an oblique shock at angle β = 30°.

Admin
Solution

Given: Air flow with:


\(p_1 = 100\) kPa
\(T_1 = -2\)°C
\(V_1 = 1650\) m/s
Find: Downstream pressure, temperature, and speed if it experiences (a) a normal shock and (b) an oblique shock
at angle \(β = 30\)°. Also find the deflection angle \(θ\).

Solution:
(a) Normal shock
First compute the speed of sound,

\(c_{1}=\sqrt{k R T_{1}}=\sqrt{1.4 \times 287 \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}} \times 271 \mathrm{~K} \times \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^{2} \cdot \mathrm{N}}}=330 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

Then the upstream Mach number is

\(M_{1}=\frac{V_{1}}{c_{1}}=\frac{1650 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{330 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=5.0\)

 

From the normal-shock flow functions, Eqs. 13.20, at \(M_{1}=5.0\)

 

\(\begin{gathered}M_{2}^{2}=\frac{M_{1}^{2}+\frac{2}{k-1}}{\frac{2 k}{k-1} M_{1}^{2}-1}\\\quad\\\frac{p_{0_{2}}}{p_{0_{1}}}=\frac{\left[\frac{\frac{k+1}{2} M_{1}^{2}}{1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1}^{2}}\right]^{k /(k-1)}}{\left[\frac{2 k}{k+1} M_{1}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{k+1}\right]^{1 /(k-1)}} \\\quad\\\frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}=\frac{\left(1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1}^{2}\right)\left(k M_{1}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{2}\right)}{\left(\frac{k+1}{2}\right)^{2} M_{1}^{2}} \\\quad\\\frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}=\frac{2 k}{k+1} M_{1}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{k+1} \\\quad\\\frac{\rho_{2}}{\rho_{1}}=\frac{V_{1}}{V_{2}}=\frac{\frac{k+1}{2} M_{1}^{2}}{1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1}^{2}}\end{gathered}\) (13.20 a, b, c, d, e)

 

\(M_1\) \(M_2\) \(p_2/p_1\) \(T_2/T_1\) \(V_2/V_1\)
5.0 0.4152 29.00 5.800 0.2000

From these data

\(\begin{aligned}&T_{2}=5.800 T_{1}=(5.800) 271 \mathrm{~K}=1572 \mathrm{~K}=1299^{\circ} \mathrm{C} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad T_2}\\\quad\\&p_{2}=29.00 p_{1}=(29.00) 100 \mathrm{kPa}=2.9 \mathrm{MPa} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_2}\\\quad\\&V_{2}=0.200 V_{1}=(0.200) 1650 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}=330 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad V_2}\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) Oblique shock
First compute the normal and tangential components of velocity,

\(\begin{aligned}&V_{1_{n}}=V_{1} \sin \beta=1650 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \times \sin 30^{\circ}=825 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \\\quad\\&V_{1_{t}}=V_{1} \cos \beta=1650 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \times \cos 30^{\circ}=1429 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

 

Then the upstream normal Mach number is

\(M_{1_{n}}=\frac{V_{1_{n}}}{c_{1}}=\frac{825 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{330 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=2.5\)

From the oblique-shock flow functions, Eqs. 13.48, at \(M_{1_{n}}\)=2.5

 

\(\begin{gathered}M_{2_{n}}^{2}=\frac{M_{1_{n}}^{2}+\frac{2}{k-1}}{\frac{2 k}{k-1} M_{1_{n}}^{2}-1} \\\quad\\\frac{p_{0_{2}}}{p_{0_{1}}}=\frac{\left[\frac{\frac{k+1}{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}}{1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}}\right]^{k /(k-1)}}{\left[\frac{2 k}{k+1} M_{1_{n}}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{k+1}\right]^{1 /(k-1)}} \\\quad\\\frac{T_{2}}{T_{1}}=\frac{\left(1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}\right)\left(k M_{1_{n}}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{2}\right)}{\left(\frac{k+1}{2}\right)^{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}} \\\quad\\\frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}=\frac{2 k}{k+1} M_{1_{n}}^{2}-\frac{k-1}{k+1}\\\quad\\\frac{\rho_{2}}{\rho_{1}}=\frac{V_{1_{n}}}{V_{2_{n}}}=\frac{\frac{k+1}{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}}{1+\frac{k-1}{2} M_{1_{n}}^{2}}\end{gathered}\) (13.48 a, b, c, d, e)

 

\(M_{1_n}\) \(M_{2_n}\) \(p_2/p_1\) \(T_2/T_1\) \(V_{2_n}/V_{1_n}\)
2.5 0.5130 7.125 2.138 0.300

 

From these data

\(\begin{aligned}T_{2} &=2.138 T_{1}=(2.138) 271 \mathrm{~K}=579 \mathrm{~K}=306^{\circ} \mathrm{C} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad T_2}\\\quad\\p_{2} &=7.125 p_{1}=(7.125) 100 \mathrm{kPa}=712.5 \mathrm{kPa} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad p_2}\\\quad\\V_{2_{n}} &=0.300 V_{1_{n}}=(0.300) 825 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}=247.5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s} \\\quad\\V_{2_{t}} &=V_{1_{t}}=1429 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad V_2}\end{aligned}\)

 

The downstream velocity is given by the Pythagorean sum of the velocity components,

\(V_{2}=\sqrt{\left(V_{2_{n}}^{2}+V_{2_{t}}^{2}\right)}=\sqrt{\left(247.5^{2}+1429^{2}\right)} \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}=1450 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

Note that

\(c_{2}=\sqrt{k R T_{2}}=\sqrt{1.4 \times 287 \frac{\mathrm{N} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{K}} 579 \mathrm{~K} \times \frac{\mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}}{\mathrm{s}^{2} \cdot \mathrm{N}}}=482 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

so that the downstream Mach number is

\(M_{2}=\frac{V_{2}}{c_{2}}=\frac{1450 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{482 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=3.01\)

 

Although the downstream normal Mach number must be subsonic, the actual downstream Mach number could be subsonic or supersonic (as in this case).
The deflection angle can be obtained from Eq. 13.46b

\(V_{2_{n}}=V_{2} \sin (\beta-\theta)\)

or

\(\begin{aligned}\theta &=\beta-\sin ^{-1}\left(\frac{V_{2_{n}}}{V_{2}}\right)=30^{\circ}-\sin ^{-1}\left(\frac{247.5}{1450}\right) \\\quad\\&=30^{\circ}-9.8^{\circ}=20.2^{\circ}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad \theta}\end{aligned}\)

 

This Example illustrates:

  • That an oblique shock involves deflection of the flow through angle θ
  • Use of normal shock functions for solution of oblique shock problems.
  • The important result that for a given supersonic flow an oblique shock will always be weaker than a normal shock, because \(M_{1_n} < M_1\).
  • That while \(M_{2_n} < 1\) always, \(M_2\) can be subsonic or supersonic (as in this case).

The Excel workbook for oblique shocks is convenient for performing these calculations. (Alternatively, the normal shock relations Excel add-ins, available on the Web site, are useful for these calculations.)
