Experimental Determination of Coefficient of Static Friction

A cube of mass 12.0 kg sits on a horizontal surface. To estimate the coefficient of static friction, you apply a steadily increasing horizontal force and note its value just before the box begins to slide on the surface. When the horizontal force is 68.0 N, the cube begins to slide. What is the coefficient of static friction between the surfaces?

Admin
Solution

We start by drawing an FBD showing the forces on the cube (Figure 5-28). There are four forces: the applied horizontal force, \(\overrightarrow{ F }\); the force of gravity, \(\overrightarrow{ F }_{ g }\); the normal force, exerted on the crate by the surface, \(\overrightarrow{ N }\); and the force of static friction, \(\vec{f}_{ S }\). Since the applied force, \(\overrightarrow{ F }\) , is attempting to move the box to the right, the force of static friction, \(\vec{f}_{ S }\), opposes this and therefore must point to the left, as shown in Figure 5-28.

We define the x-axis horizontally, with positive to the right, and the y-axis vertically, with the positive direction upward. Newtonâ€™s second law is applied in each axis direction. For the y-direction there is no acceleration, so we have the following, where N is the magnitude of the normal force:

 

\(\sum \overrightarrow{ F }_{y}=m \overrightarrow{ a }_{y}=0\)

 

\(N \hat{ j }-m g \hat{ j }=0\)

 

\(N=m g\) (1)

 

Therefore, in this case, the normal force is simply the following (we keep an extra significant digit at this stage but will round to the correct number in the final answer):

 

\(N=(12.0 kg )\left(9.81 m / s ^{2}\right)=117.7 N\) (2)

 

Until the point just prior to motion, there is also no acceleration in the x-direction. If we let F and \(f_{ s }^{\max }\) represent the magnitudes of the applied horizontal force and the maximum static frictional force, we have

 

\(\sum \overrightarrow{ F }_{x}=m \overrightarrow{ a }_{x}=0\)

 

\(F \hat{ i }-f_{ s }^{\max } \hat{ i }=0\)

 

\(f_{ s }^{\max }=F\) (3)

 

Therefore, the maximum static friction force will equal the applied horizontal force just prior to motion, 68.0 N.

Finally, we can use the definition of the coefficient of static friction, Equation 5-6, to find the value of the coefficient:

 

\(f_{ s }^{\max }=\mu_{ s } N\)

 

\(\mu_{ s }=\frac{f_{ s }^{\max }}{N}=\frac{68.0 N }{117.7 N }=0.578\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

The value we obtain for the coefficient of static friction is reasonable (see Table 5-1, later in the chapter, for some typical values of the coefficient of static friction). Since \(\mu_{ s }\) is the ratio of two forces, it does not have dimensional units. This method, perhaps with a spring scale, can be used to experimentally estimate the coefficient of static friction between two surfaces.
