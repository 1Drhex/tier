Guest Question.

For two spin-1/2 particles you can construct symmetric and antisymmetric states (the triplet and singlet combinations, respectively). For three spin-1/2 particles you can construct symmetric combinations (the quadruplet, in ), but no completely anti-symmetric configuration is possible.

(a) Prove it. Hint: The “bulldozer” method is to write down the most general linear combination:
\( \chi(1,2,3)=a|\uparrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+b|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+c|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+d|\uparrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle+e|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+f|\downarrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+g|\downarrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+h|\downarrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle \).

What does antisymmetry under \( 1 \leftrightarrow 2 \) tell you about the coefficients? (Note that the eight terms are mutually orthogonal.) Now invoke antisymmetry under \( 2 \leftrightarrow 3 \) .

(b) Suppose you put three identical noninteracting spin-1/2 particles in the infinite square well. What is the ground state for this system, what is its energy, and what is its degeneracy? Note: You can’t put all three in the position state \( \psi_{1} \) (why not?); you’ll need two in \( \psi_{1} \) and the other in \( \psi_{2} \) .

But the symmetric configuration

\( \left[\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)+\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)+\psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)\right] \).

is no good (because there’s no antisymmetric spin combination to go with it), and you can’t make a completely antisymmetric combination of those three terms. …In this case you simply cannot construct an antisymmetric product of a spatial state and a spin state. But you can do it with an appropriate linear combination of such products. Hint: Form the Slater determinant () whose top row is \( \psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1}, \psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right)|\downarrow\rangle_{1} , \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1} \).

(c) Show that your answer to part (b), properly normalized, can be written in the form

\( \Phi(1,2,3)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}[\Phi(1,2) \phi(3)-\Phi(1,3) \phi(2)+\Phi(2,3) \phi(1)] \).

where \( \Phi(i, j) \) is the wave function of two particles in the n = 1 state and the singlet spin configuration,

\( \Phi(i, j)=\psi_{1}\left(x_{i}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{j}\right) \frac{\left|\uparrow_{i} \downarrow j\right\rangle-\left|\downarrow_{i} \uparrow_{j}\right\rangle}{\sqrt{2}} \) (5.35).

and \( \phi(i) \) is the wave function of the ith particle in the n = 2 spin up state: \( \phi(i)=\psi_{2}\left(x_{i}\right)\left|\uparrow_{i}\right\rangle . \text { Noting that } \Phi(i, j) \) is antisymmetric in \( i \leftrightarrow j \text {, check that } \Phi(1,2,3) \) is antisymmetric in all three exchanges \( (1 \leftrightarrow 2,2 \leftrightarrow 3 \text {, and } 3 \leftrightarrow 1) \).

Solution

(a) \( \chi(2,1,3)=a|\uparrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+b|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+c|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+d|\downarrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+e|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+f|\uparrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle+g|\downarrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+h|\downarrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle . \).

\( \chi(2,1,3)=-\chi(1,2,3) \Rightarrow a=b=g=h=0, \quad e=-c, \quad f=-d \).

(Because the individual states are orthogonal, the associated coefficients must be equal.)

\( \chi(1,3,2)=a|\uparrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+b|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+c|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+d|\uparrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle+e|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+f|\downarrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+g|\downarrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle+h|\downarrow \downarrow \downarrow\rangle \).

\( \chi(1,3,2)=-\chi(1,2,3) \Rightarrow a=d=e=h=0, \quad c=-b, \quad g=-f \).

So all 8 coefficients are zero, and χ(1, 2, 3) = 0.

(b) You can’t put all 3 in \( \psi_{1} \) because this would be a symmetric combination, and we just showed that there is no antisymmetric spin state to go with it.

\( \left|\begin{array}{lll} \psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1} & \psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right)|\downarrow\rangle_{1} & \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1} \\ \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{2} & \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right)|\downarrow\rangle_{2} & \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{2} \\ \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{3} & \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\downarrow\rangle_{3} & \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{3} \end{array}\right| \).

\( =\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle+\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle+\psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle \)

\( -\psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle-\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle-\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle \).

The energy is \( E=\frac{\pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{2 m a^{2}}\left(1^{2}+1^{2}+2^{2}\right)=\frac{3 \pi^{2} \hbar^{2}}{m a^{2}} \), Degeneracy is d = 2, (we could have started with \( \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)|\downarrow\rangle_{1} \) instead of \( \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1} \).)

(c) For normalization, divide by \( \sqrt{6} \) (since the terms are orthonormal):

\( \Phi(1,2,3)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\left[\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)(|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle-|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle)+\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right)(|\downarrow \uparrow \uparrow\rangle-|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle)\right. \)

\( \left.+\psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)(|\uparrow \uparrow \downarrow\rangle-|\uparrow \downarrow \uparrow\rangle)\right] \).

\( =\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\left[\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right)\left(|\uparrow\rangle_{1}|\downarrow\rangle_{2}-|\downarrow\rangle_{1}|\uparrow\rangle_{2}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{3}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{3}+\psi_{1}\left(x_{1}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)\left(|\downarrow\rangle_{1}|\uparrow\rangle_{3}-|\uparrow\rangle_{1}|\downarrow\rangle_{3}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{2}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{2}\right. \)

\( \left.+\psi_{1}\left(x_{2}\right) \psi_{1}\left(x_{3}\right)\left(|\uparrow\rangle_{2}|\downarrow\rangle_{3}-|\downarrow\rangle_{2}|\uparrow\rangle_{3}\right) \psi_{2}\left(x_{1}\right)|\uparrow\rangle_{1}\right] \).

\( =\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}[\Phi(1,2) \phi(3)-\Phi(1,3) \phi(2)+\Phi(2,3) \phi(1)] \).

\( \Phi(2,1,3)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}[\Phi(2,1) \phi(3)-\Phi(2,3) \phi(1)+\Phi(1,3) \phi(2)] \).

\( =\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}[-\Phi(1,2) \phi(3)-\Phi(2,3) \phi(1)+\Phi(1,3) \phi(2)]=-\Phi(1,2,3) \),

and the same goes for the other two permutations.
