Guest Question.

A 600-kVA, single-phase transformer with 0.012 pu resistance and 0.06 pu reactance is connected in parallel with a 300-kVA transformer with 0.014 pu resistance and 0.045 pu reactance to share a load of 800 kVA at 0.8 pf lagging. Find how they share the load (a) when both the secondary voltages are 440 V and (b) when the open-circuit secondary voltages are respectively 445 V and 455 V.

Admin
Solution

(a) The pu impedances expressed on a common base of 600 kVA are
\(\bar{Z_{1} } = 0.012 + j 0.06 = 0.061\angle 79°\)
\(\bar{Z_{2} } =2\left(0.014 + j 0.045\right) = 0.094 \angle 73°\)
\(\bar{Z_{1} }+\bar{Z_{2} }= 0.04 + j 0.15 = 0.155\angle 75°\)
The load is
\(\bar{S_{L} }=800\left(0.8 – j 0.6\right) = 800\angle –37° \)kVA
From Eqs (3.86)
\(\bar{S_{1} }=\frac{\bar{Z_{2} }}{\bar{Z_{1} }+\bar{Z_{2} }} \bar{S_{L} }\)
and (3.87)
\(\bar{S_{2} }=\frac{\bar{Z_{1} }}{\bar{Z_{1} }+\bar{Z_{2} }} \bar{S_{L} }\)
\(\bar{S_{1} }=800 \angle –37° \times \frac{0.094\times 73°}{0 .155\angle 75°} = 485 \angle –39° = 377 – j 305.2\)
\(\bar{S_{2} }=800 \angle –37° \times \frac{0.061\times 79°}{0. 155\angle 75°} = 315 \angle –33° = 264 – j171.6\)
It may be noted that the transformers are not loaded in proportion to their ratings. At a total load of 800 kVA,
the 300 kVA transformer operates with \(5\%\) overload because of its pu impedance (on common kVA base) being less than twice that of the 600 kVA transformer.
The maximum kVA load the two transformers can feed in parallel without any one of them getting overloaded
can now be determined. From above it is observed that the 300 kVA transformer will be the first to reach its fullload as the total load is increased. In terms of magnitudes
\(\frac{0.061}{0.155} S_{L}(max) = S_{2} (rated) = 300\) kVA
\(S_{L}(max)=\frac{300\times 0.155}{0.061} = 762.3\) kV A
while the sum of the ratings of the two transformers is 900 kVA. This is consequence of the fact that the transformer
impedances (on common base) are not in the inverse ratio of their ratings.
(b) In this case it is more convenient to work with actual ohmic impedances. Calculating the impedances referred to secondary
\(\bar{Z_{1} }(actual)=\left(0.012 + j 0.06\right) \times \frac{440}{\frac{600\times 1000}{440} } = 0.0039 + j 0.0194 = 0.0198 \angle 79°\)
\(\bar{Z_{2} }(actual)=\left(0.028 + j 0.09\right) \times \frac{440}{\frac{600\times 1000}{440} } =0.009 + j 0.029 = 0.0304\angle 73°\)
\(\bar{Z_{1} }+\bar{Z_{2} }= 0.0129 + j 0.0484 = 0.05\angle 75°\)
The load impedance \(\bar{Z_{L} }\) must also be estimated. Assuming an output voltage on load of 440 V,
\(\bar{V_{L} } ^{*} \bar{I_{L} } \times 10^{-3} =\left(\bar{V_{L} } ^{2}/ \bar{Z_{L} } \right) \times 10^{-3} = 800 \angle –37°\)
\(\bar{Z_{L} }=\frac{\left(440\right) ^{2} }{800\times 10^{3}\angle -37° } =0.242 \angle 37° = 0.1936 + j 0.1452\)
From Eqs (3.98)
\(\bar{I_{2}}=\frac{\bar{E_{2}}\bar{Z_{1}}-\left(\bar{E_{1}}-\bar{E_{2}}\right)\bar{Z_{L}} }{\bar{Z_{1}}\bar{Z_{2}}+\bar{Z_{L}}\left(\bar{Z_{1}}+\bar{Z_{2}}\right) } \)
and (3.99)
\(\bar{I_{1}}=\frac{\bar{E_{1}}\bar{Z_{2}}-\left(\bar{E_{1}}-\bar{E_{2}}\right)\bar{Z_{L}} }{\bar{Z_{1}}\bar{Z_{2}}+\bar{Z_{L}}\left(\bar{Z_{1}}+\bar{Z_{2}}\right) } \)
\(\bar{I_{1}}=\frac{445\times 0. 0304 \angle 73° -10 \times 0.242\angle 37°}{0 .0198 \angle 79° \times 0.0304\angle 73°+ 0.242\angle 37°\times 0.05\angle 75°} =940\angle -34° \)A
\(\bar{I_{2}}=\frac{445- 0. 0198 \angle 79° -10 \times 0.242\angle 37°}{0 .0198 \angle 79° \times 0.0304\angle 73°+ 0.242\angle 37°\times 0.05\angle 75°} =883\angle -44°\) A
The corresponding kV As are
\( \bar{S_{1}}=440\times 940\times 10^{-3}\angle-34° =413.6\angle -34°\)
\( \bar{S_{2}}=440\times 883\times 10^{-3}\angle-4444° =388\angle -44°\)
The total output power will be
\(413.6 cos 34° + 388 \cos 44°\)
This is about \(3\%\) less than \(800\times 0.8 = 640\) kW required by the load because of the assumption of the value of the output voltage in order to calculate the load impedance.
The secondary circulating current on no-load is
\(\frac{\left(\bar{E_{1}}-\bar{E_{2}}\right) }{\left|\bar{Z_{1}}+\bar{Z_{2}}\right| } =\frac{-10}{0.05}=-200\)A
which corresponds to about 88 kVA and a considerable waste as copper-loss.
