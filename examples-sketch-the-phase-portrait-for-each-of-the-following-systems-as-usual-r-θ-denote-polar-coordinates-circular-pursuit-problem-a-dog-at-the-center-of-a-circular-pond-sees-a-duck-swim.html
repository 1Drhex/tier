Guest Question.

Examples

Sketch the phase portrait for each of the following systems. (As usual, \(r, \theta\) denote polar coordinates.)

(Circular pursuit problem) A dog at the center of a circular pond sees a duck swimming along the edge. The dog chases the duck by always swimming straight toward it. In other words, the dog’s velocity vector always lies along the line connecting it to the duck. Meanwhile, the duck takes evasive action by swimming around the circumference as fast as it can, always moving counterclockwise.

a) Assuming the pond has unit radius and both animals swim at the same constant speed, derive a pair of differential equations for the path of the dog. (Hint: Use the coordinate system shown in Figure 2 and find equations for \(d R / d \theta\) and \(d \phi / d \theta\).) Analyze the system. Can you solve it explicitly? Does the dog ever catch the duck?

b) Now suppose the dog swims \(k\) times faster than the duck. Derive the differential equations for the dog’s path.

c) If \(k=\frac{1}{2}\), what does the dog end up doing in the long run?

Note: This problem has a long and intriguing history, dating back to the mid-1800s at least. It is much more difficult than similar pursuit problems – there is no known solution for the path of the dog in part (a), in terms of elementary functions. See Davis (1962, pp. 113-125) and Nahin (2007) for nice analyses and guides to the literature.

Solution

a)

Since the dog and the duck are moving at the same speed, they will both move the same amount in the time it takes \(\theta\) to change by \(\Delta \theta\). Thus the problem can be done using \(\Delta \theta\) instead of \(\Delta t\) in the derivation.

During a small amount of time \(\Delta t\), the length of the segment \(R\) decreases from the dog swimming a length of \(\Delta \theta\) and increases from the duck moving along the perimeter of the circle also by a length of \(\Delta \theta\). We’ll eventually be taking a limit, so we can make our lives easier with some approximations.

The line segment along the perimeter of the circle is approximately the length of the arc that the line segment spans, and the angle bordering \(\phi\) is approximately a right angle. Using these approximations, the law of cosines, and a trick for taking a limit, we can arrive at a differential equation for \(R\).

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta R & \approx-\Delta \theta+c-R \\&=-\Delta \theta+\sqrt{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}-2 \Delta \theta R \cos \left(\phi+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)}-R \\&=-\Delta \theta+\frac{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}-2 \Delta \theta R \cos \left(\phi+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)-R^{2}}{\sqrt{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}-2 \Delta \theta R \cos \left(\phi+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)}+R} \\&=-\Delta \theta+\frac{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+2 \Delta \theta R \sin (\phi)}{\sqrt{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}+2 \Delta \theta R \sin (\phi)}+R} \\\frac{\Delta R}{\Delta \theta} &=-1+\frac{\Delta \theta+2 R \sin (\phi)}{\sqrt{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}+2 \Delta \theta R \sin (\phi)}+R} \\\underset{\Delta \theta \rightarrow 0}{\lim}\frac{\Delta R}{\Delta \theta} &=-1+\frac{\Delta \theta+2 R \sin (\phi)}{\sqrt{(\Delta \theta)^{2}+R^{2}+2 \Delta \theta R \sin (\phi)}+R} \\R^{\prime} &=-1+\frac{2 R \sin (\phi)}{\sqrt{R^{2}}+R}=-1+\sin (\phi)\end{aligned}\)

For the \(\phi^{\prime}\) equation we can use some basic geometry.

Next we have to remember that \(\triangle a c b\) is similar to \(\triangle d c e\) (angles subtended by the same arc are equal), meaning the ratio between \(\overline{a b}\) and \(\overline{d e}\) is equal to the ratio of \(\overline{a c}\) and \(\overline{c e}\).

Approximating \(\overline{a b}\) as \(\widehat{a b}=\Delta \theta\) and \(\overline{d e}\) as \(\widehat{d e}\)

\(\overline{a b}: \overline{d e}=\overline{a c}: \overline{c d} \Rightarrow \widehat{d e} \approx \frac{2 \cos (\phi)-R}{R} \Delta \theta\)

Next we have to remember that the arc made by the inscribed angle \(\phi\) is half of the arc of the central angle (inscribed angle theorem).

Now putting it all together, the change in the central angle \(2 \Delta \phi\) is from an increase of arc \(\widehat{d e}\) and a decrease of arc \(\Delta \theta\).

\(\begin{aligned}2 \Delta \phi &=\widehat{d e}-\Delta \theta \approx \frac{2 \cos (\phi)-R}{R} \Delta \theta-\Delta \theta=\frac{2 \cos (\phi)-2 R}{R} \Delta \theta \\R \frac{\Delta \phi}{\Delta \theta} & \approx \cos (\phi)-R \\\underset{\Delta \theta \rightarrow 0}{\lim} R \frac{\Delta \phi}{\Delta \theta} &=R \phi^{\prime}=\cos (\phi)-R\end{aligned}\)

As for whether or not the dog catches the duck, the answer is no. The proof is difficult enough that it is the subject of a published paper. Please take a look at Applications of Center Manifolds by Keith Promislow if you’re interested.

b)

Repeating the derivation now with the speed of the dog as \(k \Delta \theta\)

\(\begin{aligned}R^{\prime} &=-k+\sin (\phi) \\R \phi^{\prime} &=\cos (\phi)-R\end{aligned}\)

c)

Assuming there is a limit cycle

\(\begin{gathered}R^{\prime}=-k+\sin (\phi)=0 \Rightarrow \phi=\arcsin (k) \\R \phi^{\prime}=\cos (\phi)-R=0 \Rightarrow R=\cos (\phi)=\sqrt{1-k^{2}}\end{gathered}\)

So for \(k=\frac{1}{2}\) the dog will endlessly chase the duck while asymptotically approaching a circle of radius \(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) as a limit cycle.
