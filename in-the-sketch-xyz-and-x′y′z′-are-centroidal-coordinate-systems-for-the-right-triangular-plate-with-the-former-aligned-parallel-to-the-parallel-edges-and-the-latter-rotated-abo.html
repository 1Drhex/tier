Guest Question.

In the sketch \(x y z\) and \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) are centroidal coordinate systems for the right triangular plate, with the former aligned parallel to the parallel edges and the latter rotated about the \(z\) axis through angle \(\theta\). The mass of the plate is \(400 \mathrm{~kg}\). Because the plate is bisected by the \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime}\) plane, \(I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=0 .\) There are values of \(\theta\) for which \(I_{x^{\prime} y^{\prime}}=0\), thereby making \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) principal axes for the plate. Determine \(\theta\) and the corresponding principal moments of inertia by using a rotation transformation. Then perform the same evaluation by using the properties of the inertia ellipsoid.

Admin
Solution

The primary intent here is to lessen the abstract nature of the ellipsoid of inertia by seeing its features for an actual body. We may find the inertia properties relative to \(x y z\) by letting the length \(a\) of the right triangular prism in the Appendix approach zero. The \(z\) and \(x\) axes are swapped with the corresponding axes in the tabulation, but the \(y\) axis here is reversed from the sense described there. This means that we should interchange the subscripts of the inertia properties to match the present definitions and also set \(I_{x y}\) and \(I_{y z}\) equal to the negative of the tabulated expressions. Thus we have

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{x x}=\frac{1}{36}(400)(2)\left(3.6^{2}\right)=288, \quad I_{y y}=\frac{1}{36}(400)(2)\left(2.4^{2}\right)=128, \\\\I_{z z}=I_{x x}+I_{y y}=416 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \\\\I_{x y}=+\frac{1}{36}(400)(3.6)(2.4)=96, \quad I_{y z}=I_{x z}=0.\end{array}\)

It is worth noting \(I_{z z}\) was evaluated as the sum of the other moments of inertia. This is identically true for any thin flat slab when the origin of \(x y z\) lies in the plane of the slab and the \(z\) axis is normal to that plane. The rotation transformation from \(x y z\) to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) is

\([R]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \theta & \sin \theta & 0 \\\\-\sin \theta & \cos \theta & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right] \)

We wish to determine all inertia properties with respect to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\), so we implement the full rotation transformation:

\(\left[I^{\prime}\right]=[R]\left[\begin{array}{ccc}288 & -96 & 0 \\\\-96 & 128 & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 416\end{array}\right][R]^{\mathrm{T}} \)

 

\(=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}288(\mathrm{c} \theta)^{2}+128(\mathrm{~s} \theta)^{2}-192 \mathrm{~s} \theta \mathrm{c} \theta & 96-192(\mathrm{c} \theta)^{2}-160 \mathrm{~s} \theta \mathrm{c} \theta & 0 \\\\96-192(\mathrm{c} \theta)^{2}-160 \mathrm{~s} \theta \mathrm{c} \theta & 288(\mathrm{~s} \theta)^{2}+128(\mathrm{c} \theta)^{2}+192 \mathrm{~s} \theta \mathrm{c} \theta & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 416\end{array}\right],\)

where \(\mathrm{s} \theta\) and \(\mathrm{c} \theta\) are abbreviations for \(\sin \theta\) and \(\cos \theta .\) We find the value of \(\theta\) that makes \(I_{x^{\prime} y^{\prime}}\) vanish by applying the identities for the sine and cosine of \(2 \theta\) to the \((1,2)\) element of \(\left[I^{\prime}\right]\), which leads to

\(-96 \cos (2 \theta)-80 \sin (2 \theta)=0 \Longrightarrow \theta=64.903^{\circ},-25.097^{\circ}.\)

These two possible angles differ by \(90^{\circ}\), so they merely correspond to alternate labeling of the \(x^{\prime}\) and \(y^{\prime}\) axes. The principal moments of inertia for \(\theta=64.903^{\circ}\) are

\(I_{x^{\prime} x^{\prime}}=I_{1}=83.04, \quad I_{y^{\prime} y^{\prime}}=I_{2}=332.96, \quad I_{z^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=I_{3}=416 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}.\)

Solution of this problem by use of the ellipsoid of inertia barely resembles the preceding operations. Because we know that \(z\) is a principal axis, we focus on the \(x y\) plane by writing the defining equation, Eq. (5.2.30), for the case in which \(z=0\), which gives

\(I_{x x} x^{2}+I_{y y} y^{2}+I_{z z} z^{2}-2 I_{x y} x y-2 I_{x z} x z-2 I_{y z} y z=1\) (5.2.30)

 

\(288 x^{2}+128 y^{2}-192 x y=1.\)

This is an ellipse. The multivalued nature of the value of \(y\) as a function of \(x\) makes it convenient to use polar coordinates. By definition, the distance from the origin to a point on the ellipsoid of inertia is \(\rho\), so we define

\(x=\rho \cos \phi, \quad y=\rho \sin \phi.\)

The equation for the \(x y\) ellipse then becomes

\(\rho^{2}\left[288(\cos \phi)^{2}+128(\sin \phi)^{2}-192(\sin \phi)(\cos \phi)\right]=1.\)

We obtain the graph of this ellipse by solving the preceding equation for the value of \(\rho\) corresponding to numerous values of \(\phi\) covering a \(2 \pi\) range.

The major and minor diameters depicted in this graph could be located by several methods, each of which is based on the same property. In general, \(\rho\) has an extreme value at the principal axes. Hence the major and minor diameters correspond to points at which \(\rho\) has a maximum or minimum value. We could locate both lines by visually searching for the points on the ellipse that are farthest and closest to the origin. Another procedure is to search through a table of values of \(\phi\) and \(\rho\) for the points where \(\rho\) is largest and smallest. Both procedures have limited precision. A mathematical approach is to use calculus to locate values of \(\phi\) for which \(d \rho / d \phi=0 .\) To expedite such an evaluation we introduce the identities for \(\cos (2 \phi)\) and \(\sin (2 \phi)\) into the equation for the \(x y\) ellipse, which gives

\(\rho^{2}(208+80 \cos 2 \phi-96 \sin 2 \phi)=1.\)

Implicit differentiation of this equation with respect to \(\phi\) yields

\(\begin{array}{l}2 \rho \frac{d \rho}{d \phi}(208+80 \cos 2 \phi-96 \sin 2 \phi) \\\\\quad+\rho^{2}(-160 \sin 2 \phi-192 \cos 2 \phi)=0.\end{array} \)

Setting \(d \rho / d \phi=0\) shows that the principal axes correspond to \(160 \sin 2 \phi+\) \(192 \cos 2 \phi=0\), which is the same as the condition identified from the rotation transformation. Thus the values of \(\phi\), and the corresponding \(\rho\), are

\(\begin{array}{l}\phi=64.903^{\circ} \Longrightarrow \rho_{1}=0.109740, \\\\\phi=-25.097^{\circ} \Longrightarrow \rho_{2}=0.054803.\end{array} \)

The principal moments of inertia are the reciprocals of the squares of the extreme values of \(\rho\), so

\(\large I_{1}=\frac{1}{\rho_{1}^{2}}=83.04, \quad I_{2}=\frac{1}{\rho_{2}^{2}}=332.96 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2} .\)

These are the same principal values as those found earlier. The key aspect to bear in mind is that both procedures, explicitly setting the offdiagonal elements of \(\left[I^{\prime}\right]\) to zero and geometrically analyzing the inertia ellipsoid, were expedited by the fact that \(z\) was known to be a principal axis. Consequently, only a single angle needed to be evaluated. Either approach would be substantially more difficult to implement if multiple direction angles needed to be found. Also worth noting is the fact that the orientation of the principal axes has no simple geometrical explanation, unlike the case for symmetrical bodies.
