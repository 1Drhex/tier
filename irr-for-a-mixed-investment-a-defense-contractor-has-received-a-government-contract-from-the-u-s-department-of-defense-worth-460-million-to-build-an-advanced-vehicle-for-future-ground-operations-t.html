IRR for a Mixed Investment

A defense contractor has received a government contract from the U.S. Department of Defense worth $460 million, to build an advanced vehicle for future ground operations. The DOD will pay $50 million when the contract is signed, another $360 million at the end of the first year, and the $50 million balance at the end of second year. The expected cash outflows required to produce these weapon systems are estimated to be $150 million now, $100 million during the first year, and $218 million during the second year. The firmâ€™s MARR is 25% for this type of technically challenging project. The cash flow is as follows.

Solution

Given: Cash flow shown and MARR = 25%.
Find: (a) Compute the NPW, (b) i*, and (c) RIC at MARR = 25%. Also, determine whether to accept the project.

(a) PW(25%) = -$100 + $260(P/F, 25%, 1) – $168(P/F, 25%, 2)
= $0.48(or $480,000) \(\gt\) 0

(b) Since the project has a two-year life, we may solve the net-present-worth equation directly via the quadratic formula:

\(-\$100 + \frac{\$260}{\left(1+i^{*} \right) } – \frac{\$168}{\left(1+i^{*} \right)^{2} }= 0\)

If we let \(X = \frac{1}{\left(1+i^{*} \right) }\) , we can rewrite the preceding expression as

\(-100 + 260X -168X^{2} = 0\)

Solving for \(X\) gives \(X\) = 5/6 and 5/7, or i* = 20% and 40%. As shown in Figure 7.8, the NPW profile intersects the horizontal axis twice: once at 20% and again at 40%. The investment is obviously not a simple one; thus, neither 20% nor 40% represents the true internal rate of return of this government project.

(c) As calculated in (b), the project has multiple rates of return. The project fails the net investment test, as shown in Table 7.5.

At n = 0, there is a net investment to the firm, so the project balance expression becomes

\(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{0} = – \$100\) .

The net investment of $100 million that remains invested internally grows at the interest rate i for the next period. With the receipt of $260 in year 1, the project balance becomes

\(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{1} = – \$100\left(1+i\right)+\$260\)

= 160 – 100i

At this point, we do not know whether \(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{1}\) is positive or negative; we want to know this in order to test for net investment. It depends on the value of i, which we want to determine. Therefore, we need to consider two situations:

(1) i \(\lt\) 1.6, and (2) i \(\gt\) 1.6.

Case 1: \(i\lt 1.6\rightarrow PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{1}\gt 0\) .

Since this indicates a positive balance, the cash released from the project would be returned to the firmâ€™s investment pool to grow at the MARR until it is required back in the project. By the end of year 2, the cash placed in the investment pool would have grown at the rate of 25% [to (160 – 100i)(1 + 0.25)] and should be available to meet the required investment in the amount of $168 in period 2. This also happens to be the last period and then the terminal balance must be zero

\(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{2}\) = (160 – 100i)(1 + 0.25) – 168
= -125i + 32
= 0

Solving for i yields

RIC = IRR = 0.2560, or 25.60% 7 25%

Case 2: \(i\gt 1.6\rightarrow PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{1}\lt 0\) .

The firm is still in an investment mode. Therefore, the balance at the end of year 1 that remains invested will grow at the rate i for the next period. With the investment of $168 required in year 2 and the fact that the net investment must be zero at the end of the project life, the balance at the end of year 2 should be

\(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{2}\) = (160 – 100i)(1 + i) – 168

\(= -8 + 60i – 100i^{2}\)

= 0

Solving for i gives

IRR = 0.20 or 0.40 \(\lt\) 1.6

which violates the initial assumption that i \(\gt\) 1.6. Therefore, case 1 is the only correct situation. Since it indicates that

IRR(= RIC) \(\gt\) MARR, the project is acceptable, resulting in the same decision as obtained in part (a) by applying the NPW criterion.

Comments: In this example, we have seen that the RIC is dependent on the choice of a MARR of 25%. We could easily develop a functional relationship between RIC and MARR. Knowing that case 1 is the correct situation, we simply substitute 25% with MARR to obtain the equation

\(PB\left(i,25\%\right) _{2}\) = (160 – 100i)(1 + MARR) – 168
= 0

i = RIC = \(\frac{160 MARR – 8}{100 \left(1 + MARR\right) }\)

As you can see in Figure 7.9, as MARR increases, the investment return (RIC) increases. If we set MARR = i* (either 20% or 40%), then we see RIC = i*, indicating that the interest rates that make the projectâ€™s NPW equal to zero become the true IRR. Graphically, if we plot the function between RIC and MARR, the true IRR is the point at which the function crosses the 45Â° line. It is the point at which the firm just breaks even on the investment. It becomes clear that the project just breaks even if MARR is equal to 20% or 40%, and then it is a profitable investment if MARR is greater than 20% but less than 40%. You will see that this is the only range in which the projectâ€™s NPW becomes positive, giving the same accept signal.
