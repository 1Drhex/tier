Guest Question.

In a plant location near a furnace, a net radiant energy flux of \(800 \enspace W/m^{2} \) is incident on a vertical metal surface 3.5 m high and 2 m wide. The metal is insulated on the back side and painted black so that all the incoming radiation is lost by free convection to the surrounding air at \( 30^{◦} C \). What average temperature will be attained by the plate?

Admin
Solution

We treat this problem as one with constant heat flux on the surface. Since we do not know the surface temperature, we must make an estimate for determining \(T_{f} \) and the air properties. An approximate value of h for free-convection problems is \(10 W/m^{2} ·^{ ◦} C \), and so, approximately,

\(\Delta T=\frac{q_{w} }{h}≈\frac{800}{10} =80^{◦} C \)

Then

\(T_{f} ≈\frac{80}{2} +30=70^{◦} C =343\; K \)

At \( 70^{◦} C \) the properties of air are

\(ν = 2.043×10^{−5} m^{2} /s \qquad β=\frac{1}{T_{f} } =2.92×10^{−3} K^{−1 } \)

 

\(k = 0.0295 \; W/m· ^{◦} C \qquad Pr=0.7 \)

From Equation (7-30)

\(Gr^{∗} _{x}=Gr_{x} Nu_{x}=\frac{gβq_{w} x^{4} }{kν^{2} } \hspace{30 pt} [7-30] \)

, with x=3.5 m,

\(Gr^{∗} _{x}=\frac{gβq_{w} x^{4} }{kν^{2} } =\frac{(9.8)(2.92×10^{−3} )(800)(3.5)^{4} }{(0.0295)(2.043×10^{−5} )^{2} }=2.79×10^{14} \)

We may therefore use Equation (7-32)

\(Nu_{x} =0.17 (Gr^{∗} _{x}Pr)^{1/4} \qquad 2×10^{13}<Gr^{∗} _{x}\; Pr <10^{16} ; q_{w} =const \hspace{30 pt} [7-32]\)

to evaluate \(h_{x} \):

\(h_{x}=\frac{k}{x} (0.17)(Gr^{∗} _{x} Pr)^{1/4} =\frac{0.0295}{3.5}(0.17)(2.79×10^{14} ×0.7)^{1/4} =5.36 W/m^{2}· ^{◦} C\enspace \; [0.944 \; Btu/h · ft^{2}·^{◦} F] \)

In the turbulent heat transfer governed by Equation (7-32), we note that

\(Nu_{x}= \frac{hx}{k}∼(Gr^{∗} _{x} )^{1/4} ∼(x^{4} )^{1/4} \)

or \(h_{x } \) does not vary with x, and we may take this as the average value. The value of \(h=5.41\; W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C \) is less than the approximate value we used to estimate \(T_{f} \) . Recalculating \(\Delta T \), we obtain:

\(\Delta T=\frac{q_{w} }{h}=\frac{800}{5.36}=149^{◦} C \)

Our new film temperature would be

\(T_{f} =30+\frac{149}{2}=104.5^{◦} C \)

At \( 104.5^{◦} C \) the properties of air are

 

\(ν = 2.354×10^{−5} m^{2} /s \hspace{30 pt} β=\frac{1}{T_{f} } =2.65×10^{−3} /K \)

 

\(k = 0.0320 \> W/m· ^{◦} C \quad Pr=0.695 \)

Then

\(Gr^{∗} _{x}= \frac{(9.8)(2.65×10^{−3} )(800)(3.5)^{4} }{(0.0320)(2.354×10^{−5} )^{2} } =1.75×10^{14} \)

and \(h_{x} \) is calculated from

\(h_{x} =\frac{k}{x} (0.17)(Gr^{∗} _{x} Pr)^{1/4}=\frac{(0.0320)(0.17)}{3.5}[(1.758×10^{14} )(0.695)]^{1/4}= 5.17\; W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C\; [−0.91 Btu/h · ft^{2} · ^{◦} F] \)

 

Our new temperature difference is calculated as

\(\Delta T =(T_{w} −T_{∞} )_{av}=\frac{q_{w} }{h}=\frac{800}{5.17} =155^{◦} C \)

The average wall temperature is therefore

\(T_{w,av} =155+30=185^{◦} C \)

Another iteration on the value of \(T_{f} \) is not warranted by the improved accuracy that would result.
