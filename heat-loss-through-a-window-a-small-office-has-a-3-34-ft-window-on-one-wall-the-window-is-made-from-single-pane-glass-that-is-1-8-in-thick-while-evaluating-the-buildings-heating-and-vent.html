Heat Loss through a Window A small office has a 3 × 4 ft window on one wall. The window is made from single-pane glass that is \(\frac{1}{8} \text { in. }\) thick. While evaluating the building’s heating and ventilation system, an engineer needs to calculate the heat loss through the window on a winter day. Although the temperature difference of the air inside and outside the office is much larger, the two surfaces of the glass differ by only 3 ºF. In units of watts, what quantity of heat is lost through the window each hour?

To calculate the conductive flow of heat through the window, we apply Equation (7.9). The thermal conductivity of glass is listed in Table 7.5 as 0.50 (Btu/h)/(ft ? ºF). After calculating the energy loss in the USCS dimensions of Btu, we will convert to the SI unit of watt by applying the conversion factor 1 Btu 51055 J from Table 7.1.

 

\(Q=\frac{\kappa A \Delta t}{L}\left(T_{ h }-T_{1}\right)\) (Equation 7.9)

Solution

To keep the dimensions in Equation (7.9) consistent, we first convert the window’s thickness as follows:

 

\(Q=\frac{\kappa A \Delta t}{L}\left(T_{ h }-T_{1}\right)\) (Equation 7.9)

 

\(\begin{aligned}L &=(0.125 \text { in. })\left(\frac{1}{12} \frac{ ft }{ in }\right) \\&=1.042 \times 10^{-2} ft\end{aligned}\)

 

The heat loss in 1 h becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q &\left.=\frac{\left(0.50( Btu / h ) /\left( ft \cdot{ }^{\circ} F \right)\right)(3 ft )(4 ft )(1 h )}{1.042 \times 10^{-2} ft }\left(3^{\circ} F \right) \leftarrow\left[Q=\frac{\kappa A \Delta t}{L}\left(T_{\eta}-T\right)\right)\right] \\&=1728\left(\frac{ Btu / h }{ ft \cdot{ }^{\circ} F }\right)\left( ft ^{2}\right)( h )\left(\frac{1}{ ft }\right)\left({ }^{\circ} F \right) \\&=1728 Btu\end{aligned}\)

 

Expressed in the SI, this quantity of heat is equivalent to

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q &=(1728 Btu )\left(1055 \frac{ J }{ Btu }\right) \\&=1.823 \times 10^{6}( Btu )\left(\frac{ J }{ Btu }\right) \\&=1.823 \times 10^{6} J\end{aligned}\)

 

Because the heat flows continuously during a 1 h period, the average rate at which heat is lost becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{Q}{\Delta t} &=\left(1.823 \times 10^{6} \frac{ J }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{1}{3600} \frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=506.4\left(\frac{ J }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=506.4 \frac{ J }{ s } \\&=506.4 W\end{aligned}\)

 

where we have used the definition of the derived SI unit watt.
