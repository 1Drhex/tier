Guest Question.

Calculate the total takeoff distance for our Gulfstream-like airplane at standard sea level, assuming a takeoff gross weight of 73,000 lb. The design features of the are the same as those given in , with the added information that the wingspan is 75 ft. Assume that the variation of engine thrust with during takeoff is given by Eq. (6.74), where \(k_{1}^{*}=27,700 \mathrm{lb}, k_{2}^{*}=21.28 \mathrm{lb} \cdot \mathrm{s} / \mathrm{ft}\), and \(\left.k_{3}^{*}=1.117 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{lb} \cdot \mathrm{s} / \mathrm{ft}\right)^{2}\). The height of the wing above the ground during the ground roll is \(5.6 \mathrm{ft}\). Assume the runway is dry concrete, with \(\mu_{r}=0.04\).

Admin
Solution

The total takeoff distance, as shown in Fig. \(6.12\), is the sum of \(s_{g}\) and \(s_{a}\). Let us first calculate the ground roll \(s_{g}\), using Eq. \((6.90)\). The information needed in Eq. (6.90) is obtained as follows.
The liftoff velocity \(V_{L O}\) is chosen to be equal to \(1.1 V_{\text {stall}}\). Our Gulfstream-like airplane is equipped with single-slotted Fowler flaps; based on the data in Table 5.3 for flaps deflected in the takeoff position, \(\left(C_{L}\right)_{\max } / \cos \Lambda=2.1\). As mentioned in , the wing sweep angle \(\Lambda=27^{\circ} 40^{\prime}\). Hence, \(\left(C_{L}\right)_{\max }=2.1 \cos 27^{\circ} 40^{\prime}=1.86 .\) From Eq. (5.7),
\(V_{\text {stall }}=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\rho_{\infty}} \frac{W}{S} \frac{1}{\left(C_{L}\right)_{\max }}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(76.84)}{(0.002377)(1.86)}}=186.4 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s}\)
Hence,
\(V_{L O}=1.1 V_{\text {stall }}=1.1(186.4)=205.1 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s}\)
From Eq. \((6.85)\), evaluated at \(V_{\infty}=0.7 V_{L O}\),
\(K_{T}=\left(\frac{T}{W}-\mu_{r}\right)_{0.7 v_{L O}}\)
To evaluate \(T\) at \(0.7 V_{\text {LO }}\), use Eq. (6.74) as follows.

\(\begin{gathered}T=k_{1}^{*}-k_{2}^{*} V_{\infty}+k_{3}^{*} V_{\infty}^{2}=27,700-21.28 V_{\infty}+1.117 \times 10^{-2} V_{\infty}^{2} \\\text { Since } 0.7 V_{\text {LO }}=0.7(205.1)=143.6 \mathrm{ft} / \mathrm{s}, \\T=27,700-21.28(143.6)+1.117 \times 10^{-2}(143.6)^{2} \\=27,700-3,055.8+230.3=24,875 \mathrm{lb}\end{gathered}\)
Thus,
\(K_{T}=\left(\frac{T}{W}-\mu_{r}\right)_{0.7 v_{L O}}=\frac{24,875}{73,000}-0.04=0.301\)
For the evaluation of \(K_{A}\) as given by Eq. (6.86), the following information is needed. From , \(C_{D .0}=0.015\). The increase in the zero-lift drag coefficient due to the extended landing gear is estimated in Eq. \((6.76)\), where we will assume that \(K_{\mathrm{uc}}\) is approximately \(4.5 \times 10^{-5}\) for the case of moderate flap deflection (see previous discussion in Section 6.7.1 ). Equation (6.76) is repeated here:
\(\Delta C_{D, 0}=\frac{W}{S} K_{\mathrm{uc}} m^{-0.215}\)
where \(W / S\) is in units of newtons per square meter and \(m\) is in units of kilograms. Since \(1 \mathrm{lb}=4.448 \mathrm{~N}, 1 \mathrm{ft}=0.3048 \mathrm{~m}\), and \(1 \mathrm{lb}_{m}=0.4536 \mathrm{~kg}\), we have
\(\begin{aligned}\frac{W}{S} &=\frac{73,000 \mathrm{lb}}{950 \mathrm{ft}^{2}} \frac{4.448 \mathrm{~N}}{1 \mathrm{lb}}\left(\frac{1 \mathrm{ft}}{0.3048 \mathrm{~m}}\right)^{2}=3,679 \mathrm{~N} / \mathrm{m}^{2} \\m &=73,000 \mathrm{lb}_{m} \frac{0.4536 \mathrm{~kg}}{1 \mathrm{lb}_{m}}=33,113 \mathrm{~kg}\end{aligned}\)

Therefore,
\(\Delta C_{D, 0}=\frac{W}{S} K_{\mathrm{uc}} m^{-0.215}=(3,679)\left(4.5 \times 10^{-5}\right)(33,113)^{-0.215}=0.0177\)
It is interesting to note that the zero-lift drag coefficient is more than doubled by the extended landing gear. The value of \(k_{1}\) in Eq. (6.86) was given in ;\( k_{1}=0.02\). Also given in is \(e=0.9 .\) In Eq. (6.86), \(G\) is obtained from Eq. (6.77).
\(G=\frac{(16 h / b)^{2}}{1+(16 h / b)^{2}}=\frac{[16(5.6 / 75)]^{2}}{1+[16(5.6 / 75)]^{2}}=\frac{1.427}{2.427}=0.588\)
Finally, as discussed in Section 6.7.1, we will assume that \(C_{L}=0.1\) during the ground roll. Therefore, from Eq. (6.86),
\(\begin{aligned}K_{A} &=-\frac{\rho_{\infty}}{2(W / S)}\left[C_{D, 0}+\Delta C_{D .0}+\left(k_{1}+\frac{G}{\pi \mathrm{eAR}}\right) C_{L}^{2}-\mu_{r} C_{L}\right] \\&=\frac{-0.002377}{2(76.48)}\left\{0.015+0.0177+\left[0.02+\frac{0.588}{\pi(0.9)(5.92)}\right](0.1)^{2}-(0.04)(0.1)\right\} \\&=-\left(1.547 \times 10^{-5}\right)(0.0327+0.00055-0.004) \\&\left.=-(1.547) \times 10^{-5}\right)(0.02925)=-4.525 \times 10^{-7}\end{aligned}\)
In the above calculation for \(K_{A}\), note that the contribution due to zero-lift drag
\(C_{D, 0}+\Delta C_{D, 0}=0.0327\)
is much larger than that for drag due to lift
\(\left(k_{1}+\frac{G}{\pi \mathrm{eAR}}\right) C_{L}^{2}=0.00055\)
From Eq. (6.90), letting \(N=3\), we have

\(\begin{aligned}s_{g} &=\frac{1}{2 g K_{A}} \ln \left(1+\frac{K_{A}}{K_{T}} V_{\text {LO }}^{2}\right)+N V_{\text {LO }} \\&=\frac{1}{2(32.2)\left(-4.525 \times 10^{-7}\right)} \ln \left[1+\frac{-4.525 \times 10^{-7}}{0.301}(205.1)^{2}\right]+3(205.1) \\&=2,242+615=2,857 \mathrm{ft}\end{aligned}\)
To calculate the airborne segment of the total takeoff distance \(s_{a}\), that is, that distance covered over the ground while airborne necessary to clear a 35 -ft obstacle, we use Eqs. (6.98) to (6.100). From Eq. (6.98)
\(R=\frac{6.96\left(V_{\text {stall }}\right)^{2}}{g}=\frac{6.96(186.4)^{2}}{32.2}=7,510 \mathrm{ft}\)
From Eq. (6.99)
\(\theta_{\mathrm{OB}}=\cos ^{-1}\left(1-\frac{h_{\mathrm{OB}}}{R}\right)=\cos ^{-1}\left(1-\frac{35}{7,510}\right)=5.534^{\circ}\)
From Eq. (6.100)
\(s_{a}=R \sin \theta_{\mathrm{OB}}=7,510 \sin 5.534^{\circ}=724 \mathrm{ft}\)

Hence,
\(\text { Total takeoff distance }=s_{g}+s_{a}=2,857+724=3,581 \mathrm{ft}\)
It is interesting to compare the above calculation for \(s_{g}\) with the more approximate relation given by Eq. (6.95)
\(s_{g} \approx \frac{1.21(W / S)}{g \rho_{\infty}\left(C_{L}\right)_{\max }(T / W)}\)
Evaluating \(T / W\) at \(V_{\infty}=0.7 \mathrm{~V}_{L O}\), as carried cut earlier in this example, we have \(T / W=\) \(24,875 / 73,000=0.341\). Hence, Eq. (6.95) yields
\(s_{g} \approx \frac{(1.21)(76.84)}{(32.2)(0.002377)(1.86)(0.341)}=1,915 \mathrm{ft}\)
If we add the \(615 \mathrm{ft}\) covered during the rotation phase, which is neglected in Eq. (6.95), we have
\(s_{g}=1,915+615=2,530 \mathrm{ft}\)
This is to be compared with the value \(s_{g}=2,857 \mathrm{ft}\) obtained earlier. Hence, the greatly simplified expression given by Eq. (6.95) leads to a value for \(s_{g}\) that is only \(11 \%\) lower than that obtained by using our more precise analysis carried out above.
