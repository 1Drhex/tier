Guest Question.

BUOYANCY

A 15.0-kg solid gold statue is raised from the sea bottom (Fig. 12.13a). What is the tension in the hoisting cable (assumed massless) when the statue is (a) at rest and completely underwater and (b) at rest and completely out of the water?

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

In both cases the statue is in equilibrium and experiences three forces: its weight, the cable tension, and a buoyant force equal in magnitude to the weight of the fluid displaced by the statue (seawater in part (a), air in part (b)). Figure 12.13b shows the free-body diagram for the statue. Our target variables are the values of the tension in seawater (\(T_{\mathrm{sw}}\)) and in air (\(T_{\mathrm{air}}\)). We are given the mass \(m_{\mathrm{statue}}\), and we can calculate the buoyant force in seawater (\(B_{\mathrm{sw}}\)) and in air (\(B_{\mathrm{air}}\)) by using Archimedes’s principle.

EXECUTE:

(a) To find \(B_{\mathrm{sw}}\), we first find the statue’s volume V by using the density of gold from Table 12.1:

TABLE 12.1 Densities of Some Common Substances
Material \(\text { Density }\left(\mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\) Material \(\text { Density }\left(\mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\)
Air (1 atm, 20°C) 1.2 Iron, steel \(7.8 \times 10^{3}\)
Ethanol \(0.81 \times 10^{3}\) Brass \(8.61 \times 10^{3}\)
Benzen \(0.90 \times 10^{3}\) Copper \(8.9 \times 10^{3}\)
Ice \(0.92 \times 10^{3}\) Silver \(10.5 \times 10^{3}\)
Water \(1.00 \times 10^{3}\) Lead \(11.3 \times 10^{3}\)
Seawater \(1.03 \times 10^{3}\) Mercury \(13.6 \times 10^{3}\)
Blood \(1.06 \times 10^{3}\) Gold \(19.3 \times 10^{3}\)
Glycerine \(1.26 \times 10^{3}\) Platinum \(21.4 \times 10^{3}\)
Concrete \(2 \times 10^{3}\) White dwarf star \(10^{10}\)
Aluminum \(2.7 \times 10^{3}\) Neutron star \(10^{18}\)
\(V=\frac{m_{\text {statue }}}{\rho_{\text {gold }}}=\frac{15.0 \mathrm{~kg}}{19.3 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}}=7.77 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\)

The buoyant force \(B_{\mathrm{sw}}\) equals the weight of this same volume of seawater. Using Table 12.1 again:

\(\begin{aligned}B_{\mathrm{sw}} &=w_{\mathrm{sw}}=m_{\mathrm{sw}} g=\rho_{\mathrm{sw}} V g\\&=\left(1.03 \times 10^{3} \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(7.77 \times 10^{-4}\mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right) \\&=7.84 \mathrm{~N}\end{aligned}\)

The statue is at rest, so the net external force acting on it is zero. From Fig. 12.13b,

\(\begin{aligned}\sum F_{y} &=B_{\mathrm{sw}}+T_{\mathrm{sw}}+\left(-m_{\text {statue }}g\right)=0 \\T_{\mathrm{sw}} &=m_{\text {statue }} g-B_{\mathrm{sw}}=(15.0 \mathrm{~kg})\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)-7.84 \mathrm{~N} \\&=147 \mathrm{~N}-7.84\mathrm{~N}=139 \mathrm{~N}\end{aligned}\)

A spring scale attached to the upper end of the cable will indicate a tension 7.84 N less than the statue’s actual weight \(m_{\text {statue }} g=147 \mathrm{~N}\).
(b) The density of air is about 1.2 kg/\(m^3\), so the buoyant force of air on the statue is

\(\begin{aligned}B_{\text {air }} &=\rho_{\text {air }} V g \\&=\left(1.2 \mathrm{~kg} /\mathrm{m}^{3}\right)\left(7.77 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)\left(9.80\mathrm{~m}/ \mathrm{s}^{2}\right) \\&=9.1 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~N}\end{aligned}\)

This is negligible compared to the statue’s actual weight \(m_{\text {statue }} g=147 \mathrm{~N}\). So within the precision of our data, the tension in the cable with the statue in air is \(T_{\text {air }}=m_{\text {statue }} g=147 \mathrm{~N}\).

 

EVALUATE: Note that the buoyant force is proportional to the density of the fluid in which the statue is immersed, not the density of the statue. The denser the fluid, the greater the buoyant force and the smaller the cable tension. If the fluid had the same density as the statue, the buoyant force would be equal to the statue’s weight and the tension would be zero (the cable would go slack). If the fluid were denser than the statue, the tension would be negative: The buoyant force would be greater than the statue’s weight, and a downward force would be required to keep the statue from rising upward.
