Guest Question.

Consider the matrices \(A=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right)\) and \(B=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 2i & 0 \\ i & 0 & -5i \end{matrix} \right)\).

(a) Are A and B Hermitian? Calculate AB and BA and verify that Tr(AB) = Tr(BA) ; then calculate [A , B] and verify that Tr([A , B]) = 0.

\(b) Find the eigenvalues and the normalized eigenvectors of A. Verify that the sum of the eigenvalues of A is equal to the value of Tr(A) calculated in (a) and that the three eigenvectors form a basis.

(c) Verify that \(U^{\dagger } AU\) is diagonal and that \(U^{-1}=U^{\dagger}\), where U is the matrix formed by the normalized eigenvectors of A.

(d) Calculate the inverse of \(A^{′}=U^{\dagger}AU\) and verify that \(A^{′-1}\) is a diagonal matrix whose eigenvalues are the inverse of those of \(A^{′}\).

Admin
Solution

(a) Taking the Hermitian adjoints of the matrices A and B (see (2.188))

\(\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} A_{11} & A_{12} & A_{13} & … \\ A_{21} & A_{22} & A_{23} & … \\ A_{31} & A_{32} & A_{33} & … \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{matrix} \right\rgroup ^{\dagger } =\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} A^{*}_{11} & A^{*}_{21} & A^{*}_{31} & … \\ A^{*}_{12} & A^{*}_{22} & A^{*}_{32} & … \\ A^{*}_{13} & A^{*}_{23} & A^{*}_{33}& … \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{matrix} \right\rgroup\).

 

\(A^{\dagger }=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right), B^{\dagger }\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -2i & 0 \\ 3 & 0 & 5i \end{matrix} \right)\),

we see that A is Hermitian and B is not. Using the products

\(AB= \left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 21 \\ 1 & 2i & -5 \\ -i & -2 & 5i \end{matrix} \right),AB= \left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 3i & -3 \\ 0 & 2i & 2 \\ 7i & 5 & 5i \end{matrix} \right)\),

we can obtain the commutator

\(\left[A,B\right]=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -3i & 24 \\ 1 & 0 & -7 \\ -8i & -7 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\).

From (2.408)

\(AB= \left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 21 \\ 1 & 2i & -5 \\ -i & -2 & 5i \end{matrix} \right),AB= \left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 3i & -3 \\ 0 & 2i & 2 \\ 7i & 5 & 5i \end{matrix} \right)\),

we see that

\(Tr(AB)=7+2i+5i=7+7i=Tr(BA)\).

That is, the cyclic permutation of matrices leaves the trace unchanged; see (2.206).

\(Tr(\hat{A}\hat{B}\hat{C}\hat{D}\hat{E})=Tr(\hat{E}\hat{A}\hat{B}\hat{C}\hat{D})=Tr(\hat{D}\hat{E}\hat{A}\hat{B}\hat{C})=Tr(\hat{C}\hat{D}\hat{E}\hat{A}\hat{B})=\ldots.\)

On the other hand, (2.409)

\(\left[A,B\right]=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -3i & 24 \\ 1 & 0 & -7 \\ -8i & -7 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\).

shows that the trace of the commutator [A , B] is zero : Tr([A , B]) = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0.

(b) The eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A were calculated in (see (2.266),

\(a_{1}=7, a_{2}=\sqrt{2}, a_{3}=-\sqrt{2}\).

(2.268),

\(\mid a_{1}〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right)\).

(2.272),

\(\mid a_{2}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right\rgroup\).

(2.274)).

\(\mid a_{3}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right\rgroup\).

We have \(a_{1}=7, a_{2}=\sqrt{2}\) and \(a_{3}=-\sqrt{2}\):

\(\mid a_{1}〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right),\mid a_{2}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \end {matrix} \right\rgroup,\mid a_{3}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2 (2+\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right\rgroup\).

One can easily verify that the eigenvectors \(\mid a_{1}〉,\mid a_{2}〉 \) and \(\mid a_{3}〉\) are mutually orthogonal: \(〈a_{i}\mid a_{j}〉=\delta _{ij} \) where i, j = 1, 2, 3. Since the set of \(\mid a_{1}〉,\mid a_{2}〉 \) and \(\mid a_{3}〉\) satisfy the completeness condition

\(\sum\limits_{j=1}^{3}\mid a_{j}〉〈a_{j}\mid=\left(\begin {matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\),

and since they are orthonormal, they form a complete and orthonormal basis.
(c) The columns of the matrix U are given by the eigenvectors (2.411):

\(\mid a_{1}〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right),\mid a_{2}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \end {matrix} \right\rgroup,\mid a_{3}〉=\left\lgroup\begin{matrix} 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2 (2+\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right\rgroup\).

 

\(U=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right)\).

We can show that the product \(U^{\dagger } AU\) is diagonal where the diagonal elements are the eigenvalues of the matrix A; \(U^{\dagger } AU\) is given by

\(\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2} )}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2+\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end {matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{i(\sqrt {2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt {2})}} \end{matrix} \right)\)

 

\(=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\sqrt{2} \end{matrix} \right)\).

We can also show that \(U^{\dagger }U=1\):

\(\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2} )}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2+\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end {matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 (2-\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{i(\sqrt{2} -1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2} )}} \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\).

This implies that the matrix U is unitary: \(U^{\dagger}=U^{-1}\). Note that, from (2.413),

\(U=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end{matrix} \right)\).

we have \(\left|\det (U)\right| =\left|-i\right| =1\).

(d) Using (2.414)

\(\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2} )}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2+\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \end {matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -i \\ 0 & i & -1 \end{matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt {2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt{2})}} \\ 0 & \frac{i(\sqrt {2}-1)}{\sqrt{2(2-\sqrt{2})}} & -\frac{i(\sqrt{2}+1)}{\sqrt{2(2+\sqrt {2})}} \end{matrix} \right)\)

 

\(=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\sqrt{2} \end{matrix} \right)\).

we can verify that the inverse of \(A^{′}=U^{\dagger}AU\) is a diagonal matrix whose elements are given by the inverse of the diagonal elements of \(A^{′}\):

\(A^{′}=\left(\begin{matrix} 7 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\sqrt{2} \end{matrix} \right)\Longrightarrow A^{′-1}=\left (\begin{matrix} \frac{1}{7} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{matrix} \right)\).
