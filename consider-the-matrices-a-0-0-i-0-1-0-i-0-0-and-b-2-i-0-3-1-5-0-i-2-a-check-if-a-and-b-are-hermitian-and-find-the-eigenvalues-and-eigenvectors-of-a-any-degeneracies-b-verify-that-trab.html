Guest Question.

Consider the matrices \(A=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\) and \(B=\left(\begin{matrix} 2 & i & 0 \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \\ 0 & -i & -2 \end{matrix} \right)\).

(a) Check if A and B are Hermitian and find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A. Any degeneracies?

(b) Verify that Tr(AB) = Tr(BA), det(AB) = det(A) det(B), and \(\det (B^{\dagger } )=(\det (B))^{*}\).

(c) Calculate the commutator [A , B] and the anticommutator {A , B}.

(d) Calculate the inverses \(A^{-1},B^{-1}\), and \((AB)^{-1}\). Verify that \((AB)^{-1}= B^{-1}A^{-1}\).

(e) Calculate \(A^{2}\) and infer the expressions of \(A^{2n}\) and \(A^{2n+1}\). Use these results to calculate the matrix of \(e^{xA}\).

Admin
Solution

(a) The matrix A is Hermitian but B is not. The eigenvalues of A are \( a_{1}=-1\) and \(a_{2}=a_{3}=1\) and its normalized eigenvectors are

\(\mid a_{1}〉=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ i \end{matrix} \right), \mid a_{2}〉=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -i \end{matrix} \right), \mid a_{3}〉=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right)\). (2.429)

Note that the eigenvalue 1 is doubly degenerate, since the two eigenvectors \(\mid a_{2}〉\) and \(\mid a_{3}〉\) correspond to the same eigenvalue \(a_{2}=a_{3}=1\).

(b) A calculation of the products (AB) and (BA) reveals that the traces Tr(AB) and Tr(BA) are equal:

\(Tr(AB)=Tr\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 & -2i \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \\ -2i & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=1,\)

 

\(Tr(AB)=Tr\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i & 2i \\ -5i & 1 & 3i \\ 2i & -i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=1=Tr(AB)\). (2.430)

From the matrices A and B, we have \(\det (A)=i(i)=-1, \det (B)=-4+16i\). We can thus write

\(\det (AB)=\det \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 & -2i \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \\ -2i & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=4-16i=(-1)(-4+16i)=\det (A)\det (B)\). (2.431)

On the other hand, since \((B)=-4+16i\) and \((B^ {\dagger } )=-4-16i\), we see that \(\det (B^{\dagger } )=-4-16i=(-4+16i)^{*}=(\det (B))^{*}\).

(c) The commutator [A , B] is given by

\(AB-BA=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 & -2i \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \\ -2i & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)-\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i & 2i \\ -5i & 1 & 3i \\ 2i & -i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1-i & -4i \\ 3+5i & 0 & 5-3i \\ -4i & 1+i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\) (2.432)

and the anticommutator {A , B} by

\(AB+BA=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 & -2i \\ 3 & 1 & 5 \\ -2i & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)+\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i & 2i \\ -5i & 1 & 3i \\ 2i & -i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1+i & 0 \\ 3-5i & 2 & 5+3i \\ 0 & 1-i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\). (2.433)

(d) A calculation similar to (2.200)leads to the inverses of A, B, and AB:

\(A^{-1}=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right), B^{-1}=\frac{1}{68}\left(\begin{matrix} 22+3i & 8-2i & 20-5i \\ -6-24i & 4+16i & 10+40i \\ -12+3i & 8-2i & -14-5i \end{matrix} \right),\) (2.434)

 

\((AB)^{-1}=\frac{1}{68}\left(\begin{matrix} -5-20i & 8-2i & -3+22i \\ 40-10i & 4+16i & 24-6i \\ -5+14i & 8-2i & -3-12i \end{matrix} \right)\). (2.435)

From (2.434) it is now easy to verify that the product \(B^{-1} A^{-1}\) is equal to \((AB)^{-1}\):

\(B^{-1}A^{-1}=\frac{1}{68}\left(\begin{matrix} -5-20i & 8-2i & -3+22i \\ 40-10i & 4+16i & 24-6i \\ -5+14i & 8-2i & -3-12i \end{matrix} \right)=(AB)^{-1}\). (2.436)

(e) Since

\(A^{2}=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)=I\), (2.437)

we can write \(A^{3}=A,A^{4}=I,A^{5}=A\), and so on. We can generalize these relations to any value of \(n:A^{2n}=I\) and \(A^{2n+1}=A\) :

\(A^{2n}=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)=I, A^{2n+1}=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)=A\). (2.438)

Since \(A^{2n}=I\) and \(A^{2n+1}=A\), we can write

\(e^{xA}=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{n}A^{n}}{n!}= \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n}A^{2n}}{(2n)!}+\sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n+1}A^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}=I \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n} }{(2n)!} +A\sum\limits_{n=0} ^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n+1} }{(2n+1)!}\). (2.439)

The relations

\(\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n} }{(2n)!}=\cos hx, \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{x^{2n+1} }{(2n+1)!}=\sin hx \), (2.440)

lead to

\(e^{xA}= I\cos hx+A\sin hx=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\cos hx+\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -i & 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right) \sin hx\)

 

\(=\left(\begin{matrix} \cos hx & 0 & i\sin hx \\ 0 & \cos hx+\sin hx & 0 \\ -i\sin hx & 0 & \cos hx \end{matrix} \right)\). (2.441)
