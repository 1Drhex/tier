Comments:
1. Note the temperature dependence of the thermophysical properties of aluminum and silicon carbide. For example, from Table A.2 for silicon carbide,\(\alpha (1000K)\approx 0.1\times \alpha (300K)\); hence properties of this material have a strong temperature dependence.

2. The physical interpretation of α is that it provides a measure of heat transport (k) relative to energy storage \((\rho c_{p})\) . In general, metallic solids have higher α, whereas nonmetallics (e.g., paraffin) have lower values of α.

3. Linear interpolation of property values is generally acceptable for engineering calculations.

4. Use of the low-temperature (300 K) density at higher temperatures ignores thermal expansion effects but is also acceptable for engineering calculations.

5. The IHT software provides a library of thermophysical properties for selected solids, liquids, and gases that can be accessed from the toolbar button, Properties. See Example 2.1 in IHT.
