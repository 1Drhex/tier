Guest Question.

The four-bar linkage in the posture shown in Fig. 3.7a is driven by crank 2 at a constant angular velocity ω2 = 900 rev/min ccw. Determine the angular velocities of the coupler link and the output link, and the velocities of points E and F.

Admin
Solution

The loop-closure equation is given by Eq. (1) in (Fig. 3.17b) and is reproduced here:

\(\overset{\sqrt{}I}{r_{2}} + \overset{\sqrt{}?}{r_{3}} – \overset{\sqrt{}?}{r_{4}} – \overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{r_{1}} = 0.\) (1)

The two scalar component equations are

\(r_{2} \cos \theta_{2}+r_{3} \cos \theta_{3}-r_{4} \cos \theta_{4}-r_{1} \cos \theta_{1}=0\),

 

\(r_{2} \sin \theta_{2}+r_{3} \sin \theta_{3}-r_{4} \sin \theta_{4}-r_{1} \sin \theta_{1}=0\). (2)

Since the input is the angular displacement of link 2, the method of kinematic coefficients necessitates differentiating Eqs. (2) with respect to the input variable θ2. Rearranging the result gives

\(-r_{3} \sin \theta_{3} \theta_{3}^{\prime}+r_{4} \sin \theta_{4} \theta_{4}^{\prime}=r_{2} \sin \theta_{2}\)

and

\(r_{3} \cos \theta_{3} \theta_{3}^{\prime}-r_{4} \cos \theta_{4} \theta_{4}^{\prime}=-r_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\) (3)

where

\(\theta_{3}^{\prime}=\frac{d \theta_{3}}{d \theta_{2}} \quad \text { and } \quad \theta_{4}^{\prime}=\frac{d \theta_{4}}{d \theta_{2}}\) (4)

 

are referred to as the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 3 and 4, respectively. Algebraic forms for the kinematic coefficients can be obtained using Cramer’s rule. Writing Eqs. (3) in matrix form gives

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}-r_{3} \sin \theta_{3} & r_{4} \sin\theta_{4} \\r_{3} \cos \theta_{3} & -r_{4} \cos \theta_{4}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}\theta_{3}^{\prime} \\\theta_{4}^{\prime}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c}r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} \\-r_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\end{array}\right]\). (5)

The determinant of the (2×2) coefficient matrix can be written as

\(\Delta=-r_{3} r_{4} \sin \left(\theta_{4}-\theta_{3}\right)\) (6)

and provides geometric insight into special postures of the linkage (). For example, when this determinant tends toward zero, the kinematic coefficients tend toward infinity. Note that the determinant is zero (that is, the transmission angle θ4 − θ3 is zero) either when (a) θ3 = θ4 or when \(\text {(b)}\theta_{3}=\theta_{4} \pm 180^{\circ}\) —that is, when links 3 and 4 are fully extended or aligned on top of each other.

From Eqs. (5), the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 3 and 4 are

\(\theta_{3}^{\prime}=\frac{r_{2} \sin \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{4}\right)}{r_{3} \sin \left(\theta_{4}-\theta_{3}\right)} \quad \text { and } \quad \theta_{4}^{\prime}=\frac{r_{2} \sin \left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{3}\right)}{r_{4} \sin \left(\theta_{4}-\theta_{3}\right)}\) (7)

 

Recall from that the position values are θ3 = 20.92° and θ4 = 64.05°. Therefore, the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 3 and 4 are

\(\theta_{3}^{\prime}=+0.2718 rad / rad \quad \text { and } \quad \theta_{4}^{\prime}=+0.5265 rad / rad\) (8)

where the positive signs indicate that links 3 and 4 are rotating in the same direction as the input link, 2.

The angular velocities of links 3 and 4, obtained from the chain rule, are

\(\omega_{3}=\theta_{3}^{\prime} \omega_{2} \quad \text { and } \quad \omega_{4}=\theta_{4}^{\prime} \omega_{2} .\) (9)

Substituting Eqs. (7) into Eqs. (9) gives the same symbolic results as Eqs. (5) in . Also, substituting Eqs. (8) and the input angular velocity into Eqs. (9), the angular velocities of links 3 and 4 are

ω3 = 25.62 rad/s ccw and ω4 = 49.62 rad/s ccw. (10)

These answers are in good agreement with the results obtained in from the graphic method [Eqs. (4) and (5)] and the analytic method [Eqs. (14)].

The position of coupler point E with respect to ground pivot A (Fig. 3.7a) can be written as

\(\overset{??}{r_{E}} = \overset{\sqrt{}I}{r_{2}} + \overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{r_{EB}}.\) (11)

The x and y components of this vector equation are

\(x_{E}=r_{2} \cos \theta_{2}+r_{E B} \cos \left(\theta_{3}-\phi\right),\)

\(y_{E}=r_{2} \sin \theta_{2}+r_{E B} \sin \left(\theta_{3}-\phi\right),\) (12)

where Φ = tan-1 (REG/RGB) = tan-1 (4 in/10 in) = 21.80°.

Differentiating Eqs. (12) with respect to the input variable θ2, the first-order kinematic coefficients for point E are

 

\(x_{E}^{\prime}=\frac{d x_{E}}{d \theta_{2}}=-r_{2} \sin \theta_{2}-r_{E B} \sin \left(\theta_{3}-\phi\right) \theta_{3}^{\prime}\)

 

\(y_{E}^{\prime}=\frac{d y_{E}}{d \theta_{2}}=r_{2} \cos \theta_{2}+r_{E B} \cos \left(\theta_{3}-\phi\right) \theta_{3}^{\prime} .\) (13)

 

Substituting the known values into these equations, the first-order kinematic coefficients for point E are

\(x_{E}^{\prime}=-3.4191 in / rad \quad \text { and } \quad y_{E}^{\prime}=+0.9269 in / rad\) (14)

The velocity of point E can be written from the chain rule as

\(V _{E}=\left(x_{E}^{\prime} \hat{ i }+y_{E}^{\prime} \hat{ j }\right) \omega_{2}\) (15)

Substituting Eqs. (14) and the input angular velocity into this equation, the velocity of point E is

\(V _{E}=-26.85 \hat{ i }+7.28 \hat{ j } ft / s\)

Therefore, the speed of point E is VE = 27.8 ft/s, which agrees closely with the result obtained from the graphic method in (VE = 27.6 ft/s).

The velocity of point F can be obtained in a similar manner, and the result is

\(V _{F}=-20.60 \hat{ i }+23.82 \hat{ j } ft / s\)

Therefore, the speed of point F is VF = 31.5 ft/s, which agrees closely with the result obtained from the graphic method in (VF = 31.8 ft/s).
