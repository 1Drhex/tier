Finding the Critical Value of Inductor for Continuous Load Current

The rms input voltage to the circuit in Figure 3.24a is 220 V, 60 Hz. (a) If the dc output voltage is \(V_{dc} = 100 V\) at \(I_{dc} = 10 A\), determine the values of critical inductance \(L_e, α\), and \(I_{rms}\). (b) If \(I_{dc }= 15 A\) and \(L_e = 6.5 mH\), use Table 3.6 to determine the values of \(V_{dc}, α, β\), and \(I_{rms}\).

Solution

\(ω = 2π × 60 = 377 rad/s\), \(V_s = 120 V, V_m = \sqrt{2} ×120 = 169.7 V\).

a. Voltage ratio \(x = V_{dc}/V_m = 100/169.7 = 0.5893 = 58.93\%; α = sin^{-1}(x) = 36.87°\).
Equation (3.76)

\(k(x)=\frac{I_{dc}}{I_pk}=\sqrt{1-x^2}+x\left(\frac{2}{\pi }-\frac{\pi }{2} \right)\) (3.76)

gives the average current ratio \(k = I_{dc}/I_{pk} = 0.2575 = 25.75\%\). Thus, \(I_{pk} = I_{dc}/k = 10/0.2575 = 38.84 A\). The critical value of inductance is

\(L_{cr}=\frac{V_m}{ωI_{pk}}=\frac{169.7}{377\times 38.84} =11.59 mH \)

Equation (3.76) gives the rms current ratio \(k_r = I_{rms}/I_{pk} = 32.4\%\). Thus,

\(I_{rms} = k_r I_{pk} = 0.324 ×38.84 = 12.58 A.\)

b.\( L_e = 6.5 mH, I_{pk }= V_m/(ωL_e) = 169.7/(377 × 6.5 mH) = 69.25 A\).

 

\(k=\frac{I_{dc}}{I_pk}=\frac{15}{69.25}=21.66\%\)

Using linear interpolation, we get

\(x=x_n +\frac{(x_n+1 – x_n)(k – k_n)}{k_{n+1} – k_n}\)

 

\(=60 +\frac{((65 – 60)(21.66 – 23.95)}{15.27 – 23.95}=61.32\%\)

 

\(V_{dc} = xV_m = 0.6132 ×169.7 = 104.06 V\)

 

\(\alpha =\alpha _n+\frac{(α_{n+1} – α_n)(k – k_n)}{k_{n+1} – k_n}\)

 

\(=36.87+\frac{(40.54 – 36.87)(21.66 – 23.95)}{15.27 – 23.95}= 37.84°\)

 

\(β = β_n+\frac{(β_{n+1} – β_n)(k – k_n)}{k_{n+1} – k_n}\)

 

\(= 216.87+\frac{(220.54 – 216.87)(21.66 – 23.95)}{15.27 – 23.95}= 217.85°\)

 

\(k_r=\frac{I_{rms}}{I_{pk}}=k_{r(n)}+\frac{(k_{r(n+1)} – k_{r(n)})(k – k_n)}{k_{n+1} – k_n}\)

 

\(=31.05+\frac{(26.58 – 31.05)(21.66 – 23.95)}{15.27 – 23.95}= 29.87\%\)

Thus, \(I_{rms} = 0.2987 × I_{pk} = 0.2987 × 69.25 = 20.68 A\).
