Guest Question.

A 50 Hz, three-phase transmission line is 125 miles long. It has a total series impedance of (35 + j 140) Ω per phase and a shunt admittance of \(400 × 10 ^{- 6} ∠ 90 Ω^{- 1}\). The line delivers 40 MW at 220 kV and 0.9 p.f. lagging. Determine the sending voltage, power and efficiency using the following medium-line models:

(a) Load-end admittance model.
(b) T model.
(c) P model.

Admin
Solution

The receiving voltage and current are common to all three calculations.
The received voltage per phase is

 

\(V_{R}=\frac{220}{\sqrt{3} }=127 k V\)

 

Taking the received voltage as the reference phasor,

\(V_{R} = 127 kV ∠0 = (127 + j 0) kV\)

The magnitude of the received current is

 

\(I_{R}=\frac{40\times 10^6}{\sqrt{3\times 220\times 10^3\times 0.9} }=116.6 A\)

 

The current lags the voltage by an angle of \(\cos^{-1}0.9 = 25.8°.\)

The receiving end current is

\(I_{R} = 116.6 ∠ -25.8° = 116.6(0.9 – j 0.435) = (104.9 – j 50.72) A\)

 

(a) Load-end admittance model

In this model, all the shunt admittance is placed at the receiving end of the line. The solution can be divided into seven distinct steps.
Step 1: Draw the per-phase equivalent circuit and show all known parameters (Fig. Fig. 35.11).

Step 2: Calculate the shunt-charging current \(I_{ch}\).
As the voltage at the receiving end is known, the current through the shunt admittance is given by

\(I_{ch} = YV_{R} = j 400 × 10 ^{- 6} × 127 × 10^3 = j 50.8 = 50.8 ∠ 90 A\)

Note that the charging current leads the voltage by 90° as it must for a capacitor.
Step 3: Calculate the line current \(I_{L}\).
The line current is the sum of \(I_{R} and I_{ch}\):
\(I_{L} = I_{R} + I_{ch} = (104.9 – j 50.72) + j 50.8 = (104.9 + j 0.08) A\)

Step 4: Calculate the voltage drop across the line \(ΔV_{1}\).

\(ΔV_{1} = I_{L}Z = (104.9 + j 0.08)(35 + j 140) = (3660.3 + j 14688.8) V\)

Step 5: Calculate the supply voltage \(V_{S}.\)

\(V_{S} = V_{R} + ΔV_{1} = 127 000 + 3600.3 + j 14 688.8 = (130 660 + j 14 688) V\)

Hence \(V_{S} = 131.4 kV ∠ 6.37\)

The line-to-line voltage magnitude is 227.6 kV.
Step 6: Calculate the sending current \(I_{S}.\)
For the load end admittance line model the sending current must equal the line current:

\(I_{S} = I_{L} = (104.9 + j 0.08) = 105 ∠ 0.043 A \)

Step 7: Calculate the sending power factor, power and efficiency.

Sending power factor = \(\cos (6.37 – 0.043) = 0.993 \)

The sending power is

 

\(P_{S}= \sqrt{3}V_{L}L_{L}\cos\phi =\sqrt{3}\times 227\times 10^3\times 105\times 0.993=41.2 M W \)

The efficiency of the transmission is

 

\(\frac{P_{R}}{P_{S}}\times 100 %= \frac{40}{41.2}\times 100 % = 97 per cent \)

 

(b) T model
In the T model all the shunt admittance is placed at the mid-point of the line. The solution can be divided into eight distinct steps.

Step 1: Draw the per-phase equivalent circuit and show all known parameters (Fig. 35.12).

Step 2: Calculate the voltage drop across the line \(ΔV_{1}.\)

\(ΔV_{1} = (17.5 + j 70)(104.9 – j 50.72) = (5785 + j 6455) V\)

Step 3: Calculate the voltage at the mid-point of the line.

\(V_{mid} = V_{R} + ΔV_{1} = 127 000 + (5785 + j 6455) = 132 785 + j 6455 V\)

Step 4: Calculate the charging current \(I_{ch}.\)

\(I_{ch} = V_{mid}Y = (132 785 + j 6455)(j 400 × 10^{- 6}) = ( – 2.58 + j 53.1) A\)

Step 5: Calculate the supply current \(I_{S}.\)
The current flowing in the first part of the line \(I_{S}\) is the sum of \(I_{R} and I_{ch}:\)

\(I_{s} = (104.9 – j 50.72) + ( – 2.58 + j 53.1) = (102.3 + j 2.38) = 102.3 ∠ 1.33 A\)

Step 6: Calculate the voltage drop across the line \(ΔV_{2}.\)

\(ΔV_{2} = (17.5 + j 70)(102.3 + j 2.38) = 1623.4 + j7119.4 V\)

Step 7: Calculate the supply voltage \(V_S\).

\(V_{S} = V_{Mid} + ΔV_{2} = 132 785 + j 6455 + 1623.4 + j 7119.4 \\ = (134 408 + j 13 574) V\)

Hence \(V_{S} = 135 kV ∠ 5.72\)

The line-to-line voltage magnitude is 233.9 kV.

Step 8: Calculate the sending power factor, power and efficiency.
Sending power factor \(= \cos(5.72 – 1.33) = 0.997\)

The sending power is

 

\(P_{S}= \sqrt{3}V_{L}L_{L}\cos\phi =\sqrt{3}\times 233.9\times 10^3\times 102.3\times 0.997=41.32 M W\)

 

The efficiency of the transmission is

 

\(\frac{P_{R}}{P_{S}}\times 100 %= \frac{40}{41.32}\times 100 % = 96.8 per cent\)

 

(c) P model

In the p model the shunt admittance is split into two; half is placed at the sending end of the line and half is placed at the receiving end. The solution can be divided into eight distinct steps.

Step 1: Draw the per-phase equivalent circuit and show all known parameters (Fig. 35.13).

Step 2: Calculate the charging current \(I_{ch2}.\)

\(I_{ch2} = V_{R}\frac{Y}{2} = (127 + j 0) × 10^3 × j 200 × 10 ^{- 6} = j 25.4 = 25.4 ∠ 90 A\)

Step 3: Calculate the line current IL.

\(I_{L} = I_{R} + I_{ch2} = (104.9 – j 50.72) + j 25.4 = (104.9 – j 25.32) A\)

Step 4: Calculate \(ΔV_{1}.\)

\(ΔV_{1} = (35 + j 140)(104.9 – j 25.32) = (7216.3 + j 13 799) V\)

Step 5: Calculate \(V_{S}.\)

\(V_{S} = V_{R} + ΔV_{1} = 127 000 + 7216.3 + j 13 799 = (134 216 + j 13 799) V\)

Hence \(V_{S} = 134.9 kV ∠ 5.87\)

The line-to-line voltage magnitude is 233.6 kV.

Step 6: Calculate \(I_{ch1}.\)

\(I_{ch1} = V_{S}\frac{Y}{2} = (134.2 + j 13.8) × 10^3 × ( j 200 × 10^{-6}) = (- 2.76 + j 26.84) A\)

Step 7: Calculate \(I_{S}.\)

\(I_{S} = I_{L} + I_{ch1} = (104.9 – j 25.32) + ( – 2.76 + j 26.84) \\ = (102.14 + j 1.52) = 102.14 ∠ 0.852 A\)

Step 8: Calculate the sending power factor, power and efficiency.

The sending power is \(= \cos (5.87 – 0.852) = 0.996\)

The sending power is

 

\(P_{S}= \sqrt{3}V_{L}L_{L}\cos\phi =\sqrt{3}\times 233.6\times 10^3\times 102.14\times 0.996=41.16 M W\)

 

The efficiency of the transmission is

 

\(\frac{P_{R}}{P_{S}}\times 100 % = \frac{40}{41.16}\times 100 % = 97.1 per cent\)

It is useful to compare the results of the calculations for the three different line models. They are tabulated in Table 35.1. It is clear that there is little significant difference between the T and the p models. Calculations using the p model are slightly more straightforward than those for the T model.
The load-end model is less accurate than either the T or the p model but the calculation is less time consuming. The choice of model depends on the accuracy required.
It should be noted that throughout the remainder of this chapter the short-line transmission line model will be used.
