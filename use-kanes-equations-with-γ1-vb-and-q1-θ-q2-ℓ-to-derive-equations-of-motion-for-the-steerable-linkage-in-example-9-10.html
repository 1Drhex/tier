Guest Question.

Use Kane’s equations with \(\dot{\gamma}_{1}=v_{B}\) and \(q_{1}=\theta, q_{2}=\ell\) to derive equations of motion for the steerable linkage in .

Admin
Solution

As a closure to our exploration of alternative techniques for deriving equations of motion, we apply Kane’s equations to a system in which the derivation of the Gibbs-Appell function encountered considerable complexity. Whether the same level of complexity is encountered here is a key question to be addressed. We begin with a free-body diagram. For the sake of clarity, we use separate diagrams to describe the actual forces and the inertial equivalents. (The diagram on the left is like the one in , except that it now shows the fixed \(X Y Z\) coordinate system that is selected as the global system.) Bar \(A B\) is in planar motion, so the forces acting on it are equivalent to a force \(m_{1} \bar{a}_{G}\) at the center of mass and a couple \(I_{G} \ddot{\theta}\) acting about the vertical axis. Collar \(A\) follows its stationary guide in any movement because the quasi-velocity is unconstrained. Hence the inertial effect for this body is merely \(m_{2} \bar{a}_{A}\) along the guide. In contrast, the wheel executes a spatial rotation, so the equivalent system is a force \(m_{3} \bar{a}_{B}\) and a couple \(\left(d \bar{H}_{B} / d t\right)_{\mathrm{w}}\).

There is no need to carry out a kinematical analysis here, because the present motion variables are the same as those previously used to describe \(S .\) The only quasivelocity is \(v_{B}\), so Kane’s equation for this system is

\(\begin{array}{c}m_{1} \bar{a}_{G} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{G}}{\partial v_{B}}+I_{G} \ddot{\theta} \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial v_{B}}+\left(-m_{2} \ddot{\ell} \bar{I}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{A}}{\partial v_{B}}+m_{3} \bar{a}_{B} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}+\left(\frac{d \bar{H}_{B}}{d t}\right)_{\mathrm{w}} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_{\mathrm{w}}}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\=k \Delta \bar{I} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{A}}{\partial v_{B}}+F[\cos (\theta+\beta) \bar{I}+\sin (\theta+\beta) \bar{J}] \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}.\end{array}\)

It should be noted that the reaction forces \(\bar{N}_{A}\) and \(\bar{N}_{B}\) do not appear here because the respective partial velocities will be perpendicular to these constraint forces as a result of using unconstrained quasi-coordinates. Before we perform the main substitutions, it is useful to simplify the preceding by using Eqs. (5) in Example \(9.10\) to describe the motion of the center of mass. We also account for the facts that \(\bar{v}_{A}=-\dot{\ell} \bar{I}\), that \(\bar{F}\) acts parallel to \(\bar{v}_{B}\), and that \(\bar{\omega}_{\mathrm{w}} \cdot \bar{i}^{\prime}=0 .\) Thus, we have

\(\begin{array}{l}\frac{1}{4} m_{1}\left(-\ddot{\ell} \bar{I}+\bar{a}_{B}\right) \cdot\left(-\frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}} \bar{I}+\frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}\right)+I_{G} \ddot{\theta} \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial v_{B}}+m_{2} \ddot{\ell} \frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\\quad+m_{3} \bar{a}_{B} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}+\left(I_{1} \alpha_{y^{\prime}} \bar{j}^{\prime}+I_{2} \alpha_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial\left(\omega_{y}^{\prime} \bar{j}^{\prime}+\omega_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\\quad+\left[\left(\omega_{y}^{\prime} \bar{j}^{\prime}+\omega_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right) \times\left(I_{1} \omega_{y}^{\prime} \bar{j}^{\prime}+I_{2} \omega_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)\right]_{\text {wheel }} \cdot \frac{\partial\left(\omega_{y}^{\prime} \bar{j}^{\prime}+\omega_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\=-k \Delta \frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}}+F\left(\bar{e}_{t}\right)_{B} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial v_{B}}\left[\left(v_{B}\right)\left(\bar{e}_{t}\right)_{B}\right].\end{array}\)

It is evident that the last of the inertial terms in the preceding equation vanishes. Combining like terms in the remainder reduces Kane’s equation to

\(\begin{aligned}\left(\frac{1}{4} m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{\ell} \frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}}+\left(\frac{1}{4} m_{1}+m_{3}\right) \bar{a}_{B} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\ -\frac{1}{4} m_{1}\left(\ddot{\ell} \bar{I} \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}+\bar{a}_{B} \cdot \frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}} \bar{I}\right)+I_{G} \ddot{\theta} \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\ +I_{1} \alpha_{y^{\prime}} \frac{\partial \omega_{y}^{\prime}}{\partial v_{B}}+I_{2} \alpha_{z^{\prime}} \frac{\partial \omega_{z^{\prime}}}{\partial v_{B}} \\\\=-k\Delta \frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}}+F . \end{aligned}\)

The acceleration terms were derived in . We obtain the partial velocities by differentiating the kinematical equations and velocity expressions that were derived there, which gives

\(\frac{\partial \dot{\ell}}{\partial v_{B}}=-\frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}, \quad \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial v_{B}}=\frac{1}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}\),

 

\(\frac{\partial \bar{v}_{B}}{\partial v_{B}}=\cos (\theta+\beta) \bar{I}+\sin (\theta+\beta) \bar{J}\),

 

\(\frac{\partial \omega_{y^{\prime}}}{\partial v_{B}}=\frac{1}{R}, \frac{\partial \omega_{z^{\prime}}}{\partial v_{B}}=\frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial v_{B}}\).

Substitution of these expressions and the previously derived acceleration terms converts Kane’s equation for this system to

\(\begin{array}{l}\left(\frac{1}{4} m_{1}+m_{2}\right)\left[-\dot{v}_{B} \frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}-\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta) \sin \theta \cos \beta}{(\cos \theta)^{3}}+v_{B} \dot{\beta} \frac{\sin \beta}{\cos \theta}\right]\left(-\frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}\right) \\\\\quad+\left(\frac{1}{4} m_{1}+m_{3}\right)\left[\dot{v}_{B} \cos (\theta+\beta)-\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L} \frac{(\sin (\theta+\beta))^{2}}{\cos \theta}\right.\end{array}\)

\(\left.-v_{B} \dot{\beta} \sin (\theta+\beta)\right] \cos (\theta+\beta)+\left(\frac{1}{4} m_{1}+m_{3}\right)\left[\dot{v}_{B} \sin (\theta+\beta)\right.\)

\(\left.+\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta) \cos (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}+v_{B} \dot{\beta} \cos (\theta+\beta)\right] \sin (\theta+\beta)\)

\(-\frac{1}{4} m_{1}\left[-\dot{v}_{B} \frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}-\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta) \sin \theta \cos \beta}{(\cos \theta)^{3}}\right.\)

\(\left.+v_{B} \dot{\beta} \frac{\sin \beta}{\cos \theta}\right] \cos (\theta+\beta)-\frac{1}{4} m_{1}\left[\dot{v}_{B} \cos (\theta+\beta)\right.\)

\(\left.-\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L} \frac{(\sin (\theta+\beta))^{2}}{\cos \theta}-v_{B} \dot{\beta} \sin (\theta+\beta)\right]\left(-\frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}\right)\)

\(+I_{G}\left[\frac{\dot{v}_{B}}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}+\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L^{2}} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta) \cos \beta}{(\cos \theta)^{3}}\right.\)

\(\left.+\frac{v_{B} \dot{\beta}}{L} \frac{\cos (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}\right] \frac{1}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}+I_{1}\left(\frac{\dot{v}_{B}}{R}\right) \frac{1}{R}\)

\(+\left[\frac{\dot{v}_{B}}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}+\frac{v_{B}^{2}}{L^{2}} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta) \cos \beta}{(\cos \theta)^{3}}\right.\)

\(\left.+\frac{v_{B} \dot{\beta}}{L} \frac{\cos (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}+\ddot{\beta}\right] \frac{1}{L} \frac{\sin (\theta+\beta)}{\cos \theta}\)

\(=k\left(\ell-\ell_{0}\right) \frac{\cos \beta}{\cos \theta}+F\).

We could go on to simplify this equation of motion by collecting the coefficients of \(\dot{v}_{B}, v_{B}^{2} / L\), and \(v_{B} \dot{\beta} .\) There is no need to do so in the present context, because the main features are evident. We see that, for a given set of generalized coordinates and quasi-velocities, the effort to analyze the kinematical features of a system is the same for the Gibbs-Appell equations and Kane’s equations. From that common starting point, the steps leading to the final equations are considerably less intricate for Kane’s equations. The comparison of the two examples here suggests that this aspect is more important than the ability to construct the Gibbs-Appell function in a standard procedure.
