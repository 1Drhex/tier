Guest Question.

Show that the expression \( v=a t^{2} \) is dimensionally correct, where \(v \)represents speed, \(a\) acceleration, and \(t \) a time interval

Admin
Solution

For the speed term, we have from Table 1.6

TABLE 1.6 Dimensions and Common Units of Area, Volume, Speed, and Acceleration
System Area (\(L^{2}\)) Volume (\(L^{3}\)) Speed (L/T) Acceleration (\(L/T^{2}\))
SI \(m^{2}\) \(m^{3}\) m/s \(m/s^{2}\)
British engineering \(ft^{2}\) \(ft{3}\) ft/s \(ft/s^{2}\)

\([v]=\frac{\mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{T}}\)
The same table gives us \( \mathrm{L} / \mathrm{T}^{2} \) for the dimensions of acceleration, and so the dimensions of a t are

\(=\left(\frac{\mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{T}^{2}}\right)(\mathrm{T})=\frac{\mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{T}}\)

Therefore, the expression is dimensionally correct. (If the expression were given as \( v=a t^{2} \), it would be dimensionally i n correct. Try it and see!)
