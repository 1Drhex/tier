Guest Question.

Consider the isothermal comer of finite length as depicted in Fig. 7-4a. The surface material is similar to the one of the infinitely long comer of the previous example, i.e., the absorptance and emittance obey FresneFs equations with m = n-ik- 23.452(1 – i), and a hemispherical emittance of ε = 0.1. However, in the present example we assume that the material is reflecting in a nonspecular fashion with a bidirectional reflection fimction of

\(\rho ^{\prime \prime } (s_{i},s_{r} ) = \frac{\rho ^{\prime } (s_{i})}{\pi C_{n} (s_{i}) } (1+ s_{s}\cdot s_{r} )^{n} ,\)

where \(s_{i}\) is the direction of incoming radiation, \(s_{s}\) is the specular reflection direction (i.e., \(\theta _{s} = \theta _{i}, \psi _{s} = \psi _{i} + \pi\) ), and \(s_{r}\) . is the actual direction of reflection. This form of the bidirectional reflection fiinction describes a surface that has a reflectance maximum in the specular direction, and whose reflectance drops off equally in all directions away from the specular direction (i.e., with changing polar angle and/or azimuthal angle). Since the directional-hemispherical reflectance must obey \(\rho ^{\prime } (s_{i})= 1 - \epsilon ^{\prime } (s_{i})\) , the function \(C_{n}(s_{i})\) follows from equation (3.37) as

\(\)C_{n}(s_{i})= \frac{1}{\pi }\int_{2\pi }^{}{(1+ s_{s}\cdot s_{r} )^{n} } \cos \theta _{r} d\Omega _{r}.

Determine the local radiative heat loss rates from the plates for the case that both plates are isothermal at the same temperature.

Solution

The direction vectors s may be expressed in terms of polar angle θ and azimuthal angle ψ, or \(S=\sin \theta (\cos \psi t_{1}+\sin \psi t_{2} ) +\cos \theta n\), where n is the unit surface normal and \(t_{1}\) and \(t_{2}\) are two perpendicular unit vectors tangential to the surface. Therefore, the bidirectional reflection fimction may be written as

\(\rho ^{\prime \prime } ( \theta _{i},\psi _{i}, \theta _{r}, \psi _{r} )= \frac{\rho ^{\prime }(\theta _{i},\psi _{i}) }{\pi C_{n}(\theta _{i}) } \left[1+ \cos \theta _{i} \cos \theta _{r}- \sin \theta _{i} \sin \theta _{r} \cos (\psi_{i} – \psi_{r})\right]^{n},\) (7.21a)
\(C_{n} (\theta _{i}) =\frac{1}{\pi } \int_{0}^{2\pi }{}\int_{0}^{\pi /2}{(1+ \cos \theta _{i} \cos \theta +\sin \theta _{i} \sin \theta \cos \psi)^{n} } \cos \theta \sin \theta d\theta d\psi\) (7.21b)

The bidirectional reflection function within the plane of incidence (\( \psi _{r}= \psi _{i} or \psi _{i}+ \pi\)) is shown in Fig. 7-4b for two different incidence directions and three different values of n. Obviously, for n = 0 the surface reflects diflaisely (but the amount of reflection, as well as absorption and emission, depends on direction through Fresnel’s equation). As n grows, the surface becomes more specular, and purely specular reflection would be reached with \(n\rightarrow \infty\) . For this configuration and surface material we should like to determine the heat lost from the plates using the net radiation method.
As indicated in Fig. l-4a we shall apply the net radiation method, equations (7.18) \(I_{ik}(\lambda ) =\epsilon _{ik} (\lambda )I_{bi}(\lambda )+\pi \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{\rho _{jik} (\lambda ) F_{i-j} } , i,k=1,2,…,N\) and (7.20) \(q_{i}(\lambda ) = \pi \sum\limits_{k=1}^{N}{} I_{ik}(\lambda ) F_{i-k} – \pi \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{} I_{jk}(\lambda ) F_{i-j} = \pi \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{} (I_{ij}-I_{ji})F_{i-j}\)
\(= \epsilon _{i} (\lambda ) E_{bi} (\lambda ) – \pi \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{} \alpha _{ij}(\lambda ) I_{ji}(\lambda ) F_{i-j} , i= 1,2,…,N\) , by breaking up each surface into \(M\times N\) subsurfaces (M divisions in the x- and y-directions, N in the z-direction).
Considering the intensity at node (i, k) on the bottom surface directed toward node (\(j_{o}, k_{o}\)) on the vertical wall, we find that equation (7.18) becomes, after division by \(I_{b}\),

\(\Phi _{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o} }= \frac{I_{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}} }{I_{b} } = \epsilon _{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}} + \sum\limits_{j_{i}=1 }^{M}{} \sum\limits_{k_{i}=1}^{N} \pi \rho _{j_{i},k_{i}\rightarrow i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}} F_{i,k\rightarrow j_{i},k_{i}} \Phi _{j_{i},k_{i}\rightarrow i,k}.\) (7.22)

In this relation we have made use of the fact that a node on the bottom surface can only see nodes on the side wall and vice versa. Also, by symmetry we have

\(\Phi _{i,k \rightarrow j_{o},k_{o} }= \Phi _{j,k\rightarrow i_{o},k_{o}} if j=i and i_{o}=j_{o},\)

and

\(\Phi _{i,k \rightarrow j_{o},k_{o} }= \Phi _{i,N+1-k\rightarrow j_{o}.N+1- k_{o} },\)

that is, the intensity must be symmetric to the two planes x =y and z = L/2. We, therefore, have a total of M \(\times\) (N/2) unknowns (assuming N to be even) and need to apply equation (7.22) for i = 1,2,…, M and k= 1,2,…, N/2 . To calculate the necessary \(\epsilon ^{\prime }\) and \(\rho ^{\prime \prime}\)values, one must establish a number of polar and azimuthal angles. From Fig. 7-4a it follows that

\((\cos \theta _{i} )_{i,k\rightarrow j_{i},k_{i}} =\frac{y_{ji} }{\sqrt{x^{2}_{i} + y^{2}_{ji} + (z_{k}- z_{ki})^{2} } } ,\)
\((\cos \theta _{r} )_{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}} =\frac{y_{jo} }{\sqrt{x^{2}_{i} + y^{2}_{jo} + (z_{k}- z_{ko})^{2} } }\)

Using the values for \((\cos \theta _{r} )_{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}}\) one can readily calculate the directional emittances \(\epsilon _{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}} = 1- \rho ^{\prime} (\cos \theta _{r} )\)
from Fresnel’s equation as given in . Similarly, \(\rho ^{\prime} (\cos \theta _{i} )\) and \(C_{n}(\cos \theta _{i} )\) are determined from Fresnel’s equation and equation (7.21), respectively; and all values of \(\rho _{j_{i},k_{i}\rightarrow i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}}\) follow from equation (7.21). All necessary view factors may be calculated from equation (4.40) \(2A_{1}F_{1-2} = F(x_{2},y_{2}, z_{3} ) – F(x_{2},y_{1}, z_{3} ) – F(x_{1},y_{2}, z_{3} ) + F(x_{1},y_{1}, z_{3})\)
\(+F(x_{1},y_{2}, z_{2} )-F(x_{1},y_{1}, z_{2} ) – F(x_{2},y_{2}, z_{2} ) + F(x_{2},y_{1}, z_{2} )\)
\(– F(x_{2},y_{2}, z_{3}-z_{1} ) + F(x_{2},y_{1}, z_{3}-z_{1} ) + F(x_{1},y_{2}, z_{3}-z_{1} ) – F(x_{1},y_{1}, z_{3}-z_{1} ) +\)
\(F(x_{2},y_{2}, z_{2}-z_{1} )- F(x_{2},y_{1}, z_{2}-z_{1} )- F(x_{1},y_{2}, z_{2}-z_{1} ) + F(x_{1},y_{1}, z_{2}-z_{1} )\),, for arbitrarily oriented perpendicular plates. For all view factors the opposing surfaces are of identical and constant size with \(x_{2}-x_{1} = y_{2}- y_{1}= w/M\) and \(z_{1} = z_{3}- z_{2}= L/M\). Offsets \(x_{1} and y_{1}\) may vary between 0 and (M – l)w/M and \(z_{2}\) between 0 and (N – 1 )L/N. Thus, using S5mimetry and reciprocity, one must evaluate a total of (M/2)\(\times\)M\(\times\) N view factors. In many of today’s workstations and computers all different values of directional emittance, the factor \(\rho ^{\prime}/C_{n}\) in the bidirectional reflection function, and all view factors may be calculated—once and for all—and stored (requiring memory allocation for often millions of numbers). The bidirectional reflection function itself depends on surface locations and on all possible incoming as well as all possible outgoing directions. Even after employing symmetry and reciprocity (for the bidirectional reflection function), this would require storing [M\(\times\)(N/2)]\(\times\) \([M\times N]^{2}/2 = (MN)^{3}/4\) numbers. Unless relatively few subdivisions are used (say M,N < 10), it will be impossible to precalculate and store values of the bidirectional reflection function; rather, part of it must be recalculated every time it is required.
The nondimensional intensities are now easily found from equation (7.22) by successive approximation: A first guess for the intensity field is made by setting \(\Phi _{i,k \rightarrow j_{o},k_{o} }=\epsilon _{i,k\rightarrow j_{o},k_{o}}\) Improved values for \(\Phi _{i,k \rightarrow j_{o},k_{o} }\), are found by evaluating equation (7.22) again and again until the intensities have converged to within specified error bounds. The local net radiative heat flux may then be determined from equation (7.20b) as

\(\Psi _{i,k} =\frac{q_{i,k} }{\epsilon E_{b} } = 1-\frac{1}{\epsilon }\sum\limits_{j_{i}=1 }^{M}{} \sum\limits_{k_{i}=1}^{N}{}\epsilon _{i,k\rightarrow j_{i},k_{i} } F_{i,k\rightarrow j_{i},k_{i}} \Phi _{j_{i},k_{i}\rightarrow i,k}\)

Some representative results for the local radiative heat flux near z = L/2 (i.e., for k = N/2) are shown in Fig. 7-5 for the case of w = I (square plates). Clearly, taking into consideration substantially different reflective properties has rather small effects on the local heat transfer rates. Obviously, as the surface becomes more specular (increasing n) the heat loss rates increase (since less radiation will be reflected back to the emitting surface), but the increases are very minor except for the region close to the vertex (and even there, they are less than 4%).
The directional distribution of the emittance is just as important as that of the bidirectional reflection function: The curve labeled “diffuse” shows the case of diffuse emission and reflection, i.e.,\(\epsilon ^{\prime } (s)=\alpha ^{\prime } (s)=\epsilon =0.1 and \pi \rho ^{\prime \prime } (s_{i},s_{r} )=\rho ^{\prime} = 1-\epsilon =0.9\). In contrast, the curve labeled “Fresnel, n = 0”
corresponds to the case of \(\epsilon ^{\prime } (s)=\alpha ^{\prime } (s) = 1- \rho ^{\prime}(s)\) evaluated from Fresnel’s equation and \(\pi \rho (s_{i},s_{r} )=\rho ^{\prime}(s_{i}).\) All lines in Fig. 7-5 have been calculated by breaking up each surface into 20 x 20 subsurfaces.
Also included are the data points for results obtained by breaking up each surface into only 20 \(\times\) 20 (solid symbols) and 4 \(\times\) 4 surfaces (open symbols). Local heat fluxes are predicted accurately with few subsurfaces, even for strongly nondifFuse reflection. Total heat loss is predicted even more accurately, with maximum errors of < 0.6% (2\(\times\)2 subsurfaces) and < 0.3% (4\(\times\)4 subsurfaces), respectively.
The results should be compared with those of Toor [7] for w/L \(\rightarrow\) 0, as shown in Fig. 6-18: The “diffuse” case of Fig. 7-5 virtually coincides with the corresponding case in Fig. 6-18, while the n= 8 case falls very close to the specular case with Fresnel-varying reflectance of Toor (solid line in Fig. 6-18).

For the present example at least, taking into account the directional behavior of emittance and reflectance is rarely justifiable in view of the additional complexity and computational effort required. Only if the radiative properties are known with great accuracy, and if heat fluxes need to be determined with similar accuracy, should this type of analysis be attempted. Similar statements may be made for most other configurations. For example, if Example 7.4 is recalculated for directly opposed parallel quadratic plates, the effects of Fresnel’s equation and the bidirectional reflection function are even less: Heat fluxes for diffuse reflection—whether Fresnel’s equation is used or not—differ by less than 0.6%, while differences due to the value of n in the bidirectional reflection function never exceed 0.2%. Only in configurations with collimated irradiation and/or strong beam-channeling possibilities should one expect substantial impact as a result of the directional variations of surface properties.
