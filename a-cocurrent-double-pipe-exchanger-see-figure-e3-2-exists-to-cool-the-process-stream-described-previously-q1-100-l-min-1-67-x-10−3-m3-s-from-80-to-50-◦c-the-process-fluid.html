Guest Question.

A cocurrent double-pipe exchanger (see Figure E3.2) exists to cool the process stream described previously (\(q_{1} = 100\) L/min\( = 1.67 × 10^{−3} m^{3}/s\)) from 80 to\( 50^{ ◦}C\). The process fluid flows in the inner pipe, and the chilled water flows in the outer pipe (Figure 3.10),\( T_{2F} = 5^{◦}C\). Determine the flow rate and exchanger exit temperature of the chilled water that will be needed.
The following table shows the pipe characteristics for the exchanger.

Schedule 40 S steel pipe as inner pipe in double-pipe heat exchanger

1.25 in. \(D_{Nominal}\)
3.5 × \(10^{−2} m\) 1.38 in. \(D_{1D}\)
4.2 × \(10^{−2} m\) 1.66 in \(D_{OD}\)
1.67 m/s 5.5 ft./s (at \(q_{1}\)) \(V _{(linear velocity)}\)
Admin
Solution

Because both fluids have waterlike properties the Level I analysis is simplified to

\(T_{2}=T_{2F}-\frac{q_{1}}{q_{2}}(T_{1}-T_{1F})\)

\(T_{2}=5^{\circ }C-\frac{100}{q_{2}}(50-80^{\circ }C)\)

The Level I balance relates the utility stream exit temperature to the utility stream flow rate, but does not provide a unique solution.
The theoretical minimum flow rate can be found if the exchanger is operated such that the utility stream exists at 50 ◦C, which is the maximum allowable value.
This is given by a Level II analysis as

\(q_{2Min}=\frac{q_{1}(T_{1F}-T_{1\infty })}{T_{2\infty }-T_{2F}}\)

where \(T_{1\infty }=T_{2\infty }=50^{\circ }C\)

thus,

\(q_{2Min}=\frac{100(80-50)}{50-5}\)=67 L/min

The relationship between T2 and q2 given by\(T_{2}=(5^{\circ }C-(100/q_{2})(-30^{\circ }C))\) can be seen in Figure E3.3.
However, only one combination will provide the desired cooling with the existing exchanger. To proceed, a Level III analysis is required, which necessitates an estimate of the overall heat transfer coefficient U.
A previous experiment allowed U to be estimated as

\(U=1\frac{kW}{m^{2}K}\)

The Level III analysis, Equation (3.4.8),

\(T_{1Z}=\frac{1}{\Theta +1}(T_{1F}+\Theta T_{2F} )+\frac{\Theta }{\Theta +1}(T_{1F}-T_{2F})e^{-\beta Z}\) (3.4.8)

which is one solution to the coupled energy balance equations describing this exchanger, can be used or we can use Eq. (3.4.10),

\(\ln \frac{(T_{1}-T_{2})}{(T_{1F}-T_{2F})}=\left(-\frac{Ua}{\rho _{1}q_{1}\hat{C_{p1}} }-\frac{Ua}{\rho _{2}q_{2}\hat{C_{p2}} } \right)\) (3.4.10)

which is another solution to the coupled energy balance equation. Equation (3.4.8) can be solved for q2 because we know and and the process
fluid inlet and exit temperatures:

\(\Theta =\frac{q_{2}}{1.67\times 10^{-3}}\)

\(\beta L=\frac{(1kJ/m^{2}sK)(7.37m^{2})}{(1000kg/m^{3})(4.184 kJ/kgK)(1.67\times 10^{-3}m^{3}/s)}\left(1+\frac{1}{\Theta } \right)\)

\(=1.05\left(1+\frac{1}{\Theta } \right)\)

and, because L = 67 m,

\(\beta =0.016\left(1+\frac{1}{\Theta } \right)m^{-1}\)

Equation (3.4.8) is an implicit equation and must be solved by any method that selects and then computes the right-hand side and compares it with\( T_{1} = 50 ^{◦}C\). Such a procedure yields \(q_{2}\) = 78 L/min. \(T_{2}\) is calculated from the Level I balance:

\(T_{2}=5-\frac{100}{78}(50-80)\)
\(=43^{\circ }C\)

Alternatively, Equation (3.4.10) can be used to obtain \(T_{2} \)as follows

\(Q_{Load}=(1.67/1000m^{3}/s)(1000 kg/m^{3})(4.185kJ/kgK)(50-80K)\)

=-210 kJ/s

Equation (3.4.10) becomes

-210 kJ/s=\((1.0 kJ/sm^{2}K)(7.27m^{2})\Delta T_{LM},\)

\(\Delta T_{LM}=\frac{(T_{2}-50)-(5-80)}{\ln(\frac{T_{2}-50}{5-80} )}\)

An iterative solution yields\( T_{2} = 43^{ ◦}C\).
\(q_{2} \)is found from the Level I balance:

\( q_{2}=q-{1}\frac{(T_{1F}-T_{1})}{(T_{2}-T_{2F})} \)
=78 L/min

This solution is shown as the • in Figure E3.3.
