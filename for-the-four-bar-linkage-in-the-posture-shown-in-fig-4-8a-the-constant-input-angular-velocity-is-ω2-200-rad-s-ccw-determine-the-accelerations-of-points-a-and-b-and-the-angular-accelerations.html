Guest Question.

For the four-bar linkage in the posture shown in Fig. 4.8a, the constant input angular velocity is ω2 = 200 rad/s ccw. Determine the accelerations of points A and B, and the angular accelerations of links 3 and 4.

Admin
Solution

GRAPHIC SOLUTION
The velocity polygon must be drawn first, since the velocities of points A and B, and the angular velocities of links 3 and 4 are required for the acceleration analysis. The magnitude of the velocity of point A is

\(V_{A}=\omega_{2} R_{A O_{2}}=(200 rad / s )(6 / 12 ft )=100 ft / s.\)

Using this value, the velocity polygon can now be drawn as shown in Fig. 4.8b. Then, from the polygon, we measure the magnitudes of the velocity difference of points B and A, and the velocity of point B, respectively, as

\(V_{B A}=128 ft / s \text { and } V_{B}=129 ft / s\)

Therefore, the angular velocities of links 3 and 4, respectively, are

 

\(\omega_{3}=\frac{V_{B A}}{R_{B A}}=\frac{128 ft / s }{(18 / 12) ft }=85.3 rad / s ccw\),

 

\(\omega_{4}=\frac{V_{B O_{4}}}{R_{B O_{4}}}=\frac{129 ft / s }{(12 / 12) ft }=129 rad / s ccw,\)

 

where the directions are obtained from an examination of the velocity polygon. Now, the acceleration of point B can be obtained as follows. First, the acceleration-difference equation, Eq. (4.10), is written as

AP = AQ +APQ, (4.10)

AB = AA +ABA

or as

\(A_{O_{4}} + A_{BO_{4}} = A_{O_{2}} + A_{AO_{2}} + A_{BA}.\)

Next, this equation is written in terms of components as

\(\overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{A^{n}_{BO_{4}}} + \overset{?\sqrt{}}{A^{t}_{BO_{4}}} = \overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{A^{n}_{AO_{2}}} + A^{t}_{AO_{2}} + \overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{A^{n}_{BA}} + \overset{?\sqrt{}}{A^{t}_{BA}}.\) (1)

where

\(A_{A O_{2}}^{t}=\alpha_{2} R_{A O_{2}}=0,\)

\(A_{A}=A_{A O_{2}}^{n}=\omega_{2}^{2} R_{A O_{2}}=(200 rad / s )^{2}(6 / 12) ft =20000 ft / s ^{2},\)

\(A_{B A}^{n}=\frac{V_{B A}^{2}}{R_{B A}}=\frac{(128 ft / s )^{2}}{(18 / 12) ft }=10923 ft / s ^{2},\)

\(A_{B O_{4}}^{n}=\frac{V_{B O_{4}}^{2}}{R_{B O_{4}}}=\frac{(129 ft / s )^{2}}{(12 / 12) ft }=16641 ft / s ^{2}.\)

The two unknowns—that is, the magnitudes of the two tangential components of the acceleration-difference vectors—can now be obtained from the acceleration polygon. Choosing a convenient scale and an acceleration origin, OA, we construct \(A _{A O_{2}}^{n}\) (the terminus is denoted as point A), \(A _{B A}^{n}\), and then \(A _{B A}^{t}\)which is temporarily of indefinite length, since the magnitude of this vector is not yet known (see Fig. 4.8c).

Beginning again at the acceleration origin, OA, and using the left-hand side of Eq.(1),we now construct\(A _{B O_{4}}^{n}\)and then \(A _{B O_{4}}^{t}\) (which is temporarily of indefinite length, since the magnitude of this vector is also not yet known). The intersection of the two vectors, \(A _{B A}^{t}\) and \(A _{B O_{4}}^{t}\) , completes the acceleration polygon, as shown in Fig. 4.8c. The point of intersection is labeled the acceleration-image point, B. The circled numbers indicate the order of the construction steps; the methods of finding the directions of the vectors are also indicated, using the symbol || to indicate parallelism and the symbol ⊥ to indicate perpendicularity.

From the acceleration polygon, the two unknown magnitudes are measured as

\(A_{B O_{4}}^{t}=11900 ft / s ^{2} \text { and } A_{B A}^{t}=2000 ft / s ^{2}\)

The line from point OA to point B in the polygon is the magnitude of the acceleration of point B and is measured as

AB = 20 500 ft/s2.

The angular accelerations of links 3 and 4 are then computed as follows:

\(\alpha_{3}=\frac{A_{B A}^{t}}{R_{B A}}=\frac{2000 ft / s ^{2}}{(18 / 12) ft }=1333 rad / s ^{2} cw,\)

\(\alpha_{4}=\frac{A_{B O_{4}}^{t}}{R_{B O_{4}}}=\frac{11900 ft / s ^{2}}{(12 / 12) ft }=11900 rad / s ^{2} cw,\)

where the directions are obtained from an examination of the tangential components of the acceleration polygon.

ANALYTIC SOLUTION
The first two steps are to perform a posture analysis and a velocity analysis of the linkage. Since these steps were presented in Chaps. 2 and 3, only the results are presented here. The results of the posture analysis are shown in Fig. 4.9. In vector form, the position-difference vectors corresponding to the links are

\(R _{A O_{2}}=\left(\frac{6}{12}\right) ft \angle 135^{\circ}=-0.35355 \hat{ i }+0.35355 \hat{ j } ft,\)

 

\(R _{B A}=\left(\frac{18}{12}\right) ft \angle 22.4^{\circ}=1.38682 \hat{ i }+0.57161 \hat{ j } ft,\)

 

\(R _{B O_{4}}=\left(\frac{12}{12}\right) ft \angle 68.4^{\circ}=0.36812 \hat{ i }+0.92978 \hat{ j } ft.\)

 

For the given input angular velocity \(\omega _{2}=200 \hat{ k } rad / s,\) the results of the velocity analysis are

\(\omega_{3}=84.253 \hat{ k } rad / s \text { and } \omega_{4}=129.39 \hat{ k } rad / s.\)

Next, the known acceleration components are

\(A _{A O_{2}}^{n}= \omega _{2} \times\left( \omega _{2} \times R _{A O_{2}}\right)=14142 \hat{ i }-14142 \hat{ j } ft / s ^{2}= A _{A}\) (2)

\(A _{B A}^{n}= \omega _{3} \times\left( \omega _{3} \times R _{B A}\right)=-9844 \hat{ i }-4058 \hat{ j } ft / s ^{2}\) (3)

\(A _{B O_{4}}^{n}= \omega _{4} \times\left( \omega _{4} \times R _{B O_{4}}\right)=-6163 \hat{ i }-15567 \hat{ j } ft / s ^{2}\) (4)

Although the angular accelerations α3 and α4 have unknown magnitudes, we can incorporate them into the solution in the following manner:

 

\(A _{B A}^{t}= \alpha _{3} \times R _{B A}=\left|\begin{array}{ccc}\hat{ i } & \hat{ j } & \hat{ k } \\0 & 0 & \alpha_{3} \\1.38682 ft & 0.57161 ft & 0\end{array}\right|=-0.57161 \alpha_{3} \hat{ i }+1.38682 \alpha_{3} \hat{ j}ft,\) (5)

 

\(A _{B O_{4}}^{t}= \alpha _{4} \times R _{B O_{4}}=\left|\begin{array}{ccc}\hat{ i } & \hat{ j } & \hat{ k } \\0 & 0 & \alpha_{4} \\0.36812 ft & 0.92978 ft & 0\end{array}\right|=-0.92978 \alpha_{4} \hat{ i }+0.36812 \alpha_{4}\hat{j} ft .\) (6)

 

Writing the acceleration-difference equation for point B and noting that \(A _{A O_{2}}^{t}= 0\) gives

\(A _{B O_{4}}^{n}+ A _{B O_{4}}^{t}= A _{A O_{2}}^{n}+ A _{B A}^{n}+ A _{B A}^{t}\) (7)

Then, substituting Eqs. (2) through (6) into Eq. (7) and separating the \(\hat{ i } \text { and } \hat{ j }\) components, we obtain the following pair of simultaneous equations:

\((0.57161 ft ) \alpha_{3}-(0.92978 ft ) \alpha_{4}=10461 ft / s ^{2}\) (8)

\(-(1.38682 ft ) \alpha_{3}+(0.36812 ft ) \alpha_{4}=-2633 ft / s ^{2}\) (9)

Solving these equations, the angular accelerations of links 3 and 4, respectively, are

\(\alpha_{3}=-1300 \hat{ k } rad / s ^{2} \quad \text { and } \quad \alpha_{4}=-12050 \hat{ k } rad / s ^{2},\) (10)

where the two negative signs indicate that both angular accelerations are clockwise. The acceleration of point B can now be written from Eq. (4.11) as

\(A _{P}= A _{Q}+ A _{P Q}^{n}+ A _{P Q}^{t}\) (4.11)

 

\(A _{B}= A _{B O_{4}}^{n}+ A _{B O_{4}}^{t}\) (11)

The procedure is to substitute Eq. (10) into Eq. (6), which gives

\(A _{B O_{4}}^{t}=11204 \hat{ i }-4436 \hat{ j } ft / s ^{2}\) (12)

Then, substituting Eqs. (4) and (12) into Eq. (11), the acceleration of point B is

\(A _{B}=5041 \hat{ i }-20003 \hat{ j } ft / s ^{2}=20628 ft / s ^{2} \angle-75.86^{\circ}\)

Note that the four answers from the analytic method are in good agreement with, but more accurate than, the results of the graphic method.
