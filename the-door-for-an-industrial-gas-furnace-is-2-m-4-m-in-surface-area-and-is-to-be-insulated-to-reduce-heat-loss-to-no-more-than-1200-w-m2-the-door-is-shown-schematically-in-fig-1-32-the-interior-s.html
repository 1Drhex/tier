Guest Question.

The door for an industrial gas furnace is \(2\space m\times 4\space m\) in surface area and is to be insulated to reduce heat loss to no more than \(1200\space W/m^{2}\) The door is shown schematically in Fig. 1.32. The interior surface is 3/8 -in.-thick Inconel 600 sheet, and the outer surface is 1/4 -in.-thick sheet of stainless steel 316. Between these metal sheets a suitable thickness of insulation material is to be placed. The effective gas temperature inside the furnace is 1200°C, and the overall heat transfer coefficient between the gas and the door is \(U_{i}= 20\space W/m^{2}K\) . The heat transfer coefficient between the outer surface of the door and the surroundings at 20°C is \(\overset{-}{h}=5\space W/m^{2}\) . Select a suitable insulation material and size its thickness.

Admin
Solution

From Fig. 1.7 we estimate the thermal conductivity of the metal sheets to be approximately 43 W/m K. The thermal resistances of the two metal sheets are approximately

\(R=L / k \sim \frac{0.625 {in} .}{43 {~W} / {m}{K}} \times \frac{1 {~m}}{39.4 \text { in. }} \sim 3.7 \times 10^{-4} {~m}^{2} {~K} / {W}\)

These resistances are negligible compared to the other three resistances shown in the simplified thermal circuit below:

The temperature drop between the gas and the interior surface of the door at the specified heat flux is:

\(\Delta T=\frac{q / A}{U}=\frac{1200 {~W} / {m}^{2}}{20 {~W} / {m}^{2} {~K}}=60 {~K}\)

Hence, the temperature of the Inconel will be about 1140°C. This is acceptable since no appreciable structural load is applied.

From Fig. 1.31 we see that only milled alumina-silica chips can withstand the maximum temperature in the door. Thermal conductivity data are available only between 300 and 650°C. The trend of the data suggests that at higher temperatures when radiation becomes the dominant mechanism, the increase of\( k_{eff}\) with T will become more pronounced. We shall select the value at 650°C (0.27 W/mK) and then apply a safety factor to the insulation thickness. The temperature drop at the outer surface is :

\(\Delta T=\frac{1200 {~W} / {m}^{2}}{5 {~W} / {m}^{2} {~K}}=240^{\circ} {C}\)

Hence, ΔT across the insulation is \(1180^{\circ }C-(240+60)^{\circ }C =880 \space K \) . The insulation thickness for k=0.27 W/m K is :

\(L=\frac{k \Delta T}{q / A}=\frac{0.27 {~W} / {m} {K} \times 880 {~K}}{1200 {~W} / {m}^{2}}=0.2 {~m}\)

In view of the uncertainty, in the value of keff, and the possibility that the insulation may become more compact with use, a prudent design would double the value of insulation thickness. Additional insulation would also reduce the temperature of the outer surface of the door for safety, comfort, and ease of operation
