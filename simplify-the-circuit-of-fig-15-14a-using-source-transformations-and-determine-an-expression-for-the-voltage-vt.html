Guest Question.

Simplify the circuit of Fig. 15.14a using source transformations, and determine an expression for the voltage v(t).

Admin
Solution

With no initial currents or voltages specified, and a u(t) multiplying the voltage source, we conclude that there is no energy initially stored in the circuit. Thus, we draw the frequency-domain circuit as shown in Fig. 15.14b.

Our strategy will be to perform several source transformations in succession in order to combine the two 2/s Ω impedances and the 10 Ω resistor; we must leave the 9s Ω impedance alone as the desired quantity
V(s) appears across its terminals. We may now transform the voltage source and the leftmost 2/s Ω impedance into a current source

 

\(I ( s )=\left(\frac{2 s }{ s ^{2}+9}\right)\left(\frac{ s }{2}\right)=\frac{ s ^{2}}{ s ^{2}+9}\) A

 

in parallel with a 2/s Ω impedance.

As depicted in Fig. 15.15a, after this transformation, we have \(Z _{1} \equiv(2 / s ) \| 10=20 /(10 s +2) \Omega\) facing the current source. Performing another source transformation, we obtain a voltage source \(V _{2}( s )\) such that

 

\(V _{2}( s )=\left(\frac{ s ^{2}}{ s ^{2}+9}\right)\left(\frac{20}{10 s +2}\right)\)

 

This voltage source is in series with \(Z _{1}\) and also with the remaining 2/s impedance; combining \(Z _{1}\) and 2/s into a new impedance \(Z _{2}\) yields

 

\(Z _{2}=\frac{20}{10 s +2}+\frac{2}{ s }=\frac{40 s +4}{ s (10 s +2)}\) Ω

 

The resulting circuit is shown in Fig. 15.15b. At this stage, we are now ready to obtain an expression for the voltage V(s) using simple voltage division:

 

\(V ( s )=\left(\frac{ s ^{2}}{ s ^{2}+9}\right)\left(\frac{20}{10 s +2}\right) \frac{9 s }{9 s +\left[\frac{40 s +4}{ s (10 s +2)}\right]}\)

 

\(=\frac{180 s ^{4}}{\left( s ^{2}+9\right)\left(90 s ^{3}+18 s ^{2}+40 s +4\right)}\)

 

Both terms in the denominator possess complex roots. Employing MATLAB to expand the denominator and then determine the residues,

 

EDU» d1 = ’sˆ2 + 9’;
EDU» d2 = ’90*sˆ3 + 18*sˆ2 + 40*s + 4’;
EDU» d = symmul(d1,d2);
EDU» denominator = expand(d);
EDU» den = sym2poly(denominator);
EDU» num = [180 0 0 0 0];
EDU» [r p y] = residue(num,den);

 

we find

 

\(V ( s )=\frac{1.047+j 0.0716}{ s -j 3}+\frac{1.047-j 0.0716}{ s +j 3}-\frac{0.0471+j 0.0191}{ s +0.04885-j 0.6573}\)

 

\(-\frac{0.0471-j 0.0191}{ s +0.04885+j 0.6573}+\frac{5.590 \times 10^{-5}}{ s +0.1023}\)

 

Taking the inverse transform of each term, writing 1.047 + j0.0716 as \(1.049 e^{j 3.912^{\circ}} \text { and } 0.0471+j 0.0191 \text { as } 0.05083 e^{j 157.9^{\circ}}\) results in

 

\(v(t)=1.049 e^{j 3.912^{\circ}} e^{j 3 t} u(t)+1.049 e^{-j 3.912^{\circ}} e^{-j 3 t} u(t)\)

 

\(+0.05083 e^{-j 157.9^{\circ}} e^{-0.04885 t} e^{-j 0.6573 t} u(t)\)

 

\(+0.05083 e^{+j 157.9^{\circ}} e^{-0.04885 t} e^{+j 0.6573 t} u(t)\)

 

\(+5.590 \times 10^{-5} e^{-0.1023 t} u(t)\)

 

Converting the complex exponentials to sinusoids then allows us to write a slightly simplified expression for our voltage

 

\(v(t)=\left[5.590 \times 10^{-5} e^{-0.1023 t}+2.098 \cos \left(3 t+3.912^{\circ}\right)\right.\)

 

\(\left.+0.1017 e^{-0.04885 t} \cos \left(0.6573 t+157.9^{\circ}\right)\right] u(t)\) V
