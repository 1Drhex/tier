Finding Electrostatic Equilibrium

GOAL Apply Coulomb’s law in one dimension.

PROBLEM Three charges lie along the x-axis as in Figure 15.7. The positive charge \(q_{1}=15 \mu C \text { is at } x=2.0 m\), and the positive charge \(q_{2}=6.0 \mu C\) is at the origin. Where must a negative charge \(q_{3}\) be placed on the x-axis so that the resultant electric force on it is zero?

STRATEGY If \(q_{3}\) is to the right or left of the other two charges, the net force on \(q_{3}\) can’t be zero because then \(\overrightarrow{ F }_{13} \text { and } \overrightarrow{ F }_{23}\) act in the same direction. Consequently, \(q_{3}\) must lie between the two other charges. Write \(\overrightarrow{ F }_{13} \text { and } \overrightarrow{ F }_{23}\) in terms of the unknown coordinate position x, then sum them and set them equal to zero, solving for the unknown. The solution can be obtained with the quadratic formula.

Admin
Solution

Write the x-component of \(\overrightarrow{ F }_{13}\): \(F_{13 x}=+k_{e} \frac{\left(15 \times 10^{-6} C \right)\left|q_{3}\right|}{(2.0 m -x)^{2}}\)

 

Write the x-component of \(\overrightarrow{ F }_{23}\): \(F_{23 x}=-k_{e} \frac{\left(6.0 \times 10^{-6} C \right)\left|q_{3}\right|}{x^{2}}\)

 

Set the sum equal to zero: \(k_{e} \frac{\left(15 \times 10^{-6} C \right)\left|q_{3}\right|}{(2.0 m -x)^{2}}-k_{e} \frac{\left(6.0 \times 10^{-6} C \right)\left|q_{3}\right|}{x^{2}}=0\)

 

Cancel \(k_{e}, 10^{-6} \text {, and } q_{3}\) from the equation and rearrange terms (explicit significant figures and units are temporarily suspended for clarity): (1) \(6(2-x)^{2}=15 x^{2}\)

 

Put this equation into standard quadratic form, \(a x^{2}+b x+c=0 \text { : }\) \(\begin{aligned}6\left(4-4 x+x^{2}\right) &=15 x^{2} \rightarrow 2\left(4-4 x+x^{2}\right)=5 x^{2} \\3 x^{2}+8 x-8 &=0\end{aligned}\)

 

Apply the quadratic formula: \(x=\frac{-8 \pm \sqrt{64-(4)(3)(-8)}}{2 \cdot 3}=\frac{-4 \pm 2 \sqrt{10}}{3}\)

 

Only the positive root makes sense: \(x=0.77 m\)

 

REMARKS Notice that physical reasoning was required to choose between the two possible answers for x, which is nearly always the case when quadratic equations are involved. Use of the quadratic formula could have been avoided by taking the square root of both sides of Equation (1); however, this shortcut is often unavailable.
