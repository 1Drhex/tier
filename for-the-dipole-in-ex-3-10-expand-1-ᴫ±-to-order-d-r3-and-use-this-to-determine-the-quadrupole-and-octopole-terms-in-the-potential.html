Guest Question.

For the dipole in , expand 1/ᴫ± to order \((d / r)^{3}\), and use this to determine the quadrupole and octopole terms in the potential.

Admin
Solution

Using Eq. 3.94 with \(r^{\prime}=d / 2 \text { and } \alpha=\theta \) (Fig. 3.26):

\(\frac{1}{ᴫ}=\frac{1}{r} \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }{} \left(\frac{r^{\prime}}{r}\right)^{n} P_{n}(\cos \alpha)\) (3.94)

\(\frac{1}{ᴫ+}=\frac{1}{r} \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }{} \left(\frac{d}{2 r}\right)^{n} P_{n}(\cos \theta)\) ;

for ᴫ _ , we let \(\theta \rightarrow 180^{\circ}+\theta, \text { so } \cos \theta \rightarrow-\cos \theta\) :

\(\frac{1}{ᴫ_{-}}=\frac{1}{r} \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }{} \left(\frac{d}{2 r}\right)^{n} P_{n}(-\cos \theta)\) .

But \(P_{n}(-x)=(-1)^{n} P_{n}(x)\) , so

\(V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} q\left(\frac{1}{ᴫ_{+}}-\frac{1}{ᴫ_{-}}\right)=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} q \frac{1}{r} \sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }{} \left(\frac{d}{2 r}\right)^{n}\left[P_{n}(\cos \theta)-P_{n}(-\cos \theta)\right]=\frac{2 q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r} \sum\limits_{n=1,3,5,\cdot \cdot \cdot }^{}{} \left(\frac{d}{2 r}\right)^{n} P_{n}(\cos \theta)\) .

Therefore

\(V_{ dip }=\frac{2 q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{2 r} P_{1}(\cos \theta)=\frac{q d \cos \theta}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r^{2}}\) , while \(V_{\text {quad }}=0 \text {. }\) .

\(V_{\text {oct }}=\frac{2 q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r}\left(\frac{d}{2 r}\right)^{3} P_{3}(\cos \theta)=\frac{2 q}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{d^{3}}{8 r^{4}} \frac{1}{2}\left(5 \cos ^{3} \theta-3 \cos \theta\right)=\frac{q d^{3}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{1}{8 r^{4}}\left(5 \cos ^{3} \theta-3 \cos \theta\right)\) .
