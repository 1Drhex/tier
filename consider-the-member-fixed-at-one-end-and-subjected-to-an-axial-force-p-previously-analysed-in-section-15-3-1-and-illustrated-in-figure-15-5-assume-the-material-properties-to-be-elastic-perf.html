Guest Question.

Consider the member fixed at one end and subjected to an axial force P, previously analysed in Section 15.3.1 and illustrated in Figure 15.5. Assume the material properties to be elastic– perfectly plastic with values detailed in Figure 15.5c. Determine the deformations, expressed in terms of strain at the level of the reference axis (taken at mid-height of the section) and curvature, with a cross- sectional analysis implemented with the Newton–Raphson method for the following load steps: (1) \( N_e = 1000 \) kN, \( M_e = 0 \) kNm; (2) \( N_e = 2200 \) kN, \( M_e = 0 \) kNm; and (3) \( N_e = 2600 \) kN, \( M_e = 0 \) kNm. For the convergence criteria, use \( norm_{tol2} \) defined in Equation 15.59b equal to 0.001.

\( norm_{tol2} = \frac{\left|r ^{(i+1)}_R\right| }{\left|r_e\right| } \) (15.59b).

Admin
Solution

We will discretise the cross-section into three layers to account for the presence of different materials, as shown in Figure 15.18. A higher number of layers is usually adopted in the modelling, but for this simple problem, three layers are acceptable and have been specified here to keep the complexity of the solution to a minimum.

On the basis of the elastic–perfectly plastic material assumptions, the partial derivatives of the stress–strain relationship can be re-written based on Equations 15.56 as:

\( \frac{\partial \sigma }{\partial \varepsilon } = \frac{\partial (E\varepsilon )}{\partial \varepsilon } = E \) for \( \left|\varepsilon \right|\leq \varepsilon _y \) (15.56a).

\( \frac{\partial \sigma }{\partial \varepsilon } = \frac{\partial (f_y )}{\partial \varepsilon } = 0 \) for \( \left|\varepsilon \right|> \varepsilon _y \) (15.56b).

\( \frac{\partial \sigma }{\partial \varepsilon } = \frac{\partial (E\varepsilon )}{\partial \varepsilon } = E \) for \( \left|\varepsilon \right|\leq \varepsilon _y \) (15.60a).

\( \frac{\partial \sigma }{\partial \varepsilon } = \frac{\partial (f_y )}{\partial \varepsilon } = 0 \) for \( \left|\varepsilon \right|> \varepsilon _y \) (15.60b).

Load step 1: \( N_e = 1000 \) kN, \( M_e = 0 \) kNm.

Iteration 1 (i = 1)

The initial tangent rigidity \( r_t(ε^{(1)}) \) to be used in the analysis is calculated assuming the member to be unloaded with \( ε^{(1)}_r = 0 \) and \( κ^{(1)} = 0 \) , which is based on an initial ‘guessed’ condition of the structure being undeformed. The strains are all zero and, consequently, the internal actions and their derivatives are calculated as (Equations 15.57 and 15.58):

\( N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr) = \int\limits_{A}{\sigma }dA = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr) A_j} \) (15.57a).

\( M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr) =-\int\limits_{A}{y\sigma }dA = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{y_j\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr) A_j} \) (15.57b).

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } = \int\limits_{A} {\frac{∂ \sigma}{∂\varepsilon}}dA = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{\frac{∂\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j \) (15.58a).

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = – \int\limits_{A} {y\frac{∂ \sigma}{∂\varepsilon}}dA = – \sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{y_j\frac{∂\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j \) (15.58b).

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } =- \int\limits_{A} {y\frac{∂ \sigma}{∂\varepsilon}}dA = -\sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{y_j\frac{∂\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j \) (15.58c).

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = + \int\limits_{A} {y^2\frac{∂ \sigma}{∂\varepsilon}}dA = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{n_j}{y^2_j\frac{∂\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r,\kappa ^{(i)}\bigr)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j \) (15.58d).

\( N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3}{\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r,\kappa ^{(1)}\bigr) A_j} = E_1 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_1\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_1 + E_2 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_2\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_2 + E_3 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_3\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_3 = 0 \) N.

\( M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = -\sum\limits_{j=1}^{3}{y_j\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r,\kappa ^{(1)}\bigr) A_j} = -y_1 E_1 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_1\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_1 -y_2 E_2 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_2\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_2 -y_3 E_3 \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r – y_3\kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) A_3 = 0 \) Nmm.

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂ σ (y_j)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j = E_1 A_1 + E_2 A_2 + E_3 A_3 \)

\( = (2 ×10^5 )× (2 ×10^3 )+ (2 ×10^5 )× (2 ×10^3 )+ (2 ×10^5 )× (2 ×10^3) = 12 ×10^8 \) N.

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = – \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y_j A_j = – E_1 y_1A_1 – E_2 y_2A_2 – E_3 y_3A_3 \)

\( = – (2 ×10^5) × 20 × (2 ×10^3 ) – (2 ×10^5 ) × 20 × (2 ×10^3 ) – (2 ×10^5 ) × (-20) × (2 ×10^3 ) = 0 \) Nmm.

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } = – \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y_j A_j = – E_1 y_1A_1 – E_2 y_2A_2 + E_3 y_3A_3 \)

\( = – (2 ×10^5) × 20 × (2 ×10^3 ) – (2 ×10^5 ) ×0 × (2 ×10^3 ) – (2 ×10^5 ) × (-20) × (2 ×10^3 ) = 0 \) Nmm.

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y^2_j A_j = + E_1 y^2_1A_1 + E_2 y^2_2A_2 + E_3 y^2_3A_3 \)

\( = + (2 ×10^5) × 20^2 × (2 ×10^3 ) + (2 ×10^5 ) × 0^2 × (2 ×10^3 ) + (2 ×10^5 ) × (-20)^2 × (2 ×10^3 ) = 3.2 × 10^{11} \) Nmm².

The residual loads to be used in the first iteration are (Equation 15.51):

\( N^{(i)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon^{(i)}_r , \kappa^{(i)}\Bigr) \) (15.51a).

\( M^{(i)}_R = M_e – M\Bigl(\varepsilon^{(i)}_r , \kappa^{(i)}\Bigr) \) (15.51b).

\( N^{(1)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon^{(1)}_r , \kappa^{(1)}\Bigr) = 1000 ×10^3 − 0 =1000 ×10^3 \) N.

\( M^{(1)}_R = M_e – M\Bigl(\varepsilon^{(1)}_r , \kappa^{(1)}\Bigr) = 0 − 0 = 0 \) Nmm.

We can now write the system of Equations 15.50 to solve for the unknown \( Δε^{(1)}_r \) and \( Δκ^{(1)} \) :

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } Δ \varepsilon ^{(i)}_r + \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } Δ \kappa^{(i)} = N^{(i)}_R \) (15.50a).

\( \frac{∂M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } Δ \varepsilon ^{(i)}_r + \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } Δ \kappa^{(i)} = M^{(i)}_R \) (15.50b).

\( 12 × 10^{18} × Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r + 0 × Δ \kappa^{(1)} = 1000 × 10^3 \).

\( 0 × Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r + 3.2 × 10^{11} Δ \kappa^{(1)} = 0 \) .

from which: \( Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r = 0.8333 × 10^{-3}\) and \( Δ \kappa^{(1)} = 0 mm^{–1} \) .

The convergence of the solution is calculated based on \( norm_{tol2} \) (Equation 15.59b), which requires the evaluation of the residual internal actions \( N^{(2)}_R \) and \( M^{(2)}_R \) (Equations 15.51). To achieve this, we calculate the strain values for the second iteration:

\( norm_{tol2} = \frac{\left|r ^{(i+1)}_R\right| }{\left|r_e\right| } \) (15.59b).

\( N^{(i)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa^{(i)}\Bigr) \) (15.51a).

\( M^{(i)}_R = M_e – M\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa^{(i)}\Bigr) \) (15.51b).

\( \varepsilon ^{(2)}_r = \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r + Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r = 0 + 0.8333 × 10^{-3} = 0.8333 × 10^{-3} \).

\( \kappa^{(2)} = \kappa^{(1)} + Δ \kappa^{(1)} = 0 mm^{–1} \).

and the internal actions based on \( \varepsilon ^{(2)}_r \) and \( \kappa^{(2)} \):

\( N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3}{\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r,\kappa ^{(2)}\bigr) A_j} = 1000 × 10^3 \) N.

\( M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3}{y_j\sigma \bigl(y_j,\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r,\kappa ^{(2)}\bigr) A_j} = 0 \) kNm.

The residual loads are then evaluated as:

\( N^{(2)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 1000 × 10^3 – 1000 × 10^3 = 0 \) N.

\( M^{(2)}_R = M_e – M\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 0-0 = 0 \) kNm.

on the basis of which:

\( norm_{tol2} = \frac{\left|r ^{(i+1)}_R\right| }{\left|r_e\right| } = \frac{\sqrt{N^{(2)^2}_R + M^{(2)^2}_R }}{\sqrt{N^2_e + M^2_e}} = \frac{\sqrt{0^2 + 0^2 }}{\sqrt{(1000 × 10^3)^2 +0^2}} = 0 \)

which satisfies the convergence criterion being \( norm_{tol2} \) below 0.001. A nil value for \( norm_{tol2} \) is expected when dealing with linear–elastic materials. We can now move to the next load step.
If we would have used \( norm_{tol1} \) (Equation 15.59a) as the convergence criterion, its value at the end of iteration 1 would be:

\( norm_{tol1}=\frac{\left|\Delta \varepsilon ^{(i)}\right| }{\left|\varepsilon ^{(i+1)}\right| } \) (15.59a).

\( norm_{tol1}=\frac{\left|\Delta \varepsilon ^{(1)}\right| }{\left|\varepsilon ^{(2)}\right| } = \frac{\sqrt{\Delta \varepsilon^{(1)^2}_r + Δ κ^{(1)^2}_r }}{\sqrt{\varepsilon^{(2)^2}_r + κ^{(2)^2}}} = \frac{\sqrt{(0.8333 × 10^{-3})^2 + 0^2}}{\sqrt{(0.8333 × 10^{-3})^2 + 0^2}} = 1 \) .

and using this criterion, we would have required a second iteration to be performed in this case.
This is the case because the materials are still in the linear–elastic range.

Load step 2: \( N_e = 2200 \) kN, \( M_e = 0 \) kNm.

We will now continue the analysis from the previous load step adopting the strain values
obtained at the end of the previous load step for our first iteration with i = 1 (at load step 2): \( \varepsilon^{(1)}_r = 0.8333 × 10^{-3} \) and \( κ^{(1)} = 0 mm^{-1} \) We have already calculated the corresponding internal actions as \( N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = 1000 × 10^{-3} \) N and \( M\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = 0 \) kNm. The residual actions to be used in the first iteration of load step 2 are:

\( N^{(1)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = 2200 × 10^3 – 1000 × 10^3 = 1200 × 10^3 \)N.

\( M^{(1)}_R = M_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr) = 0 \) Nmm.

Considering the fact that at \( \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r = 0.8333 × 10^{-3} \) and \( κ^{(1)} = 0 mm^{-1} \), the material properties of the cross-sections are still linear–elastic:

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂ σ (y_j)}{∂\varepsilon}} A_j = E_1 A_1 + E_2 A_2 + E_3 A_3 = 12 × 10^8 \) N.

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = – \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y_j A_j = – E_1 y_1A_1 – E_2 y_2A_2 – E_3 y_3A_3 = 0 \) Nmm.

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } = – \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y_j A_j = – E_1 y_1A_1 – E_2 y_2A_2 + E_3 y_3A_3 = 0 \) Nmm.

\( \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(1)}_r , \kappa ^{(1)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{3} {\frac{∂\sigma (yj) }{∂\varepsilon}}y^2_j A_j = E_1 y^2_1A_1 + E_2 y^2_2A_2 + E_3 y^2_3A_3 = 3.2 × 10^{11} \) Nmm².

The system of Equations 15.50 can then be written as:

\( \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } Δ \varepsilon ^{(i)}_r + \frac{∂ N \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } Δ \kappa^{(i)} = N^{(i)}_R \) (15.50a).

\( \frac{∂M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\varepsilon _r } Δ \varepsilon ^{(i)}_r + \frac{∂ M \Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(i)}_r , \kappa ^{(i)}\Bigr)}{∂\kappa } Δ \kappa^{(i)} = M^{(i)}_R \) (15.50b).

\( 12 × 10^8 × Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r + 0 × Δ \kappa ^{(1)} = 1200 × 10^3 \).

\( 0 × Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r + 3.2 × 10^{11} × Δ \kappa ^{(1)} = 0 \).

and solving gives \( Δ \varepsilon ^{(1)}_r = 1 × 10^{-3} \) and \( Δ \kappa ^{(1)} = 0 mm^{-1} \).

Based on this:

\( N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 2066.6 × 10^{3} \) N, and \( M\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 0 \) kNm.

and

\( N^{(2)}_R = N_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 2200 × 10^3 – 2066.6 × 10^3 = 133.3 × 10^3 \)N.

\( M^{(2)}_R = M_e – N\Bigl(\varepsilon ^{(2)}_r , \kappa ^{(2)}\Bigr) = 0-0 = 0 \) Nmm.

Convergence is then evaluated with \( norm_{tol2}\) :

\( norm_{tol2}= \frac{\sqrt{N^{(2)^2}_R + M^{(2)^2}_R }}{\sqrt{N^2_e + M^2_e}} = \frac{\sqrt{(133.3 × 10^3)^2 + 0^2 }}{\sqrt{(2200 × 10^3)^2 +0^2}} = 0.0606 \).

which is greater than the limit value 0.001 and the analysis continues to the next iteration i = 2.

It is worth pointing out that the ability to converge to a solution when material 2 also starts to yield is possible only because the initial ‘guessed’ solution used at the beginning of load step 3 did not cause yielding in material 2. In fact, if we were to repeat the same calculation starting with higher values for \( ε^{(1)}_r \) and \( κ^{(1)} \), we would not have been able to find a solution because \( r_t(ε^{(i)}) \) would become a zero matrix, therefore not enabling the calculation of the unknowns \( Δε^{(1)}_r \) and \( Δκ^{(1)} \).
This consideration highlights the importance of carefully interpreting numerical results, especially when obtained from nonlinear analyses.
It is noted that this solution is identical to that calculated in the illustrative example in Section 15.3.1.
