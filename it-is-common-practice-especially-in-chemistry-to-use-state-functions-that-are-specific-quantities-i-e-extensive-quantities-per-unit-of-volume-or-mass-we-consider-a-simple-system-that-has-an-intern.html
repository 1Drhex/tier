Guest Question.

It is common practice, especially in chemistry, to use state functions that are specific quantities, i.e. extensive quantities per unit of volume or mass.We consider a simple system that has an internal energy \(U(S, V, {N_A})\) where A = 1, . . . , r. The volume densities u, s and \(n_A\) are defined as,

\(u=\frac{U}{V}\), \(s=\frac{S}{V}\) and \(n_A=\frac{N_A}{V}\).

The mass densities \(u^*, s^* and v^*\) are defined as,

\(u*=\frac{U}{M}\), \(s*=\frac{S}{M}\) and \(v*=\frac{V}{M}\).

The mass chemical potential \(μ*_ A\) and mass concentration \(c*_ A\) of substance A are defined as,

\(\mu ^{*}_{A} =\frac{\mu _A}{M^{*}_{A} }\). and \(c^{*}_{A} =\frac{N_AM^{*}_{A} }{M}\).

where \(M^{*}_{A}\) is the molar mass of substance A.

1. Determine the Gibbs, Euler and Gibbs–Duhem relations expressed in terms of u, s and \(n_A\).
2. Determine the Gibbs, Euler and Gibbs–Duhem relations expressed in terms of \(u^∗, s^∗, v^∗\) and \(c^{*}_{A}\).

Admin
Solution

1. The Gibbs relation (4.1) divided by the volume V is written as,

\(dU = TdS − pdV +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AdN_A}\).

\(\frac{dU}{V}=T\frac{dS}{V}-p\frac{dV}{V}\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _A\frac{dN_A}{V} }\).

Moreover, the differentials can be expanded as,

\(du=d\Bigl(\frac{U}{V} \Bigr)=\frac{dU}{V}-U\frac{dV}{V^2}\).

\(ds=d\Bigl(\frac{S}{V} \Bigr)=\frac{dS}{V}-S\frac{dV}{V^2}\).

\(dn_A=d\Bigl(\frac{N_A}{V} \Bigr)=\frac{dN_A}{V}-N_A\frac{dV}{V^2}\).

Thus,

\(du − Tds −\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _Adn_A} +\Biggl(U-TS+pV-\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AN_A}\Biggr)\frac{dV}{V^2} =0\).

Using the Euler relation (4.7), the term in brackets vanishes and we find the volume-specific Gibbs relation, i.e.

\(U=TS-pV+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AN_A}\).

\(du = Tds +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _Adn_A}\).

The Euler relation (4.1) divided by the volume V is written as,

\(dU = TdS − pdV +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AdN_A}\).

\(\frac{U}{V}=T\frac{S}{V} -p+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _A\frac{N_A}{V} }\).

which yields the volume specific Euler relation, i.e.

\(u=Ts-p+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r} {\mu_An_A}\).

The differentiation of the volume specific Euler relation is written as,

\(du = Tds + s dT − dp +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{(\mu _Adn_A+n_Ad\mu _A)}\).

Taking into account the volume-specific Gibbs relation, we obtain the volume-specific Gibbs–Duhem relation, i.e.

\(s dT − dp +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{n _Ad\mu _A}\).

2. The Gibbs relation (4.1) divided by the mass M is written as

\(dU = TdS − pdV +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AdN_A}\).

\(\frac{dU}{M}=T\frac{dS}{M} -p\frac{dV}{M} +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _A\frac{dN_A}{M}}\).

Moreover, the differentials can be written as,

\(du^*=d\Bigl(\frac{U}{M} \Bigr)=\frac{dU}{M}-U\frac{dM}{M^2}\).

\(ds^*=d\Bigl(\frac{S}{M} \Bigr)=\frac{dS}{M}-S\frac{dM}{M^2}\).

\(dv^*=d\Bigl(\frac{V}{M} \Bigr)=\frac{dV}{M}-V\frac{dM}{M^2}\).

\(\frac{dc^{*}_{A} }{M_A} =d\Bigl(\frac{N_A}{M} \Bigr)=\frac{dN_A}{M}-N_A\frac{dM}{M^2}\).

Thus,

\(du^*-Tds^*+pdv^*-\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu ^{*}_{A}dc^{*}_{A} }+\biggl(U-TS+pV-\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AN_A} \biggr) \frac{dM}{M^2} =0\).

Using the Euler relation (4.7), the term in brackets vanishes and we find the mass-specific Gibbs relation, i.e.

\(U=TS-pV+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AN_A}\).

\(du^*=Tds^*-pdv^*+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu ^{*}_{A}dc^{*}_{A} }\).

The Euler relation (4.1) divided by the mass M is written as,

\(dU = TdS − pdV +\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu _AdN_A}\).

\( \frac{U}{M}=T\frac{S}{M}-p\frac{V}{M}+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\frac{\mu _A}{M_A}\frac{N_AM_A}{M} }\).

which yields the mass-specific Euler relation, i.e.

\(u^*=Ts^*-pv^*+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{\mu ^{*}_{A}c^{*}_{A} }\).

The differentiation of the mass-specific Euler relation is written as,

\(du^*=Tds^*+s^*dT-pdv^*-v^*dp+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{(\mu ^{*}_{A}dc^{*}_{A}+c^{*}_{A}d\mu ^{*}_{A} )} \).

Taking into account the mass specific Gibbs relation, we obtain the mass specific Gibbs– Duhem relation, i.e.

\(s^*dT-v^*dp+\sum\limits_{A=1}^{r}{c^{*}_{A}d\mu ^{*}_{A} } =0\).
