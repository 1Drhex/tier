Guest Question.

Let \(T: \mathscr{P}_{2} \rightarrow \mathscr{P}_{2}\) be the linear transformation defined by

\( T(p(x))=p(2 x-1) \)

(a) Find the matrix of \(T\) with respect to the basis \(\mathcal{B}=\left\{1+x, 1-x, x^{2}\right\}\) of \(\mathscr{P}_{2}\). (b) Show that \(T\) is diagonalizable and find a basis \(\mathcal{C}\) for \(\mathscr{P}_{2}\) such that \([T]_{\mathcal{C}}\) is a diagonal matrix.

Admin
Solution

(a) In , we found that the matrix of \(T\) with respect to the standard basis \(\mathcal{E}=\left\{1, x, x^{2}\right\}\) is

\( [T]_{\mathcal{E}}=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -4 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{array}\right] \)

The change-of-basis matrix from \(\mathcal{B}\) to \(\mathcal{E}\) is

\( P=P_{\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \mathcal{B}}=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \)

It follows that the matrix of \(T\) with respect to \(\mathcal{B}\) is

\( \begin{aligned} [T]_{\mathcal{B}} &=P^{-1}[T]_{\mathcal{E}} P \\ &=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & -4 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \\ &=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 0 & -\frac{3}{2} \\ -1 & 2 & \frac{5}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned} \)

(Check this.)

(b) The eigenvalues of \([T]_{\varepsilon}\) are 1,2 , and 4 (why?), so we know from Theorem \(4.25\) that \([T]_{\varepsilon}\) is diagonalizable. Eigenvectors corresponding to these eigenvalues are

\( \left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right],\left[\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right],\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right] \)

respectively. Therefore, setting

\( P=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \text { and } D=\left[\begin{array}{lll} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{array}\right] \)

we have \(P^{-1}[T]_{\varepsilon} P=D\). Furthermore, \(P\) is the change-of-basis matrix from a basis \(\mathcal{C}\) to \(\mathcal{E}\), and the columns of \(P\) are thus the coordinate vectors of \(\mathcal{C}\) in terms of \(\mathcal{E}\). It follows that

\( \mathcal{C}=\left\{1,-1+x, 1-2 x+x^{2}\right\} \)

and \([T]_{c}=D\).

Theorem 4.25 \(\text { If } A \text { is an } n \times n \text { matrix with } n \text { distinct eigenvalues, then } A \text { is diagonalizable. }\)
