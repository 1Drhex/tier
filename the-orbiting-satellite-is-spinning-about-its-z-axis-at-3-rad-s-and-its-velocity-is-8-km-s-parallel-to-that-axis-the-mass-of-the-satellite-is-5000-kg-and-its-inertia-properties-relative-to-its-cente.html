Guest Question.

The orbiting satellite is spinning about its \(z\) axis at \(3 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\), and its velocity is \(8 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{s}\) parallel to that axis. The mass of the satellite is \(5000 \mathrm{~kg}\), and its inertia properties relative to its center of mass \(C\) are \(I_{x x}=32~000, I_{y y}=\) \(40~000, I_{z z}=3~600, I_{x y}=I_{x z}=I_{y z}=0 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2} .\) A \(2\text{-}\mathrm{kg}\) meteorite, whose velocity is \(-9 \bar{i}+12 \bar{j} \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{s}\), impacts the satellite at point \(A\) in the \(y z\) plane and then is embedded in the satellite’s wall. The embedding process is completed in an interval of \(400 \mathrm{~ms}\). Determine the velocity of the satellite’s center of mass and its angular velocity immediately after the collision. Also determine the average impact force exerted by the meteorite and how much energy is dissipated in the collision. (The deposition of energy is one of the damage mechanisms.)

Admin
Solution

This example explores how to treat impulsive forces by use of systems concepts. The only force that is significant during the impact is the interaction force between the meteorite and the satellite, which is very large because the embedding process is very short and there is a large velocity difference at the point of impact. Both bodies move freely, so the interaction force \(\bar{F}\) is the only force to consider. The body-fixed reference frame is already defined, so there is no need to draw a free-body diagram.

The external force and moment resultants vanish for this system. Consequently both linear and angular momentum are conserved. There is no fixed point in this situation, so we formulate the angular momentum with respect to the center of mass of both bodies, which we designate as point \(G .\) The meteorite’s small size makes it permissible to consider it to be a particle, so its angular momentum relative to its own center of mass may be ignored. The linear momentum part of Eqs. (6.4.6) therefore requires that

\(\begin{array}{c}\displaystyle\sum\limits_{j=1}^N m\left(\bar{v}_{G j}\right)_{2}=\sum_{j=1}^{N} m\left(\bar{v}_{G j}\right)_{1}+\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \Sigma \bar{F} d t, \\\\\displaystyle\sum\limits_{j=1}^N \left(\bar{H}_{G j}\right)_{2}+\sum_{j=1}^{N}\left(\bar{r}_{G j / B} \times m_{j} \bar{v}_{G j / B}\right)_{2} \\\\=\displaystyle\sum\limits_{j=1}^N\left(\bar{H}_{G j}\right)_{1}+\sum_{j=1}^{N}\left(\bar{r}_{G j / B} \times m_{j} \bar{v}_{G j / B}\right)_{1}+\int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \Sigma \bar{M}_{B} d t.\end{array}\) (6.4.6)

 

\(m_{s}\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}+m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{2}=m_{s}\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{1}+m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}, (1)\)

whereas the angular momentum equation gives

\(\begin{array}{l}\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{2}+\left(\bar{r}_{C / G}\right)_{2} \times m_{s}\left(\bar{v}_{C / G}\right)_{2}+\left(\bar{r}_{m / G}\right)_{2} \times m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{m / G}\right)_{2} \\\\=\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{1}+\left(\bar{r}_{C / G}\right)_{1} \times m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{C / G}\right)_{1}+\left(\bar{r}_{m / G}\right)_{1} \times m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{m / G}\right)_{1}.\end{array} (2) \)

The positions and velocities relative to the center of mass are governed by the first moment of mass. Using the satellite’s center and the meteorite’s location as alternative reference points for this evaluation leads to

\(\large \begin{array}{c}\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right) \bar{r}_{G / C}=m_{m} \bar{r}_{m / C} \Longrightarrow \bar{r}_{C / G}=-\bar{r}_{G / C}=-\frac{m_{m}}{\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)} \bar{r}_{m / C}, \\\\\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right) \bar{r}_{G / m}=m_{s} \bar{r}_{C / m} \Longrightarrow \bar{r}_{m / G}=-\bar{r}_{G / m}=-\frac{m_{s}}{\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)}\left(-\bar{r}_{m / C}\right) .\end{array} \)

These relations are general, so they may be differentiated to describe the relative velocity terms in Eq. (2). The result is

\(\bar{v}_{C / G}=-\frac{m_{m}}{\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)} \bar{v}_{m / C}, \quad \bar{v}_{m / G}=\frac{m_{s}}{\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)} \bar{v}_{m / C}.\)

Substitution of these relations into Eq. (2) simplifies that equation to

\(\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{2}+\frac{m_{s} m_{m}}{m_{s}+m_{m}}\left(\bar{r}_{m / C}\right)_{2} \times\left(\bar{v}_{m / C}\right)_{2}=\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{1}+\frac{m_{s} m_{m}}{m_{s}+m_{m}}\left(\bar{r}_{m / C}\right)_{1} \times\left(\bar{v}_{m / C}\right)_{1}. (3)\)

By definition, \(\bar{v}_{m / C}=\bar{v}_{m}-\bar{v}_{C}\). The initial values of both velocities are given. To determine the final relative velocity we observe that embedding of the meteorite means that its velocity will match that of point \(A\) on the satellite. Therefore its final velocity is related to the velocity of point \(C\) by the fact that points \(A\) and \(C\) belong to the same rigid body. Also, as a consequence of taking the embedding process to be impulsive, positions are the same at the beginning and end of the impact, so we take \(\left(\bar{r}_{m / C}\right)_{2}=\left(\bar{r}_{m / C}\right)_{1}=\bar{r}_{A / C} .\) Another aspect of ignoring position changes is that the given body-fixed \(x y z\) coordinate system may be used to describe vectors throughout the duration of the impact. Thus we have

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}=-9000 \bar{i}+12000 \bar{j}, \quad\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{1}=8000 \bar{k} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}, \quad \bar{\omega}_{1}=3 \bar{k} \quad \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}, \\\\\left(\bar{v}_{m / C}\right)_{1}=\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}-\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{1}=-9000 \bar{i}+12000 \bar{j}-8000 \bar{k} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}, \\\\\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{2}=\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}+\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}, \quad\left(\bar{v}_{m / C}\right)_{2}=\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}.\end{array} (4) \)

The initial values enables us to compute the right side of Eqs. (1) and (3), which represent the constant values of the respective momenta. For this evaluation we know \(\bar{H}_{C}\) from the standard relation for angular momentum in conjunction with the given centroidal inertia properties. The result is

\(\begin{aligned}\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}+m_{m} \bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C} &=5000\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{1}+2\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1} \\\\&=(-0.018 \bar{i}+0.024 \bar{j}+40.000 \bar{k})\left(10^{6}\right) \mathrm{kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}, (5)\end{aligned} \)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{2}+\frac{m_{s} m_{m}}{m_{s}+m_{m}} \bar{r}_{A / C} \times\left(\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}\right) &=\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{1}+\frac{10000}{5002} \bar{r}_{A / C} \times\left(\bar{v}_{m / C}\right)_{1} \\\\&=235106 \bar{i}+161935 \bar{j}-10791 \bar{k} \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2} / \mathrm{s} . (6)\end{aligned} \)

In view of the vectorial nature of Eqs. (5) and (6), their components give six scalar equations. The unknowns are the components of \(\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}\) and \(\omega_{2}\), but Eq. (6) does not depend on \(\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}\). Thus we first solve Eq. (6) for \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\). To assist that solution, we evaluate the cross product term by representing \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\) in terms of its components, \(\omega_{x}, \omega_{y}\), and \(\omega_{z}:\)

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{m_{s} m_{m}}{m_{s}+m_{m}} \bar{r}_{A / C} \times\left(\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}\right)=& 164.8140 \omega_{x} \bar{i}+\left(161.9352 \omega_{y}-21.5914 \omega_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(-21.5914 \omega_{y}+2.8788 \omega_{z}\right) \bar{k} (7)\end{aligned} \)

Solving Eq. (6) for the components of \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\) is expedited by writing the equation in matrix form. In matrix notation Eq. (7) may be written as

\(\begin{array}{c}\frac{m_{s} m_{m}}{m_{s}+m_{m}} \quad\left\{r_{A / G}\right\} \otimes\left\{\{\omega\}_{2} \otimes\left\{r_{A / G}\right\}\right\}=[J]\{\omega\}_{2}, \\\\{[J]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}164.8140 & 0 & 0 \\\\0 & 161.9352 & -21.5914 \\\\0 & -21.5914 & 2.8788\end{array}\right] .}\end{array} \)

The corresponding form of Eq. (6) is

\(\left[\left[I_{C}\right]+[J]\right]\{\omega\}_{2}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}235106 & 161935 & -10791\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}, \)

which is readily solved for \(\{\omega\}_{2}\). We then can find \(\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}\) by substituting the result into Eq. (5), such that

\(\begin{aligned}\left(m_{s}+m_{m}\right)\left\{v_{C}\right\}_{2} &=m_{s}\left\{v_{C}\right\}_{1}+m_{m}\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}-m_{m}\{\omega\}_{2} \otimes\left\{r_{A / C}\right\} \\\\&=\left[\begin{array}{llll}-17.920321 & 23.868431 & 40~000\end{array}\right]^{\mathrm{T}} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s} .\end{aligned} \)

In vector notation the solutions are

\(\bar{\omega}_{2}=7.30941 \bar{i}+4.03046 \bar{j}-2.97105 \bar{j} ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s},\)

 

\(\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{2}=-3.58263 \bar{i}+4.77178 \bar{j}+7996.805 \bar{k} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}.\)

We readily find the impact force \(\bar{F}\) by applying the linear impulse-momentum principle to the meteorite isolated from its surroundings. Toward that end we use Eq. (4) to evaluate the velocity of the meteorite after it is embedded, which yields

\(\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{2}=-43.422034 \bar{i}+70.556508 \bar{j}+7988.033489 \bar{k}.\)

The only force whose impulse is important is that of \(\bar{F}\), which we represent by its average value over the 400 -ms duration, from which we find that

\((\bar{F})_{\mathrm{av}}=\frac{1}{\Delta t} m_{m}\left[\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{2}-\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}\right]=44.78 \bar{i}-59.65 \bar{j}+39.94 \bar{k} \mathrm{kN}.\)

This is the average force acting on the meteorite; the maximum is likely to be substantially greater. The force acting on the satellite is opposite.

Conservative forces, being position dependent, cannot become large during the interval of the impact, so the change in potential energy must be negligible during an impact. Thus the work-energy principle for the system states that

\(W_{1 \rightarrow 2}=T_{2}-T_{1}.\)

We compute the kinetic energy of the satellite by adding the translational part that is due to the motion of its center of mass to rotational energy about the center of mass. We then find the kinetic energy of the system by adding the contribution of the meteorite:

\(T=\frac{1}{2}m_{s}\left\{v_{C}\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left\{v_{C}\right\}+\frac{1}{2}\{\omega\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[I_{C}\right]\{\omega\}+\frac{1}{2}m_{m}\left\{v_{m}\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left\{v_{m}\right\}.\)

Substitution of the velocity parameters at the initial and final instants yields

\(\begin{array}{c}T_{1}=1.602250\left(10^{11}\right), \quad T_{2}=1.599373\left(10^{11}\right) \mathrm{J} \\\\W_{1 \rightarrow 2}=-2.877\left(10^{8}\right) \mathrm{J}\end{array} \)

It will be noted that \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) are quite close, so an accurate determination of the energy change necessitated extra precision in the calculations. The fact that \(W_{1 \rightarrow 2}\) is negative means that mechanical energy is lost. It is converted to other types of energy, such as heat and plastic material deformation. Although the value of \(W_{1 \rightarrow 2}\) is small relative to the total kinetic energy, it is an enormous amount of energy transfer. These observations, in combination with the large value of \(\bar{F}_{\mathrm{av}}\), make it evident that this impact would be devastating for the satellite.

The momentum conservation equations, Eqs. (5) and (6), have an interesting interpretation. At the conclusion of the impulse process, the meteorite is embedded in the satellite, so they are essentially one body. The left side of the linear momentum equation, Eq. (5), is actually the product of the total mass and the velocity of the center of mass of this body. Similarly, the left side of the angular momentum equation, Eq. (6), is the product of the inertia matrix of this new body and its angular velocity. In both cases, the motion variables change because the inertial properties of the combined body are different from those of the satellite before the impact.

In closure, it is useful to observe that formulating the angular momentum with respect to the system’s center of mass considerably complicated the analysis. The smallness of the meteorite’s mass causes that center of mass to be nearly coincident with that of the satellite. If we had ignored the difference between these two points, it would not have been necessary to consider first moments of mass, and the general angular impulse–momentum equation would have reduced to

\(\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{2}+\bar{r}_{A / C} \times m_{m}\left(\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{r}_{A / C}\right) \approx\left(\bar{H}_{C}\right)_{1}+\bar{r}_{A / C} \times m_{m}\left[\left(\bar{v}_{m}\right)_{1}-\left(\bar{v}_{C}\right)_{1}\right],\)

which yields

\(\bar{\omega}_{2} \approx 7.31232 \bar{i}+4.03206 \bar{j}-2.97343 \bar{k} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}.\)

This value is extremely close to the value obtained from the full analysis. The average force and mechanical-energy loss would be essentially unchanged. However, the distinction between the center of mass of the system and of the satellite would have been important if \(m_{s}\) and \(m_{m}\) were of similar orders of magnitude.
