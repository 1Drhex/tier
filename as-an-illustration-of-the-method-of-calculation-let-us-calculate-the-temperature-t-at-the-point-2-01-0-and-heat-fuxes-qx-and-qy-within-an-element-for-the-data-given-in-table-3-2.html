As an illustration of the method of calculation, let us calculate the temperature, T at the point (2.0,1.0) and heat fuxes \( q_{x}\) and \( q_{y}\) within an element for the data given in Table 3.2. Calculate the temperature T, and the heat flux components \( q_{x}\) and \( q_{y}\) if the thermal conductivity of the material is 2 W / cm K. Draw the isothermal line for \( 60^{\circ} C\) in the triangle.

Solution

The temperature at any location within the triangle is given by Equation (3.38)

\(T=N_{i} T_{i}+N_{j} T_{j}+N_{k} T_{k}=\left[\begin{array}{lll}N_{i} & N_{j} & N_{k}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}T_{i} \\T_{j} \\T_{k}\end{array}\right\}\)

The shape functions at the point (2.0,1.0) are calculated using Equation (3.39)

\(\begin{array}{l}N_{i}=\frac{1}{2 A}\left(a_{i}+b_{i} x+c_{i} y\right) \\N_{j}=\frac{1}{2 A}\left(a_{j}+b_{j} x+c_{j} y\right) \\N_{k}=\frac{1}{2 A}\left(a_{k}+b_{k} x+c_{k} y\right)\end{array}\)

by substituting x and y coordinates given in Table 3.2. The result is

\(\begin{array}{l}N_{i}=\frac{1}{10} \\N_{j}=\frac{5}{10} \\N_{k}=\frac{4}{10} . (3.45)\end{array}\)

The substitution of the nodal temperatures and the above shape function values into Equation (3.38) results in the temperature of the point (2.0,1.0) being,

\(T=N_{i} T_{i}+N_{j} T_{j}+N_{k}T_{k}=\frac{1}{10}(50)+\frac{5}{10}(70)+\frac{4}{10}(100)=80^{\circ} C . (3.46)\) \(\text { The components of heat flux in thex and y directions are calculated as }\) \(\left\{\begin{array}{l}q_{x} \\q_{y}\end{array}\right\}=-\frac{k}{2 A}\left[\begin{array}{lll}b_{i} & b_{j} & b_{k} \\c_{i} & c_{j} & c_{k}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}T_{i} \\T_{j} \\T_{k}\end{array}\right\}=-\frac{2}{10}\left[\begin{array}{c}50 \\200\end{array}\right] (3.47)\)

The position of the \( 60^{\circ} C\) isotherm may be obtained from Figure 3.8. From the given temperature values, it is clear that one \( 60^{\circ} C\) point lies on the side ij (point P ) and another lies on the side ik (point Q). It should be noted that the temperature varies linearly along these sides, that is, temperature is directly proportional to distance.In order to determine the location of P on i j, we have the following linear relation between the distances and temperature values, viz.,

\(\frac{60.0-50.0}{70.0-50.0}=\frac{\sqrt{\left(x_{P}-x_{i}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{P}y_{i}\right)^{2}}}{\sqrt{\left(x_{j}-x_{i}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{j}-y_{i}\right)^{2}}}\) (3.48)

From the data given, it is clear that the y coordinate on the ij side are equal to zero and thus the above equation is simplified to

\(\frac{10.0}{20.0}=\frac{\left(x_{P}-x_{i}\right)}{\left(x_{j}-x_{i}\right)}\) (3.49)

\(\begin{aligned}&\text { which results in } x_{p}=2.0 cm . \text { The location of } Q \text { along ik can bedetermined in a similar fashion }\\&a s\end{aligned}\)

\(\frac{60.0-50.0}{100.0-50.0}=\frac{y_{Q}-y_{i}}{y_{k}-y_{i}}\) (3.50)

which gives \( y_{Q}=0.5 cm\). The x coordinate of this point ts zero. The line joining P and Q will be the \( 60^{\circ} C\) isotherm (Figure 3.8). It should be noted that the same principle can be used for arbitrary triangles.
