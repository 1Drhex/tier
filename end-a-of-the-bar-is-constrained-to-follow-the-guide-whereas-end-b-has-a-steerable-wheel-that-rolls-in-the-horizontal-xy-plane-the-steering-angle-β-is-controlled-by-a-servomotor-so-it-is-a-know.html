Guest Question.

End \(A\) of the bar is constrained to follow the guide, whereas end \(B\) has a steerable wheel that rolls in the horizontal \(X Y\) plane. The steering angle \(\beta\) is controlled by a servomotor, so it is a known function of time. Let the position coordinates of pivot \(B\) and angle \(\theta\) be generalized coordinates, and let the quasi-velocities be \(\dot{\theta}\) and the velocity components of point \(G\) relative to the body-fixed \(x y z\) coordinate system. (a) Determine the constraint equations relating the quasi-velocities. (b) Determine the kinematical equations relating the generalized coordinates to the quasi-velocities.

Admin
Solution

This example demonstrates that the basic procedure for formulating velocity constraint equations by use of quasi-velocities differs little from the procedure for generalized velocities. However, to highlight this attribute, the generalized coordinates and quasi-velocities that have been selected are not the parameters an experienced practitioner would use. It is specified that the generalized coordinates are \(q_{1}=X_{A}, q_{2}=Y_{A}\), and \(q_{3}=\theta\), and that the quasi-velocities are \(\dot{\gamma}_{1}=\left(v_{G}\right)_{x}, \dot{\gamma}_{2}=\left(v_{G}\right)_{y}\), and \(\dot{\gamma}_{3}=\dot{\theta} .\) Collar \(A\) requires that \(\bar{v}_{A}\) be parallel to the \(X\) axis, whereas wheel \(B\) requires that \(\bar{v}_{B}\) be at angle \(\beta\) relative to the \(x\) axis. Thus we begin by relating the velocity of each end to the velocity of the center of mass and the angular velocity, which are known in terms of the quasi-velocities:

\(\begin{array}{l}\bar{v}_{A}=\bar{v}_{G}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{A / G}=\left(v_{G}\right)_{x} \bar{i}+\left(v_{G}\right)_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\theta} \bar{k} \times\left(-\frac{L}{2} \bar{i}\right), \\\\\bar{v}_{B}=\bar{v}_{G}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{B / G}=\left(v_{G}\right)_{x} \bar{i}+\left(v_{G}\right)_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\theta} \bar{k} \times\left(\frac{L}{2} \bar{i}\right).\end{array} (1) \)

The constraints require that \(\bar{v}_{A}\) be parallel to the \(X\) axis and \(\bar{v}_{B}\) be at angle \(\beta\) above the \(x\) axis. Both conditions can be imposed with cross products and unit vectors in the respective directions, specifically,

\(\begin{array}{l}\bar{v}_{A} \times(\cos \theta \bar{i}-\sin \theta \bar{j})=\overline{0}, \\\\\bar{v}_{B} \times(\cos \beta \bar{i}+\sin \beta \bar{j})=\overline{0}.\end{array} (2)\)

We substitute Eqs. (1) to eliminate \(v_{A}\) and \(v_{B}\), neither of which is a quasi-velocity. The cross products have only \(\bar{k}\) components, so evaluation of Eqs. (2) leads to

\(\begin{array}{l}-\left(v_{G}\right)_{x} \sin \theta-\left[\left(v_{G}\right)_{y}-\frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta}\right] \cos \theta=0, \\\\\left(v_{G}\right)_{x} \sin \beta-\left[\left(v_{G}\right)_{y}+\frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta}\right] \cos \beta=0.\end{array} \)

When each quasi-velocity is designated by the appropriate \(\dot{\gamma}\) symbol, these constraint equations become

\(\begin{array}{l}\dot{\gamma}_{1} \sin \theta+\dot{\gamma}_{2} \cos \theta-\frac{L}{2} \dot{\gamma}_{3} \cos \theta=0, \\\\\dot{\gamma}_{1} \sin \beta-\dot{\gamma}_{2} \cos \beta-\frac{L}{2} \dot{\gamma}_{3} \cos \beta=0.\end{array} \)

Both of these have the standard linear velocity constraint form in Eq. (9.3.4), with

\displaystyle\sum\limits_{n=1}^{N} A_{j n}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{\gamma}_{n}+B_{j}\left(q_{i}, t\right)=0 ; j=1, \ldots, J. (9.3.4)

 

\(\begin{array}{ll}A_{1,1}=\sin \theta, A_{1,2}=\cos \theta, \quad A_{1,3}=-\frac{L}{2} \cos \theta, \quad B_{1}=0, \\\\A_{2,1}=\sin \beta, \quad A_{2,2}=-\cos \beta, \quad A_{2,3}=-\frac{L}{2} \cos \beta, \quad B_{2}=0.\end{array} \)

There are three quasi-velocities that must satisfy two velocity constraints, so there is one degree of freedom.

The kinematical equations express the rates at which the generalized coordinates \(X_{B}, Y_{B}\), and \(\theta\) change in terms of the quasi-velocities. We observe that \(\dot{\theta}\) is defined to be \(\dot{\gamma}_{3}\), so one kinematical equation is an identity. To obtain the other kinematical equations associated with \(X_{B}\) and \(Y_{B}\) we observe that these variables describe the position of point \(B\). Thus, instead of describing the velocity of point \(B\) according to Eqs. (1), we could have expressed \(\bar{v}_{B}\) in terms of the generalized velocities as

\(\bar{v}_{B}=\dot{X}_{B} \bar{I}+\dot{Y}_{B} \bar{J}=\left(v_{G}\right)_{x} \bar{i}+\left[\left(v_{G}\right)_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\theta} \frac{L}{2}\right] \bar{j}.\)

Taking components of each expression and replacing the various symbols with their quasi-velocity designations yields

\(\begin{array}{c}\dot{X}_{B}=\dot{\gamma}_{1} \cos \theta-\left(\dot{\gamma}_{2}+\frac{L}{2} \dot{\gamma}_{3}\right) \sin \theta, \\\\\dot{Y}_{B}=\dot{\gamma}_{1} \sin \theta+\left(\dot{\gamma}_{2}+\frac{L}{2} \dot{\gamma}_{3}\right) \cos \theta ,\\\\\dot{\theta}=\dot{\gamma}_{3},\end{array} \)

which matches Eq. (9.3.5) with

\(\dot{q}_{j}=\displaystyle\sum\limits_{n=1}^K C_{j n}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{\gamma}_{n}+D_{j}\left(q_{i}, t\right) ; j=1, \ldots, N. (9.3.5)\)

\(C_{1,1}=\cos \theta, \quad C_{1,2}=-\sin \theta, \quad C_{1,3}=-\frac{L}{2} \sin \theta, \quad D_{1}=0,\)

\(C_{2,1}=\sin \theta, \quad C_{2,2}=\cos \theta, \quad C_{2,3}=\frac{L}{2} \cos \theta, \quad D_{2}=0,\)

\(C_{3,1}=C_{3,2}=0, \quad C_{3,3}=1, \quad D_{3}=0 .\)
