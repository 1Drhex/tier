Guest Question.

U-Bolt’s Dimensional Changes For the 10 mm diameter steel U-bolt in , determine the (a) strain, (b) change in length, and (c) change in diameter of the bolt’s 325 mm long straight section. Use the rule-of-thumb value E =210 GPa for the elastic modulus, and take the Poisson’s ratio as v =0.3. (See Figure 5.14.)

The stress in the U-bolt’s straight section was previously found in to be \(\sigma=5.093 \times 10^{7}\) Pa. Calculate the strain, change in length, and change in diameter by applying Equations (5.5), (5.3), and (5.8) to parts (a), (b), and (c), respectively.

 

\(\sigma=E \varepsilon\) (Equation 5.5)

 

\(\varepsilon=\frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (Equation 5.3)

 

\(\Delta d=-v d \frac{\Delta L}{L}\) (Equation 5.8)

Admin
Solution

(a) The strain in the straight section is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\varepsilon &=\frac{5.093 \times 10^{7} Pa }{210 \times 10^{9} Pa } \leftarrow\left[\varepsilon=\frac{\sigma}{E}\right] \\&=2.425 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ Pa }{ Pa } \\&=2.425 \times 10^{-4}\end{aligned}\)

 

Because this value is a small dimensionless number, we will write it as
the percentage ε =0.02425%.

 

(b) The change in the U-bolt’s length (the elongation) is

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta L &=\left(2.425 \times 10^{-4}\right)(0.325 m ) \quad \leftarrow[\Delta L=\varepsilon L] \\&=7.882 \times 10^{-5} m\end{aligned}\)

 

Convert this numerical value to the derived SI unit of microns following the definition in Table 3.2:

 

Table 3.2 Certain Derived Units in the SI
Quantity SI Derived Unit Abbreviation Definition
Length micrometer or micron µm 1µm= 10-6 m
Volume liter L 1 L = 0.001 m3
Force newton N 1 N = 1 (kg . m)/s2
Torque, or moment of a force newton-meter N . m __
Pressure or stress pascal Pa 1 Pa = 1 N/m2
Energy, work, or heat joule J 1 J = 1 N . m
Power watt Q 1 W = 1 J/s
Temperature degree Celsius oC oC = K – 273.15
Although a change in temperature of 1 kelvin equals a change of 1 degree Celsius, numerical values are converted using the formula.

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta L &=\left(7.882 \times 10^{-5} m \right)\left(10^{6} \frac{\mu m }{ m }\right) \\&=78.82( m )\left(10^{6} \frac{\mu m }{ m }\right) \\&=78.82 \times \mu m\end{aligned}\)

 

Here the SI prefix “micro” represents the factor of one-millionth.

 

(c) The diameter change is even smaller than the U-bolt’s elongation:

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta d &=-(0.3)(0.01 m )\left(\frac{7.882 \times 10^{-5} m }{0.325 m }\right) \leftarrow\left[\Delta d=-v d \frac{\Delta L}{L}\right] \\&=-7.276 \times 10^{-7}( m )\left(\frac{ m }{ m }\right) \\&=-7.276 \times 10^{-7} m\end{aligned}\)

 

To suppress the large negative power-of-ten exponent, apply the “nano” prefix from Table 3.3:

 

Table 3.3 Order-of-Magnitude Prefixes in the SI
Name Symbol Multiplicative Factor

pico p 0.000,000,000,001 = 10-12

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta d &=\left(-7.276 \times 10^{-7} m \right)\left(10^{9} \frac{ nm }{ m }\right) \\&=-727.6( m )\left(\frac{ nm }{ m }\right) \\&=-727.6 nm\end{aligned}\)

 

Discussion
The U-bolt’s elongation is small indeed; this is expected because the U-bolt is made of steel. The elongation is approximately the same as the diameter of a human hair or slightly larger than the 632.8 nm wavelength of light in a helium–neon laser. The bolt’s diameter therefore contracts by an amount only somewhat more than one wavelength of light. Even though the bolt carries a load of 4 kN (approximately 900 lb), the changes in its dimensions are imperceptible to the eye and would require specialized equipment to measure.

ε = 0.02425%

ΔL = 78.82 μm

Δd = −727.6 nm
