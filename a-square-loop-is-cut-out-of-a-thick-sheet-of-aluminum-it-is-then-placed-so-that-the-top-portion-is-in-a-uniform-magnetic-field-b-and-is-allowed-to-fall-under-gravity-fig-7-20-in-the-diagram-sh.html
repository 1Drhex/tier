Guest Question.

A square loop is cut out of a thick sheet of aluminum. It is then placed so that the top portion is in a uniform magnetic field B, and is allowed to fall under gravity (Fig. 7.20). (In the diagram, shading indicates the field region; B points into the page.) If the magnetic field is 1 T (a pretty standard laboratory field), find the terminal velocity of the loop (in m/s). Find the velocity of the loop as a function of time. How long does it take (in seconds) to reach, say, 90% of the terminal velocity? What would happen if you cut a tiny slit in the ring, breaking the circuit? [Note: The dimensions of the loop cancel out; determine the actual numbers, in the units indicated.]

Admin
Solution

\(ε =B l v=I R \Rightarrow I=\frac{B l}{R} v \Rightarrow\) upward magnetic force \(=I l B=\frac{B^{2} l^{2}}{R} v\) . This opposes the gravitational force downward:

\(m g-\frac{B^{2} l^{2}}{R} v=m \frac{d v}{d t} ; \frac{d v}{d t}=g-\alpha v, \text { where } \alpha \equiv \frac{B^{2} l^{2}}{m R} . \quad g-\alpha v_{t}=0 \Rightarrow v_{t}=\frac{g}{\alpha}=\frac{m g R}{B^{2} l^{2}}\) .

\(\frac{d v}{g-\alpha v}=d t \Rightarrow-\frac{1}{\alpha} \ln (g-\alpha v)=t+\text { const. } \Rightarrow g-\alpha v=A e^{-\alpha t} ; \text { at } t=0, v=0, \text { so } A=g\) .

\(\alpha v=g\left(1-e^{-\alpha t}\right) ; \quad v=\frac{g}{\alpha}\left(1-e^{-\alpha t}\right)=v_{t}\left(1-e^{-\alpha t}\right)\) .

At 90% of terminal velocity, \(v / v_{t}=0.9=1-e^{-\alpha t} \Rightarrow e^{-\alpha t}=1-0.9=0.1 ; \ln (0.1)=-\alpha t ; \ln 10=\alpha t\) ; \(t=\frac{1}{\alpha} \ln 10, \text { or } t_{90 \%}=\frac{v_{t}}{g} \ln 10\) .

Now the numbers: \(m=4 \eta A l\) ,where \(\eta\) is the mass density of aluminum, A is the cross-sectional area, and l is the length of a side. \(R=4 l / A \sigma \text {, where } \sigma\) is the conductivity of aluminum. So

\(v_{t}=\frac{4 \eta \operatorname{Alg} 4 l}{A \sigma B^{2} l^{2}}=\frac{16 \eta g}{\sigma B^{2}}=\frac{16 g \eta \rho}{B^{2}} \text {, and }\left\{\begin{array}{l}\rho=2.8 \times 10^{-8} \Omega m \\g=9.8 m / s ^{2} \\\eta=2.7 \times 10^{3} kg / m ^{3} \\B=1 T \end{array}\right\}\) .

So

\(v_{t}=\frac{(16)(9.8)\left(2.7 \times 10^{3}\right)\left(2.8 \times 10^{-8}\right)}{1}=1.2 cm / s ; t_{90 \%}=\frac{1.2 \times 10^{-2}}{9.8} \ln (10)=2.8 ms\) .

If the loop were cut, it would fall freely, with acceleration g.
