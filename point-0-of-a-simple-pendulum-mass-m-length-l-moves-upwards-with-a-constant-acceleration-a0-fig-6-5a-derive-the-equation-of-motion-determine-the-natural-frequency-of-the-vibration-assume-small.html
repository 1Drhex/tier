Guest Question.

Point 0 of a simple pendulum (mass m, length l) moves upwards with a constant acceleration \(a_{0} \) (Fig. 6.5a).

Derive the equation of motion. Determine the natural frequency of the vibration (assume small amplitudes) and the force in the string.

Admin
Solution

Solution We introduce the ξ, η-coordinate system as depicted in Fig. 6.5b. It is a translating coordinate system with point 0 as the origin. According to (6.20) the equation of motion in the moving system is

\(ma_{r}=F+F_{f} +F_{c}\) . (6.20) (a)

The (real) force F acting at the mass is given by (see Fig. 6.5b)

\(F=-S\sin \varphi e_{\xi } +\left(S\cos \varphi -mg\right)e_{\eta }\) (b)

and the fictitious forces are

\(F_{f}=-ma_{f} =-ma_{0}e_{\eta }, F_{c}=-ma_{c}=0\) , (c)

since ω = 0. The components of the relative acceleration \(a_{r}\) follow from the coordinates of the point mass in the moving system through differentiation:

\(\xi =l\sin \varphi , \eta =-l\cos \varphi\) ,

\(\dot{\xi }=l\dot{\varphi }\cos \varphi , \dot{\eta }=l\dot{\varphi }\sin \varphi \) ,

\(\ddot{\xi }=l\ddot{\varphi }\cos \varphi -l\dot{\varphi }^{2}\sin \varphi , \ddot{\eta } =l\ddot{\varphi }\sin \varphi +l\dot{\varphi }^{2}\cos \varphi \) .

This yields the relative acceleration

\(a_{r}=\ddot{\xi }e_{\xi }+\ddot{\eta }e_{\eta }=\left(l\ddot{\varphi }\cos \varphi -l\dot{\varphi }^{2} \sin \varphi \right)e_{\xi }+\left(l\ddot{\varphi }\sin \varphi +l\dot{\varphi }^{2} \cos \varphi \right)e_{\eta }\) . (d)

If we insert (b) – (d) into (a) we obtain the components of the equation of motion in the directions of the axes ξ and η:

\(m\left(l\ddot{\varphi }\cos \varphi -l\dot{\varphi }^{2}\sin \varphi \right)=-S\sin \varphi \) , (e)

\(m\left(l\ddot{\varphi }\sin \varphi +l\dot{\varphi }^{2}\cos \varphi \right)=S\cos \varphi-mg-ma_{0} \) . (f)

These are two equations for the unknowns φ and S. We can eliminate S if we multiply (e) with cosφ and (f) with sinφ and subsequently add the two equations. This leads to the equation of motion

\(ml\ddot{\varphi }=-mg\sin \varphi -ma_{0}\sin \varphi \rightarrow \ddot{\varphi } +\frac{g+a_{0} }{l}\sin \varphi =0 \) . (g)

In the case of small amplitudes (sin φ ≈ φ) of vibration, Equation (g) reduces to the differential equation for harmonic oscillations

\(\ddot{\varphi }+\omega ^{2}\varphi =0 \)

with the natural frequency

\(\omega =\sqrt{\frac{g+a_{0} }{l} } \) .

If point 0 is accelerated upwards, the natural frequency is larger than the natural frequency for a fixed point 0. On the other hand, if point 0 is accelerated downwards (\(a_{0} \) < 0) the pendulum oscillates more slowly. In the special case of point 0 in a free fall (\(a_{0} \) = −g) we get ω = 0.

If we multiply (e) with sinφ and (f) with cosφ and subtract the equations we obtain the force in the string:

\(S=m\left[l\dot{\varphi }^{2}+\left(g+a_{0} \right)\cos \varphi \right] \) .
