Guest Question.

Figure 7.20a shows a cylindrical rod having diameter of 12 mm and length of 100 mm. The pin is of a material having thermal conductivity 230 W/(m-ºC), speciﬁc heat 900 J/(kg-ºC), and density 2700 kg/m3. The right-hand end of the rod is held in contact with a medium at a constant temperature of 30ºC. At time zero, the entire rod is at a temperature of 30ºC when a heat source is applied to the left end, bringing the temperature of the left end immediately to 80ºC and maintaining that temperature indeﬁnitely. Using the forward difference method and four two-node elements, determine both transient and steady-state temperature distributions in the rod. No internal heat is generated.

Admin
Solution

In the solution for this example, we set up the general procedure then present the results for one solution using one time step for the transient portion. The node numbers and element numbers are as shown in Figure 7.20b. Since the length and area of each element are the same, we compute the element capacitance matrix as

\(\ \left[C^{(e)} \right] =\frac{c\rho AL}{6}\left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ] = \frac{900(2700)\frac{\pi }{4}(0.012)^{2}(0.025) }{6}\left [ \begin{matrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 2.2902 & 1.1451 \\ 1.1451 & 2.2902 \end{matrix} \right ] J/ ºC \)

where we have implicitly performed the integrations indicated in Equation 7.115 and leave the details as an end-of-chapter exercise. Similarly, the element conductance matrix is

\(\ \left[C^{(e)} \right]=c \rho A \int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{\left [ \begin{matrix} N_{1} \\ N_{2} \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} N_{1} & N_{2} \end{matrix} \right ] dx}=c \rho A \int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{\left[N\right] ^{T}\left[N\right]dx } \) (7.115)

 

\(\ \left[K^{(e)} \right]=\frac{KA}{L}\left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] =\frac{200\frac{\pi }{4}(0.012)^{2} }{0.025} \left [ \begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.9408 & -0.9408 \\ -0.9408 & 0.9408 \end{matrix} \right ] W/ ºC \)

For the one-dimensional case with uniform geometry and material properties, the system assembly is straightforward and results in the global matrices

\(\ \left[C\right] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 2.2902 & 1.1451 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1.1451 & 4.5804 & 1.1451 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1.1451 & 4.5804 & 1.1451 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1.1451 & 4.5804 & 1.1451 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1.1451 & 2.2902 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

 

\(\ \left[K\right]=\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.9408 & -0.9408 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -0.9408 & 1.8816 & -0.9408 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -0.9408 & 1.8816 & -0.9408 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -0.9408 & 1.8816 & -0.9408 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -0.9408 & 0.9408 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

As no internal heat is generated \(\ \left\{F_{Q} \right\} =0\) and, as we have speciﬁed boundary temperatures, the ﬂux forcing term is an unknown. Note that, in the transient case, the ﬂux terms
at the boundaries (the “reactions”) are time dependent and can be computed at each time step, as will be explained. Hence, the gradient “force vector” is
\(\ \left\{F_{8} \right\}=\left [ \begin{matrix} q_{1}A \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ -q_{5}A \end{matrix} \right ] \)
Having taken care of the boundary conditions, we now consider the initial conditions and examine the totality of the conditions on the solution procedure. It should be clear that, since we have the temperature of two nodes speciﬁed, the desired solution should provide the temperatures of the other three nodes and, therefore, should be a 3×3 system. The reduction to the 3×3 system is accomplished via the following observations:

1. If \(\ T_{1}=80 ºC = constant, then \dot{T_{1} }=0 \)

2. If \(\ T_{5}=30 ºC = constant, then \dot{T_{5} }=0 \)

The equations can be modiﬁed accordingly. In this example, the general equations become

\(\ \left[C\right] \left \{ \begin{matrix} 0 \\ \dot{T_{2} } \\ \dot{T_{3} } \\ \dot{T_{4} } \\ 0 \end{matrix} \right \} +\left[K\right] \left \{ \begin{matrix} 80 \\ T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \\ 30 \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} q_{1}A \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ -q_{5}A \end{matrix} \right \} \)

Consequently, the ﬁrst and ﬁfth equations become

\(\ 1.1451\dot{T_{2} } +0.9408(80)-0.9408T_{2}= q_{1}A \)

 

\(\ 1.1451\dot{T_{4} }-0.9408T_{4}+0.9408(30)= -q_{5}A \)

respectively. The three remaining equations are then written as

\(\ \left [ \begin{matrix} 4.5804 & 1 .1451 & 0 \\ 1 .1451 & 4.5804 & 1 .1451 \\ 0 & 1 .1451 & 4.5804 \end{matrix} \right ] \left \{ \begin{matrix} \dot{T_{2} } \\ \dot{T_{3} } \\ \dot{T_{4} } \end{matrix} \right \} +\left [ \begin{matrix} 1.8816 & −0.9408 & 0 \\ −0.9408 & 1.8816 & −0.9408 \\ 0 & −0.9408 & 1.8816 \end{matrix} \right ]\left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 75.264 \\ 0 \\ 28.224 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

For this example, the capacitance matrix is inverted (using a spreadsheet program) to obtain

\(\ \left[C\right] ^{-1}=\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.2339 & −0.0624 & 0.0156 \\ −0.0624 & 0.2495 & −0.0624 \\ 0.0156 & −0.0624 & 0.2339 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

where \(\ \left[C\right] \) now represents the reduced 3×3 capacitance matrix. Utilizing Equation 7.121 and multiplying by \(\ \left[C\right] ^{-1} \) yields

\(\ \left[C\right] \left\{T(t_{i+1} )\right\}=\left[C\right]\left\{T(t_{i} )\right\}- \left[K\right]\left\{T(t_{i} )\right\}\Delta t+\left\{F_{Q}(t_{i} ) \right\}\Delta t +\left\{F_{g}(t_{i} ) \right\}\Delta t \) (7.121)

\(\ \left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \end{matrix} \right \} _{i+1} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \end{matrix} \right \} _{i}-\left [ \begin{matrix} 0.4988 & −0.3521 & 0.0880 \\ −0.3521 & 0.5869 & −0.3521 \\ 0.0880 & −0.3521 & 0.4988 \end{matrix} \right ] \left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \end{matrix} \right \} _{i} \Delta t +\left \{ \begin{matrix} 18.0456 \\ −6.4558 \\ 7.7762 \end{matrix} \right \}\Delta t \)

as the two-point recurrence relation.

Owing to the small matrix involved, the recurrence relation was programmed into a standard spreadsheet program using time step Δt = 0.1 sec. Calculations for nodal temperatures \(\ T_{2} , T_{3} and T_{4} \) are carried out until a steady state is reached. Time histories of each of the nodal temperature are shown in Figure 7.21. The ﬁgure shows that steady-state conditions \(\ T_{2}=67.5ºC \) , \(\ T_{3}= 55ºC \) , and \(\ T_{4}= 42.5ºC \) are attained in about 30 sec. Interestingly, the results also show that the temperatures of nodes 3 and 4 initially decrease. Such phenomena are physically unacceptable and associated with use of a consistent capacitance matrix, as is discussed in Chapter 10.
