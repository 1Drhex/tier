Guest Question.

The standard potential of the cell \(Pt(s)|H_2(g)|HBr(aq)|AgBr(s)|Ag(s)\) was measured over a range of temperatures, and the data were found to fit the following polynomial:

\(E^⦵_{cell}/V = 0.07131 − 4.99×10^{−4}(T/K − 298) − 3.45×10^{−6}(T/K − 298)^2\)

The cell reaction is \(AgBr(s) + \frac{1}{2} H_2(g) → Ag(s) + HBr(aq)\), and has \(ν = 1\). Evaluate the standard reaction Gibbs energy, enthalpy, and entropy at \(298 K\).

 

Admin
Solution

Collect your thoughts The standard Gibbs energy of reaction is obtained by using eqn 6C.3

\(E^⦵_{cell}=-\frac{\Delta_rG^⦵}{vF}\) Standard cell potential[definition] (6C.3)

after evaluating \(E^⦵_{cell}\) at \(298\, K\) and by using \(1\, V C = 1\, J\). The standard reaction entropy is obtained by using eqn 6C.6,

\(\frac{dE^⦵_{cell}}{dT}=\frac{\Delta_rS^⦵}{vF}\) Temperature coefficient of standard cell potential (6C.6)

which involves differentiating the polynomial with respect to \(T\) and then setting \(T = 298\, K\). The standard reaction enthalpy is obtained by combining the values of the standard Gibbs energy and entropy.

At \(T = 298\, K, E^⦵_{cell} = 0.071 31\, V\), so
\(Δ_rG^⦵ = −νFE^⦵_{cell} = −(1) × (9.6485 × 10^4 \,C mol^{−1}) × (0.071 31\, V) = −6.880 × 10^3\, C V mol^{−1} = −6.880\, kJ mol^{−1}\)
The temperature coefficient of the standard cell potential is

\(\frac{dE^⦵_{cell}}{dT}=-4.99\times 10^{-4}\,VK^{-1}-2(3.45\times10^{-6})(T/K-298)VK^{-1}\)

At \(T = 298\, K\) this expression evaluates to

\(\frac{dE^⦵_{cell}}{dT}=-4.99\times 10^{-4}\,VK^{-1}\)

So, from eqn 6C.6 the standard reaction entropy is

\(\Delta_rS^⦵=vF\frac{dE^⦵_{cell}}{dT}=(1)\times(9.6485\times 10^4\, Cmol^{-1})\times(-4.99\times 10^{-4}\,VK^{-1})=-48.1\,JK^{-1}mol^{-1}\)

The negative value stems in part from the elimination of gas in the cell reaction. It then follows that

\(\Delta_rH^⦵=Δ_rG^⦵ +T\Delta_rS^⦵=-6.880\,kJmol^{-1}+(298\, K)\times(-0.0481\,kJK^{-1}mol^{-1})=-21.2\, kJmol^{-1}\)

Comment. One difficulty with this procedure lies in the accurate measurement of small temperature coefficients of cell potential. Nevertheless, it is another example of the striking ability of thermodynamics to relate the apparently unrelated, in this case to relate electrical measurements to thermal properties.
