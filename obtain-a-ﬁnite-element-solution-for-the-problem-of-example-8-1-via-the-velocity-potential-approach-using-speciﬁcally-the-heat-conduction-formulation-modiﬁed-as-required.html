Guest Question.

Obtain a ﬁnite element solution for the problem of via the velocity potential approach, using, speciﬁcally, the heat conduction formulation modiﬁed as required.

Admin
Solution

First let us note the analogies

\(\ u=-\frac{\partial \phi }{\partial x} \Rightarrow q_{x} =-K_{x}\frac{dT}{dx} \)

 

\(\ v=-\frac{\partial \phi }{\partial y} \Rightarrow q_{y} =-K_{y}\frac{dT}{dy} \)

so that, if \(\ K_{x}=K_{y}=1 \) , then the velocity potential is directly analogous to temperature and the velocity components are analogous to the respective ﬂux terms. Hence, the boundary conditions, in terms of thermal variables become

\(\ q_{x}=U \) \(\ q_{y}=0 \) on a-b

\(\ q_{x}=q_{y}=0 \) on b-c and a-e-d

T = constant= 0 on c-d (the value is arbitrary)

However, we can assess accuracy of the velocity potential solution by examination of the results in terms of the boundary conditions. For example, along the upper horizontal boundary, the y-velocity component must be zero, from which it follows that lines of constant Φ must be perpendicular to the boundary. Visually, this condition appears to be reasonably well-satisﬁed in Figure 8.8. An examination of the actual data presents a slightly different picture. Clearly, the values of the y-velocity component v are not zero, so additional solutions using reﬁned element meshes are in order.
