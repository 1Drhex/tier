Guest Question.

Find bases of the image and kernel of the matrix :

\( A=\begin{bmatrix} 1&2&0&1&2\\1&2&0&2&3\\1&2&0&3&4\\1&2&0&4&5 \end{bmatrix} \)
Admin
Solution

We can spot the redundant columns, \( \vec{v}_{2}=2\vec{v}_{1}, \vec{v}_{3} = 0\) , and \( \vec{v}_{5} = \vec{v}_{1}+\vec{v}_{4}\) Proposition 3.2.4:

To construct a basis of the image of a matrix A , list all the column vectors of A, and omit the redundant vectors from this list. (3.2.4)

tells us that the nonredundant columns:

\(\vec{v}_{1}=\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\1 \\1 \\1\end{array}\right], \quad \vec{v}_{4}=\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\2 \\3 \\4\end{array}\right]\)

form a basis of the image of A. Thus \(\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{im}) = 2\) .
Applying the method outlined in to the redundant vectors \(\vec{v}_{2}, \vec{v}_{3},\) and \(\vec{v}_{5}\) , we can generate three vectors in the kernel of \( A\). We will organize our work in a table:

Redundant Vector Relation Vector in Kernel of A
\(\vec{v}_{2}=2\vec{v}_{1}\) \(-2\vec{V}_{1}+\vec{V}_{2}=\vec{0}\) \(\vec{w}_{2}=\begin{bmatrix}-2\\1\\0\\0\\0 \end{bmatrix}\)
\(\vec{v}_{3}=\vec{0}\) \(\vec{v}_{3}=\vec{0}\) \(\vec{w}_{3}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\\0\\0 \end{bmatrix}\)
\(\vec{v}_{5}=\vec{v}_{1}+\vec{v}_{4}\) \(-\vec{v}_{1}-\vec{v}_{4}+\vec{v}_{5}=\overrightarrow{0}\) \(\vec{w}_{5}=\begin{bmatrix}-1\\0\\0\\-1\\1 \end{bmatrix}\)

To facilitate the transition from the relation to the vector in the kernel, it can be useful to write the coefficients of a relation above the corresponding columns of the matrix, as follows (for the last relation):

\(\begin{matrix} -1 & () & () & -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \)

 

\( A=\begin{bmatrix} 1&2&0&1&2\\1&2&0&2&3\\1&2&0&3&4\\1&2&0&4&5 \end{bmatrix} \)

We claim that the three vectors \(\vec{w}_{2},\vec{w}_{3},\vec{w}_{5}\) constructed above form a basis of the kernel of A. Theorem 3.2.5 tells us that these vectors are linearly independent, since vector \(\vec{w}_{i}\) has a 1 in the \(i\) th component, while the preceding vectors have a 0 in that component.

Consider vectors \(\vec{v}_{1}, . . . , \vec{v}_{m}\) in \(R^n\). If \(\vec{v}_{1}\) is nonzero, and if each of the vectors \(\vec{v}_{i}\) (for i > 2 ) has a nonzero entry in a component where all the preceding vectors \(\vec{v}_{1}, . . . , \vec{v}_{i-1}\) have a 0 , then the vectors \(\vec{v}_{1}, . . . , \vec{v}_{m}\) are linearly independent. (Theorem 3.2.5)

From Theorem 3.3.7:

For any n x m matrix A, the equation:

\(\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{ker} A)+\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{im} A)=m \) (Theorem 3.3.7)

we know that \(\operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{ker}) = 5 — \operatorname{dim}(\operatorname{im}) = 3\) . Because \(\vec{w}_{2},\vec{w}_{3},\vec{w}_{5}\) are three linearly independent vectors in the three-dimensional space ker(\(A\)), they form a basis of ker(\(A\)), by Theorem 3.3.4c.

c. If \(m\) vectors in \(V\) are linearly independent, then they form a basis of \(V\). (Theorem 3.3.4c)
