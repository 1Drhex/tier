Guest Question.

A blending tank that provides nearly perfect mixing is connected to a downstream unit by a long transfer pipe. The blending tank operates dynamically like a first-order process.

The mixing characteristics of the transfer pipe, on the other hand, are somewhere between plug flow (no mixing) and perfectly mixed. A test is made on the transfer pipe that shows that it operates as if the total volume of the pipe were split into five equal-sized perfectly stirred tanks connected in series.

The process (tank plus transfer pipe) has the following characteristics:

\(\begin{aligned}V_{ tank } &=2 m ^{3} \\V_{\text {pipe }} &=0.1 m ^{3} \\q_{\text {total }} &=1 m ^{3} / min\end{aligned}\)

where \(q_{\text {total }}\) represents the sum of all flow rates into the process.

(a) Using the information provided above, what would be the most accurate transfer function \(C_{\text {out }}^{\prime}(s) / C_{\text {in }}^{\prime}(s)\) for the process (tank plus transfer pipe) that you can develop? Note: \(c_{ in }\) and \(c_{\text {out }}\) are inlet and exit concentrations.

(b) For these particular values of volumes and flow rate, what approximate (low-order) transfer function could be used to represent the dynamics of this process?

(c) What can you conclude concerning the need to model the transfer pipe’s mixing characteristics very accurately for this particular process?

(d) Simulate approximate and full-order model responses to a step change in \(c_{\text {in }}\).

Admin
Solution

a)

Transfer Function for the blending tank:

\(G_{b t}(s)=\frac{K_{b t}}{\tau_{b t} s+1}\)

where \(K_{b t}=\frac{q_{\text {in }}}{\sum q_{i}} \neq 1\) and \(\tau_{b t}=\frac{2 m ^{3}}{1 m ^{3} / min }=2 min\)

Transfer Function for the transfer line

\(G_{t l}(s)=\frac{K_{t l}}{\left(\tau_{t l} s+1\right)^{5}}\)

where:

\(\begin{aligned}&K_{t l}=1 \\&\tau_{t l}=\frac{0.1 m ^{3}}{5 \times 1 m ^{3} / min }=0.02 min\end{aligned}\)

Thus,

\(\frac{C_{o u t}^{\prime}(s)}{C_{i n}^{\prime}(s)}=\frac{K_{b t}}{(2 s+1)(0.02 s+1)^{5}}\)

which is a \(6^{\text {th }}\)-order transfer function.

b)

Since \(\tau_{b t}>>\tau_{t l}[2>>0.02]\), we can approximate \(\frac{1}{(0.02 s+1)^{5}}\) by e \(^{-\theta_{s}}\)

\(\text { where } \theta=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{5}{(0.02)}=0.1\)

 

\(\therefore \quad \frac{C_{o u t}^{\prime}(s)}{C_{i n}^{\prime}(s)} \approx \frac{K_{b t} e^{-0.1 s}}{2 s+1}\)

c)

Because \(\tau_{b t} \approx 100 \tau_{t l}\), we anticipate that this approximate \(TF\) will yield results very close to those from the original \(TF\) (part (a)). This approximate \(TF\) is exactly the same as would have been obtained using a plug flow assumption for the transfer line. Thus we conclude that investing a lot of effort into obtaining an accurate dynamic model for the transfer line is not worthwhile in this case.

Note: if \(\tau_{b t} \approx \tau_{t l}\), this conclusion would not be valid.
