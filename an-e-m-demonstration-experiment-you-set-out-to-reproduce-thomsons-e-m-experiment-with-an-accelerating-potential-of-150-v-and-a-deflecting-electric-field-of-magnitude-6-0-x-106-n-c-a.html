Guest Question.

AN \(e/m\) DEMONSTRATION EXPERIMENT

You set out to reproduce Thomson’s \(e/m\) experiment with an accelerating potential of 150 V and a deflecting electric field of magnitude 6.0 × \(10^6\) N/C. (a) How fast do the electrons move? (b) What magnetic-field magnitude will yield zero beam deflection? (c) With this magnetic field, how will the electron beam behave if you increase the accelerating potential above 150 V?

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

This is the situation shown in Fig. 27.23.
We use Eq. (27.14) to determine the electron speed and Eq. (27.13) to determine the required magnetic field \(B\).

\(v=\frac{E}{B}\) (27.13)

\(\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}=e V \quad \text { or } \quad v=\sqrt{\frac{2 e V}{m}}\) (27.14)

EXECUTE:

(a) From Eq. (27.14), the electron speed \(v\) is

\(\begin{aligned}v &=\sqrt{2(e / m) V}=\sqrt{2\left(1.76 \times 10^{11} \mathrm{C} /\mathrm{kg}\right)(150 \mathrm{~V})} \\&=7.27 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}=0.024c\end{aligned}\)

(b) From Eq. (27.13), the required field strength is

\(B=\frac{E}{v}=\frac{6.0 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~N} / \mathrm{C}}{7.27 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=0.83 \mathrm{~T}\)

(c) Increasing the accelerating potential \(V\) increases the electron speed \(v\). In Fig. 27.23 this doesn’t change the upward electric force \(eE\), but it increases the downward magnetic force \(evB\). Therefore the electron beam will turn downward and will hit the end of the tube below the undeflected position.

 

EVALUATE: The required magnetic field is relatively large because the electrons are moving fairly rapidly (2.4% of the speed of light).
If the maximum available magnetic field is less than 0.83 T, the electric field strength \(E\) would have to be reduced to maintain the desired ratio \(E/B\) in Eq. (27.15) (\(\frac{E}{B}=\sqrt{\frac{2 e V}{m}} \quad \text { so } \quad \frac{e}{m}=\frac{E^{2}}{2 V B^{2}}\)).
