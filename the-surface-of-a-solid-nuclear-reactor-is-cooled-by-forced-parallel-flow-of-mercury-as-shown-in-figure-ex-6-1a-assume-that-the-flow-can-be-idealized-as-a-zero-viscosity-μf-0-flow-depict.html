Guest Question.

The surface of a solid nuclear reactor is cooled by forced, parallel flow of mercury, as shown in Figure (a). Assume that the flow can be idealized as a zero-viscosity \((\mu _{f} = 0) \) flow depicted in Figure.
(a) Draw the thermal circuit diagram.

(b) Determine the boundary-layer thickness at the tail end of the reactor surface.

(c) Determine the surface-convection heat flow at locations (i) x = L/4, (ii) L/2,
and (iii) L.
(d) Determine the Nusselt number based on L.
(e) Determine the heat transfer coefficient h.
(f) Determine the surface-convection resistance per unit area \(A_{ku}R_{ku}\) at location
x = L.
(g) As we will show, for laminar flow with Pr > 0.6, the local Nusselt number is
given by \(Nu_{L} = 0.332Re^{1/2}_{ L} Pr^{1/3}\), where \(Re_{L} = Pe_{L}Pr^{−1}\) and \(Re_{L}\) is the Reynolds number based on the L. Determine \(Nu_{L}\) from this expression and compare it with that for zero viscosity.

\( L = 6 cm, w = 2 cm, T_{s} = 314^{\circ }C, T_{f,∞} = 20^{\circ }C, and u_{f,∞} = 20 cm/s.\)

Solution

(a) The thermal circuit diagram is shown in Figure (b).
(b) The thermal boundary-layer thickness (the only boundary layer, since \((\mu _{f}=0)\) is given by \(\eta (\delta _{\alpha })=\frac{\delta ^{\ast }_{\alpha }}{2}Pe_{L}^{1/2}\equiv 1.8 or \delta _{\alpha }\equiv 3.6\left(\frac{\alpha _{f}L}{u_{f,\infty }} \right) ^{1/2}\) as

\( \delta _{\alpha }\equiv 3.6\left(\frac{\alpha _{f}L}{u_{f,\infty }} \right) ^{1/2} \)

Here, we have from Table for Hg at T = 420 K

\(\alpha _{f}=\left(\frac{k}{\rho c_{p}} \right) _{f}=\frac{11.4(W/m-K)}{13, 270(kg/m^{3})×130(J/kg-K)} = 6.608×10^{−6} m^{2}/s \) Table

and L = 6 cm = 0.06 m,\( u_{f,∞} = 20 cm/s = 0.2 m/s\) .

Using these for the evaluation of \( \delta _{\alpha }\) , we have

\( \delta _{\alpha }=3.6\times \left[\frac{6.607 × 10^{−6} ( m^{2}/s) × 0.06( m)}{0.20(m)} \right] ^{1/2}= 5.069 × 10^{−3} m = 5.069 mm. \)

(c) The surface-convection heat flux is given by \( q_{ku}=\frac{T_{s}-T_{f,\infty }}{A_{ku}R_{ku}} =\frac{k_{f}}{L}\frac{Pe_{L}^{1/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}} (T_{s}-T_{f,\infty })=\frac{(\rho c_{p}k)_{f}^{1/2}u_{f,\infty }^{1/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}L^{1/2}} (T_{s}-T_{f,\infty })\) as

\( q_{ku}=\frac{k_{f}}{L}\frac{Pe_{L}^{1/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}} (T_{s}-T_{f,\infty }) \)

 

\( Pe_{L}=\frac{u_{f,\infty }L}{\alpha _{f}} \)

Here we have
\( T_{s} − T_{f,∞} = 314(^{\circ }C) − 20(^{\circ }C) = 294^{\circ }C\)

 

\( Pe_{L}=\frac{u_{f,\infty }}{\alpha _{f}} L=\frac{0.20(m/s)}{6.609 × 10^{−6} (m^{2}/s)} L= (3.027 × 10^{4})L.\)

Then

\( q_{ku}=\frac{11.4(W/m-K)}{L(m)} \times \left[\frac{0.20(m/s)}{6.607 × 10^{−6} (m^{2}/s)} L(m)\right] ^{1/2}\frac{1}{\pi ^{1/2}} × 294(^{\circ }C). \)

(i) For \( L=\frac{0.06(m)}{4} =0.015m, q_{ku}= 2.687 × 10^{6} W/m^{2} \)

(ii) for \( L=\frac{0.06(m)}{2} =0.03m, q_{ku}= 1.900 × 10^{6} W/m^{2} \)

(iii) for L=0.06 m \( q_{ku}= 1.344 × 10^{6} W/m^{2} \)

(d) The Nusselt number based on L is defined by relation \(Nu_{L}\equiv N_{ku,f}=\frac{1}{R^{\ast }_{ku}} =\frac{R_{k,f}}{R_{ku}} =\frac{A_{k}R_{k,f}}{A_{ku}R_{ku}} =\frac{L}{A_{ku}R_{ku}k_{f}} =\frac{q_{ku}L}{(T_{s}-T_{f,\infty })k_{f}} \) and for this flow is given by \(Nu_{L}=\frac{Pe_{L}^{1/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}} \), i.e.,

\(Nu_{L}\ =\frac{L}{A_{ku}R_{ku}k_{f}} =\frac{Pe_{L}^{1/2}}{\pi ^{1/2}}\)

Here, for L = 0.06 m we have

\(Nu_{L}\ =\left[\frac{0.20(m/s) × 0.06(m)}{6.609 × 10^{−6} (m^{2}/s)} \right] ^{1/2}\frac{1}{\pi ^{1/2}}=(1.816 × 10^{3} )^{ 1/2}\frac{1}{\pi ^{1/2}}=23.83\)

(e) The heat transfer coefficient is given by \(Nu_{L}\ =\frac{q_{k.u}L}{(T_{s}-T_{f,\infty })k_{f}}\equiv \frac{hL}{k_{f}} or h\equiv Nu_{L}\frac{k_{f}}{L} \) as

\( h= Nu_{L}\frac{k_{f}}{L} \)

Here we have for L = 0.06 m

\( h = 23.83 ×\frac{11.4(W/m-^{\circ }C)}{0.06(m)} = 4.529 × 10^{3} W/m^{2}-^{\circ } C\)

(f) The surface-convection resistance can be determined from \(A_{ku}R_{ku}=\frac{L}{K_{f}}\frac{\pi ^{1/2}}{Pe_{L}^{1/2}} =\frac{L^{1/2}}{k_{f}^{1/2}}\frac{\pi ^{1/2}}{(\rho c_{p})_{f}^{1/2}u_{f,\infty }^{1/2}}=\frac{\pi ^{1/2}L^{1/2}}{(\rho c_{p}k)_{f}^{1/2}u_{f,\infty }^{1/2}} \), or since h is already calculated, we can use \(h\equiv \frac{1}{A_{ku}R_{ku}} or A_{ku}h\equiv \frac{1}{R_{ku}} \), i.e.,

\( A_{ku}R_{ku} = \frac{1}{h} = 2.208 × 10^{−4 \circ} C/(W/m^{2})\).

(g) From Table C.24, we have Pr = 0.0136. Then

\(Re_{L} = Pe_{L}Pr^{−1} =\frac{0.20(m/s) × 0.06(m)}{6.609 × 10^{−6} (m^{2}/s)} × (0.0136)^{−1} = 1.335 × 10^{5} \)

 

\( Nu_{L} = 0.332Re^{1/2}_{ L} Pr^{1/3} = 0.332(1.335 × 10^{5} )^{ 1/2} (0.0136)^{1/3} = 28.96. \)

Using the zero viscosity results given above, we had \(Nu_{L} = 24.42\). This results in a difference of 19.42%.
