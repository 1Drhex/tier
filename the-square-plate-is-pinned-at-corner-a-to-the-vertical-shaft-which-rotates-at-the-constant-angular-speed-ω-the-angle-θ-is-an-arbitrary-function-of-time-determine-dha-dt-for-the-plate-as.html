Guest Question.

The square plate is pinned at corner \(A\) to the vertical shaft, which rotates at the constant angular speed \(\Omega .\) The angle \(\theta\) is an arbitrary function of time. Determine \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t\) for the plate as a function of \(\theta\).

Admin
Solution

The rotation in this example occurs about two axes, so this solution will give a more complete picture of the general procedures. Nevertheless, the motion is sufficiently simple that we will be able to understand the physical meaning of the result. To form \(\bar{H}_{A}\), the origin of \(x y z\) must coincide with point \(A .\) We align the \(x\) axis with edge \(A D\) and the \(y\) axis with edge \(A B\), which are parallel to the centroidal axes described in the Appendix. The inertia properties obtained from the parallel axis theorems are

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{x x}=I_{y y}=\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}, \quad I_{z z}=\frac{2}{3} m L^{2}, \\\\I_{x y}=\frac{1}{4} m L^{2}, \quad I_{x z}=I_{y z}=0.\end{array} \)

The \(\Omega\) rotation occurs about the fixed vertical axis, which we designate as \(\bar{K}\). The \(\theta\) rotation occurs about the pin, whose axis is perpendicular to the plate; this is the body-fixed \(z\) axis. The general expressions for the angular velocity of \(x y z\) and of the reference frames for the rotation directions are

\(\bar{\omega}=\Omega \bar{e}_{1}+\dot{\theta} \bar{e}_{2}, \quad \bar{e}_{1}=\bar{K}, \quad \bar{\Omega}_{1}=\overline{0}, \quad \bar{e}_{2}=\bar{k}, \quad \bar{\Omega}_{2}=\bar{\omega}. \)

The angular acceleration corresponding to constant \(\Omega\) and variable \(\dot{\theta}\) are

\(\bar{\alpha}=\ddot{\theta} \bar{e}_{2}+\dot{\theta}\left(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{e}_{2}\right).\)

It is convenient to let \(\gamma=\pi / 4+\theta\), so that

\(\begin{array}{l}\bar{\omega}=\Omega(\sin \gamma \bar{i}+\cos \gamma \bar{j})+\dot{\theta} \bar{k}, \\\\\bar{\alpha}=\Omega \dot{\theta}(\cos \gamma \bar{i}-\sin \gamma \bar{j})+\ddot{\theta} \bar{k}.\end{array} \)

Equation (5.2.3) for the present rotation gives

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{H}_{A}=&\left(I_{x x} \omega_{x}-I_{x y} \omega_{y}-I_{x z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \omega_{y}-I_{y x} \omega_{x}-I_{y z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(I_{z z} \omega_{z}-I_{z x} \omega_{x}-I_{z y} \omega_{y}\right) \bar{k}\end{aligned} \) (5.2.3)

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{H}_{A} &=\left(I_{x x} \omega_{x}-I_{x y} \omega_{y}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \omega_{y}-I_{y x} \omega_{x}\right) \bar{j}+I_{z z} \omega_{x} \bar{k} \\\\&=m L^{2}\left[\Omega\left(\frac{1}{3} \sin \gamma-\frac{1}{4} \cos \gamma\right) \bar{i}+\Omega\left(\frac{1}{3} \cos \gamma-\frac{1}{4} \sin \gamma\right) \bar{j}+\frac{2}{3} \dot{\theta} \bar{k}\right],\end{aligned} \)

and the corresponding expression for the body-fixed derivative of \(\bar{H}_{A}\) is

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t} &=\left(I_{x x} \alpha_{x}-I_{x y} \alpha_{y}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \alpha_{y}-I_{y x} \alpha_{x}\right) \bar{j}+I_{z z} \alpha_{x} \bar{k} \\\\ &=m L^{2}\left[\Omega \dot{\theta}\left(\frac{1}{3} \cos \gamma+\frac{1}{4} \sin \gamma\right) \bar{i}+\Omega \dot{\theta}\left(-\frac{1}{3} \sin \gamma-\frac{1}{4} \cos \gamma\right) \bar{j}+\frac{2}{3} \ddot{\theta} \bar{k}\right]. \end{aligned}\)

The rate at which angular momentum changes is correspondingly found to be

\(\dot{\bar{H}}_{A}=\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{H}_{A}\)

\(\begin{aligned}=& m L^{2} \Omega \dot{\theta}\left(\frac{2}{3} \cos \gamma+\frac{1}{2} \sin \gamma\right) \bar{i}+m L^{2} \Omega \dot{\theta}\left(-\frac{2}{3} \sin \gamma-\frac{1}{2} \cos \gamma\right) \bar{j} \\ &+m L^{2}\left[\frac{2}{3} \ddot{\theta}+\frac{1}{4} \Omega^{2}\left((\cos \gamma)^{2}-(\sin \gamma)^{2}\right)\right] \bar{k}. \end{aligned}\)

The result was requested as a function of \(\theta\), which we can recover by setting \(\gamma=\) \(\pi / 4+\theta\) in the trigonometric terms. The identities for the sine and cosine of a sum lead to

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{H}_{A}=& \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12} m L^{2} \Omega \dot{\theta}(7 \cos \theta-\sin \theta) \bar{i}+\frac{\sqrt{2}}{12} m L^{2} \Omega \dot{\theta}(-7 \cos \theta-\sin \theta) \bar{j} \\\\&+m L^{2}\left[\frac{2}{3} \ddot{\theta}-\frac{1}{4} \Omega^{2} \sin 2 \theta\right] \bar{k}.\end{aligned} \)

To understand this result it is useful to eliminate \(\gamma\) in the preceding expression for \(\bar{H}_{A}\). Doing so yields

\(\bar{H}_{A}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{24} m L^{2} \Omega(\cos \theta+7 \sin \theta) \bar{i}+\frac{\sqrt{2}}{24} m L^{2} \Omega(\cos \theta-7 \sin \theta) \bar{j}+\frac{2}{3} m L^{2} \dot{\theta} \bar{k}.\)

We may separately examine the manner in which \(\Omega\) and \(\dot{\theta}\) cause \(\bar{H}_{A}\) to change. Let us designate the portion of \(\bar{H}_{A}\) that lies in the plane of the plate as \(\bar{H}_{\|}=\left(H_{A}\right)_{x} \bar{i}+\) \(\left(H_{A}\right)_{y} \bar{j} .\) When \(\theta=0\) the components of \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) have equal magnitude. In that case \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) is along the diagonal to corner \(C\), which is aligned with vertical rotation axis. When \(\theta>0\), we see that \(\left(H_{A}\right)_{x}>\left(H_{A}\right)_{y}\) , which means that the angle from the \(y\) axis to \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) exceeds \(\pi / 4 .\) In fact, this angle, which is given by

\(\beta=\tan ^{-1}\left[\frac{\left(H_{A}\right)_{x}}{\left(H_{A}\right)_{y}}\right]=\tan ^{-1}\left(\frac{\cos \theta+7 \sin \theta}{\cos \theta-7 \sin \theta}\right)\).

can be shown to be greater than \(\gamma=\pi / 4+\theta\), so the angle from the \(y\) axis to \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) exceeds the angle to the \(\Omega\) rotation vector. Thus \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) is situated as shown in the second sketch.

Rotation about the vertical axis causes the tip of \(\bar{H}_{\|}\) to move in the negative \(\bar{k}\) direction. This portion combines with the term \(I_{z z} \ddot{\theta}\) corresponding to the changing magnitude of \(\bar{H}_{A} \cdot \bar{k}\) to produce the \(\bar{k}\) component of \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t\)

This is not the sole effect of the \(\Omega\) rotation on \(\bar{H}_{A}\). The sketch shows \(\bar{H}_{A} \cdot \bar{k}=\) \(I_{z z} \dot{\theta} .\) Rotation about the vertical axis at \(\Omega\) causes the tip to this vector to move in the horizontal direction, parallel to the plane of the plate. This is one of the effects producing the \(\bar{i}\) and \(\bar{j}\) components of \(d \bar{H}_{A} / d t\). The other source of these components is the fact that changing \(\theta\) changes \(\left(H_{A}\right)_{x}\) and \(\left(H_{A}\right)_{y}\).

The fact that two rotations are present in this system makes it more difficult than in the to visualize the reasons why the angular momentum changes. As system complexity increases, we will come to rely on the analytical result, and only occasionally seek a physical explanation for some particularly interesting aspect.
