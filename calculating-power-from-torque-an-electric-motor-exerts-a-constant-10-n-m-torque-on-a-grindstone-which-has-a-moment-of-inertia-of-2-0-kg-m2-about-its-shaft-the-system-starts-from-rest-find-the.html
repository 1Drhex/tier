CALCULATING POWER FROM TORQUE

An electric motor exerts a constant 10-N * m torque on a grindstone, which has a moment of inertia of 2.0 kg *\( m^2\) about its shaft. The system starts from rest. Find the work W done by the motor in 8.0 s and the grindstone’s kinetic energy K at this time. What average power \(P_{av}\) is delivered by the motor?

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

The only torque acting is that due to the motor. Since this torque is constant, the grindstone’s angular acceleration \(\alpha_{z}\) is constant. We’ll use Eq. (10.7) (\(\sum \tau_{z}=I \alpha_{z}\)) to find \(\alpha_{z}\), and then use this in the kinematics equations from Section 9.2 to calculate the angle ∆θ through which the grindstone rotates in 8.0 s and its final angular velocity \(w_z\). From these we’ll calculate W, K, and \(P_{av}\).

EXECUTE:

We have \(\Sigma \tau_{z}=10 \mathrm{~N} \cdot \mathrm{m} \text { and } I=2.0 \mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{2}\), so \(\sum \tau_{z}=I \alpha_{z} \text { yields } \alpha_{z}=5.0 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\). From Eqs. (9.11) (\(\theta=\theta_{0}+\omega_{0 z} t+\frac{1}{2} \alpha_{z} t^{2}\)) and (10.21) (\(W=\tau_{z}\left(\theta_{2}-\theta_{1}\right)=\tau_{z} \Delta \theta\)).

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta \theta &=\frac{1}{2} \alpha_{z} t^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(5.0 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(8.0 \mathrm{~s})^{2}=160 \mathrm{rad} \\W &=\tau_{z} \Delta \theta=(10 \mathrm{~N} \cdot\mathrm{m})(160 \mathrm{rad})=1600 \mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)

From Eqs. (9.7) (\(\omega_{z}=\omega_{0 z}+\alpha_{z} t\)) and (9.17) (\(K=\frac{1}{2} I \omega^{2}\)),

\(\begin{aligned}\omega_{z} &=\alpha_{z} t=\left(5.0 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(8.0\mathrm{~s})=40 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s} \\K &=\frac{1}{2} I \omega_{z}^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(2.0\mathrm{~kg} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{2}\right)(40 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s})^{2}=1600\mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)

The average power is the work done divided by the time interval:

\(P_{\mathrm{av}}=\frac{1600 \mathrm{~J}}{8.0 \mathrm{~s}}=200 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{s}=200 \mathrm{~W}\)

 

EVALUATE: The initial kinetic energy was zero, so the work done W must equal the final kinetic energy K [Eq. (10.22) (\(W_{\text {tot }}=\int_{\omega_{1}}^{\omega_{2}} I \omega_{z} d \omega_{z}=\frac{1}{2} I \omega_{2}^{2}-\frac{1}{2} I \omega_{1}^{2}\))]. This is just as we calculated. We can check our result \(P_{av}\) = 200 W by considering the instantaneous power \(P=\tau_{z} \omega_{z}\). Because \(\omega_{z}\) increases continuously, P increases continuously as well; its value increases from zero at t = 0 to \((10 \mathrm{~N} \cdot \mathrm{m})(40 \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s})=400 \mathrm{~W} \text { at } t=8.0 \mathrm{~s}\).

Both \(\omega_{z}\) and P increase uniformly with time, so the average power is just half this maximum value, or 200 W.
