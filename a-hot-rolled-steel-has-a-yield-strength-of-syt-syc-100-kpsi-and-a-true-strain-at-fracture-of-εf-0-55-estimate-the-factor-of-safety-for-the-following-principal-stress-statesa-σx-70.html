Guest Question.

A hot-rolled steel has a yield strength of \( S_{yt}= S_{yc}= 100 \) kpsi and a true strain at fracture of \( \epsilon _f=0.55 \) . Estimate the factor of safety for the

following principal stress states:
(a) \( \sigma _x = 70 \ kpsi \ , \ \sigma _y = 70 \ kpsi ,\tau _{xy}=0 \ kpsi \)
(b) \( \sigma _x = 60 \ kpsi \ , \ \sigma _y = 40\ kpsi , \tau _{xy}=-15 \ kpsi \)
(c) \( \sigma _x = 0 \ kpsi \ , \ \sigma _y = 40\ kpsi , \tau _{xy}=45 \ kpsi \)
(d) \( \sigma _x = -40 \ kpsi \ , \ \sigma _y = -60 \ kpsi , \tau _{xy}=15 \ kpsi \)
(e)\( \sigma _1 = 30 \ kpsi \ , \ \sigma _2 = 30 \ kpsi , \sigma _3=30 \ kpsi\)

Admin
Solution

Since \(ε_f \gt 0.05 \) and \( S_{yt} \ and S_{yc} \) are equal, the material is ductile and both the distortion energy (DE) theory and maximum-shear-stress (MSS) theory apply. Both will be used for comparison. Note that cases a to d are plane stress states.
(a) Since there is no shear stress on this stress element, the normal stresses are equal to the principal stresses. The ordered principal stresses are \( \sigma _A = \sigma _1=70 , \sigma_B= \sigma _2= 70 \ ,\sigma _3 = 0 \) kpsi.

DE From Eq. (5–13),
\(\acute{\sigma } = \left(\sigma ^2_A – \sigma _A\sigma _B + \sigma ^2_B \right) ^{{1}/{2}}\)

 

\(\acute{\sigma } = \left[70 ^2 – 70\left(70\right) + 70 ^2 \right] ^{{1}/{2}}=70\) kpsi

From Eq. (5–19),
\( \acute{\sigma } = \frac{S_y}{n} \)

\( n = \frac{S_y}{\acute{\sigma } } = \frac{100}{70} = 1.43\)

MSS Noting that the two nonzero principal stresses are equal, \(τ_{max} \) will be from the largest Mohr’s circle, which will incorporate the third principal stress at zero.
From Eq. (3–16),

 

\(\tau _{{1}/{2}} =\frac{\sigma _{1} – \sigma _{1}}{ 2} \)

 

\(\tau _{{2}/{3}} =\frac{\sigma _{2} – \sigma _{3}}{ 2} \)

 

\(\tau _{{1}/{3}} =\frac{\sigma _{1} -\sigma _{3}}{ 2} \)

 

\(\tau_{max} = \frac{\sigma _{1} – \sigma _{3}}{ 2} = \frac{70 – 0}{ 2} = 35\) kpsi

From Eq. (5–3),

\(\tau _{max} = \frac{S_y}{ 2n } \ or \ \sigma _{1} – \sigma _{3} = \frac{S_y}{ n } \)

 

\( n = \frac{{S_y}/{2}}{ \tau _{max} }=\frac{{100}/{2}}{35} = 1.43\)

 

(b) From Eq. (3–13),
\(\sigma _1,\sigma _2=\frac{\sigma _x+\sigma _y}{2} \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _x – \sigma _y}{2}\right)^2+\tau ^2_{xy} } \)

 

the nonzero principal stresses are
\(\sigma _{A} , \sigma _{B}= \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{60-40}{2} \right)^2+\left(-15\right)^2 }=68.0 , 32.0\) kpsi

The ordered principal stresses are

\( \sigma _{A}= \sigma _{1}= 68.0, \sigma _{A}= \sigma _{2}= 32.0, \sigma _{3}= 0 \) kpsi.

DE \(\acute{\sigma } =\left[68^2-68\left(32\right)+68^2 \right] ^{{1}/{2}}=59.0\) kpsi

 

\( n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{59.0}=1.70\)

MSS Noting that the two nonzero principal stresses are both positive, \( \tau _{max}\) will be from the largest Mohr’s circle which will incorporate the third principle stress at zero.

From Eq. (3–16),

\(\tau_{max} = \frac{\sigma _{1} – \sigma _{3}}{ 2} = \frac{68.0- 0}{ 2} = 34.0 \) kpsi

\( n = \frac{{S_y}/{2}}{ \tau _{max} }=\frac{{100}/{2}}{34.0} = 1.47\)

 

(c) This time, we shall obtain the factors of safety directly from the xy components of stress.

DE From Eq. (5–15),

\( \acute{\sigma} =\left(\sigma ^2_x-\sigma _x\sigma _y+\sigma ^2_y+3\tau ^2_{xy}\right) ^{{1}/{2}}=\left[\left(40\right)^2+3\left(45\right)^2 \right] ^{{1}/{2}}=87.6\) kpsi

 

\( n = \frac{{S_y}/{2}}{ \tau _{max} }=\frac{{100}/{2}}{87.6} = 1.14\)

MSS Taking care to note from a quick sketch of Mohr’s circle that one nonzero principal stress will be positive while the other one will be negative, τmaxcan be obtained from the extreme-value shear stress given by Eq. (3–14)

 

\(\tau _1 , \tau _2=\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _x-\sigma _y}{2}^2 \right)+\tau ^2_{xy} } \)

 

without finding the principal stresses.
\(\tau _{max}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma _x-\sigma _y}{2} \right)^2+\tau ^2_{xy} }= \sqrt{\left(\frac{0-40}{2} \right)^2+45^2 }=49.2 \) kpsi

 

\( n = \frac{{S_y}/{2}}{ \tau _{max} }=\frac{{100}/{2}}{49.2} = 1.02 \)

 

For comparison purposes later in this problem, the nonzero principal stresses can be obtained from Eq. (3–13) to be 70.0 kpsi and −30 kpsi.
(d) From Eq. (3–13), the nonzero principal stresses are

 

\(\sigma _{A} , \sigma _{B}=\frac{-40+\left(-60\right) }{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{60-40}{2} \right)^2+\left(15\right)^2 } = -32.0 ,-68.0 \) kpsi

 

The ordered principal stresses are \( \sigma _1= 0, \sigma _A= \sigma _2= −32.0, \sigma _B= \sigma _3= −68.0 \) kpsi.

 

DE \( \acute{\sigma} =\left[\left(-32\right)^2-\left(-32\right) \left(-68\right)+\left(-68\right)^2 \right] ^{{1}/{2}}=59.0 \) kpsi

 

\( n=\frac{S_y}{\acute{\sigma } } =\frac{100}{59.0} =1.70\)

 

MSS From Eq. (3–16),

\(\tau_{max} =\frac{\sigma _1-\sigma _3}{2} =\frac{0-\left(-68.0\right) }{2} =34.0\) kpsi

 

\(n = \frac{{S_y}/{2}}{ \tau _{max} }=\frac{{100}/{2}}{34.0} = 1.47\)

 

(e) The ordered principal stresses are

\( \sigma _1= 30 \ , \sigma _2= 30 \ ,\sigma _3= 30\) kpsi

DE From Eq. (5–12)
\(\acute{\tau } =\left[\frac{\left(\sigma _1-\sigma _2\right)^2+ \left(\sigma _2-\sigma _3\right)^2+\left(\sigma _3-\sigma _1\right)^2}{2} \right]^{{1}/{2}} \),

 

\(\acute{\sigma}=\left[\frac{\left(30-30\right)^2+ \left(30-30\right)^2+\left(30-30\right)^2}{2} \right]^{{1}/{2}} =0\) kpsi

 

\(n=\frac{S_y}{\acute{\sigma } } =\frac{100}{0} \longrightarrow \infty \)

 

MSS From Eq. (5–3),

 

\(n=\frac{S_y}{\sigma _1-\sigma _3 } =\frac{100}{30-30} \longrightarrow \infty \)

 

A tabular summary of the factors of safety is included for comparisons.

(a) (b) (c) (d) (e)
DE 1.43 1.7 1.14 1.7 ∞
MSS 1.43 1.47 1.02 1.47 ∞

Since the MSS theory is on or within the boundary of the DE theory, it will always predict a factor of safety equal to or less than the DE theory, as can be seen in the table.
For each case, except case (e), the coordinates and load lines in the \( \sigma _A,\sigma _B \) plane are shown in Fig. 5–11.
Case (e) is not plane stress. Note that the load line for case (a) is the only plane stress case given in which the two theories agree, thus giving the same factor of safety.
