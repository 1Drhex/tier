Guest Question.

ANALYZING A CARNOT ENGINE II

Suppose 0.200 mol of an ideal diatomic gas (γ = 1.40) undergoes a Carnot cycle between 227°C and 27°C, starting at \(p_a\) = 10.0 × \(10^5\) Pa at point a in the pV-diagram of Fig. 20.13. The volume doubles during the isothermal expansion step a → b. (a) Find the pressure and volume at points a, b, c, and d. (b) Find Q, W, and ∆U for each step and for the entire cycle. (c) Find the efficiency directly from the results of part (b), and compare with the value calculated from Eq. (20.14).

\(e_{\text {Carnot }}=1-\frac{T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}=\frac{T_{\mathrm{H}}-T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\) (20.14)

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

This problem involves the properties of the Carnot cycle and those of an ideal gas. We are given the number of moles n and the pressure and temperature at point a (which is at the higher of the two reservoir temperatures); we can find the volume at a from the ideal-gas equation pV = nRT. We then find the pressure and volume at points b, c, and d from the known doubling of volume in step a → b, from equations given in this section, and from pV = nRT. In each step we use Eqs. (20.10) and (20.11) to find the heat flow and work done and Eq. (19.13) to find the internal energy change.

\(Q_{\mathrm{H}}=W_{a b}=n R T_{\mathrm{H}} \ln \frac{V_{b}}{V_{a}}\) (20.10)

\(Q_{\mathrm{C}}=W_{c d}=n R T_{\mathrm{C}} \ln \frac{V_{d}}{V_{c}}=-n R T_{\mathrm{C}} \ln \frac{V_{c}}{V_{d}}\) (20.11)

\(d U=n C_{V} d T\) (19.13)

EXECUTE:

(a) With \(T_{\mathrm{H}}=(227+273.15) \mathrm{K}=500 \mathrm{~K} \text { and } T_{\mathrm{C}}=\)\((27+273.15) \mathrm{K}=300 \mathrm{~K}, p V=n R T\) yields

\(\begin{aligned}V_{a} &=\frac{n R T_{\mathrm{H}}}{p_{a}}=\frac{(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(8.314\mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(500 \mathrm{~K})}{10.0 \times 10^{5}\mathrm{~Pa}} \\&=8.31 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\end{aligned}\)

The volume doubles during the isothermal expansion a → b:

\(V_{b}=2 V_{a}=2\left(8.31 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)=16.6 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\)

Because the expansion a → b is isothermal, \(p_{a} V_{a}=p_{b} V_{b}\), so

\(p_{b}=\frac{p_{a} V_{a}}{V_{b}}=5.00 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~Pa}\)

For the adiabatic expansion b → c, we use the equation \(T_{\mathrm{H}} V_{b}^{\gamma-1}=T_{\mathrm{C}} V_{c}^{\gamma-1}\) that follows Eq. (20.12) (\(\frac{Q_{\mathrm{C}}}{Q_{\mathrm{H}}}=-\left(\frac{T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\right) \frac{\ln \left(V_{c} / V_{d}\right)}{\ln \left(V_{b} / V_{a}\right)}\)) as well as the idealgas equation:

\(\begin{aligned}V_{c} &=V_{b}\left(\frac{T_{\mathrm{H}}}{T_{\mathrm{C}}}\right)^{1 /(\gamma-1)}=\left(16.6 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)\left(\frac{500 \mathrm{~K}}{300 \mathrm{~K}}\right)^{2.5} \\&=59.6 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3} \\p_{c} &=\frac{n RT_{\mathrm{C}}}{V_{c}}=\frac{(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(8.314 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot\mathrm{K})(300 \mathrm{~K})}{59.6 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}} \\&=0.837 \times10^{5} \mathrm{~Pa}\end{aligned}\)

For the adiabatic compression d → a, we have \(T_{\mathrm{C}} V_{d}^{\gamma-1}=T_{\mathrm{H}} V_{a}^{\gamma-1}\) and so

\(\begin{aligned}V_{d} &=V_{a}\left(\frac{T_{\mathrm{H}}}{T_{\mathrm{C}}}\right)^{1 /(\gamma-1)}=\left(8.31 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}\right)\left(\frac{500 \mathrm{~K}}{300 \mathrm{~K}}\right)^{2.5} \\&=29.8 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3} \\p_{d} &=\frac{n RT_{\mathrm{C}}}{V_{d}}=\frac{(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(8.314 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot\mathrm{K})(300 \mathrm{~K})}{29.8 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}} \\&=1.67 \times10^{5} \mathrm{~Pa}\end{aligned}\)

(b) For the isothermal expansion a → b, ∆\(U_{ab}\) = 0. From Eq. (20.10),

\(\begin{aligned}W_{a b} &=Q_{\mathrm{H}}=n R T_{\mathrm{H}} \ln \frac{V_{b}}{V_{a}} \\&=(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(8.314 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(500\mathrm{~K})(\ln 2)=576 \mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)

For the adiabatic expansion b → c, \(Q_{bc}\) = 0. From the first law of thermodynamics, \(\Delta U_{b c}=Q_{b c}-W_{b c}=-W_{b c}\), the work \(W_{bc}\) done by the gas in this adiabatic expansion equals the negative of the change in internal energy of the gas. From Eq. (19.13) we have \(d U=n C_{V} d T\), where \(\Delta T=T_{\mathrm{C}}-T_{\mathrm{H}}\). Using \(C_{V}=20.8 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K}\) for an ideal diatomic gas, we find

\(\begin{aligned}W_{b c} &=-\Delta U_{b c}=-n C_{V}\left(T_{\mathrm{C}}-T_{\mathrm{H}}\right)=nC_{V}\left(T_{\mathrm{H}}-T_{\mathrm{C}}\right) \\&=(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(20.8\mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(500 \mathrm{~K}-300 \mathrm{~K})=832\mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)

For the isothermal compression c → d, ∆\(U_{cd}\) = 0; Eq.(20.11) gives

\(\begin{aligned}&W_{c d}=Q_{\mathrm{C}}=n R T_{\mathrm{C}} \ln \frac{V_{d}}{V_{c}} \\&=(0.200\mathrm{~mol})(8.314 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(300 \mathrm{~K})\left(\ln \frac{29.8 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}}{59.6 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{~m}^{3}}\right)\\&=-346 \mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)
For the adiabatic compression d → a, \(Q_{da}\) = 0 and

\(\begin{aligned}W_{d a} &=-\Delta U_{d a}=-n C_{V}\left(T_{\mathrm{H}}-T_{\mathrm{C}}\right)=nC_{V}\left(T_{\mathrm{C}}-T_{\mathrm{H}}\right) \\&=(0.200 \mathrm{~mol})(20.8\mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{K})(300 \mathrm{~K}-500 \mathrm{~K})=-832\mathrm{~J}\end{aligned}\)

We can tabulate these results as follows:

Process Q W ΔU
a → b 576 J 576 J 0
b → c 0 832 J -832 J
c → d -346 J -346 J 0
d → a 0 -832 J 832 J
Total 230 J 230 J 0

(c) From the above table, \(Q_H\) = 576 J and the total work is 230 J. Thus

\(e=\frac{W}{Q_{\mathrm{H}}}=\frac{230 \mathrm{~J}}{576 \mathrm{~J}}=0.40=40 \%\)

We can compare this to the result from Eq. (20.14),

\(e=\frac{T_{\mathrm{H}}-T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}=\frac{500 \mathrm{~K}-300 \mathrm{~K}}{500 \mathrm{~K}}=0.40=40 \%\)

EVALUATE: The table in part (b) shows that for the entire cycle Q = W and ∆U = 0, just as we would expect: In a complete cycle, the net heat input is used to do work, and there is zero net change in the internal energy of the system. Note also that the quantities of work in the two adiabatic processes are negatives of each other. Can you show from the analysis leading to Eq. (20.13) (\(\frac{Q_{\mathrm{C}}}{Q_{\mathrm{H}}}=-\frac{T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}} \text { or } \frac{\left|Q_{\mathrm{C}}\right|}{\left|Q_{\mathrm{H}}\right|}=\frac{T_{\mathrm{C}}}{T_{\mathrm{H}}}\)) that this must always be the case in a Carnot cycle?
