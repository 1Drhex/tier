A Horizontal Mass–Spring System

A 2.00 kg mass, resting on a frictionless table, is attached to a spring of spring constant 20.0 N/m. The spring is stretched 20.0 cm by pulling on the mass, which is held stationary and then released.

(a) Calculate the angular frequency, frequency, and period.

(b) What are the amplitude and the phase constant of oscillations?

(c) What are the maximum speed and maximum acceleration of the mass?

(d) What is the displacement of the mass from equilibrium as a function of time?

Admin
Solution

The mass–spring system forms a simple harmonic oscillator, and when the mass is displaced from its equilibrium, it will undergo simple harmonic motion.

We choose a coordinate system with the origin at the equilibrium position of the mass and the positive x-axis toward the right. The initial conditions for this problem are

 

\(x(0)=0.200 m \quad \text { and } \quad v(0)=0 m / s\)

 

(a) The angular frequency, frequency, and period of the system are

 

\(\omega=\sqrt{\frac{20.0 N / m }{2.00 kg }}=3.16 rad / s\)

 

\(f=\frac{\omega}{2 \pi}=\frac{3.16 rad / s }{2 \pi rad }=0.503 Hz\)

 

\(T=\frac{1}{f}=1.99 s\)

 

(b) As the system oscillates, the position and velocity of the mass at any time t are given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}&x(t)=A \cos (\omega t+\phi) \\&v(t)=-\omega A \sin (\omega t+\phi)\end{aligned}\)

 

To determine A and \(\phi\), we use the given initial position and velocity of the mass. Evaluating x(t) and v(t) at t = 0, we obtain

 

\(x(0)=A \cos \phi\) (1)

 

\(v(0)=-\omega A \sin \phi\) (2)

 

Dividing both sides of Equation (2) by \(-\omega\), squaring and adding it to the square of Equation (1), and using the trigonometric identity \(\sin ^{2} \phi+\cos ^{2} \phi=1\), we can determine A:

 

\(A=\sqrt{(x(0))^{2}+\frac{(v(0))^{2}}{\omega^{2}}}\) (3)

 

To determine \(\phi\), divide Equation (2) by Equation (1):

 

\(\tan \phi=\frac{\sin \phi}{\cos \phi}=-\frac{v(0)}{\omega x(0)}\) (4)

 

\(\phi=\tan ^{-1}\left(-\frac{v(0)}{\omega x(0)}\right)\) (5)

 

Equations (3) and (5) tell us that A and \(\phi\) can be calculated when we know the position and the velocity of an oscillator at t = 0.

Inserting the values for x(0) and v(0) into Equations (3) and (5), we get

 

\(A=\sqrt{(0.200 m )^{2}+\frac{(0 m / s )^{2}}{(3.16 rad / s )^{2}}}=0.200 m\)

 

\(\phi=\tan ^{-1}\left(-\frac{0 m / s }{(3.16 rad / s ) \times(0.20 m )}\right)=\tan ^{-1} 0=0 rad\)

 

(c) The maximum speed of the mass is

 

\(v_{\max }=\omega A=3.16 rad / s \times 0.200 m =0.632 m / s\) (6)

 

and its maximum acceleration is

 

\(a_{\max }=\omega^{2} A=(3.16 rad / s )^{2} \times 0.200 m =2.00 m / s ^{2}\)

 

(d) The displacement of the mass from the equilibrium position at any time t is given by

 

\(x(t)=(0.200 m ) \cos (3.16 t)\) (7)

 

Notice that a unique determination of the phase constant requires information about the oscillator’s displacement and velocity at the start. A plot of the function \(\tan ^{-1} \theta\) is shown in Figure 13-17. For any \(\theta\) there is a single value between \(-\pi / 2 \text { and } \pi / 2\).
