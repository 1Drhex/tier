Guest Question.

Determine the Fourier series of the waveform shown in Fig. 17.1. Obtain the amplitude and phase spectra.

Admin
Solution

The Fourier series is given by Eq. (17.3), namely,

\( f(t) = \underbrace{a _0}_{dc} + \underbrace{\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{(a_n \cos n\omega _0t+b_n\sin n \omega _0 t)} }_{ac} \) (17.3)

\( f(t) = {a _0} +{\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{(a_n \cos n\omega _0t+b_n\sin n \omega _0 t)} } \) (17.1.1)

Our goal is to obtain the Fourier coefficients \( a_0, a_n \) and \( b_n \) using Eqs. (17.6), (17.8), and (17.9). First, we describe the waveform as

\( a_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)} dt \) (17.6)

\( a_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)\cos n\omega _0t}dt \) (17.8)

\( b_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)\sin n\omega _0t}dt \) (17.9)

\( f(t)=\begin{cases} 1, & 0<t<1 \\ 0, &1<t<2 \end{cases} \) (17.1.2)

and f (t) = f (t + T). Since T = 2, \( ω_0= 2 π/T = π \). Thus,

\( a_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)} dt = \frac{1}{2}\Biggl[\int_{0}^{1}{1}dt +\int_{1}^{2}{0} dt\Biggr]=\frac{1}{2} t\mid ^1_0 = \frac{1}{2} \) (17.1.3)

Using Eq. (17.8) along with Eq. (17.15a),

\( \int{\cos a t} dt = \frac{1}{a } \sin a t \) (17.15a)

\( a _n=\frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)\cos n\omega _0t} dt \\ = \frac{2}{2} \Biggl[\int_{0}^{1}{1\cos n\pi t} dt + \int_{1}^{2}{0\cos n\pi t} dt \\ = \frac{1}{n\pi }\sin n\pi t \mid ^1_0 = \frac{1}{n\pi }[\sin n\pi -\sin (0)] =0 \) (17.1.4)

From Eq. (17.9) with the aid of Eq. (17.15b),

\( \int{\sin a t} dt = -\frac{1}{a } \cos a t \) (17.15b)

\( b_n = \frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T}{f(t)\sin n\omega _0t} dt \\ = \frac{2}{2} \Biggl[\int_{0}^{1}{1\sin n\pi t} dt + \int_{1}^{2}{0\sin n\pi t} dt\Biggr] \\ = -\frac{1}{n\pi } \cos n\pi t\mid ^1_0 \\ = -\frac{1}{n\pi } (\cos n\pi -1) ,\cos n\pi = (-1)^n \\ = \frac{1}{n\pi }[1-(-1)^n] = \begin{cases} \frac{2}{n\pi } & n=odd \\ 0, & n= even \end{cases} \)

Substituting the Fourier coefficients in Eqs. (17.1.3) to (17.1.5) into Eq. (17.1.1) gives the Fourier series as

\( f(t) = \frac{1}{2}+\frac{2}{π } \sin π t + \frac{2}{3\pi } \sin 3\pi t + \frac{2}{5\pi } \sin 5\pi t + ……..\) (17.1.6)

Since f(t) contains only the dc component and the sine terms with the fundamental component and odd harmonics, it may be written as

\( f(t) = \frac{1}{2} +\frac{2}{\pi } \sum\limits_{k=1}^{\infty }{\frac{1}{n}\sin n\pi t }\) n = 2k – 1 (17.1.7)

By summing the terms one by one as demonstrated in Fig. 17.2, we notice how superposition of the terms can evolve into the original square. As more and more Fourier components are added, the sum gets closer and closer to the square wave. However, it is not possible in practice to sum the series in Eq. (17.1.6) or (17.1.7) to infinity. Only a partial sum (n=1,2,3,…….N , where N is finite) is possible. If we plot the partial sum (or truncated series) over one period for a large N as in Fig. 17.3, we notice that the partial sum oscillates above and below the actual value of f(t) . At the neighborhood of the points of discontinuity (x=0,1,2,….. ), there is overshoot and damped oscillation. In fact, an overshoot of about 9 percent of the peak value is always present, regardless of the number of terms used to approximate f(t). This is called the Gibbs phenomenon.

Finally, let us obtain the amplitude and phase spectra for the signal in Fig.17.1. Since \( α_n=0\)

\( A_n = \sqrt{a ^2_n + b^2_n} = \left|b_n\right| = \begin{cases} \frac{2}{n\pi } & n = odd\\0, & n= even\end{cases}\) (17.1.8)

\( \phi _n = -\tan ^{-1}\frac{b_n}{\alpha _n} = \begin{cases}-90^\circ & n = odd\\0, & n = even \end{cases}\) (17.1.9)

The plots of \( A_n\) and \( \phi _n\) for different values \( n ω_0 = n π\) of provide the amplitude and phase spectra in Fig. 17.4. Notice that the amplitudes of the harmonics decay very fast with frequency.
