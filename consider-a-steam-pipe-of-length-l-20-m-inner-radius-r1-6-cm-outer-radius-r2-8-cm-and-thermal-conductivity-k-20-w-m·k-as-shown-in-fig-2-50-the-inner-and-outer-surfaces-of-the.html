Guest Question.

Consider a steam pipe of length L = 20 m, inner radius r1 = 6 cm, outer radius r2 = 8 cm, and thermal conductivity k = 20 W/m·K, as shown in Fig. 2–50. The inner and outer surfaces of the pipe are maintained at average temperatures of T1 = 150°C and T2 = 60°C, respectively. Obtain a general relation for the temperature distribution inside the pipe under steady conditions, and determine the rate of heat loss from the steam through the pipe.

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION A steam pipe is subjected to specified temperatures on its surfaces. The variation of temperature and the rate of heat transfer are to be determined

Assumptions 1 Heat transfer is steady since there is no change with time. 2 Heat transfer is one-dimensional since there is thermal symmetry about the centerline and no variation in the axial direction, and thus T = T(r). 3 Thermal conductivity is constant. 4 There is no heat generation.

Properties The thermal conductivity is given to be k = 20 W/m·K.

Analysis The mathematical formulation of this problem can be expressed as

\( \frac{d}{d r}\left(r \frac{d T}{d r}\right)=0\)

with boundary conditions

\( T\left(r_{1}\right)=T_{1}=150^{\circ} \mathrm{C} \)

 

\( T\left(r_{2}\right)=T_{2}=60^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\)

Integrating the differential equation once with respect to r gives

\( r \frac{d T}{d r}=C_{1}\)

where \( C_{1}\) is an arbitrary constant. We now divide both sides of this equation by r to bring it to a readily integrable form,

\( \frac{d T}{d r}=\frac{C_{1}}{r}\)

Again integrating with respect to r gives (Fig. 2-51)

\( T(r)=C_{1} \ln r+C_{2}\) (a)

We now apply both boundary conditions by replacing all occurrences of r and \( \pi\) r)in Eq. (a) with the specified values at the boundaries. We get

\( T\left(r_{1}\right)=T_{1} \rightarrow C_{1} \ln r_{1}+C_{2}=T_{1} \)

 

\( T\left(r_{2}\right)=T_{2} \rightarrow C_{1} \ln r_{2}+C_{2}=T_{2}\)

which are two equations in two unknowns, \( C_{1}\) and \( C_{2}\). Solving them simultaneously gives

\( C_{1}=\frac{T_{2}-T_{1}}{\ln \left(r_{2} / r_{1}\right)} \text { and } C_{2}=T_{1}-\frac{T_{2}-T_{1}}{\ln \left(r_{2} / r_{1}\right)} \ln r_{1}\)

Substituting them into Eq. (a) and rearranging, the variation of temperature within the pipe is determined to be

\( T(r)=\frac{\ln \left(\gamma / r_{1}\right)}{\ln \left(r_{2} / r_{1}\right)}\left(T_{2}-T_{1}\right)+T_{1} (2-58) \)

The rate of heat loss from the steam is simply the total rate of heat conduction through the pipe, and is determined from Fourier’s law to be

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {cylinder }}=-k A \frac{d T}{d r}=-k(2 \pi r L) \frac{C_{1}}{r}=-2 \pi k L C_{1}=2 \pi k L \frac{T_{1}-T_{2}}{\ln \left(r_{2} / r_{1}\right)} (2-59) \)

The numerical value of the rate of heat conduction through the pipe is determined by substituting the given values

\( \dot{Q}=2 \pi(20 \mathrm{~W} / \mathrm{m} \cdot \mathrm{K})(20 \mathrm{~m}) \frac{(150-60)^{\circ} \mathrm{C}}{\ln (0.08 / 0.06)}=786 \mathrm{~kW}\)

Discussion Note that the total rate of heat transfer through a pipe is constant, but the heat flux \( \dot{q}=\dot{Q} /(2 \pi r) \) is not since it decreases in the direction of heat transfer with increasing radius.
