Guest Question.

The walls of a combustion chamber, 0.5 m in diameter and 2 m long, have an emissivity of 0.5 and are maintained at 750 K. If the combustion products containing 10% carbon dioxide and 10% water vapour are at \( 150 kN/m^{2}\) and 1250 K, what is the net rate of radiation to the walls?

Admin
Solution

The partial pressures of carbon dioxide \( (P_{c}) \) and of water \( (P_{w}) \) are :
\( P_{c} = P_{w} = (10/100)150=15.0kN/m^{2}\space or \space (15.0/100) = 0.15bar \)
From Table :

Geometry Characteristic Length Mean Beam Length, \( L_{e} \)
Sphere—radiation to surface Diameter, D 0.65D
Infinite circular cylinder—radiation to curved surface Diameter, D 0.95D
Semiinfinite cylinder—radiation to base Diameter, D 0.65D
Cylinder of equal height and diameter— radiation to entire surface Diameter, D 0.60D
Infinite parallel planes—radiation to planes Spacing between planes, L 1.80L
Cube—radiation to any surface Side, L 0.66L
Shape of volume, V—radiation to surface of area, A Ratio:
volume/area, (V/A)		 3.6(V/A)

\( L_{e} = 3.6V/A = 3.6(\pi /4\times 0.5^{2}\times 2)/[(2\pi /4\times 0.5^{2})+(0.5\pi \times 2.0)]=0.4m \)
For water vapour :
\( P_{w}L_{e} = (0.15\times 0.4) = 0.06bar\space m \)
and from figure 1.54 , \( e_{w} = 0.075 \)
\( P = (150/100)=1.5\space bar, \space P_{w} = 0.15\space bar\space and \space 0.5(P_{w}+P) = 0.825\space bar \)
Since \( P_{w}L_{e} = 0.06\) bar m, then from Fig. 1.54:
\( C_{w} = 1.4\space and \space e_{w} = (1.4\times 0.075)= 0.105 \)
For carbon dioxide:
\( P_{c}L_{e} = (0.15\times 0.4) = 0.06 \) bar m

and from Fig. 1.55, \( e_{c} =0.037 \)
Since P= 1.5 bar, \( P_{c} = 0.15\space bar, \space and \space P_{c}L_{e} = 0.06 \) bar m, then, from Fig. 1.47:
\( C_{c} = 1.2 \space and \space e_{c} = (1.2\times 0.037)= 0.044 \)
\( (P_{w}+P_{c})L_{e} = (0.15+0.15)0.4 = 0.12 \) bar m
and :
\( P_{c}/(P_{c}+P_{w})=0.15/(0.15+0.15)=0.5 \)
Thus, from Fig. 1.55 for \( T_{g}>1203K \), Δe=0.001 and from Equation
\( e_{g}=e_{w}+e_{c}-\Delta e \)
\( e_{g}=e_{w}-\Delta e = (0.105 + 0.044 – 0.001) = 0.148 \)
For water vapour:
\( P_{w}L_{e}(T_{s}/T_{g})=0.06(750/1250)=0.036 \) bar m
and from Fig. 1.54 at 750K, \( e_{w} = 0.12 \)
Since \( 0.5(P_{w}+P) = 0.825 \) bar and
\( P_{w}L_{e}(T_{s}/T_{g}) = P_{c}L_{e}(T_{s}/T_{g}) = 0.036 \) bar m,
Then from Fig. 1.54:
\( C_{w} = 1.40\space and \space e_{w} = (0.12\times 1.40) = 0.168 \)
and the absorptivity, from Equation
\( a_{w}= C_{w} e_{w}(T_{g}/T_{s})^{0.45} \) is :
\( a_{w}= e_{w}(T_{g}/T_{s})^{0.45}=0.168(1250/750)^{0.45} = 0.212 \)
For carbon dioxide:
From Figure 1.55 at 750K, \( e_{c} = 0.08 \)
From Figure 1.55 at P=1.5 bar and \( P_{c}L_{e}(T_{s}/T_{g})= 0.036 \) bar m and:
\( C_{c} = 1.02 \space and \space e_{c} = (0.08\times 1.02) = 0.082 \)

and the absorptivity, from Equation
\( a_{c} = C_{c}e_{c}(T_{g}/T_{s})^{0.65} \) is :
\( a_{c} = e_{c}(T_{g}/T_{s})^{0.65} = 0.082(1250/750)^{0.65} = 0.114 \)
\( P_{w}/(P_{c}+P_{w})= 0.5 \space and \space (P_{c}+P_{w})L_{e}(T_{s}/T_{g}) = (0.036+0.036)=0.072 \) bar m
Thus, from Figure 1.56,
for \( T_{g} = 813K, \Delta e=\Delta a\lt 0.01 \) and this may be neglected.
Thus:
\( a_{g} = a_{w} + a_{c} – \Delta a = (0.212+0.114-0) = 0.326 \)
If the surrounding surface is black, then:
\( Q = \sigma A_{s}(e_{g}T^{4}_{g}-\alpha _{g}T^{4}_{s} ) \)
\( =(5.67\times 10^{-8}[(2(\pi /4)0.5^{2})+(0.5\pi \times 2.0)])[(0.148\times 1250^{4})-(0.326\times 750^{4})] \)
\( 5.03\times 10^{4}W = 50.3kW \)
For grey walls, the correction factor allowing for multiple reflection of incident radiation is:
\( C_{g} = e_{s}/[1-(1-a_{g})(1-e_{g})] = 0.5/[1-(1-0.326)(1-0.5)]=0.754 \)
and hence: net radiation to the walls,
\( Q_{w} = (50.3\times 0.754) = 37.9kW \)
