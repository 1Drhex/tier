Guest Question.

Find the z transforms of (a) δ(nT), (b) u(nT), (c) u(nT−kT)K, (d) u(nT)K \( w ^{ n} \)
, (e) u(nT)\( e^{-\alpha nT} \) , ( f) r(nT), and (g) u(nT)sin \( \omega n \) T (see Table 3.1).

Function Deﬁnition
Unit impulse \( \delta(nT)=\begin{cases} 1 & for n=0 \\ 0 & for n\neq 0 \end{cases} \)
Unit step \( u(nT)=\begin{cases} 1 & for n\geq 0 \\ 0 & for n\lt 0 \end{cases} \)
Unit ramp \( r(nT)=\begin{cases} nT & for n\geq 0 \\ 0 & for n\lt 0 \end{cases} \)
Exponential \( u(nT)e^{\alpha nT},(\alpha \gt 0) \)
Exponential \( u(nT)e^{\alpha nT},(\alpha \lt 0) \)
Sinusoid \( u(nT)\sin \omega nT \)
Admin
Solution

(a) From the definitions of the z transform and δ(nT ), we have

\( Z\delta (nT)=\delta (0)+\delta (T)z^{-1}+\delta (2T)z^{-2}+\cdot \cdot \cdot =1 \)

(b) As in part (a)

\( Zu (nT)=u (0)+u (T)z^{-1}+u (2T)z^{-2}+\cdot \cdot \cdot =1+z^{-1}+z^{-2}+\cdot \cdot \cdot \)

The series at the right-hand side is a binomial series of \( (1-z^{-1} )^{-1} \), (see Eq.(A.47) ).

Hence, we have

\( (1+b)^{r}=1+\begin{pmatrix} r \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}b+\begin{pmatrix} r \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}b^{2}+\cdot \cdot \cdot +\begin{pmatrix} r \\ s \end{pmatrix}b^{s}+\cdot \cdot \cdot \) (A.47)

\( Zu(nT)=(1-z^{-1} )^{-1}=\frac{z}{z-1} \)

(c) From the time-shifting theorem (Theorem 3.4) and part (b), we have

\( Z[u(nT-kT)K]= Kz^{-k}Zu(nT) =\frac{Kz^{-(k-1)} }{z-1} \)

d) From the complex-scale-change theorem (Theorem 3.5) and part (b), we get

\( Z[u(nT)Kw^{n} ]= KZ[(\frac{1}{w} )^{-n} u(nT)] \)

 

\( = KZu(nT)\mid_{z\rightarrow z/w}=\frac{Kz}{z-w} \)

(e) By letting K = 1 and \( w=e^{-\alpha T} \) in part (d), we obtain

\( Z[u(nT)e^{-\alpha nT} ]=\frac{z}{z-e^{-\alpha T} } \)

( f ) From the complex-differentiation theorem (Theorem 3.6) and part (b), we have

\( Zr(nT)=Z[nTu(nT)]=-Tz\frac{d}{dz}[Zu(nT)] \)

 

\( =-Tz\frac{d}{dz}[\frac{z}{(z-1)} ]=\frac{Tz}{(z-1)^{2} } \)

(g) From part (e), we deduce

\( Z[u(nT)\sin \omega nT]=Z[\frac{u(nT)}{2j}(e^{j\omega nT}-e^{-j\omega nT} ) ] \)

 

\( =\frac{1}{2j}Z[u(nT)e^{j\omega nT} ]-\frac{1}{2j}Z[u(nT)e^{-j\omega nT} ] \)

 

\( =\frac{1}{2j}(\frac{z}{z-e^{jwT} }-\frac{z}{z-e^{-j\omega T} }) \)

 

\( =\frac{z\sin \omega T}{z^{2}-2z\cos \omega T+1 } \)

 

Theorem 3.4 Time Shifting For any positive or negative integer m,

\( Zx(nT+mT)=z^{m}X(z) \)

Proof From the definition of the z transform

\( Zx(nT+mT)=\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT+mT)z^{-n} } \)

 

\( =z^{m}\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x[(n+m)T)]z^{-(n+m)} } \)

If we now make the variable substitution \( n+m=\acute{n} \) and then replace \( \acute{n} \) by n, we have

\( Zx(nT+mT)=z^{m}\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)z^{-n} }=z^{m}X(z) \)

If m is negative, then x(nT +mT) = x(nT − |m|T ) and thus the signal is delayed by |m|T s.
As a consequence, the z transform of a discrete-time signal which is delayed by an integer number of sampling periods is obtained by simply multiplying its z transform by the appropriate negative power of z. On the other hand, multiplying the z transform of a signal by a positive power of z causes the signal to be advanced or shifted to the left with respect to the time axis.

 

Theorem 3.5 Complex Scale Change For an arbitrary real or complex constant w,

\( Z[w^{-n}x(nT) ]=X(wz) \)

Proof

\( Z[w^{-n}x(nT) ]=\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{[w^{-n}x(nT) ]z^{-n} } \)

 

\( =\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)(wz)^{-n} } \)

 

\(= X(wz) \)

Evidently, multiplying a discrete-time signal by \( w^{-n} \) is equivalent to replacing z by wz in its z transform. If the signal is multiplied by \( \upsilon ^{n} \), then we can write \( \upsilon ^{n}=(1/\upsilon )^{-n} \) and thus

\( Z[\upsilon ^{n}x(nT) ]= Z[(\frac{1}{\upsilon } )^{-n}x(nT) ]=X(z/\upsilon ) \)

 

Theorem 3.6 Complex Differentiation

\( Z[nT_{1}x(nT) ]=-T_{1}z\frac{dX(z)}{dz} \)
Proof

\( Z[nT_{1}x(nT) ]=\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{nT_{1}x(nT) z^{-n} }=-T_{1}z \sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)(-n)z^{-n-1} } \)

 

\( =-T_{1}z\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)\frac{d}{dz}(z^{-n} ) } \)

 

\( =-T_{1}z\frac{d}{dz}[\sum\limits_{n=-\infty }^{\infty }{x(nT)z^{-n} } ]=-T_{1}z\frac{dX(z)}{dz} \)

Changing the order of summation and differentiation is allowed in the last equation for values of z for which X(z) converges.

Complex differentiation provides a simple way of obtaining the z transform of a discrete-time
signal that can be expressed as a product \( nT_{1}x(nT) \) by simply differentiating the z transform of X
