Guest Question.

Consider a cold medium of width I (\(0\leq x\leq L\)) with refractive index n = 1, bounded by vacuum (resulting in nonreflecting interfaces). The medium absorbs, scatters isotropically, and is subjected to a square laser pulse at x = 0, according to

\(q_{0}(0,t)=q_{0}[H(t)-H(t-t_{p})]\),

where \(t_{p}\) is the duration of the pulse and H(t) is Heaviside’s unit step function\(.^{4}\) Determine the transmissivity of the slab as a function of time. (This is essentially the example problem carried out by Mitra and Kumar [23] and by Wu and Ou [31].)

 

Admin
Solution

For a nonemitting and isotropically scattering, one-dimensional medium, the equations for the \(P_{1}\) – and \(P_{1/3}\)-approximations reduce to

\(\frac{\Game G}{\Game t^{*}}+\frac{\Game q}{\Game \tau } =-(1-w)G+wG_{c},\)
\(3a\frac{\Game q}{\Game t^{*}}+\frac{\Game G}{\Game \tau }=-3q,\)

where a = 1 for \(P_{1}\) and a=1/3 for \(P_{1/3}\), and G and q have been normalized as \(G=G_{d}/q_{0}\) and \(q=q_{d}/q_{0}\). These two equations are subject to the initial and boundary conditions

\(t^{*}=0 : G(0,\tau )=q(0,\tau )=0,\)
\(\tau =0 : -2q(t^{*},0)=G(t^{*},0),\)
\(\tau =\tau _{L}: +2q(t^{*},\tau _{L})=G(t^{*},\tau _{L})\)

The normalized isotropic scattering source is immediately found from equations (18.25) \(S_{c}(r,\widehat{s},t )=\frac{1}{4\pi }[1-\rho (r_{w})]q_{0}(r_{w},t-s/c)e^{-\tau _{c}}\Phi (\widehat{s},\widehat{s}_{c} )\) and (18.18) \(S_{c}=\frac{1}{4\pi }(G_{c}+A_{1}q_{c}\cdot \widehat{s})=\frac{1}{4\pi } [1-\rho (r_{w})]q(r_{w})e^{-\tau _{c}} (1+A_{1}\widehat{s}\cdot \widehat{s}_{c} )\) for a nonreflecting boundary, as

\(G_{c}(t^{*},\tau )=\left[H(t^{*}-\tau )-H^{*}(t^{*}-\tau -t^{*}_{p})\right]e^{-\tau }\)

The hyperbolic nature of this set of equations becomes obvious, if q is eliminated from them (by differentiating the first with respect to \(t^{*}\) and the second with respect to τ), leading to

\(\frac{\Game ^{2}G}{\Game t^{*2}}-\frac{1}{3a} \frac{\Game ^{2}G}{\Game \tau ^{2}}+\left(1-w+\frac{1}{a} \right) \frac{\Game G}{\Game t^{*}} +\frac{1-w}{a}G-\frac{w}{a}G_{c}-w\frac{\Game G_{c}}{\Game t^{*}}=0,\)

which has a signal velocity of \(1/\sqrt{3}a\) (nondimensional in terms of speed of light, c), as already indicated in the formulation for the \(P_{a}\) methods. Eliminating q also from initial and boundary conditions gives

\(t^{*}=0 : G(0,\tau )=\frac{\Game G}{\Game t^{*}}(0,\tau )=0,\)

\(\tau =0: 3\left\lgroup G(t^{*},0)+ a \frac{\Game G}{\Game t^{*}}(t^{*},0)\right\rgroup -2\frac{\Game G}{\Game \tau }(t^{*},0)=0 ,\)

\(\tau =\tau _{L}: 3\left\lgroup G(t^{*},0)+ a \frac{\Game G}{\Game t^{*}}(t^{*},0)\right\rgroup +2\frac{\Game G}{\Game \tau }(t^{*},0)=0.\)

This second-order hyperbolic equation is readily solved by the method of characteristics [47] along the characteristic lines \(\tau =\pm t^{*}/\sqrt{3}a\) , and this was done in the program transPN given in Appendix F.
Some typical results for the temporal transmission rate, \(q_{c}(\tau _{L})+q_{d}(\tau _{L})/q_{o}\), are shown in Fig. 18-5, for a slab with an optical thickness of \(\tau _{L}=5\) and a nondimensional pulse width\(t^{*}_{p}|=0.3\) and are compared with results from a Monte Carlo simulation (see Problem 20.22). The transmissivity remains zero until \(t^{*}=\tau _{L}\), since it takes the direct component that amount of time to traverse the layer. As is clearly visible in the inset, between \(\tau _{L}\lt t^{*}\lt \tau _{L}+ t^{*}_{p}\) the transmissivity is dominated by the direct component \(q_{c}(\tau _{L})/q_{0}\), while the scattered contribution builds up gradually and, for w = 1, reaches its maximum around \(t^{*}\simeq 9\).
It is apparent that the \(P_{1}\)-approximation, with its \(c/\sqrt{3}a\) signal velocity, woefully lags behind the true transmissivity, and later overshoots, while the \(P_{1/3}\)-approximation predicts the transient behavior rather accurately.
