Exploring RL Circuits

In the RL circuit shown in Figure 25-34, the battery has an emf of 20.0 V, the resistor has a resistance of 10.0 立, and the inductor has an inductance of 40.0 mH.

(a) What is the time constant of this circuit?

(b) How long after the switch is set to position a does it take the current to reach 99% of its maximum value? Express your answer in seconds and in terms of the time constant.

(c) What is the current at this time?

(d) When the current reaches 99% of its maximum value, what is the emf across the inductor?

(e) After 20.0 s, the switch is moved to position b. How long will it take for the current to decrease to 10% of its maximum value?

Admin
Solution

(a) The time constant of this circuit, according to Equation 25-45, is \(\tau=L / R=\frac{40.0 mH }{10.0 \Omega}=4.00 ms\)

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}i(t)=\frac{\varepsilon}{R} e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}} \\V_{ L } \equiv \varepsilon_{ L }=\varepsilon e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\end{array} \text { where } \tau=L / R\right.\) {25-45}

 

(b) To find how long it will take the current to reach 99% of its maximum value, we use Equation 25-43:

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}i(t)=\frac{\varepsilon}{R}\left(1-e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\right) \\V_{ L } \equiv \varepsilon_{ L }=-\varepsilon e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\end{array}\right.\) {25-43}

 

\(i(t)=\frac{\varepsilon}{R}\left(1-e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\right)\)

 

\(0.99 \frac{\varepsilon}{R}=\frac{\varepsilon}{R}\left(1-e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}\right)\)

 

\(e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}=0.01 \Rightarrow t=-\tau \ln 0.01\)

 

Since \(\ln 0.01=-4.61 \Rightarrow\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}t &=4.61 \tau=4.61 \frac{L}{R}=4.61\left(\frac{40.0 \times 10^{-3} H }{10.0 \Omega}\right) \\&=4.61(4.00 ms )=18.4 ms\end{aligned}\)

 

The current will reach 99% of its maximum value after \(4.61 \tau \text { or } 18.4 ms\).

(c) The maximum value of the current is

 

\(I_{\max }=\frac{\varepsilon}{R}=\frac{20.0 V }{10.0 \Omega}=2.00 A\)

 

and 99% of this value is 1.98 A.

(d) When the current reaches 99% of its maximum value, the emf across the inductor can be calculated using Equation 25-45:

 

\(\varepsilon_{ L }=\varepsilon e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}}=(20.0 V ) e^{-\frac{18.4 ms }{4.00 ms }}=(20.0 V ) e^{-4.6}=0.201 V\)

 

(e) The switch is moved to position b after 20.0 s. Since this time interval is over 4000 times the circuit time constant (4.00 ms), we can assume that at the time the switch was moved to position b, the current had already reached its maximum value. Let \(t_{10}\) represent the time when the current reaches 10% of its initial value. Then, \(i\left(t_{10}\right)=0.1 I_{\max }=0.1 \varepsilon / R\). Substituting into Equation 25-45 gives

 

\(\begin{aligned}0.1 I_{\max } &=\frac{\varepsilon}{R} e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}} \Rightarrow 0.1 \frac{\varepsilon}{R}=\frac{\varepsilon}{R} e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}} \\0.1 &=e^{-\frac{t}{\tau}} \Rightarrow t=-\tau \ln 0.1=2.3 \tau \\&=2.3(4.00 ms )=9.2 ms\end{aligned}\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

When the current is changing rapidly, the back emf across an inductor is large, and when the current is steady, the back emf across the inductor becomes negligible. Thus, the presence of an inductor opposes the rapid changes in the circuit. Therefore, we conclude that due to the presence of an inductor in an electric circuit, the current cannot reach its maximum value or diminish to zero instantaneously.
