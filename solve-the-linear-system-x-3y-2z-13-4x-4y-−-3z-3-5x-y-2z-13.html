Guest Question.

Solve the linear system:

\(x + 3y + 2z = 13\)
\(4x + 4y − 3z = 3\)
\(5x + y + 2z = 13\)

Admin
Solution

How can we find the unknowns \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\)?

We use Gaussian elimination with back substitution.
The augmented matrix is:

\(\begin{matrix} _{Row~1} & _{R~1} \\ _{Row~2} & _{R~2} \\ _{Row~3} & _{R~3} \end{matrix} \left\lgroup \begin{matrix} _{1} & _{3} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \\ _{4} & _{4} & _{- 3} & _{|} & _{3} \\ _{5} & _{1} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \end{matrix} \right\rgroup Need~to~convert~the~entries~in~this~triangle~to~zeros.\)

Our aim is to convert this augmented matrix so that there are 0’s in the bottom left hand corner, that is;
the first 4 in the second row reduces to zero, and the 5 and 1 from the bottom row reduce to zero. Hence
4 → 0, 5 → 0 and 1 →0.

To get 0 in place of the first 4 in the middle row we multiply row 1, \(R_{1}\), by 4 and take the result away from row 2, \(R_{2}\), that is \(R_{2} – 4 R_{1}\). To get 0 in place of 5 in the bottom row we multiply row 1, \(R_{1}\), by 5 and take the result away from row 3, \(R_{3}\), that is \(R_{3} – 5 R_{1}\). Combining the two row operations, \(R_{2} – 4 R_{1}\) and \(R_{3} – 5 R_{1}\), we have

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{2} = R_{2} – 4 R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{3} = R_{3} – 5 R_{1} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{3} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \\ _{4 – \left(4\times 1\right) } & _{4 – \left(4\times 3\right) } & _{- 3- \left(4\times 2\right) } & _{|} & _{3 – \left(4\times 13\right) } \\ _{5 – \left(5\times 1\right) } & _{1 – \left(5\times 3\right) } & _{2 – \left(5\times 2\right) } & _{|} & _{13 – \left(5\times 13\right) } \end{matrix} \right)\)

We call the new row 2 and \(3 – R^{\dagger }_{2}\) and \(R^{\dagger }_{3}\) respectively. This simplifies to:

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{3} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \\ _{0} & _{- 8} & _{- 11} & _{|} & _{- 49} \\ _{0} & _{- 14} & _{- 8} & _{|} & _{- 52} \end{matrix} \right)\)

We have nearly obtained the required matrix with zeros in the bottom left hand corner. We need a 0 in
place of \(– 14\) in the new bottom row, \(R^{\dagger }_{3}\). We can only use the second and third rows, \(R^{\dagger }_{2}\) and \(R^{\dagger }_{3}\).

Why?
Because if we use first row, \(R_{1}\), we will get a non-zero number in place of the zero already established in \(R^{\dagger }_{3}\).

How can we obtain a 0 in place of \(– 14\)?

\(R^{\dagger }_{3} – \frac{14}{8} R^{\dagger }_{2}\) because \(– 14 – \left[\frac{14}{8} \times \left(- 8\right) \right] = 0\) (gives 0 in place of \(– 14\))

Therefore

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\dagger \dagger }_{3} = R^{\dagger }_{3} – \frac{14}{8} R^{\dagger }_{2} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{3} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \\ _{0} & _{- 8} & _{- 11} & _{|} & _{- 49} \\ _{0} & _{- 14 – \left[\frac{14}{8} \times \left(- 8\right) \right] } & _{- 8 – \left[\frac{14}{8} \times \left(- 11\right) \right] } & _{|} & _{- 52 – \left(\frac{14}{8} \times \left(- 49\right) \right) } \end{matrix} \right)\)

which simplifies to

\(\begin{matrix} _{} & _{x~} & _{y~} & _{~z} ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~\end{matrix}\)

\(\begin{matrix} _{R_{1} } \\ _{R^{\dagger }_{2} } \\ _{R^{\dagger \dagger }_{3} } \end{matrix} \left(\begin{matrix} _{1} & _{3} & _{2} & _{|} & _{13} \\ _{0} & _{- 8} & _{- 11} & _{|} & _{- 49} \\ _{0} & _{0} & _{45 /4} & _{|} & _{135 /4} \end{matrix} \right) ~~~~~~~~~~~\left(^{\ast } \right)\)

This time we have called the bottom row \(R^{\dagger \dagger }_{3}\). From this row, \(R^{\dagger \dagger }_{3}\), we have

\(\frac{45}{4} z = \frac{135}{4}\) which gives \(z = \frac{135}{4} = 3\)

How can we find the unknown, \(y\)?

By expanding the second row \(R^{\dagger }_{2}\) in \(\left(^{\ast } \right)\) we have

\(– 8 y – 11 z = – 49\)

We know from above that \(z = 3\), therefore, substituting \(z = 3\) gives

\(– 8 y – 11 \left(3\right) = – 49\)

\(– 8 y – 33 = – 49\)

\(– 8 y = – 49 + 33 = – 16\) which yields \(y = \left(- 16\right) /\left(- 8\right) = 2\)

So far we have \(y = 2\) and \(z = 3\).

How can we find the last unknown, \(x\)?

By expanding the first row \(R_{1}\), of \(\left(^{\ast } \right)\) we have

\(x + 3 y + 2 z = 13\)

Substituting our values already found, \(y = 2\) and \(z = 3\), we have

\(x + \left(3\times 2\right) + \left(2\times 3\right) = 13\)

\(x + 6 + 6 = 13\) which gives \(x = 1\)

Hence \(x = 1\), \(y = 2\) and \(z = 3\) is our solution. We can illustrate these equations as shown in Fig. 1.10.

We can check that this solution is correct by substituting these \(x = 1\), \(y = 2\) and \(z = 3\) into the given
equations.
