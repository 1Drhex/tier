Guest Question.

Dynamic similarity may be obtained by using a cryogenic wind tunnel in which nitrogen at low temperature and high pressure is employed as the working fluid. If nitrogen at 5 atm and 183 K is used to test the low-speed aerodynamics of a prototype that has a 24.38 m wing span and is to fly at standard sea-level conditions at a speed of 60 m/s, determine

1. The scale of the model to be tested

2. The ratio of forces between the model and the full-scale aircraft

Conditions of dynamic similarity should prevail. The speed of sound in nitrogen at 183 K is 275 m/s.

Admin
Solution

For dynamic similarity to exist, we know that both model and prototype must be geometrically similar and that the Reynolds number and the Mach number must be the same. A table such as the following is helpful.

 

Model Prototype
Characteristic length L 24.38 m
Velocity \( \upsilon \) 60 m/s
Viscosity \( \mu \) \( 1.789 \cdot 10^{-5} Pa \cdot s \)
Density \( \rho \) \( 1.225 kg/m^{3} \)
Speed of sound 275 m/s 340 m/s

 

The conditions listed for the prototype have been obtained from Appendix I. Equating Mach numbers, we obtain

\( M_{m} = M_{p} \)

 

\( \upsilon = \frac{275}{340} 60 = 48.5 m/s \)

 

Equating the Reynolds numbers of the model and the prototype, we obtain

\( R e_{m} = R e_{p} \)

 

\( \frac{\rho 48.5 L}{\mu } = \frac{1.225 \cdot 60 \cdot 24.38}{1.789 \cdot 10^{-5}} = 1.002 \times 10^{8} \)

 

Using equation (\( \mu = \frac{4}{3} m \lambda Z \)), we may evaluate m for nitrogen. From Appendix K, \( \varepsilon /\kappa = 91.5 K \) and \( \sigma = 3.681 \mathring{A} \) for nitrogen so that \( \kappa T/\varepsilon = 2 \) and \( \Omega \mu = 1.175 \) (Appendix K). Thus,

\( \mu = 2.6693 \cdot 10^{-6} \frac{\sqrt{28 \cdot 183}}{\left ( 3.681 \right )^{2} \left ( 1.175 \right )} = 1.200 \cdot 10^{-5} Pa \cdot s \)

 

The density may be approximated from the perfect gas law

 

\( \rho = \frac{P}{P_{1}} \frac{M}{M_{1}} \frac{T_{1}}{T} \rho_{1} \)
so that

\( \rho = 5 \left ( \frac{28}{28.96} \right ) \left ( \frac{288}{183} \right ) 1.225 = 9.32 kg/m^{3} \)

 

Solving for the wing span of the model, we obtain

\( L = 3.26 m \left ( 10.7 ft \right ) \)

 

The ratio of the forces on the model to the forces experienced by the prototype may be determined equating values of Eu between the model and the prototype. Hence

 

\( \left ( \frac{F}{\rho V^{2} A_{R}} \right )_{model} = \left ( \frac{F}{\rho V^{2} A_{R}} \right )_{prototype} \)

 

where \( A_{R} \) is a suitable reference area. For an aircraft, this reference area is the projected wing area. The ratio of model force to prototype force is then given by

 

\( \frac{F_{m}}{F_{p}} = \frac{\rho_{m}}{\rho_{p}} \frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{p}^{2}} \frac{A_{R.m}}{A_{R.p}} = \frac{\left ( \rho V^{2} \right )_{m}}{\left ( \rho V^{2} \right )_{p}} \left ( \frac{l_{m}}{l_{p}} \right )^{2} \)

 

where the ratio of reference areas can be expressed in terms of the scale ratio. Substituting numbers,

 

\( \frac{F_{m}}{F_{p}} = \frac{9.32}{1.225} \left ( \frac{48.5}{60.0} \right )^{2} \left ( \frac{3.26}{24.38} \right )^{2} = 0.089 \)

 

The forces on the model are seen to be 8.9% the prototype forces.
