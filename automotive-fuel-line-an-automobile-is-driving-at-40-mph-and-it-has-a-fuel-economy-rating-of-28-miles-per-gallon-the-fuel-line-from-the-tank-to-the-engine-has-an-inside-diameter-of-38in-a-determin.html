Automotive Fuel Line An automobile is driving at 40 mph, and it has a fuel economy rating of 28 miles per gallon. The fuel line from the tank to the engine has an inside diameter of \(\frac{3}{8} \text { in. }\) (a) Determine the volumetric flow rate of fuel in units of \(ft ^{3} / s\). (b) In the dimensions of in./s, what is the gasoline’s average velocity? (c) What is the Reynolds number for this flow? We can use the given information for the automobile’s speed and fuel economy rating to find the volumetric rate at which gasoline is consumed. Then, knowing the cross-sectional area of the fuel line [Equation (5.1)], we will apply Equation (6.10) to determine the flow’s average velocity. Lastly, we calculate the Reynolds number using Equation (6.6), where the characteristic length is the fuel line’s diameter. The density and viscosity of gasoline are listed in Table 6.1.

 

\(A=\pi \frac{d^{2}}{4}\) (Equation 5.1)

 

\(q=A v_{\text {avg }}\) (Equation 6.10)

 

\(R e=\frac{\rho v l}{\mu}\) (Equation 6.6)

 

Table 6.1 Density and Viscosity Values for Sevweral Gases and Liquid at Room Temperature and Pressure

Helium 0.182 3.53 × 10-4 1.9 × 10-5 4.1 × 10-7
Freshwter 1000 1.94 1.0 × 10-3 2.1 × 10-5
Seawater 1026 1.99 1.2 × 10-3 2.5 × 10-5
Gasoline 680 1.32 2.9 × 10-4 6.1 × 10-6
SAE 30 oil 917 1.78 0.26 5.4 × 10-3

Solution

(a) The volumetric flow rate is the ratio of the vehicle’s speed and the fuel economy rating:

 

\(\begin{aligned}q &=\frac{40 mi / h }{28 mi / gal } \\&=1.429\left(\frac{ mi }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ gal }{ mi }\right) \\&=1.429 \frac{ gal }{ h }\end{aligned}\)

 

Converting from a per-hour basis to a per-second basis, this rate is equivalent to

 

\(\begin{aligned}q &=\left(1.429 \frac{ gal }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{1}{3600} \frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=3.968 \times 10^{-4}\left(\frac{ gal }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=3.968 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ gal }{ s }\end{aligned}\)

 

By applying a factor from Table 6.3, we next convert this quantity to the dimensionally consistent basis \(\text { of } ft ^{3} \text { : }\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}q &=\left(3.968 \times 10^{-4} \frac{ gal }{ s }\right)\left(0.1337 \frac{ ft ^{3} / s }{ gal / s }\right) \\&=5.306 \times 10^{-5}\left(\frac{ gal }{ s }\right)\left(\frac{ ft ^{3} / s }{ gal / s }\right) \\&=5.306 \times 10^{-5} \frac{ ft ^{3}}{ s }\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) The cross-sectional area of the fuel line is

 

\(\begin{aligned}A &=\frac{\pi}{4}(0.375 \text { in. })^{2} \leftarrow\left[A=\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}\right] \\&=0.1104 in ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

or \(A=7.670 \times 10^{-4} ft ^{2}\) since 1 ft =12 in. The average velocity of the gasoline is

 

\(\begin{aligned}v_{\text {avg }} &=\frac{5.306 \times 10^{-5} ft ^{3} / s }{7.670 \times 10^{-4} ft ^{2}} \leftarrow\left[q=A v_{\text {avg }}\right] \\&=6.917 \times 10^{-2}\left(\frac{ ft ^{2} \cdot ft }{ s }\right)\left(\frac{1}{ ft ^{2}}\right) \\&=6.917 \times 10^{-2} \frac{ ft }{ s }\end{aligned}\) \(\text { or } v_{\text {avg }}=0.8301 in . / s\)

 

(c) Since the fuel line’s diameter is \(d=\frac{3}{8} \text { in. }=3.125 \times 10^{-2} ft\), the Reynolds number for this flow is

 

\(\begin{aligned}R e &=\frac{\left(1.32 slug / ft ^{3}\right)\left(6.917 \times 10^{-2} ft / s \right)\left(3.125 \times 10^{-2} ft \right)}{6.1 \times 10^{-6} \text { slug } /( ft \cdot s )} \leftarrow\left[R e=\frac{\rho v l}{\mu}\right] \\&=467.8\left(\frac{\text { slug }}{ ft ^{3}}\right)\left(\frac{ ft }{ s }\right)( ft )\left(\frac{ ft \cdot s }{ slug }\right) \\&=467.8\end{aligned}\)
