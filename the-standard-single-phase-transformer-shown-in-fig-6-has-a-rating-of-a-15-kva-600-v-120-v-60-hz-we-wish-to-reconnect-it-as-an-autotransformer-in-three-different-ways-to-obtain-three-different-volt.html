Guest Question.

The standard single-phase transformer shown in Fig. 6 has a rating of a 15 kVA, 600 V/120 V, 60 Hz. We wish to reconnect it as an autotransformer in three different ways to obtain three different voltage ratios:
a. 600 V primary to 480 V secondary
b. 600 V primary to 720 V secondary
c. 120 V primary to 480 V secondary
Calculate the maximum load the transformer can carry in each case.

Admin
Solution

Nominal current of the 600 V winding is

 

\(I_{1}=S / E_{1}=15000 / 600=25 A\)

 

Nominal current of the 120 V winding is

 

\(I_{2}=S / E_{2}=15000 / 120=125 A\)

 

a. To obtain 480 V, the secondary voltage (120 V) between terminals \(X_{1}, X_{2}\) must subtract from the primary voltage (600 V). Consequently, we connect terminals having the same polarity together, as shown in Fig. 7. The corresponding schematic diagram is given in Fig. 8.

 

Note that the current in the 120 V winding is the same as that in the load. Because this winding has a nominal current rating of 125 A, the load can draw a maximum power.

 

\(S_{ a }=125 A \times 480 V =60 kVA\)

 

The currents flowing in the circuit at full-load are shown in Fig. 8. Note the following:

 

1. If we assume that the current of 125 A flows from \(X_{1} \text { to } X_{2}\) in the winding, a current of 25 A must flow from \(H _{2} \text { to } H _{1}\) in the other winding. All other currents are then found by applying Kirchhoffâ€™s current law.

 

2. The apparent power supplied by the source is equal to that absorbed by the load:

 

\(S=100 A \times 600 V =60 kVA\)

 

b. To obtain a ratio of 600 V/720 V, the secondary voltage must add to the primary voltage: 600 + 120 = 720 V. Consequently, terminals of opposite polarity \(\left( H _{1} \text { and } X _{2}\right)\) must be connected together, as shown in Fig. 9.

 

The current in the secondary winding is again the same as that in the load, and therefore the maximum load current is again 125 A. The maximum load is now

 

\(S_{ b }=125 A \times 720 V =90 kVA\)

 

The previous examples show that when a conventional transformer is connected as an autotransformer, it can supply a load far greater than the rated capacity of the transformer. As mentioned earlier, this is one of the advantages of using an autotransformer instead of a conventional transformer. However, this is not always the case, as the next part of our example shows.

 

c. To obtain the desired ratio of 120 V to 480 V, we again connect \(H _{1} \text { and } X _{1}\) (as in solution a), but the source is now connected to terminals \(X _{1}, X _{2} \text { (Fig. 10). }\)

 

This time, the current in the 600 V winding is the same as that in the load; consequently, the maximum load current cannot exceed 25 A. The corresponding maximum load is, therefore,

 

\(S_{ c }=25 A \times 480 V =12 kVA\)

 

This load is lessthan the nominal rating (15 kVA) of the standard transformer.

 

We want to make one final remark concerning these three autotransformer connections. The temperature rise of the transformer is the same in each case, even though the loads are respectively 60 kVA, 90 kVA, and 12 kVA. The reason is that the currents in the windings and the flux in the core are identical in each case and so the losses are the same.
