Interdependent Projects with a Limited Budget

Consider six investment projects as shown in Table 15.9 and their interdependencies as follows:

  • Projects A and B are mutually exclusive.
  • Projects C and F are independent projects.
  • Project D is contingent on project C.
  • Project E is contingent on project B.

Solution

Given: IRRs for six different projects with interdependencies as specified and the marginal cost of capital as a function of budget.
Find: (a) Number of mutually exclusive alternatives, (b) Optimal capital budget, and (c) an appropriate MARR with a budget limit of $600.

Discussion: If we calculate the IRR for each project as in Table 15.10, we know that project F is not a good one as its IRR is less than 12.5% (cost of capital to borrow $150). Therefore, we can eliminate the project from the mutually exclusive list.

(b) Optimal capital budget and the projects to be funded:

  • Total investment approach: If we want to adopt the net present worth maximization, we need to calculate the NPW for each alternative at its appropriate cost of capital. For example, to find the net present worth for ALT 6 which requires $300, we look up the MCC table to see how much it would cost to finance the amount. It is 13%. Then, we use 13% to calculate the NPW, which is $133. We do the same for the rest of alternatives, and find that ALT 9 is the best alternative. The optimal capital budget is $500.
  • Incremental Rate of Return Approach: To determine the optimal capital budget, we need to identify the best alternative out of 12 alternatives. Since the MARR is a function of projects selected, we may conduct the incremental analysis to determine the best alternative. In doing so, we need to do a series of pairwise comparisons as shown in Table 15.12.

  • For example, ALT4 versus ALT 3 – the higher cost investment ALT 4 will cost additional $100 over ALT 3. According to the cost of capital schedule, this additional $100 will be financed at the marginal cost of capital of 12.5% if we decided to go with ALT 4.
  • Incremental analysis (ALT 4 – ALT 3):

(-$100, $20, $20, $80) \(\rightarrow \Delta\)IRR = 7.6% \(\lt\) 12.5%

  • So, it is not worth spending additional $100 to fund ALT 4. Then we drop ALT 4 from the list. We go to the next alternative on the list, which ALT 5.
  • Incremental analysis (ALT 5 – ALT 3):

(-$100, $50, $50, $50)\(\rightarrow \Delta\)IRR = 23.4% \(\gt\) 12.5%

So, we prefer ALT 5 over ALT 3. This process continues until no alternative left to compare. It turns out that ALT 9 will be the best alternative, which requires $500 investment with the marginal cost of capital of 14%. The optimal capital budget is depicted in Figure 15.7.

(c) With a budget limit of $800, which projects to fund? What is the appropriate MARR?

If you are allowed to borrow up to $800, you would only do so up to $500, as the best portfolio still remains at (CBE), and your MARR should be 14%.
