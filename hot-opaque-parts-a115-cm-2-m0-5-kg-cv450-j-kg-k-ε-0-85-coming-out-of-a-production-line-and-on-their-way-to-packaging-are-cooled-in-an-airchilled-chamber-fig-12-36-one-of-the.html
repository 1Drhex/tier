Guest Question.

Hot opaque parts \( \left(A_{1}=15 cm ^{2}, m=0.5 kg , c_{v}=450 J / kg \cdot K , \varepsilon=0.85\right) \), coming out of a production line and on their way to packaging, are cooled in an airchilled chamber (Fig. 12-36). One of the reasons for cooling the parts prior to the packing operation is to alleviate the risk of thermal burn hazard. For the parts to be safe for handling by the operators, each part should be cooled to below \( 45^{\circ} C\). To monitor the temperatures of the parts, two identical radiometers are placed at a distance of 1 m from the parts on the conveyor at the entrance and exit of the cooling chamber. Throughout the entire process, irradiation on the parts from the surroundings is estimated to be \( 300 W / m ^{2}\). Inside the cooling chamber, 215 W of heat is removed from each part in the duration of 1 minute. If the radiometer at the cooling chamber entrance detects an irradiation of\( 0.21 W / m ^{2}\), determine the irradiation that the radiometer at the cooling chamber exit would measure. Would the parts exiting the cooling chamber be safe for handling?

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION In this example, the concepts of Prevention through Design (PtD) are applied in conjunction with the concepts of radiation intensity and radiative properties.

Assumptions 1 Both part surface and radiometer can be approximated as differential surfaces since both are very small compared to the square of the distance between them. 2 Temperatures of parts are uniform. 3 Kirchhoff’s law is applicable. 4 Parts are opaque and their properties are constant.

Properties The specific heat and the emissivity of the parts are given as \( c_{y}=(450 J / kg \cdot K\) and \( \varepsilon=0.85 . \) The Stefan-Boltzmann constant is \( \sigma=5.67 \times 10^{-8} W / m ^{2} \cdot K ^{4}\)

Analysis From Kirchhoff’s law, the emissivity of the parts is

\( \varepsilon=\alpha=0.85\)

 

The parts are opaque \( \tau=0\) and the reflectivity of the parts is

 

\( \rho=1-\alpha=1-0.85=0.15\)

 

The solid angle subtended by both radiometers when viewed from the parts at the entrance and exit of the cooling chamber is

\( \omega_{2-1} \cong \frac{A_{n, 2}}{L^{2}}=\frac{A_{2} \cos \theta_{2}}{L^{2}}\)

 

The intensities of the radiation emitted and reflected (radiosity) by the parts \( A_{1}\) at the entrance and exit of the cooling chamber are

\( I_{1, \text { in }}=\frac{J_{\text {in }}}{\pi}=\frac{E_{\text {in }}+G_{\text {ref }}}{\pi}=\frac{\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { in }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}}{\pi} \text { and } I_{1, \text { out }}=\frac{J_{\text {out }}}{\pi}=\frac{E_{\text {out }}+G_{\text {ref }}}{\pi}=\frac{\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { out }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}}{\pi}\)

 

The irradiations measured by the radiometers \( A_{2}\) at the entrance and exit of the cooling chamber are

\( G_{2, \text { in }}=\frac{\dot{Q}_{1-2, \text { in }}}{A_{2}}=\frac{I_{1, \text { in }}\left(A_{1} \cos \theta_{1}\right) \omega_{2-1}}{A_{2}}=\frac{\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { in }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}\right) A_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}\right)\left(\cos \theta_{2}\right)}{\pi L^{2}}\)

 

and

 

\( G_{2, \text { out }}=\frac{\dot{Q}_{1-2, \text { out }}}{A_{2}}=\frac{I_{1, \text { out }}\left(A_{1} \cos \theta_{1}\right) \omega_{2-1}}{A_{2}}=\frac{\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { out }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}\right) A_{1}\left(\cos \theta_{1}\right)\left(\cos \theta_{2}\right)}{\pi L^{2}}\)

 

With both radiometers placed normal to the direction of viewing from each part that passes them

\(\left(\theta_{1}=\theta_{2}=0^{\circ}\right) \), we have

With both radiometers placed normal to the direction of viewing from each part that passes them \( \left(\theta_{1}=\theta_{2}=0^{\circ}\right) \), we have

\( G_{2, \text { in }}=\frac{\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { in }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}\right) A_{1}}{\pi L^{2}} \text { and } G_{2, \text { out }}=\frac{\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { out }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}\right) A_{1}}{\pi L^{2}}\)

 

From the given irradiation \( G_{2, \text { in }}\) detected by the radiometer at the cooling chamber entrance, the temperature of the parts at that location is

 

\(T_{1, \text { in }} =\left[\left(\frac{G_{2, in } \pi L^{2}}{A_{1}}-\rho G_{1}\right)\left(\frac{1}{\varepsilon \sigma}\right)\right]^{1 / 4} \)

 

\( =\left\{\left[\frac{\left(0.21 W / m ^{2}\right) \pi(1.5 m )^{2}}{15 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2}}-(0.15)\left(300 W / m ^{2}\right) R B \frac{1}{(0.85)\left(5.67 \times 10^{-8} W / m ^{2} \cdot K ^{4}\right)}\right]\right\}^{1 / 4} \)

 

\( =374 K =101^{\circ} C\)

 

The temperature of the parts at the exit of the cooling chamber can be determined from the change in the internal energy and the heat removed from the parts during the cooling process:

\( Q=\dot{Q} \Delta t=\Delta U=m c_{v}\left(T_{1, \text { in }}-T_{1, \text { out }}\right) \quad \text { or } \quad T_{1, \text { out }}=T_{1, \text { in }}-\frac{\dot{Q} \Delta t}{m c_{v}}\)

 

Thus,

 

\( T_{1, \text { out }}=101^{\circ} C -\frac{(215 W )(60 s )}{(0.5 kg )(450 J / kg \cdot K )}=43.7^{\circ} C <45^{\circ} C\)

 

Finally, the irradiation \( G_{2, \text { out }}\) measured by the radiometer at the exit of the cooling chamber is

\( G_{2, \text { out }} =\frac{\left(\varepsilon \sigma T_{1, \text { out }}^{4}+\rho G_{1}\right) A_{1}}{\pi L^{2}} \)

 

\( =\frac{(0.85)\left(5.67 \times 10^{-8} W / m ^{2} \cdot K ^{4}\right)(43.7+273)^{4} K ^{4}+(0.15)\left(300 W / m ^{2}\right)}{\pi(1.5 m )^{2}}\left(15 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2}\right) \)

 

\( =0.112 W / m ^{2}\)

 

Discussion The temperature of the parts exiting the cooling chamber is below \( 45^{\circ} C\), therefore they are safe for handling in the packing operation. Having two radiometers monitoring the temperatures of the parts entering and exiting the cooling chambers, allow the engineers to determine the cooling duration necessary for the parts. Therefore, the conveyor speed can be optimized and at the same time preventing thermal burn hazard.
