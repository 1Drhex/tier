Outsourcing Production of Electric Compressors

JK Engineering Company manufactures a variety of air-conditioning units for automobiles. The company has just received an offer from an outside supplier to provide the electric compressor for the company’s heat pump system line, at a price of $42 per unit, over the next 7 years. The company is interested in this offer, since its own production of electric compressor strains many parts of heat pump production systems. The company estimates that if the supplier’s offer were accepted, the direct
labor and variable overhead costs of the heat pump system line would be reduced by 20% and the direct materials cost by 25%. Under present operations, the company manufactures all of its own heat pump system from start to finish. Fixed overhead costs charged to the heat pump system line total $500,000 each year. The cost of producing 50,000 units of heat pump system per year with and without outsourcing is given Table 6.1.

Solution

Given: Cash flows for two options and i = 18%.
Find: Unit cost for each option and the option preferred.

We may itemize the annual costs associated with each option as follows:

• Make Option: $270 per unit
• Buy option: The two additional cost components are capital cost and operating cost.

Capital cost:

CR(18%) = ($325,000 – $60,000)(A/P, 18%, 7)
+ $60,000(0.18)
= $80,326

Operating cost:

OC(18%) = $120,000

Total annual equivalent cost related to the owning and operating the inspection machine:

\(AEC\left(18\%\right)_{Inspection}\) = CR(18%) + OC(18%) = $200,326

Unit cost increment due to inspection = \(\frac{\$200,326}{50,000} = \$4.01\)

Total unit cost:

Unit cost = $218.50 (with outsourcing) + $42 + $4.01 = $264.51

Buying the electric compressor units from the outside vendor will save JK $5.49 per unit before any consideration of taxes. Figure 6.6 summarizes the make-or-buy analysis for this decision problem.

Comments: Certainly, knowing how much it costs to produce will provide important information in setting the price of the product. Two important noneconomic factors should also be considered. The first is the question of whether the quality of the supplier’s component is better than, equal to, or worse than the component the firm is presently manufacturing. The second is the reliability of the supplier in terms of providing the needed quantities of the product on a timely basis. A reduction in quality or reliability should virtually always rule out buying.
