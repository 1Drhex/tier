Analyzing an RL Circuit that has Sequential Switching

The two switches in the circuit shown in Fig. 7.31 have been closed for a long time. At t = 0, switch 1 is opened. Then, 35 ms later, switch 2 is opened.

a) Find\( i_{L}(t)\) for 0 ≤ t ≤ 35 ms.

b) Find \(i_{L}\) for t ≥ 35 ms.

c) What percentage of the initial energy stored in the 150 mH inductor is dissipated in the 18 Ω resistor?

d) Repeat (c) for the 3 Ω

e) Repeat (c) for the 6 Ω resistor.

Admin
Solution

a) For t < 0 both switches are closed, causing the 150 mH inductor to short-circuit the 18 Ω resistor. The equivalent circuit is shown in Fig. 7.32.We determine the initial current in the inductor by solving for \(i_{L}(0^{-})\) in the circuit shown in Fig. 7.32. After making several source transformations we find \(i_{L}(0^{-})\) to be 6 A. For 0 ≤ t ≤ 35 ms, switch 1 is open (switch 2 is closed), which disconnects the 60 V voltage source and the 4 Ω and 12 Ω resistors from the circuit. The inductor is no longer behaving as a short circuit (because the dc source is no longer in the circuit), so the 18 Ω resistor is no longer short-circuited. The equivalent circuit is shown in Fig. 7.33. Note that the equivalent resistance across the terminals of the inductor is the parallel combination of 9 Ω and 18 Ω , or 6 Ω. The time constant of the circuit is (150/6) ×\( 10^{-3}\), or 25 ms. Therefore the expression for\( i_{L} \)is

\(i_{L} = 6e^{-40t} \)A, 0 ≤ t ≤ 35 ms.

b) When t = 35 ms, the value of the inductor current is

\(i_{L} = 6e^{-1.4} = 1.48 A\).

Thus, when switch 2 is opened, the circuit reduces to the one shown in Fig. 7.34, and the time constant changes to \((150/9) × 10^{-3}\), or 16.67 ms. The expression for \( i_{L}\) becomes

\(i_{L} = 1.48e^{-60(t-0.035)} \)A, t ≥ 35 ms.

Note that the exponential function is shifted in time by 35 ms.

c) The 18 Ω resistor is in the circuit only during the first 35 ms of the switching sequence. During this interval, the voltage across the resistor is

\(v_{L} = 0.15\frac {d}{dt}(6e^{-40t})\)

= \(-36e^{-40t} \)V, 0 < t < 35 ms.

The power dissipated in the 18 Ω resistor is

\(p = \frac {v_{L}^{2}}{18} = 72e^{-80t} W, 0 < t < 35 ms\).

Hence the energy dissipated is

\( \omega = \int _{0}^{0.035}{72e^{-80t} dt}\)

=\(\frac {72}{-80}e^{-80t} |_{0}^{0.035} \)

= \(0.9(1 – e^{-2.8})\)

= 845.27 mJ.

The initial energy stored in the 150 mH inductor is

\( \omega_{i} = \frac {1}{2}(0.15)(36) = 2.7 J = 2700 mJ\).

Therefore (845.27/2700) × 100, 31.31% of the initial energy stored in the 150 mH inductor is dissipated in the 18 Ω resistor.

d) For 0 < t < 35 ms, the voltage across the 3 Ω resistor is

\( v_{3 \omega} = \left(\frac{v_{L}}{9}\right)(3)\)

=\(\frac {1}{3}v_{L}\)

= \(-12e^{-40t} \)V.

Therefore the energy dissipated in the 3 Ω resistor in the first 35 ms is

\( \omega _{3 \Omega}= \int _{0}^{0.035}{\frac {144e^{-80t}}{3} dt}\)

= \(0.6(1 – e^{-2.8})\)

= 563.51 mJ.

For t > 35 ms, the current in the 3 Ω resistor is

\(i_{3 Ω} = i_{L} = (6e^{-1.4})e^{-60(t-0.035)} \)A.

Hence the energy dissipated in the 3 Ω resistor for t > 35 ms is

\( \omega _{3 \Omega}= \int _{0.035}^{∞}{i_{3\Omega}^{2} × 3 dt}\)

= \( \int _{0.035}^{∞}{3(36)e^{-2.8}e^{-120(t-0.035)} dt}\)

= \(108e^{-2.8} × \frac {e^{-120(t-0.035)}}{ -120}|_{0.035}^{∞}\)

= \( \frac{108}{120}e^{-2.8} = 54.73 mJ.\)

The total energy dissipated in the 3 Ω resistor is

\( \omega _{3 \Omega}(total) = 563.51 + 54.73\)

= 618.24 mJ.

The percentage of the initial energy stored is

\( \frac {618.24}{2700} × 100 = 22.90 \%\).

e) Because the 6 Ω resistor is in series with the 3 Ω resistor, the energy dissipated and the percentage of the initial energy stored will be twice that of the 3 Ω resistor.

\( \omega _{6 \Omega}(total) = 1236.48 mJ\),

and the percentage of the initial energy stored is 45.80%.We check these calculations by observing that

1236.48 + 618.24 + 845.27 = 2699.99 mJ

and

31.31 + 22.90 + 45.80 = 100.01%.

The small discrepancies in the summations are the result of roundoff errors.
