Guest Question.

POTENTIAL OF A LINE OF CHARGE

Positive electric charge Q is distributed uniformly along a line of length 2a lying along the y-axis between \(y=-a \text { and } y=+a\) (Fig. 23.21). Find the electric potential at a point P on the x-axis at a distance x from the origin.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

This is the situation of (Section 21.5), where we found an expression for the electric field \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}}\) at an arbitrary point on the x-axis. We can find V at point P by using Eq. (23.16) to integrate over the charge distribution. Unlike the situation in , each charge element dQ is a different distance from point P, so the integration will take a little more effort.

\(V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{d q}{r}\) (23.16)

EXECUTE:

To find the potential at P due to the entire rod, we integrate dV over the length of the rod from \(y=-a \text { to } y=a\):

\(V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{Q}{2 a} \int_{-a}^{a} \frac{d y}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\)

You can look up the integral in a table. The final result is

\(V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{Q}{2 a} \ln \left(\frac{\sqrt{a^{2}+x^{2}}+a}{\sqrt{a^{2}+x^{2}}-a}\right)\)

We can check our result by letting x approach infinity.
In this limit the point P is infinitely far from all of the charge, so we expect V to approach zero; you can verify that it does.
We know the electric field at all points along the x-axis from . We invite you to use this information to find V along this axis by integrating \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}}\) as in Eq. (23.17), \(V_{a}-V_{b}=\int_{a}^{b} \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}} \cdot d \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{l}}=\int_{a}^{b} E \cos \phi d l\).
