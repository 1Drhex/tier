Guest Question.

In a vacuum chamber a current of \( 10A \) is flowing in a long, straight wire, which has a very high conductivity. Electrons with an initial velocity \( v_0 \) start moving perpendicularly towards the wire from a point which is a radial distance \( r_0 \) away from the wire. Given that they cannot approach any closer to the wire than \( r_0/2, \) determine \( v_0. \) Ignore the effect of the Earth’s magnetic field.

Admin
Solution

The speed of the electrons remains constant in the frame of reference fixed to the vacuum chamber \( (S) \) because the magnetic field can only change the direction of the velocity of the moving charge. Consider another frame of reference \( (S^\prime) \) moving parallel to the wire with constant speed \( v_0 \) with respect to the first one. We can write the Lorentz force experienced by a particle of charge \( Q \) in either frame:

\( \pmb F^\prime = \pmb F = Q(\pmb v \times \pmb B) = Q(\pmb v^\prime + \pmb v_0) \times \pmb B = Q(\pmb v^\prime \times \pmb B^\prime ) + Q \pmb E^\prime . \)

We first note that the electric current in both frames of reference must be the same. In frame \( S, \) there are moving electrons and static positive ions whilst the wire itself is neutral. In reference frame \( S^\prime , \) the speed of electrons and so the current of electrons will be different, but the current of the moving positive ions just compensates for this change. The consequence of this is that there is the same magnetic field in both frames \( (\pmb B^\prime = \pmb B).\) The equation above shows that in frame \( S^\prime \) we have, in addition to the unaltered magnetic field, an electric field given by \( \pmb v_0 \times \pmb B. \) (Note that the transformation of a single magnetic field from \( S \) to \( S^ \prime \) does not generally alter the magnetic field if the speed of the moving system is much less than the speed of light.)

Let us now describe an electron’s motion in reference frame \( S^ \prime . \) In this frame there is an electric field (perpendicular to the wire) of strength

\( E(r) = v_0 B(r) = \frac{μ_0 v_0 I}{2π} \frac{1}{r} , \)

where \( r \) is the distance from the wire. Using the analogy of the cylindrical capacitor, we can find an appropriate electric potential function for this field (i.e. one that is such that minus its derivative with respect to \( r \) gives the field), namely,

\( U(r) = – \frac{μ_0 v_0 I}{2π} \ln r.\)

In the frame \( S^\prime , \) the initial speed of the electron (at a distance \( r \) from the wire) is \( \sqrt{2}v_0, \) and the electron just stops when it has approached within \( r_0/2 \) of the wire. We can apply the work-energy theorem to the motion of the electron as follows,

\( \frac{1}{2} m ( \sqrt{2}v_0) ^2 = – \frac{ μ_0 v_0 I Q }{2π} (\ln \frac{r_0}{2} – \ln r_0) =\frac{ μ_0 v_0 I Q }{2π} \ln 2. \)

Inserting numerical values into this expression, gives the initial speed of the electron as

\( v_0 = \frac{\mu _0 Q}{2m \pi } I \times \ln 2 \approx 2.46 \times 10^5 \ m \ s^{-1} \approx 250 \ km \ s^{-1}. \)

Note. (i) The initial speed of the electron is very large on a macroscopic scale, but is very small for electrons; an electron attains this speed if it is accelerated through a potential difference of as little as \( 0.2 \ V. \) Since \( 250 \ km \ s^{-1} \) is much less than \( c, \) we justifiably ignored the relativistic mass increase of the electron in this problem.
(ii) It is interesting to observe that if the electron cannot approach the wire closer than \( r_0/2, \) then the maximum distance of the electron is also limited-it cannot be further away than \( 2r_0. \) More generally, if the minimum distance is \( r_0 /n, \) then the maximum distance must be \( nr_0.\) This can be proved using another frame of reference moving with velocity \( -v_0. \)
