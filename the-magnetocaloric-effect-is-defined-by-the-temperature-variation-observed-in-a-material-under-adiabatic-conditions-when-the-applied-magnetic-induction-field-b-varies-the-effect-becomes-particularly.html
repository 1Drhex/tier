Guest Question.

The magnetocaloric effect is defined by the temperature variation observed in a material under adiabatic conditions when the applied magnetic induction field B varies. The effect becomes particularly large when the material undergoes a phase transition due to the magnetic induction field variation. An application of this effect was considered for space exploration. The applied magnetic induction field may be produced by permanent magnets. Show that the infinitesimal temperature variation dT during an adiabatic process characterised by an infinitesimal magnetic induction field variation dB at constant pressure, is given by,

\( dT = − \frac{T}{c_B} \frac{∂M}{∂T} .dB \).

where \( c_B \) is the specific heat per unit volume at constant magnetic induction field B.

Admin
Solution

A reversible adiabatic process is charaterised by a constant entropy. Thus,

\( ds (T,B) = \frac{∂s (T,B)}{∂T} dT + \frac{∂s (T,B)}{∂B} dB = 0 \).

According to relation (9.122), the specific heat per unit volume at constant magnetic induction field B is defined as,

\( c_B (T,B) ≡ \frac{\partial h_m}{\partial T}\mid _B = T \frac{\partial s(T,B)}{\partial T} \), \( L_B (T,B) ≡ \frac{\partial h_m}{\partial B}\mid _T \) (9.122)

\( c_B = T \frac{∂s (T,B)}{∂T} \).

which implies that,

\( dT = − \frac{T}{c_B} \frac{∂s}{∂B}· dB \).

According to relation (9.29), the differential of the magnetic free enthalpy density yields,

\( dg_m (T, \left\{n_A\right\} , P,B) = −s dT + \sum\limits_{A=1}^{r} μ_A dn_A + E · dP − M · dB \). (9.29)

\( dg_m (T,B) = −s dT − M· dB \).

Applying the Schwarz theorem (4.67) to the magnetic free enthalpy density \( g_m \) (T,B) we obtain,

\( \frac{∂}{∂x} \Bigl(\frac{∂f(x,y)}{∂y}\Bigr) = \frac{∂}{∂y} \Bigl(\frac{∂f(x,y)}{∂x}\Bigr) \) (4.67)

\( \frac{∂}{∂B} \Bigl(\frac{∂g_m}{∂T}\Bigr) = \frac{∂}{∂T} \Bigl(\frac{∂g_m}{∂B}\Bigr) \).

which yields the Maxwell relation,

\( \frac{∂s}{∂B} = \frac{∂M}{∂T} \).

Thus, the temperature differential can be recast as,

\( dT = −\frac{T}{c_B} \frac{∂M}{∂T}· dB \).
