Guest Question.

An engineer uses a temperature sensor mounted in a thermowell to measure the temperature in a CSTR. The temperature sensor/transmitter combination operates approximately as a first-order system with a time constant of 3 s. The thermowell behaves like a first-order system with a time constant of 10 s. The engineer notes that the measured reactor temperature has been cycling approximately sinusoidally between \( 180 \space^{∘}C \space and \space 183\space ^{∘}C \) with a period of 30 s for at least several minutes. What can be concluded concerning the actual temperature in the reactor?

Admin
Solution

First, note that the sensor/transmitter and the transmission line act as two first-order processes in series Equation
\( G(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{K_{1}K_{2}}{(\tau_{1}s+1)(\tau_{2}s+1)} = \frac{K}{(\tau_{1}s+1)(\tau_{2}s+1)} \)
with an overall gain K = 1, and with the approximate transfer function
\( \frac{T^{\prime }_{meas}(s)}{T^{\prime }_{reactor}} = \frac{1}{(3s+1)(10s+1)} \) (1)
From the reported results, we conclude that some disturbance has caused the actual reactor temperature (and its deviation) to vary sinusoidally, which, in turn, has caused the recorded output to oscillate. The cycling has continued for a period of time that is much longer than the time constants of the process—that is, the instrumentation system. Hence, the transients have died out and we can infer the conditions in the reactor from the measured results, using Equation
\( \widehat{A} = \frac{KA}{\sqrt{[1-(w\tau )^{2}]^{2}+(2\zeta w\tau )^{2}} } \) (2)
for the sinusoidal response of a second-order system. From Equation (1) , \( \tau _{1} = 3s \space and \space \tau _{2} = 10s; \) ; τ and ζ are calculated from Equations
\( \tau ^{2} = \tau _{1}\tau _{2} \)
and
\( 2\zeta \tau = \tau _{1}+\tau _{2} \) :
\( \tau = \sqrt{(3)(10)} = 5.48s \)
\( \zeta = \frac{13}{2(5.477)}=1.19 \)
The frequency of the perturbing sinusoidal signal (reactor temperature) is calculated from the observed period of 30 s:
\( w = \frac{2\pi }{P} = \frac{6.28}{30} = 0.2093 \space rad/s \)
The amplitude of the output perturbation also is obtained from observed results as
\( \widehat{A} = \frac{183-180}{2} = 1.5 \space ^{\circ }C \)
Equation (2) now can be rearranged to calculate the amplitude of the actual reactor temperature
\( A = \frac{\widehat{A} }{K}\sqrt{[1-(w\tau )^{2}]^{2}+(2\zeta w\tau )^{2}} \)
from which \( A=4.12\space ^{∘}C \). Thus, the actual reactor temperature is varying between \( 181.5-4.12 = 177.38 \space ^{∘}C \) and
\( 181.5+4.12 = 185.62 \space ^{∘}C \) , nearly three times the variation indicated by the measured value.
Because the second-order process in this example is overdamped (ζ=1.19), we expect that sinusoidal perturbations in the reactor temperature always will be attenuated (reduced in amplitude) in the measurement system regardless of the frequency of the perturbation.
Further discussion of sinusoidal forcing is contained in Chapter 14 on frequency response techniques.
