HEIGHT AND RANGE OF A JECTILE I: A BATTED BASEBALL

A batter hits a baseball so that it leaves the bat at speed v0 = 37.0 m/s at an angle α0 = 53.1°.

(a) Find the position of the ball and its velocity (magnitude and direction) at t = 2.00 s

(b) Find the time when the ball reaches the highest point of its flight, and its height h at this time.

(c) Find the horizontal range R—that is, the horizontal distance from the starting point to where the ball hits the ground—and the ball’s velocity just before it hits.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

As Fig. 3.20 shows, air resistance strongly affects the motion of a baseball. For simplicity, however, we’ll ignore air resistance here and use the projectile-motion equations to describe the motion. The ball leaves the bat at t = 0 a meter or so above ground level, but we’ll ignore this distance and assume that it starts at ground level (y0 = 0). Figure 3.23 shows our sketch of the ball’s trajectory. We’ll use the same coordinate system as in Figs. 3.17 and 3.18, so we can use Eqs. (3.19) (\(x=\left(v_{0} \cos \alpha_{0}\right) t\)) through (3.22) (\(v_{y}=v_{0} \sin \alpha_{0}-g t\)).
Our target variables are (a) the position and velocity of the ball 2.00 s after it leaves the bat, (b) the time t when the ball is at its maximum height (that is, when \(v_y\) = 0) and the y-coordinate at this time, and (c) the x-coordinate when the ball returns to ground level (y = 0) and the ball’s vertical component of velocity then.

 

EXECUTE:

: (a) We want to find x, y, \(v_x\), and \(v_y\) at t = 2.00 s. The initial velocity of the ball has components

\(v_{0 x}=v_{0} \cos \alpha_{0}=(37.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}) \cos 53.1^{\circ}=22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

\(v_{0 y}=v_{0} \sin \alpha_{0}=(37.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}) \sin 53.1^{\circ}=29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

From Eqs. (3.19) (\(x=\left(v_{0} \cos \alpha_{0}\right) t\)) through (3.22) (\(v_{y}=v_{0} \sin \alpha_{0}-g t\)),

\(x=v_{0 x} t=(22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})(2.00 \mathrm{~s})=44.4 \mathrm{~m}\)

 

\(y=v_{0 y} t-\frac{1}{2} g t^{2}\)\(=(29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})(2.00 \mathrm{~s})-\frac{1}{2}\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(2.00 \mathrm{~s})^{2}=39.6 \mathrm{~m}\)

 

\(v_{x}=v_{0 x}=22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

\(v_{y}=v_{0 y}-g t=29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}-\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(2.00 \mathrm{~s})=10.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

The y-component of velocity is positive at t = 2.00 s, so the ball is still moving upward (Fig. 3.23) (\(r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\)). From Eqs. (3.24) (\(v=\sqrt{v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}}\)) and (3.25) (\(\tan \alpha=\frac{v_{y}}{v_{x}}\)), the magnitude and direction of the velocity are

\(v=\sqrt{v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}}=\sqrt{(22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}+(10.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})^{2}}=24.4 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

 

\(\alpha=\arctan \left(\frac{10.0 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}\right)=\arctan 0.450=24.2^{\circ}\)

The ball is moving at 24.4 m/s in a direction 24.2° above the horizontal.

(b) At the highest point, the vertical velocity \(v_y\) is zero. Call the time when this happens \(t_1\); then

\(v_{y}=v_{0 y}-g t_{1}=0\)

 

\(t_{1}=\frac{v_{0 y}}{g}=\frac{29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}{9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}}=3.02 \mathrm{~s}\)

The height h at the highest point is the value of y at time \(t_1\):

\(h=v_{0 y} t_{1}-\frac{1}{2} g t_{1}^{2}\)\(=(29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})(3.02 \mathrm{~s})-\frac{1}{2}\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(3.02 \mathrm{~s})^{2}=44.7 \mathrm{~m}\)

(c) We’ll find the horizontal range in two steps. First, we find the time \(t_2\) when y = 0 (the ball is at ground level):

\(y=0=v_{0 y} t_{2}-\frac{1}{2} g t_{2}^{2}=t_{2}\left(v_{0 y}-\frac{1}{2} g t_{2}\right)\)

This is a quadratic equation for \(t_2\). It has two roots:

The ball is at y = 0 at both times. The ball leaves the ground at \(t_{2}=0, \text { and it hits the ground at } t_{2}=2 v_{0 y} / g=6.04 \mathrm{~s}\).

The horizontal range R is the value of x when the ball returns to the ground at \(t_2\) = 6.04 s:

\(R=v_{0 x} t_{2}=(22.2 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s})(6.04 \mathrm{~s})=134 \mathrm{~m}\)

The vertical component of velocity when the ball hits the ground is

\(v_{y}=v_{0 y}-g t_{2}=29.6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}-\left(9.80 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}\right)(6.04 \mathrm{~s})\)= -29.6 m/s

That is, \(v_y\) has the same magnitude as the initial vertical velocity \(v_{0y}\) but the opposite direction (down). Since \(v_x\) is constant, the angle α = -53.1° (below the horizontal) at this point is the negative of the initial angle \(\alpha_{0}\) = 53.1°.

EVALUATE: : It’s often useful to check results by getting them in a different way. For example, we can also find the maximum height in part (b) by applying the constant-acceleration formula Eq. (2.13) (\(v_{x}^{2}=v_{0 x}^{2}+2 a_{x}\left(x-x_{0}\right)\)) to the y-motion:

\(v_{y}^{2}=v_{0 y}^{2}+2 a_{y}\left(y-y_{0}\right)=v_{0 y}^{2}-2 g\left(y-y_{0}\right)\)

At the highest point, \(v_y\) = 0 and y = h. Solve this equation for h; you should get the answer that we obtained in part (b). (Do you?)

Note that the time to hit the ground, \(t_2\) = 6.04 s, is exactly twice the time to reach the highest point, \(t_1\) = 3.02 s. Hence the time of descent equals the time of ascent. This is always true if the starting point and endpoint are at the same elevation and if air resistance can be ignored.

Note also that h = 44.7 m in part (b) is comparable to the 61.0-m height above second base of the roof at Marlins Park in Miami, and the horizontal range R = 134 m in part (c) is greater than the 99.7-m distance from home plate to the right-field fence at Safeco Field in Seattle. In reality, due to air resistance (which we have ignored) a batted ball with the initial speed and angle we’ve used here won’t go as high or as far as we’ve calculated (see Fig. 3.20).
