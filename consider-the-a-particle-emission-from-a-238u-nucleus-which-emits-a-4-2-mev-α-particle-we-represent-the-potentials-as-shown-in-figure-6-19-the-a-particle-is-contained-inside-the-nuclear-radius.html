Guest Question.

Consider the a-particle emission from a \({ }^{238} U\) nucleus, which emits a 4.2-MeV α particle. We represent the potentials as shown in Figure 6.19. The a particle is contained inside the nuclear radius of \(r_{N} \approx 7 \times 10^{-15}\) m. Find the barrier height and the distance the α particle must tunnel and use a square-top potential to calculate the tunneling probability.

Strategy We shall calculate the barrier height \(\left[V_{ C }\left(r=r_{N}\right)\right.\) in Figure 6.19] by calculating the Coulomb potential between an α particle and the remainder of the uranium nucleus for a separation of the nuclear radius, \(7 \times 10^{-15}\) m. We find the tunneling distance by setting the kinetic energy equal to the Coulomb potential and then use Equation (6.70) to determine the tunneling probability.

\(T=16 \frac{E}{V_{0}}\left(1-\frac{E}{V_{0}}\right) e^{-2 \kappa L}\) (6.70)

Admin
Solution

The Coulomb potential is

 

\(\begin{aligned}V_{ C }=& \frac{Z_{1} Z_{2} e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r_{N}} \\=& \frac{2(92)\left(1.6 \times 10^{-19} C \right)^{2}\left(9 \times 10^{9} N \cdot m ^{2} / C ^{2}\right)}{7 \times 10^{-15} m } \\& \times \frac{10^{-6} MeV }{1.6 \times 10^{-19} J } \\=& 38 MeV\end{aligned}\)

 

We determine the distance \(r^{\prime}\) through which the α particle must tunnel by setting \(K=V_{ C }\left(r=r^{\prime}\right)\) at that distance (see Figure 6.19). Because K = 4.2 MeV, we have

 

\(4.2 MeV =\frac{Z_{1} Z_{2} e^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} r^{\prime}}\)

 

We solve this equation for \(r^{\prime}\):

 

\(r^{\prime}=\frac{38 MeV }{4.2 MeV } r_{N}=6.3 \times 10^{-14} m =63 fm\)

 

where we have used the values above for \(V_{ C } \text { and } r_{N}\).

We make a simple, but rough, approximation of a square-top potential where V = 38 MeV for \(7 fm <r<63 fm\). We then find

 

\(\begin{aligned}\kappa &=\frac{\sqrt{2 m(V-E)}}{\hbar} \\&=\frac{\sqrt{2\left(3727 MeV / c^{2}\right)(38 MeV -4.2 MeV )}}{6.58 \times 10^{-22} MeV \cdot s } \\&=2.5 \times 10^{15} m ^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

 

where the mass of the a particle is 3727 \(MeV / c^{2}\). The barrier width L is the difference between \(r^{\prime} \text { and } r_{N}\).

 

\(\begin{aligned}L &=r^{\prime}-r_{N} \\&=63 fm -7 fm =56 fm\end{aligned}\)

 

The value of \(\kappa L=\left(2.5 \times 10^{15} m ^{-1}\right)\left(56 \times 10^{-15} m \right)=140\). Because \(k L \gg 1\), we use Equation (6.70) to calculate the tunneling probability.

 

\(\begin{aligned}T &=16\left(\frac{4.2 MeV }{38 MeV }\right)\left(1-\frac{4.2 MeV }{38 MeV }\right) e^{-280} \\&=1.6 e^{-280}=4 \times 10^{-121}\end{aligned}\)

 

which is an extremely small number.

Our assumption of a square-top potential of the full height and full width is unrealistic. A closer approximation to the potential shown in Figure 6.19 would be a square-top potential of only half the maximum Coulomb potential (19 MeV rather than 38 MeV) and a barrier width of only half L (28 fm rather than 56 fm). If we use 19 MeV in the calculation of κ we obtain \(1.7 \times 10^{15} m ^{-1}\). The tunneling probability now becomes

 

\(\begin{aligned}T=& 16 \frac{4.2 MeV }{18 MeV }\left(1-\frac{4.2 MeV }{18 MeV }\right) \\& \times \exp \left[-2\left(1.7 \times 10^{15} m ^{-1}\right)\left(2.8 \times 10^{-14} m \right)\right] \\=& 2.8 \exp (-95)=1.5 \times 10^{-41}\end{aligned}\)

 

This still seems like a very low probability, but let us see if we can determine how much time it takes the α particle to tunnel out. If the α particle has a kinetic energy of 4.2 MeV, its speed is determined nonrelativistically by

 

\(K=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}\)

 

\(v=\sqrt{\frac{2 K}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(4.2 MeV )}{3727 MeV / c^{2}}}=0.047 c=1.4 \times 10^{7} m / s\)

 

The diameter of the nucleus is about \(1.4 \times 10^{-14}\) m, so it takes the \(\alpha \text { particle }\left(1.4 \times 10^{-14} m \right) /\left(1.4 \times 10^{7} m / s \right) \approx 10^{-21}\) s to cross. The α particle must make many traverses back and forth across the nucleus before it can escape. According to our probability calculation it must make about \(10^{41}\) attempts, so we estimate the a particle may tunnel through in about \(10^{20}\) s. The half-life of a \({ }^{238} U \text { nucleus is } 4.5 \times 10^{9}\) y or about \(10^{17}\) s. Our rough estimate does not seem all that bad.
