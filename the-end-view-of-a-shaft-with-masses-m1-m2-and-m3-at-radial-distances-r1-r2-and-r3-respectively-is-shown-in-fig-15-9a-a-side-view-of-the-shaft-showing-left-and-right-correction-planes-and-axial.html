Guest Question.

The end view of a shaft with masses m1, m2, and m3 at radial distances R1, R2, and R3, respectively, is shown in Fig. 15.9a. A side view of the shaft showing left and right correction planes and axial distances l1, l2, and l3 to the three masses is shown in Fig. 15.9b. Determine the magnitudes and angular orientations of the corrections to be added in each correction plane.

Admin
Solution

Applying the second of Eqs. (a), the sum of the moments of the inertia forces about point A (in order to eliminate the moment of the left correction mass) gives

\(\sum M _{A}=m_{1} l_{1} R _{1}+m_{2} l_{2} R _{2}+m_{3} l_{3} R _{3}+m_{R} l_{R} R _{R}= 0\) (1)

This is a vector equation in which the directions of vectors can be drawn parallel, respectively, to vectors Rj in Fig. 15.9a. Consequently, the moment polygon in Fig. 15.9c can be constructed. The closing vector, mRlRRR, gives the magnitude and orientation of the correction required for the right correction plane.

Although Fig. 15.9c has been called a moment polygon, it is worth noting that the vectors making up this polygon consist of quantities proportional to the moment magnitudes and are drawn in the position vector directions. A true moment polygon would require rotating this polygon 90Â° cw, since each moment vector is properly expressed as \(l \hat{ k } \times m R \omega^{2}\).

Applying the first of Eqs. (a),

\(\sum F =m_{1} R _{1}+m_{2} R _{2}+m_{3} R _{3}+m_{R} R _{R}+m_{L} R _{L}= 0\) (2)

By constructing the force polygon of Fig. 15.9d, this equation is solved for the left correction, mLRL.
After solving for the mRRR and mLRL products, the separate quantities mR, RR, mL, and RL can be determined, since, in general, two of these magnitudes are specified in the problem statement or are chosen by the designer.
