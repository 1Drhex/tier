Guest Question.

Arod containing uniform heat sources per unit volume ˙q is connected to two temperatures as shown in Figure Example 2-11. The rod is also exposed to an environment with convection coefficient h and temperature \( T^∞\) . Obtain an expression for the temperature distribution in the rod.

Admin
Solution

We first must make an energy balance on the element of the rod shown, similar to that used to derive Equation (2-30):

\(\frac{d^{2}\theta }{dx^{2} }-\frac{hp}{KA} \theta =0\) We have

Energy in left face+heat generated in element
=energy out right face+energy lost by convection

or,

\(-KA\frac{dT}{dx}+q^{.} \; A\enspace dx =-KA(\frac{dT}{dx}+\frac{d^{2}T }{dx^{2} }dx )+h\> p\> dx\> (T-T_{∞} )\)

Simplifying, we have:

\(\frac{d^{2}T }{dx^{2} } -\frac{hp}{KA} (T-T_{∞} )+\frac{q^{.} }{K} =0\hspace{30 pt} [a]\)

 

or,with\(\theta = (T-T_{∞} )\)and\(m^{2} =h\; p/KA\)

\(\frac{d^{2}\theta }{dx} -m^{2} \theta +\frac{˙q}{K} =0\hspace{30 pt} [b]\)

We can make a further variable substitution as

\(\theta ^{′} =\theta -q^{·} /Km^{2}\)

so that our differential equation becomes

\(\frac{d^{2} \theta ^{′}}{dx^{2} }-m^{2} \theta ^{′} =0\hspace{30 pt} [c]\)

which has the general solution

\(\theta ^{′} =C_{1} e^{-mx} +C_{2} e^{mx}\hspace{30 pt} [d]\)

The two end temperatures are used to establish the boundary conditions:

\(\theta ^{′} =\theta ^{′}_{1}=T_{1} -T_{∞} -q^{.} /Km^{2} =C_{1} +C_{2}\)

 

\(\theta ^{′} =\theta ^{′}_{2}=T_{2} -T_{∞} -q^{.} /Km^{2} =C_{1}e^{-mL} +C_{2}e^{mL} \)

Solving for the constants C1 and C2 gives

\(\theta ^{′} =\frac{(\theta ^{′}_{1}e^{2mL}- \theta ^{′}_{2}e^{ml})e^{-mx}+(\theta ^{′}_{2}e^{ml}- \theta ^{′}_{1})e^{mx} }{e^{2mL-1}} \hspace{30 pt} [e]\)

 

For an infinitely long heat-generating fin with the left end maintained at T1, the temperature
distribution becomes:

\(\theta ^{′}/\theta ^{′}_{1} =e^{-mx}\hspace{30 pt} [f] \)

 

relation similar to Equation (2-32)

\(\frac{\theta }{\theta _{0} } =\frac{T-T_{∞} }{T_{0}-T_{∞} } =e^{-mx}\) for a non-heat-generating fin.

Comment:

Note that the above relationships assume one-dimensional behavior, i.e., temperature dependence only on the x-coordinate and temperature uniformity across the area A. For sufficiently large heat generation rates and/or cross-section areas, the assumption may no longer be valid. In these cases, the problem must be treated as multidimensional using the techniques described in Chapter 3.
