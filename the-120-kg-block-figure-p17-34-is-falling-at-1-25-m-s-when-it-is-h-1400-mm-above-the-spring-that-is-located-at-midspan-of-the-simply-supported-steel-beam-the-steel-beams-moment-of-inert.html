Guest Question.

The 120-kg block (Figure P17.34) is falling at 1.25 m/s when it is h = 1,400 mm above the spring that is located at midspan of the simply supported steel beam. The steel beam’s moment of inertia is \(I=70 \times 10^{6} mm ^{4}\), its depth is d = 250 mm, and its elastic modulus is E = 200 GPa. L = 5.5 m is the beam span. The spring constant is k = 100 kN/m. Calculate:
(a) the maximum beam deflection at B due to the falling block.
(b) the equivalent static load required to produce the same deflection.
(c) the maximum bending stress in the steel beam.

Admin
Solution

(a) Beam deflection at B due to falling block:
From Appendix C, for a simply-support beam with a concentrated load acting at midspan:

\(v_{B}=\frac{P L^{3}}{48 E I}\)

The spring constant of the beam can be considered as:

\(\begin{aligned}k_{\text {beam }} &=\frac{P}{v_{B}}=\frac{48 E I}{L^{3}}=\frac{48\left(200 \times 10^{9} N / m ^{2}\right)\left(70 \times 10^{6} mm ^{4}\right)(1 m / 1,000 mm )^{4}}{(5.5 m )^{3}} \\&=4,039,068.3696 N / m =4,039.0684 kN / m\end{aligned}\)

From equilibrium, the force in the spring and the load on the beam are equal:

\(F_{\text {spring }}=P_{\text {beam }}\)

and thus

\(\begin{aligned}k_{\text {spring }} \Delta_{\text {spring }} &=k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }} \\(100 kN / m ) \Delta_{\text {spring }} &=(4,039.0684 kN / m ) v_{\text {beam }} \\\Delta_{\text {spring }} &=40.390684 v_{\text {beam }} (1) \end{aligned}\)

From conservation of energy, the kinetic energy of the block plus the work done by the falling block must equal the energy stored in the spring and in the flexing beam:

\(\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}+W\left(h+\Delta_{\text {spring }}+v_{\text {beam }}\right)=\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {spring }} \Delta_{\text {spring }}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}^{2}\) (2)

Substitute Equation (1) into Equation (2) and note that h = 1,400 mm = 1.4 m:

\(\begin{aligned}&\frac{1}{2}(120 kg )(1.25 m / s )^{2}+(120 kg )\left(9.806650 m / s ^{2}\right)\left(1.4 m +40.390684 v_{\text {beam }}+v_{\text {beam }}\right) \\&=\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {spring }}\left(40.390684 v_{\text {beam }}\right)^{2}+\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}^{2}\end{aligned}\)

This quadratic equation

\(\begin{aligned}&93.75 N – m +1,647.5172 N – m +(48,708.47415 N ) v_{\text {beam }} \\&=\left(81.570352 \times 10^{6} N / m \right) v_{\text {beam }}^{2}+\left(2.019534 \times 10^{6} N / m \right) v_{\text {beam }}^{2} \\&\left(83.589886 \times 10^{6} N / m \right) v_{\text {beam }}^{2}-(48,708.47415 N ) v_{\text {beam }}-1,741.267200 N – m =0\end{aligned}\)

can be solved for the beam deflection:

\(\begin{aligned}v_{\text {beam }} &=\frac{48,708.47415 \pm \sqrt{(-48,708.47415)^{2}-4\left(83.589886 \times 10^{6}\right)(-1,741.267200)}}{2\left(83.589886 \times 10^{6}\right)} \\&=4.864751 \times 10^{-3} m ,-4.282043 \times 10^{-3} m\end{aligned}\)

Use the positive value for the beam deflection; thus, the maximum dynamic beam deflection is

\(v_{\max }=4.864751 mm =4.86 mm\)

 

(b) Equivalent static load:
The equivalent static load required to produce this deflection is:

\(P_{\max }=k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}=(4,039.0684 kN / m )\left(4.864751 \times 10^{-3} m \right)=19.6491 kN =19.65 kN\)

 

(c) Maximum bending stress:
Bending moment due to dynamic load:

\(M=\frac{P L}{4}=\frac{(19.6491 kN )(5.5 m )}{4}=27.0175 kN – m\)

The bending stress from this moment is:

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma &=\frac{M c}{I}=\frac{(27.0175 kN – m )(250 mm / 2)(1,000 N / kN )(1,000 mm / m )}{70 \times 10^{6} mm ^{4}} \\&=48.2455 MPa =48.2 MPa\end{aligned}\)
