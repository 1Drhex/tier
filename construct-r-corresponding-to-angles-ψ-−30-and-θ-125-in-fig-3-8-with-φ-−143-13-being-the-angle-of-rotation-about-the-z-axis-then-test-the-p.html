Guest Question.

Construct [R] corresponding to angles ψ = −30° and θ = 125° in Fig. 3.8, with Φ = −143.13° being the angle of rotation about the \(Z^{\prime}\) axis. Then test the procedure for ascertaining the angle and axis of rotation by extracting the values of Φ, θ, and ψ from the resulting [R].

Admin
Solution

This example entails application of all the concepts associated with rotation about an arbitrary axis. We begin by evaluating Eqs. (3.1.40) and (3.1.32) for the given angles,

\(\left[R^{\prime}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \psi \cos \theta & \sin \psi \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\-\sin \psi & \cos \psi & 0 \\\cos \psi \sin \theta & \sin \psi \sin \theta & \cos \theta\end{array}\right]\) (3.1.40)

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{c}A_{x^{\prime}} \\A_{y^{\prime}} \\A_{z^{\prime}}\end{array}\right\}=\left[R_{\phi}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A_{X^{\prime}} \\A_{Y^{\prime}} \\A_{Z^{\prime}}\end{array}\right\},\quad\left[R_{\phi}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \phi & \sin \phi & 0 \\-\sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\) (3.1.32)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\left[R^{\prime}\right]=&\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-0.4967 & 0.2868 & -0.8192 \\0.5 & 0.866 & 0 \\0.7094 & -0.4096 & -0.5736\end{array}\right], \\\\&\left[R_{\phi}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-0.8 & -0.6 & 0 \\0.6 & -0.8 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right] .\end{aligned}\)

The overall transformation is found from Eq. (3.1.34) to be

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}A_{x} \\A_{y} \\ A_{z}\end{array}\right\}=[R]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A_{X} \\ A_{Y}\\A_{Z}\end{array}\right\},\quad[R]=\left[R^{\prime}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}\left[R_{\phi}\right]\left[R^{\prime}\right]\) (3.1.34)

 

\([R]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0.1059 & -0.1789 & -0.9782 \\-0.8671 & -0.4980 & -0.0028 \\-0.4867 &0.8485 & -0.2078\end{array}\right]\)

 

We now wish to work backwards to determine the angles associated with this transformation. We could determine \({K^{\prime}}\) by solving Eqs. (3.1.38) with the aid of Matlab’s eigen or Mathcad’s eigenvec function. Instead we shall follow Eqs. (3.1.36).

\(\left[[R]-\lambda_{j}[U]\right]\left\{\xi_{j}\right\}=\{0\} \text { gives }\left\{\xi_{j}\right\}=\left\{K^{\prime}\right\} \text { if } \lambda_{j}=1 \text { and }\left\{\xi_{j}\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left\{\xi_{j}\right\}=1\) (3.1.38)

 

\([[R]-[U]]\left\{K^{\prime}\right\}=\{0\}, \quad\left\{K^{\prime}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}\ell_{Z^{\prime} X} \\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} Y} \\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z} \end{array}\right\}\) (3.1.36).

We subtract the identity matrix from [R] and recognize that only two of the rows are linearly independent because \([R] − [U] = 0\). Using the first two rows leads to

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc} 0.1059 & -0.1789 & -0.9782 \\-0.8671 & -0.4980 & -0.0028 \end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}\ell_{Z^{\prime} X} \\\ell_{Z^{\prime} Y} \\\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z} \end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 0 \end{array}\right\}\)

We have two equations for three unknowns, so we take \(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\) to be arbitrary, and solve for the other direction cosines:

\(\begin{array}{c} {\left[\begin{array}{cc}0.8941 & -0.1789 \\ -0.8671 & -1.4980\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\ell_{Z^{\prime} X} \\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} Y}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0.9782 \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z} \\0.0028 \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\end{array}\right\}}, \\\\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} X}=-1.2368 \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}, \quad \ell_{Z^{\prime} Y}=0.7141 \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}.\end{array}\)

We find \(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\) from the fact that \(\bar{K}^{\prime}\) is a unit vector, so that

\(\ell_{Z^{\prime} X}^{2}+\ell_{Z^{\prime} Y}^{2}+\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}^{2}=\left(1.2368^{2}+0.7141^{2}+1\right) \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}^{2}=1.\)

The sign of \(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\) is not defined by this relation. Because we require that 0 ≤ θ < π, setting \(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\) = cos θ would lead to two alternative values for θ. Let us use the positive root, so that θ will be acute, specifically,

\(\begin{array}{c} \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}=\frac{1}{\left(1.2368^{2}+0.7141^{2}+1\right)^{1 / 2}}=0.5736=\cos \theta, \\\\ \theta=55^{\circ} \end{array}\)

The second and third of Eqs. (3.1.41) then lead to

\(\begin{array}{c} \ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}=\cos \theta \\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} Y}=\sin \psi \sin \theta \\ \ell_{Z^{\prime} X}=\cos \psi \sin \theta \end{array}\) (3.1.41)

 

\(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Y}=\sin \psi \sin \theta=0.4096, \quad \ell_{Z^{\prime} X}=\cos \psi \sin \theta=-0.7094.\)

These relations place ψ in the second quadrant:

ψ = 150°

Equation (3.1.40) indicates that the transformation [ \(R^{\prime}\) ] corresponding to the calculated ψ and θ is

\(\left[R^{\prime}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-0.4967 & 0.2868 & -0.8192 \\-0.5 & -0.866 & 0 \\-0.7094 & 0.4096 & 0.5736\end{array}\right]\)

we use Eq. (3.1.42) to find the value of [RΦ] corresponding to the preceding, and match the result to the standard expression in Eqs. (3.1.32), which leads to

\(\left[R_{\phi}\right]=\left[R^{\prime}\right][R]\left[R^{\prime}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}\) (3.1.42)

 

\(\begin{aligned} \left[R_{\phi}\right] &=\left[R^{\prime}\right][R]\left[R^{\prime}\right]^{\mathrm{T}}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-0.8 & 0.6 & 0 \\-0.6 & -0.8 & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right], \\\\ &=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \phi & \sin \phi & 0 \\-\sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right],\end{aligned}\)

 

Φ = 143.13°.

The values of ψ and θ are 180° greater than the given values, which means that the identified \(K^{\prime}\) axis is oriented oppositely from the rotation axis that was specified in the problem statement. Correspondingly, the value of Φ that was found is the negative of the specified value. In other words, we have identified the opposite rotation about the opposing axis. This situation is a consequence of using a positive sign for the square root leading to \(\ell_{Z^{\prime} Z}\).
