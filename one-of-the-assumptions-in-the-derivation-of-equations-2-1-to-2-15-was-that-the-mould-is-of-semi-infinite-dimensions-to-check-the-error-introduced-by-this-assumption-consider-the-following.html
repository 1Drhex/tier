Guest Question.

One of the assumptions in the derivation of equations 2.1 to 2.15 was that the mould is of semi-infinite dimensions. To check the error introduced by this assumption, consider the following.
A metal with a melting temperature of \( 700^{\omicron }C\) is poured into a sand mould that is initially at \( 30^{\omicron }C\) How thick must a mould be to be considered semi-infinite for times of 1 min, 5 min and 15 min after pouring the metal?

Admin
Solution

In agreement with Fig. 2.22, assume that the mould surface is x = 0; let L be the thickness of the mould.

The semi-infinite mould condition means that the temperature at x = L does not increase substantially above To, which in this case is \( 30^{\omicron }C\)
Therefore, in equation 2.7, T(L, t)\( \approx 30^{\omicron }\) .
To determine the value of L find the maximum value of equation 2.7 for t = 1 min, 5 min and 15 min.
For this problem the values of\( T_{M} and T _{o}\) are fixed, so T(L, t) is maximized when

\( erf (\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha _{th}t} } )\) is maximum (see equation 2.7)

\( T(x,t )= T_{M}+ (T_{\omicron }-T_{M} ) erf (\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha _{th}t} } )\)

For a sand mould, from Table 2.3, \( C_{m} \)= 1.16 J/gC, \( \rho _{m} = 1500 kg/m^{3}, k_{m}= 0.6 W/ m ^{\omicron }C \)

\( \alpha _{th} =\frac{k_{m}}{C_{m}\rho _{m}} = \frac{0.6 J / s . m^{\omicron }}{1160 J/kg^{\omicron }C \times 1500 kg/m^{3}} = 3.4 \times 10^{-7}m^{2}/s\)

From Table 2.2, the error function is practically at its maximum value when z = 2. Therefore, for this example, assume

\( \frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha _{th}t} }=2\)

and setting x = L and solve for L, with t = 1 min, 5 min and 15 min.
For t = 1 rain,

\( L= 2 \times 2 \sqrt{3.4 \times 10^{-7}m^{2}/s \times 60s} = 18 mm\)

Repeating the calculation for t – 5 min and 15 min gives L = 40 mm and L = 70 mm, respectively. If the mould has a thickness of less than these values, then it cannot be considered semi-infinite and equation 2.7 will be invalid.

To check, calculate T(L, t) using equation 2.7 for one of the times, say t = 1 min:

\( T(18 mm, 1 min)= 700^{\omicron }C + (30^{\omicron }C- 700^{\omicron }C) erf (\frac{18mm}{2\sqrt{3.4 \times 10^{-7}m^{2}/s \times 60s} } ) \)

 

\( =33.4^{\omicron }C\)

close to the initial temperature of 30~ verifying the infinite mould assumption. The terms within the square brackets [ ] of equation 2.15

\( t= [\frac{\pi }{4}(\frac{\rho _{c}\Delta H_{f}}{T_{M}- T_{\omicron }} )^{2} ](\frac{V}{A} )^{2}\) are all constants and so it can be simplified to

\( t= C(\frac{V}{A} )^{2}\)

This relationship is known as Chvorinov’s rule. Although it has been derived in terms of one-dimensional solidification, this rule can be applied to more complex shapes to provide a first approximation of the solidification behaviour.
