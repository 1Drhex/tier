Guest Question.

Determination of Heat Required to Evaporate \(H _{2} O\)

A rigid vessel contains 50.0 kg of saturated liquid water and 4.3 kg of saturated vapor. The system pressure is at 10 kPa. What is the minimum amount of heat needed to evaporate all the liquid?

Admin
Solution

A schematic of the process is shown in Figure E2.12. We label the initial state as “state 1” and the fi nal state as “state 2.” The left-hand side of the fi gure shows the physical process while the right-hand side represents it on a Pv diagram.

 

This heating process occurs in a closed system at constant volume. As water boils, the pressure in the vapor phase will increase. The increase in pressure will require an increase in the temperature of the system for boiling to proceed. (Remember, a given pressure constrains the temperature of the two-phase region.) Thus, energy is required for both the evaporation of water (latent heat) and the increase in temperature (sensible heat). Since bulk kinetic and potential energy are negligible, we can write the fi rst law according to Equation (2.13a):

 

\(\Delta U=Q+W\) (2.13a)

 

\(\Delta U=Q+W\) (E2.12A)

 

Since our system is at constant volume, there is no work done and the heat needed equals the change in internal energy. This result contrasts with Figure 2.11, where enthalpy is used, since, in that case, there is Pv work. A mass balance gives:

 

\(m_{2}^{v}=m_{1}^{l}+m_{1}^{v}\)

 

The internal energy in state 1 must account for that of both the saturated liquid and the saturated vapor. Hence, we can write Equation (E2.12A) as:

 

\(U_{2}-U_{1}=\left(m_{2}^{v} \hat{u}_{2}^{v}\right)-\left(m_{1}^{l} \hat{u}_{1}^{l}+m_{1}^{v} \hat{u}_{1}^{v}\right)=Q\)

 

State 1 is completely constrained. Looking up values for internal energy in the steam tables for saturated water: pressure (Appendix B.2) gives:

 

\(\hat{u}_{1}^{l}=191.8[ kJ / kg ]\)

 

\(\hat{u}_{1}^{v}=2437.9[ kJ / kg ]\)

 

We now need to constrain state 2. We know we have saturated vapor, but we need to find a value for a thermodynamic property. Since the container is rigid, this process occurs at constant volume, as illustrated on the Pv diagram in Figure E2.12. We can fi nd the volume of the vessel as follows:

 

\(V_{1}=V_{2}=m_{1}^{l} \hat{v}_{1}^{l}+m_{1}^{v} \hat{v}_{1}^{v}=(50)(0.001)+(4.3)(14.67)=63.1 m ^{3}\)

 

We can now solve for the specifi c volume of state 2:

 

\(\hat{v}_{2}^{v}=\frac{V_{2}}{m_{2}^{v}}=\frac{63.1 m ^{3}}{54.3 kg }=1.16\left[\frac{ m ^{3}}{ kg }\right]\)

 

Looking up the value, we fi nd the specifi c volume of saturated vapor matches at:

 

\(P_{2}=0.15\) MPa

 

If this value did not match a table entry, we would have to interpolate. Looking up the internal energy of state 2 gives:

\(\hat{u}^{v}_{2}=2519.6[KJ/kg]\)

Solving Equation (E2.12B) for heat gives:

 

\(Q=\left(m_{2}^{v} \hat{u}_{2}^{v}\right)-\left(m_{1}^{l} \hat{u}_{1}^{l}+m_{1}^{v} \hat{u}_{1}^{v}\right)=(54.3)(2519.6)-[(50)(191.8)-(4.3)(2437.9)]\)

 

\(=117 \times 10^{6} J\)
