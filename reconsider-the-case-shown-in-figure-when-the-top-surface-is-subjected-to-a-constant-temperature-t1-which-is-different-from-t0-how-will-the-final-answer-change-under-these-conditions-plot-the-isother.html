Guest Question.

Reconsider the case shown in figure when the top surface is subjected to a constant temperature \( T_{1} \) which is different from \(T_{0}\). How will the final answer change under these conditions? Plot the isotherms and heat flow lines for H=50 mm, W=60 mm, \(T_{0}\)=100° C, and \( T_{1}\)=150° C for a material of thermal conductivity of \( 40 W/m^{2} K \). Also, calculate the temperature at the points given by (0.25W, 0.25H), (0.5W, 0.5H), and (0.75W, 0.75H).

Admin
Solution

In this case, evidently \( T_{m}=0 \) and therefore, Equation
\( At\space y=H,\quad 0\leq x\leq W,\space\quad \theta _{1}= T_{m}\sin \frac{\pi x}{W} \)
changes as:
\( y=H,\quad 0\leq x\leq W,\space\quad \theta = T_{1}-T_{0} = \theta _{1} \quad (say) \)

It is immediately obvious that substituting \( T_{m} = 0 \) into Equation :
\( T = T_{0}+(\frac{T_{m}}{\sinh \frac{\pi H}{W} } ) \sin \frac{\pi x}{W}\sinh \frac{\pi y}{W} \)
leads to the absurd result of \(T = T_{0}\) everywhere! Since the other three boundary conditions, Equations :
\( At\space x = 0,\quad 0\leq y\leq H,\quad \theta =0 \) ,
\( At\space y = 0,\quad 0\leq x\leq W,\quad \theta =0 \) ,
remain unchanged, we can begin from Equation :
\( \theta = T – T_{0} = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{C_{n}\sin \frac{n\pi x}{W} } \sinh \frac{n\pi y}{W} \)

Now using the new condition of \( y = H,\quad 0\leq x\leq W,\quad \theta =\theta _{1} \):

\( \theta_{1} = \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{C_{n}\sin \frac{n\pi x}{W} } \sinh \frac{n\pi H}{W} \)
This is a Fourier series and the constant \( C_{n} \) can be evaluated by expanding \( \theta _{1} \) in the form of a Fourier series over the interval
\( 0 \leq x \leq W \) as :

\( \theta_{1} = \theta_{1}\frac{2}{\pi }\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac{(-1)^{n+1}+1 }{n} }\sin \frac{n\pi x}{W} \)

Comparing the last two equations:
\( C_{n}=\frac{2}{\pi }\theta _{1}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}+1 }{n}.\frac{1}{\sinh \frac{n\pi H}{W} } \)
Alternatively, one can exploit the orthogonal properties of the sine function. Multiply both sides of Equation :
\( y = H,\quad 0\leq x\leq W,\quad \theta =\theta _{1} \)
by \( \sin \frac{m\pi x}{W} \) and integrating it over the interval x = 0 to x = w:

\( \int_{0}^{W}{\theta _{1}\sin \frac{m\pi x}{W}dx} = \int_{0}^{W}{\sin \frac{m\pi x}{W}\left\{\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{C_{n}\sin \frac{n\pi x}{W} }\sinh \frac{n\pi H}{W} \right\} dx} \) (1)

where m is an integer and using the well-known result
\( \int_{0}^{\pi }{(\sin mx.\sin nx)}dx = 0\quad when\space m\neq n \)
\( \neq 0\quad when\space m= n\)

This implies that all terms on the right hand side of Equation(1) will be zero except when m =n. This also leads to the same value of \( C_{n}\) as obtained above. Thus, the final solution is given by:

\( \theta = T – T_{0} = \frac{2}{\pi }(T_{1}-T_{0})\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{\left\{\frac{(-1)^{n+1}+1 }{n} \right\} }\sin \frac{n\pi x}{W}.\frac{\sinh \frac{n\pi y}{W} }{\sinh \frac{n\pi H}{W} } \) (2)

This is a rapidly converging series and one only needs to consider the first few terms. For instance, for the numerical values given here:

\( H= 50mm;\space W=60mm; \space k=40W/m^{2}K,\space\)
\(T_{0}=100^{\circ }C,\space and \space T_{1}=150^{\circ }C \)
\( \therefore \theta _{1} = T_{1}-T_{0} = 50^{\circ } C \)

\( \therefore \theta (0.5W,0.5H)\) is obtained by substituting x = 0.5W
and y = 0.5H in Equation (2):

\( \theta(0.5W,0.5H) = T – 100 = \frac{2}{\pi }(50)\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty }{\left\{\frac{(-1)^{n+1}+1 }{n} \right\} }\sin \frac{n\pi }{2}.\frac{\sinh \frac{5n\pi }{12} }{\sinh \frac{5n\pi }{6} } \)
