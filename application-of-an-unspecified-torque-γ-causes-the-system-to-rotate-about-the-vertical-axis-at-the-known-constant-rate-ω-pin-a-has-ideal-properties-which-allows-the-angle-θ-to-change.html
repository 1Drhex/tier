Guest Question.

Application of an unspecified torque Γ causes the system to rotate about the vertical axis at the known constant rate Ω . Pin A has ideal properties, which allows the angle θ to change freely. Compare the rate of change of the Hamiltonian of the system with the result of applying the power balance principle.

Admin
Solution

This example clarifies the difference between Jacobi’s integral and energy principles. The central quantity is the kinetic energy as a function of the generalized coordinates. The only generalized coordinate required here is the nutation angle \(\theta\) because the precession rate is specified as \(\dot{\psi}=\Omega .\) Let pin \(A\) be the origin of \(x y z\), which is attached to bar \(A B\) such that its \(x\) axis is aligned with the bar and the \(z\) axis is situated in the vertical plane. Then the angular velocity of the bar is

\(\bar{\omega}=-\Omega \cos \theta \bar{i}-\dot{\theta} j+\Omega \sin \theta \bar{k}.\)

With the pin defined to be the datum for gravitational potential energy, the corresponding energy functions are

\(T=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}\right)\left[\dot{\theta}^{2}+\Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}\right], \quad V=-m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta. (1)\)

Time does not occur explicitly in these expressions, so Eq. \((9.2 .25)\) is applicable. The generalized momentum corresponding to the sole generalized coordinate is

\(\frac{d \mathcal{H}}{d t}=\displaystyle\sum\limits_{j=1}^N Q_{j} \dot{q}_{j}. (9.2 .25)\)

 

\(p_{1}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}}=\frac{1}{3} m L^{2} \dot{\theta}, (2)\)

so \(M_{1,1}=(1 / 3) m L^{2}\). Inspection of \(T\) shows that it does not contain a term that is linear in \(\dot{\theta}\), so the kinetic energy matches the standard form, Eqs. (7.6.4), with

\(T_{2}=\frac{1}{2} \displaystyle\sum\limits_{j} \displaystyle\sum\limits_{n} M_{j n}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{q}_{j} \dot{q}_{n}, \quad T_{1}=\displaystyle\sum\limits_{j} N_{j}\left(q_{i}, t\right) \dot{q}_{j}. (7.6.4)\)

 

\(N_{1}=0, \quad T_{0}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}\right) \Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}.\)

These expressions enable us to employ Eq. (9.2.13) to derive the Hamiltonian, which leads to

\(\begin{aligned} \mathcal{H}=& \frac{1}{2}\{p\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\{p\}-\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\{p\} \\\\ &-\frac{1}{2}\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[M\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right]^{-1}\left\{N\left(q_{i}, t\right)\right\}-T_{0}\left(q_{i}, t\right)+V\left(q_{i}, t\right). \end{aligned} (9.2.13) \)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\mathcal{H} &=\frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{M_{1,1}} p_{1}^{2}-T_{0}+V \\\\&=\frac{3}{2 m L^{2}} p_{1}^{2}-\frac{1}{6} m L^{2} \Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}-m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta.\end{aligned} (3) \)

A virtual displacement increments \(\theta\) by \(\delta \theta\), but the only force that does work in such a movement is gravity, which is included in \(V\). Therefore \(Q_{1}=0\), from which it follows that Jacobi’s integral, which is the conservation form of Eq. (9.2.25), applies. We may establish the constant value \(\mathcal{H}_{0}\) of the Hamiltonian by evaluating it at the initial state. Hence any motion of the system must be such that the Hamiltonian in Eq. (3) remains constant:

\(\frac{3}{2 m L^{2}} p_{1}^{2}-\frac{1}{6} m L^{2} \Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}-m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta=\mathcal{H}_{0}.\)

We may express this conservation equation in terms of \(\theta\) by substituting Eq. (2), which yields

\(\frac{1}{6} m L^{2}\left[\dot{\theta}^{2}-\Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]-m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta=\mathcal{H}_{0}. (4)\)

This relation is an integral of the equation of motion, just like the principle of conservation of energy for a conservative, time-independent system. Knowledge of the constancy of \(\mathcal{H}\) can be used to determine a value of \(\dot{\theta}\) at a specified \(\theta .\) Another use is to check the results of a computer simulation.

Now let us examine the power balance principle for this system. The mechanical energy is \(E=T+V\), so

\(E=\frac{1}{6} m L^{2}\left[\dot{\theta}^{2}+\Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]-m g \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta.\)

The only nonconservative force that does work when the system moves is the couple \(\Gamma\) that sustains a constant precession rate \(\Omega\), so the power input is

\(\dot{W}=\Gamma \Omega\)

The work-energy principle states that \(\dot{E}=\dot{W}\), which leads to

\(\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}\left[\dot{\theta} \ddot{\theta}+\Omega^{2} \dot{\theta}(\sin \theta)(\cos \theta)\right]+m g \frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta=\Gamma \Omega. (5)\)

We may derive another differential equation for \(\theta\) from Jacobi’s integral by observing that \(d \mathcal{H} / d t=0 .\) Differentiation of Eq. (4) shows that

\(\frac{1}{3} m L^{2}\left[\dot{\theta} \ddot{\theta}-\Omega^{2} \dot{\theta}(\sin \theta)(\cos \theta)\right]+m g \frac{L}{2} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta=0. (6)\)

This is identical to Lagrange’s equation for \(\theta\) multiplied by an additional \(\theta\) factor. Taking the difference between Eqs. (5) and (6) yields an expression for \(\Gamma\) :

\(\Gamma=\frac{2}{3} m L^{2} \Omega \dot{\theta}(\sin \theta)(\cos \theta). (7)\)

Equations (5) and (6) can be obtained by an alternative formulation that uses as constrained generalized coordinates \(q_{1}=\theta\) and \(q_{2}=\psi\), which must satisfy the constraint equation that \(\dot{\psi}=\Omega .\) The Lagrange equation for \(\theta\) would be the same as Eq. (6) divided by the common factor \(\dot{\theta}\). Substitution of the condition that \(\dot{\psi}=\Omega\), \(\ddot{\psi}=0\), into the Lagrange equation for \(\psi\) would yield Eq. (7).
