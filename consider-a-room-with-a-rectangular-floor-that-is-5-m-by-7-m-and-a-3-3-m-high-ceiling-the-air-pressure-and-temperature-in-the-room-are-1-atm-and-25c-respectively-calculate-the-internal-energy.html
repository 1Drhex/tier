Guest Question.

Consider a room with a rectangular floor that is 5 m by 7 m, and a 3.3 m high ceiling. The air pressure and temperature in the room are 1 atm and 25°C, respectively. Calculate the internal energy and the enthalpy of the air in the room.

Admin
Solution

We first need to calculate the mass of air in the room. From Equation (7.1)

\(p=\rho R T(7.1)\)

\(\rho=\frac{p}{R T}\)

where each quantity must be expressed in consistent SI units. Since 1 atm \(= 1.01 ×10^{5}\)\(N/m^{2} \) and \(0°C\) is 273 K, we have

\(p=1.01 \times 10^{5} N / m ^{2} \quad \text { and } \quad T=273+25=298 K\)

Hence, \(\rho=\frac{p}{R T}=\frac{1.01 \times 10^{5}}{(287)(298)}=1.181 kg / m ^{3}\)

The volume of the room is \((5)(7)(3.3) = 115.5 m^{2}\). The mass of air in the room is therefore

\(M=(1.181)(115.5)=136.4 kg\)

From Equation (7.6a), the internal energy per unit mass is

\(e=c_{v} T(7.6a)\)

where \(c_{v}=\frac{R}{\gamma-1}=\frac{(287)}{1.4-1}=\frac{287}{0.4}=717.5 \text { joule } /( kg\cdot K )\)

 

Thus, \(e=c_{v} T=(717.5)(298)=2.138 \times 10^{5} \text { joule } / kg\)

The internal energy in the room, E, is then

\(E=M e=(136.4)\left(2.138 \times 10^{5}\right)=2.92 \times 10^{7} \text { joule }\)

From Equation (7.6b), the enthalpy per unit mass is

\(h=c_{p} T(7.6b)\)

where \(c_{p}=\frac{\gamma R}{\gamma-1}=\frac{(1.4)(287)}{0.4}=1004.5 \text { joule } /( kg \cdot K )\)

Thus, \(h=c_{p} T=(1004.5)(298)=2.993 \times 10^{5} \text { joule/kg }\)

The enthalpy in the room, H, is then

\(H=M h=(136.4)\left(2.993 \times 10^{5}\right)=4.08 \times 10^{7} \text { joule }\)

A check on two answers can be made knowing that

\(\frac{h}{e}=\frac{c_{p} T}{c_{v} T}=\frac{c_{p}}{c_{v}}=\gamma=1.4\)

From the answers,

\(\frac{H}{E}=\frac{4.08 \times 10^{7}}{2.92 \times 10^{7}}=1.4 \quad \text { It checks. }\)

This simple example is intended to reinforce two basic points.

1. Consistent units must be used when making a calculation using basic equations from physics, such as Equations (7.1), (7.6a), and (7.6b). Here we used SI units, becausethe given information was given in terms of meters and degrees Celsius, which is readily put in terms of the consistent unit of temperature, degrees Kelvin.

2. The internal energy and enthalpy per unit mass were calculated directly from the temperature of the gas, using Equations (7.6a and b). There was no need to consider a “constant volume process” or a “constant pressure process”; there is no process at all to consider here. Internal energy and enthalpy per unit mass, e and h, are simply state variables, depending only on the thermodynamic state of the system. Even though Equation (7.6a) contains \(c _{v}\), a constant volume process is not relevant here. Similarly, even though Equation (7.6b) contains \(c_{p}\), a constant pressure process is not relevant here.
