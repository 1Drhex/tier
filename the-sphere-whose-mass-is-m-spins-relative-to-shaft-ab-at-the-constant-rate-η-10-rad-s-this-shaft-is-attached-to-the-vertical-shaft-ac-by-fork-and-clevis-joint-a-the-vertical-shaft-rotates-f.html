Guest Question.

The sphere, whose mass is \(m\), spins relative to shaft \(A B\) at the constant rate \(\eta=10 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}\). This shaft is attached to the vertical shaft \(A C\) by fork-andclevis joint \(A\). The vertical shaft rotates freely, with no torque applied to its rotation axis. The mass of bar \(A B\) is \(m / 2\), and the other parameters are \(R=150 \mathrm{~mm}\) and \(L=400 \mathrm{~mm}\). Initially, \(\theta\) is held constant at \(90^{\circ}\) by a cable, and the precession rate is \(\Omega=5 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s} .\) Determine the maximum and minimum values of \(\theta\) in the motion following breakage of the cable and the corresponding precession rates. Then determine the values of \(\dot{\theta}\) and \(\Omega\) at the instant when \(\theta\) is the average of these extreme angles.

Admin
Solution

We see here that momentum and energy principles can be used simultaneously, thereby enabling us to analyze an intricate motion. We consider the sphere and shaft \(AB\) to form a system, because the forces exerted between them do no net work. We include the massless vertical shaft in this system to utilize our knowledge of how bearing \(C\) acts.

The free-body diagram of this system shows that bearing \(C\) exerts an arbitrary force and couple, except that the couple has no component about the vertical axis. This force–couple system does no work, and gravity is conservative. Therefore mechanical energy is conserved throughout the motion. We also observe that none of the forces or couples depicted in the free-body diagram exert no moment about the \(z^{\prime}\) axis, which means that the system’s angular momentum about the \(z^{\prime}\) axis also is conserved.

Both the sphere and shaft \(A B\) are in pure rotation about stationary point \(A\), so the \(x y z\) coordinate system shown in the the free-body diagram, whose \(z\) axis coincides with \(A B\), will be convenient for describing the angular momentum of both bodies. We also define another reference frame \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) that solely precesses about its \(z^{\prime}\) axis.

Point \(A\) is on the \(z^{\prime}\) axis, and it is the pivot point for the pure rotation of both the shaft and the sphere. We develop an expression for \(\bar{H}_{A}\) of each body under arbitrary conditions in order to address all aspects of the problem. The inertia properties in the Appendix and the parallel axis theorems indicate that

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(I_{z z}\right)_{s}=\frac{2}{5} m R^{2}=0.009 m \equiv I_{1}, \quad\left(I_{x x}\right)_{s}=\left(I_{y y}\right)_{s}=\frac{2}{5} m R^{2}+m L^{2}=0.169 m \equiv I_{2}, \\\\\left(I_{x x}\right)_{A B}=\left(I_{y y}\right)_{A B}=\frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{m}{2}\right) L^{2}=0.02667 m \equiv I_{3}.\end{array} \) (1)

The angular velocity of the sphere is these bodies is sum of the precession rate \(\Omega\) and the nutation rate \(\dot{\theta}\):

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{\omega}_{s}=\Omega \bar{k}^{\prime}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}^{\prime}-\eta \bar{k}=-\Omega \sin \theta \bar{i}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}+(\Omega \cos \theta-\eta) \bar{k}, \\\\\bar{\omega}_{A B}=\Omega \bar{k}^{\prime}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}^{\prime}=-\Omega \sin \theta \bar{i}+\dot{\theta} \bar{j}+\Omega \cos \theta \bar{k}.\end{array} \)

The corresponding angular momenta and kinetic energies corresponding to pure rotation are

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{s}=-I_{2} \Omega \sin \theta \bar{i}+I_{2} \dot{\theta} \bar{j}+I_{1}(\Omega \cos \theta-\eta) \bar{k}, \\\\\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{A B}=-I_{3} \Omega \sin \theta \bar{i}+I_{3} \dot{\theta} \bar{j} ,\\\\(T)_{s}=\frac{1}{2} \bar{\omega}_{s} \cdot\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{s}=\frac{1}{2}\left[I_{2} \Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{2} \dot{\theta}^{2}+I_{1}(\Omega \cos \theta-\eta)^{2}\right], \\\\(T)_{A B}=\frac{1}{2} \bar{\omega}_{A B} \cdot\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{A B}=\frac{1}{2}\left[I_{3} \Omega^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{3} \dot{\theta}^{2}\right].\end{array} (2) \)

Because no resultant moment about the \(z^{\prime}\) axis acts on the system, the angular momentum about this axis is conserved. Point \(A\) lies on this axis, so we assert that

\(\left[\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{s}+\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{A B}\right]_{2} \cdot \bar{k}^{\prime}=\left[\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{s}+\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{A B}\right]_{1} \cdot \bar{k}^{\prime} .\)

In terms of \(x y z\) components we have \(\bar{k}^{\prime}=-\sin \theta \bar{i}+\cos \theta \bar{k}\), which in combination with Eqs. (2) gives

\(\left[\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{s}+\left(\bar{H}_{A}\right)_{A B}\right] \cdot \bar{k}^{\prime}=\left(I_{2}+I_{3}\right) \Omega(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{1}(\Omega \cos \theta-\eta) \cos \theta.\)

We take instant \(t_{1}\) to be the initial condition, at which it is given that \(\Omega_{1}=5 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}\), \(\theta_{1}=90^{\circ}\), and \(\dot{\theta}_{1}=0\). The value of \(\eta\) is specified and the inertia properties are listed in Eqs. (1). Thus, conservation of angular momentum about the vertical shaft requires that

\(\left(I_{2}+I_{3}\right) \Omega_{2}\left(\sin \theta_{2}\right)^{2}+I_{1}\left(\Omega_{2} \cos \theta_{2}-\eta\right) \cos \theta_{2}=0.9783 m.\) (3)

This is one relation between \(\Omega_{2}\) and \(\theta_{2}\) that must be satisfied. Another is obtained from conservation of energy. The only force acting on the system of rigid bodies that does work is gravity. We take the elevation of point \(A\) to be the datum, so that

\(V=m g(-L \cos \theta)+\frac{m g}{2}\left(-\frac{L}{2} \cos \theta\right). \)

Correspondingly, conservation of energy requires that

\(\begin{array}{c}T_{2}+V_{2}=T_{1}+V_{1}, \\\\\frac{1}{2}\left[\left(I_{2}+I_{3}\right) \Omega_{2}^{2}\left(\sin \theta_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(I_{2}+I_{3}\right) \dot{\theta}_{2}^{2}+I_{1}\left(\Omega_{2} \cos \theta_{2}-\eta\right)^{2}\right] \\\\-1.25 m g L \cos \theta_{2}=2.896 m.\end{array} (4) \)

Thus we have two conservation principles relating \(\Omega\) and \(\theta\) at any instant \(t_{2}\).

The value of \(\theta_{2}\) is a maximum or minimum when \(\dot{\theta}_{2}=0 .\) To determine the corresponding values of \(\theta_{2}\) and \(\Omega_{2}\), we observe that Eq. (3) may be solved for \(\Omega_{2}\) as a function of \(\theta_{2}\) :

\(\large\Omega_{2}(\theta)=\frac{0.9783 m+I_{1} \eta \cos \theta_{2}}{\left(I_{2}+I_{3}\right)\left(\sin \theta_{2}\right)^{2}+I_{1} \cos \left(\theta_{2}\right)^{2}}. (5)\)

When this expression is substituted into Eq. (4), the result is an equation that contains only \(\theta_{2}\). To determine the roots of this equation, let \(E\left(\theta_{2}, \dot{\theta}_{2}, \Omega_{2}\right)\) denote the left side of Eq. (4). Thus we seek the roots of a function \(F\left(\theta_{2}\right)\) defined symbolically by

\(\large F\left(\theta_{2}\right) \equiv \frac{1}{m} E\left(\theta_{2}, 0, \Omega_{2}\left(\theta_{2}\right)\right)-2.896.\)

A plot of this function would show that it is positive at \(\theta_{2}=0\) and \(\theta_{2}=\pi\) and is negative slightly below \(\theta_{2}=\pi / 2 .\) Thus there are two roots. Numerical software readily yields these values if we use \(\theta_{2}=\pi / 4\) and \(\theta_{2}=\pi / 2\) as the initial guesses. The results, and the corresponding values of \(\Omega\) at each position, are

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\min }=36.288^{\circ}, \quad \Omega_{2}\left(\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\min }\right)=14.128 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}, \\\\\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\max }=90^{\circ}, \quad \Omega_{2}\left(\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\max }\right)=5 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\end{array} \)

The fact that the system does not rise higher than the angle at which it was released is not surprising. When the cable is severed, the system falls. As it does so, the value of \(\Omega\) must increase to conserve angular momentum, because the mass is situated closer to the vertical axis. The inertia of this descent carries the bar past the value of \(\theta\) at which a steady precession is possible. At the minimum \(\theta_{2}\) the gyroscopic rotational effects cause the system to swing upward, slowing \(\Omega .\) When the system reaches its initial elevation, it has the value of \(\Omega\) with which it was released. Because the mechanical energy is conserved, and both \(\theta\) and \(\Omega\) match the conditions when the system was released, it must be that \(\dot{\theta}\) also matches the initial value.

The second part seeks the values of \(\Omega_{2}\) and \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\) corresponding when \(\theta_{2}=\) \(0.5\left[\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\min }+\left(\theta_{2}\right)_{\max }=63.14^{\circ} .\right.\) To find \(\Omega_{2}\) we substitute this value of \(\theta_{2}\) into Eq. (3) to find

\(\Omega_{2}=6.467 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s} \text { at } \theta_{2}=63.14^{\circ} .\)

Substitution of this value into Eq. (4) converts it to a single equation for \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\), whose solution is

\(\dot{\theta}_{2}=\pm 4.0807 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s} \text { at } \theta_{2}=63.14^{\circ}.\)

The alternative sign, which results from the fact that \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\) is obtained by taking a square root, indicates that the magnitude of the nutation rate does not depend on whether the sphere is rising or falling. This is another consequence of the conservative nature of this system.
