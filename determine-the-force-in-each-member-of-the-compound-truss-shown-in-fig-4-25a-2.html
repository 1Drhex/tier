Guest Question.

Determine the force in each member of the compound truss shown in Fig. \(4.25(\mathrm{a})\).

Admin
Solution

Static Determinacy. The truss has 11 members and 7 joints and is supported by 3 reactions. Since \(m+r=2 j\) and the reactions and the members of the truss are properly arranged, it is statically determinate.
The slopes of the inclined members, as determined from the dimensions of the truss, are shown in Fig. \(4.25\) (a).

Reactions. The reactions at supports \(A\) and \(B\), as computed by applying the three equilibrium equations to the free-body diagram of the entire truss (Fig. \(4.25(\mathrm{~b})\) ), are

\( A_{x}=25 \mathrm{k} \leftarrow \quad A_{y}=5 \mathrm{k} \uparrow \quad B_{y}=35 \mathrm{k} \uparrow \)

Section \(\boldsymbol{ a a }\). Since a joint with two or fewer unknown forces cannot be found to start the method of joints, we first calculate \(F_{A B}\) by using section \(a a\), as shown in Fig. \(4.25\) (a).
The free-body diagram of the portion of the truss on the left side of section \(a a\) is shown in Fig. \(4.25\) (c). We determine \(F_{A B}\) by summing moments about point \(G\), the point of intersection of the lines of action of \(F_{C G}\) and \(F_{D G}\).

\( \begin{array}{ll} +\curvearrowleft \sum M_{G}=0 & -25(32)-5(16)+10(16)+F_{A B}(32)=0 \\ & F_{A B}=22.5 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{T}) \end{array} \)

With \(F_{A B}\) now known, the method of joints can be started either at joint \(A\) or at joint \(B\), since both of these joints have only two unknowns each. We begin with joint \(A\).

Joint \(\boldsymbol{A}\). The free-body diagram of joint \(A\) is shown in Fig. \(4.25(\mathrm{~d})\).

\( \begin{array}{rr} +\rightarrow \sum F_{x}=0 & -25+22.5+\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} F_{A C}+\frac{3}{5} F_{A D}=0 \\ +\uparrow \sum F_{y}=0 & 5+\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} F_{A C}+\frac{4}{5} F_{A D}=0 \end{array} \)

Solving these equations simultaneously, we obtain

\( \begin{array}{c} F_{A C}=-27.95 \mathrm{k} \text { and } F_{A D}=25 \mathrm{k} \\ F_{A C}=27.95 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \\ F_{A D}=25 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{T}) \end{array}\)

Joints \(\boldsymbol{C}\) and \(\boldsymbol{D}\). Focusing our attention on joints \(C\) and \(D\) in Fig. \(4.25(\mathrm{~b})\), and by satisfying the two equilibrium equations by inspection at each of these joints, we determine

\( \begin{array}{l} F_{C G}=27.95 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \\ F_{C D}=10 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \\ F_{D G}=20.62 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{T}) \end{array} \)

Joint \(\boldsymbol{G}\). Next, we consider the equilibrium of joint \(G\) (see Fig. 4.25(e)).

\( \begin{array}{l} +\rightarrow \sum F_{x}=0 \quad 5+\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(27.95)-\frac{1}{\sqrt{17}}(20.62)+\frac{1}{\sqrt{17}} F_{E G}+\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} F_{F G}=0 \\ \end{array} \)

\( \begin{array}{l} +\uparrow \sum F_{y}=0 \quad-40+\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}(27.95)-\frac{4}{\sqrt{17}}(20.62)-\frac{4}{\sqrt{17}} F_{E G}-\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} F_{F G}=0 \end{array} \)

Solving these equations, we obtain

\( \begin{array}{c} F_{E G}=-20.62 \mathrm{k} \text { and } F_{F G}=-16.77 \mathrm{k} \\ F_{E G}=20.62 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \\ F_{F G}=16.77 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \end{array} \)

Joints \(\boldsymbol{E}\) and \(\boldsymbol{F}\). Finally, by considering the equilibrium, by inspection, of joints \(E\) and \(F\) (see Fig. 4.25(b)), we obtain

\( \begin{aligned} F_{B E} &=25 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \\ F_{E F} &=10 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{T}) \\ F_{B F} &=16.77 \mathrm{k}(\mathrm{C}) \end{aligned} \)
