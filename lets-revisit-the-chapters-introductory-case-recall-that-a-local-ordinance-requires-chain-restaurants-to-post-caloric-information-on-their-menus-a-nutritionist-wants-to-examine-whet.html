Guest Question.

Let’s revisit the chapter’s introductory case. Recall that a local ordinance requires chain restaurants to post caloric information on their menus. A nutritionist wants to examine whether average drink calories declined at Starbucks after the passage of the ordinance. The nutritionist obtains transaction data for 40 Starbucks cardholders and records each cardholder’s drink calories prior to the ordinance and then after the ordinance. A portion of the data is shown in Table 10.4. Can she conclude at the 5% significance level that the ordinance reduced average drink calories?

Admin
Solution

We first note that this is a matched-pairs experiment; specifically, it conforms to a “before” and “after” type of study. Moreover, we want to find out whether average drink calories consumed prior to the ordinance are greater than average drink calories consumed after passage of the ordinance. Thus, we want to test if the mean difference \( μ_D \) is greater than zero, where \( D = X_1 − X_2, X_1 \) denotes drink calories before the ordinance, and \( X_2 \) denotes drink calories after the ordinance for a randomly selected Starbuck’s customer. We specify the competing hypotheses as

\( H_0 : μ_D ≤ 0 \)

 

\( H_A : μ_D > 0 \)

The normality condition for the test is satisfied since the sample size n ≥ 30. The value of the test statistic is calculated as \( t_{df} = \frac{\overline{d} − d_0}{s_D / \sqrt{n} } \) where \( d_0 \) equals 0. In order to determine \( \overline{d} \)and \( s_D \), we first calculate the difference \( d_i \) for each i-th customer. For instance, customer 1 consumes 141 calories prior to the ordinance and 142 calories after the ordinance, for a difference of \(d_1 = 141 − 142 = −1\). The differences for a portion of the other customers appear in the fourth column of Table 10.4.

TABLE 10.4 Data and Calculations for Example 10.7, n = 40

Customer Drink Calories
Before After \( d_i\) \( ( d_i – \overline{d} )^2 \)
1 141 142 -1 \( (−1 − 2.1)^2 = 9.61 \)
2 137 140 -3 \( (−3 − 2.1)^2 = 26.01 \)
⋮ ⋮ ⋮ ⋮ ⋮
40 147 141 6 \( (6 – 2.1)^2 = 15.21 \)
\( \sum{d_i} = 84 \) \( Σ ( d i − \overline{d} )^2 = 2593.60 \)

We obtain the sample mean as

\( \overline{d} = \frac{\sum{d_i} }{n} = \frac{84}{40} =2.10 \)

Similarly, in the last column of Table 10.4, we square the differences between \( d_i and \overline{d} \) . Summing these squared differences yields the numerator in the formula for the sample variance \( s^2_D \) . The denominator is simply n − 1, so

\( s_D^2 = \frac{\sum{}(d_i – \overline{d} )^2}{n – 1} =\frac{2,593.60}{40 – 1} = 66.5026. \)

As usual, the standard deviation is the positive square root of the sample variance—that is, \( s_D = \sqrt{66.5026} = 8.1549. \)We compute the value of the \( t_{df} \) test statistic with df = n − 1 = 40 − 1 = 39 as

\( t_{39} = \frac{\overline{d} – d_0}{s_D / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.10 – 0}{8.1549 / \sqrt{40} } = 1.629 \)

Given a right-tailed hypothesis test with df = 39, we can use the t table to approximate the p-value = \( P(T_{39} ≥ 1.629), as 0.05 < p-value < 0.10 \) Using Excel, we find that the exact p-value = 0.0557. Since the p-value > 0.05, we do not reject H0. At the 5% significance level, we cannot conclude that the posting of nutritional information decreases average drink calories.
