Guest Question.

The concentration of undesirable impurity in air (at 1 bar \(=10^{5} \mathrm{~Pa}\) ) is to be reduced from \(0.1 \%\) (or \(100 \mathrm{~Pa}\) ) to \(0.02 \%\) (or \(20 \mathrm{~Pa}\) ) by absorption in pure water. Find the height of tower required for countercurrent operations.

Data

For consistency let us use SI units throughout. For the packing

\(\begin{array}{l}k_{\mathrm{Ag}} a=0.32 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} \cdot \mathrm{Pa} \\k_{\mathrm{Al}} a=0.1 / \mathrm{hr}\end{array}\)

The solubility of \(\mathrm{A}\) in water is given by Henry’s law constant

\(H_{\mathrm{A}}=p_{\mathrm{Ai}} / C_{\mathrm{A} i}=12.5 \mathrm{~Pa} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} / \mathrm{mol}\)

The flow rates per meter squared cross section of tower are

\(\begin{array}{l}F_{g} / A_{c s}=1 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{2} \\F_{l} / A_{c s}=7 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{2}\end{array}\)

The molar density of liquid under all conditions is

\(C_{T}=56000 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\)

Figure E24.1 shows the quantities known at this point.

Admin
Solution

 

Our strategy is to first solve the material balance, then determine the tower height. Since we are dealing with dilute solutions we may use the simplified form of the material balance. So for any point in the tower \(p_{\mathrm{A}}\) and \(C_{\mathrm{A}}\) are related by Eq.4.

\(p_{\mathrm{A} 3}-p_{\mathrm{A} 1}=\frac{\left(F_{l} / A_{c s}\right)}{\left(F_{g} / A_{c s}\right)} \frac{\pi}{C_{T}}\left(C_{\mathrm{A} 3}-C_{\mathrm{A} 1}\right)\)

or

\(p_{\mathrm{A} 3}-20=\frac{\left(7 \times 10^{5}\right)\left(1 \times 10^{5}\right)}{\left(1 \times 10^{5}\right)(56000)}\left(C_{\mathrm{A} 3}-0\right)\)

or

\(C_{\mathrm{A} 3}=0.08 p_{\mathrm{A} 3}-1.6\) (i)

from which the concentration of \(\mathrm{A}\) in the liquid leaving the tower is

\(C_{\mathrm{A} 2}=0.08(100)-1.6=6.4 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\) (ii)

The expression for tower height is, from Eq. 6

\(h=\frac{V_{r}}{A_{c s}}=\frac{\left(F_{g} / A_{c s}\right)}{\pi\left(K_{\mathrm{Ag}} a\right)} \int_{20}^{100} \frac{d p_{\mathrm{A}}}{p_{\mathrm{A}}-p_{\mathrm{A}}^{*}}\) (iii)

Now evaluate terms

\(\frac{1}{\left(K_{\mathrm{Ag}} a\right)}=\frac{1}{\left(k_{\mathrm{Ag}} a\right)}+\frac{H_{\mathrm{A}}}{\left(k_{\mathrm{A} l} a\right)}=\frac{1}{0.32}+\frac{12.5}{0.1}=3.125+125=128.125\)

This expression shows that

\(\begin{array}{l}G \text { film resistance }=3.125 / 128.125=0.024, \text { or } 2.4 \% \\L \text { film resistance }=125 / 128.125=0.976, \text { or } 97.6 \%\end{array}\)

and

\(\left(K_{\mathrm{Ag}} a\right)=1 / 128.125=0.0078 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} \cdot \mathrm{Pa}\) (iv)

Next evaluate \(p_{\mathrm{A}}-p_{\mathrm{A}}^{*} .\) Thus with Eq. (i) we get

\(\begin{aligned}p_{\mathrm{A}}-p_{\mathrm{A}}^{*} &=p_{\mathrm{A}}-H_{\mathrm{A}} C_{\mathrm{A}}=p_{\mathrm{A}}-1.25\left(0.08 p_{\mathrm{A}}-1.6\right) \\p_{\mathrm{A}} &=20 \mathrm{~Pa}\end{aligned}\) (v)

Inserting Eqs. (iv) and (v) in (iii) gives

\(\begin{aligned}h &=\frac{\left(1 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{2}\right)}{\left(10^{5} \mathrm{~Pa}\right)\left(0.0078 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{hr} \cdot \mathrm{m}^{3} \cdot \mathrm{Pa}\right)} \int_{20}^{100} \frac{d p_{\mathrm{A}}}{20} \\&=(128.125)\left(\frac{100-20}{20}\right)=\underline{\underline{512.5 \mathrm{~m}}}\end{aligned}\)

Comment. Here the tower is very high, unacceptably high. Also note that most of the resistance (over \(97 \%\) ) lies in the liquid film, making this a liquid-filmcontrolling process. However, if we added component \(\mathrm{B}\) to the liquid which reacts with \(\mathrm{A},\) we should be able to speed things up. Let’s see if this is so.
