Guest Question.

Water at \( 50^{\circ} F \) enters a heat-exchanger tube having an inside diameter of 1 in. and a length of 10 ft. The water flows at 20 gal/min. For a constant wall temperature of \( 210^{\circ} F \), estimate the exit temperature of the water using (a) the Reynolds analogy, (b) the Colburn analogy, (c) the Prandtl analogy, and (d) the von Kármán analogy. Entrance effects are to be neglected, and the properties of water may be evaluated at the arithmetic-mean bulk temperature.

Admin
Solution

Considering a portion of the heat-exchanger tube shown in Figure, we see that an application of the first law of thermodynamics to the control volume indicated will yield the result that

\( \left \{ rate of heat transfer into c, v, by fluid flow \right \} + \left \{ rate of heat transfer into c, v, by convection \right \} = \left \{ rate of heat transfer out of c, v, by fluid flow \right \}\)

 

If these heat-transfer rates are designated as \( q_{1}, q_{2}\) and \( q_{3},\) they may be evaluated as follows:

\( q_{1} = \rho \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4} \upsilon_{x} c_{p} T|_{x}\)

 

\( q_{2} = h \pi D \Delta_{x} \left ( T_{s} – T \right ) \)

 

and

\( q_{3} = \rho \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4} \upsilon_{x} c_{p} T|_{x} + \Delta x \)

 

The substitution of these quantities into the energy balance expression gives

\( \rho \frac{\pi D^{2}}{4} \upsilon_{x} c_{p} \left [T|_{x + \Delta x } – T|_{x} \right ] – h \pi D \Delta x \left ( T_{s} – T \right ) = 0 \)

 

which may be simplified and rearranged into the form

\( \frac{D}{4} \frac{T|_{x + \Delta x } – T|_{x}}{\Delta x} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p} } \left ( T – T_{s} \right ) = 0 \)

 

Evaluated in the limit as \( \Delta x \rightarrow 0, \) equation (\( St = \frac{C_{f}/2}{ 1 + 5 \sqrt{C_{f}/2} \left \{ Pr – 1 + ln \left [ 1 + \frac{5}{6} \left ( Pr – 1 \right ) \right ] \right \}} \) ) reduces to

 

\( \frac{dT}{dx} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} \left ( T – T_{s} \right ) = 0 \)

 

Separating the variables, we have

 

\( \frac{dT}{T – T_{s}} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} dx = 0 \)

 

and integrating between the limits indicated, we obtain

\( \int_{T_{0}}^{T_{L}} \frac{dT}{T – T_{s}} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} \int_{0}^{L} dx = 0 \)

 

\( ln \frac{T_{L} – T_{s}}{T_{0} – T_{s}} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4L}{D} = 0 \)

 

Equation may now be solved for the exit temperature \( T_{L} \). Observe that the coefficient of the right-hand term, \( h/\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p} \), is the Stanton number. This parameter has been achieved quite naturally from our analysis.
The coefficient of skin friction may be evaluated with the aid of Figure. The velocity is calculated as

 

\( \upsilon_{x} = 20 gal/min \left ( ft^{3} /7.48 gal \right ) \left [ 144 / \left ( \pi /4 \right )\left ( 1^{2} \right ) \right ] ft^{2} \left ( min/60 s \right ) = 8.17 fps \)

 

Initially, we will assume the mean bulk temperature to be \( 90^{\circ} F\). The film temperature will then be \( 150^{\circ} F\), at which \( \nu = 0.474 \times 10^{-5} ft^{2}/s. \) The Reynolds number is

 

\( Re = \frac{D \upsilon_{x}}{\nu } = \frac{\left ( 1/12 ft \right ) \left ( 8.17 ft/s \right )}{0.474 \times 10^{-5} ft^{2}/s} = 144.000 \)

 

At this value of Re, the friction factor, \( f_{f} \), assuming smooth tubing, is 0.0042. For each of the four analogies, the Stanton number is evaluated as follows:

 

(a) Reynolds analogy

\( St = \frac{C_{f}}{2} = 0.0021 \)

 

(b) Colburn analogy

\( St = \frac{C_{f}}{2} Pr^{-2/3} = 0.0021 \left ( 2.72 \right )^{- 2/3} = 0.00108 \)

 

(c) Prandtl analogy

\( St = \frac{C_{f}/2}{1 + 5 \sqrt{C_{f}/2} \left ( Pr – 1 \right )} = \frac{0.0021}{1 + 5 \sqrt{0.0021} \left ( 1.72 \right )} = 0.00151 \)

 

(d) von Kármán analogy

\( St = \frac{C_{f}/2}{1 + 5 \sqrt{C_{f}/2} \left \{ Pr – 1 + ln \left [ 1 + \frac{5}{6} \left ( Pr – 1 \right ) \right ] \right \}} \)

 

\( St = \frac{0.0021}{1 + 5 \sqrt{0.0021} \left \{ 2.72 – 1 + ln \left [ 1 + \frac{5}{6} \left ( 2.72 – 1 \right ) \right ] \right \}} = 0.00131 \)

 

Substituting these results into equation (\( \int_{T_{0}}^{T_{L}} \frac{dT}{T – T_{s}} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} \int_{0}^{L} dx = 0 \), \( ln \frac{T_{L} – T_{s}}{T_{0} – T_{s}} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4L}{D} = 0 \)), we obtain, for \( T_{L} \), the following results:

(a) \( T_{L} = 152^{\circ} F \)

(b) \( T_{L} = 115^{\circ} F \)

(c) \( T_{L} = 132^{\circ} F \)

(d) \( T_{L} = 125^{\circ} F \)

 

Some fine-tuning of these results may be necessary to adjust the physical property values for the calculated film temperatures. In none of these cases is the assumed film temperature different than the calculated one by more than \( 6^{\circ} F \), so the results are not going to change much.

The Reynolds analogy value is much different from the other results obtained. This is not surprising, as the Prandtl number was considerably above a value of 1. The last three analogies yielded quite consistent results. The Colburn analogy is the simplest
to use and is preferable from that standpoint.
