Guest Question.

In a cylindrical fuel element for a gas cooled nuclear reactor, the energy generation can be approximated by
\(g(r) = g_o \left \{ 1 -\left (\frac{r}{r_o} \right )^{2} \right \} W/m^3\)
where \(r_o\) is the radius of the fuel element and \(g_o\) is
constant. The outer surface is maintained at constant temperature \(T_s\).
(a) For the radius of 1 cm, thermal conductivity of 10 W/m.K and \(g_o = 1.6 × 10^8 W/m^3.\) Calculate the temperature drop from centre line to surface.
(b) If the heat removal rate from the outer surface of nuclear reactor \(1.6 × 10^5 W/m^2\), what would be the temperature drop from centre to surface?

Admin
Solution

Given : A nuclear fuel rod with heat generation rate of
\(g(r) = g_o \left \{ 1 -\left (\frac{r}{r_o} \right )^{2} \right \} W/m^3\)

(a) \(g_o = 1.6 × 108 W/m^3, r_o = 1 cm = 0.01 m,k = 10 W/m.K.\)
(b) \(q = 1.6 × 10^5 W/m^2.\)
To find :
(i) Temperature drop from centre to surface for given \(g_o = 1.6 × 10^8 W/m^3.\)
(ii) Temperature drop from centre to surface for \(q = 1.6 × 10^5 W/m^2.\)

Assumptions :
(i) Steady state conditions with constant properties.
(ii) Heat transfer in radial direction only.
Analysis : (a) The governing differential equation for cylinder
\(\frac{1}{r} \frac{d}{dr}\left \{ r \frac{dT}{dr} \right \} +\frac{g(r)}{k}=0\)
Using \(g(r) = g_o [1 – (r/r_o)^2]\)
Then \(\frac{d}{dr}\left \{ r \frac{dT}{dr} \right \} = – \frac{g_o r }{k}\left [ 1 – \left (\frac{r}{r_{o}} \right )^2 \right ]\)

or \(\frac{d}{dr}\left \{ r \frac{dT}{dr} \right \} = – \frac{g_o }{k}\left [ r – \frac{r^{3}}{r_{o}^{2}} \right ]\)

Integrating with respect to r
\(r \left \{ \frac{dT}{dr} \right \} =- \frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{2} – \frac{r^4}{4 r_{o}^2} \right \} +C_1\)
\( \frac{dT}{dr} = – \frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{2} – \frac{r^4}{4 r_{o}^2} \right \} + \frac{C_1}{r}\)
Integrating again, we get
\(T(r) = -\frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{4} – \frac{r^4}{16 r_{o}^2} \right \} +C_1 ln(r) +C_2\)

Subject to boundary conditions
(i) At the centre, r = 0 ; \(\frac{dT}{dr} = 0,\)
It gives \(C_1 = 0\)
Now, the temperature distribution becomes,
\(T(r) =-\frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{4} – \frac{r^4}{16 r_{o}^2} \right \} + C_2\)

Using the boundary condition at the surface,
(ii) At \(r = r_o ; T = T_s\)
Then, \(Ts = – \frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{4} – \frac{r^4}{16 r_{o}^2} \right \} + C_2\)

It gives \(C_2 = T_s + \frac{3}{16} \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{k}\)

Substituting \(C_1\) and \(C_2\), the temperature distribution becomes
\(T(r) = − \frac{g_o}{k}\left \{ \frac{r^2}{4} – \frac{r^4}{16 r_{o}^2} \right \} + \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{k} +T_s\)

The maximum temperature occurs at the centre
(at r = 0), we get
\(T_{max} = T_c =\frac{3}{16} \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{k} + T_s\)

Temperature drop from centre line to surface
\(T_c – T_s =\frac{3}{16} \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{k}\)

Using numerical values in above equation
\(T_c – T_s =\frac{3}{16} \times \frac{1.6 × 10^8 × (0.01)^2}{10}\)

= 300°C.
(b) For heat removal rate \(q = 1.6 × 10^5 W/m^2\) The heat removal rate can be expressed as
\(Q = 2πr_o L q =\int_{0}^{r_o} g(r) (2πrL) dr\)
\(r_oq = \int_{0}^{r_o} g_o \left [ 1 – \left (\frac{r}{r_{o}} \right )^2 \right ]rdr\)

\(= g_o \int_{0}^{r_o} \left [ r – \frac{r^{3}}{r_{o}^{2}} \right ] dr\)

or\( r_oq = g_o \int_{0}^{r_o}\left [ \frac{r_{o}^2}{2} – \frac{r_{o}^4}{4 r_{o}^2} \right ]=\frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{4} \)
or \(q = \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{4} \)

or \(g_o = \frac{4q}{r_o}=\frac{4 × 1.6 × 10^5}{0.01} = 6.4 × 10^7 W/m^3\)
Then temperature drop from centre to surface
\(T_c – T_s = \frac{3}{16} \frac{g_o r_{o}^2}{k}\)
\(=\frac{3}{16} \times \frac{1.6 × 10^7 × (0.01)^2}{10}\)

= 120°C.
