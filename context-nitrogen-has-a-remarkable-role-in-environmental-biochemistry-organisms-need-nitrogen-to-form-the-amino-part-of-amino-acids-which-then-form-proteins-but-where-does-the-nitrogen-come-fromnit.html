Guest Question.

CONTEXT Nitrogen has a remarkable role in environmental biochemistry. Organisms need nitrogen to
form the amino part of amino acids, which then form proteins. But where does the nitrogen come from?
Nitrogen has a hard time combining with other elements to form rocks, and as a result the earth’s crust is incredibly nitrogen-poor: nitrogen is the 31st most abundant element (by mole, not by mass) in the earth (ater yttrium, for goodness’ sake), when it is the 6th most abundant in the universe. Instead, the nitrogen is taken from the air and converted to ammonia, through a process called nitrogen ixation, primarily by microorganisms. Iron-based enzymes catalyze this reaction at temperatures near 300 K, where the average kinetic energy of a single nitrogen molecule (as we’ll ind) is nowhere near enough to overcome the activation barrier of over 500 kJ mol-1 for the gas-phase reaction to combine N2 and H2.

PROBLEM Find the average translational kinetic energy (in \(KJ mol^{-1}\)) and the rms speed along the X axis \(\left\langle v^{2}X \right\rangle ^{1/2 }\) for a nitrogen molecule at 298 k.

Admin
Solution

A useful quantity is the thermal energy:

\(K_{B}T = (1.381\cdot 10^{-23}JK^{-1} ) (298 K)= 4.115\cdot 10^{-21} J = 2.478 KJ mol^{-1}\)

(Let’s keep one too many signiicant digits until the end.) this figure is a useful one to remember: the thermal energy at room temperature is about \(2.5 KJ mol^{-1}\). the average energy is then

\(\varepsilon = \frac{3K_{B}T}{2} = 3.72 KJ mol^{-1}\)

he rms speed along X is given by

\(\left\langle v^{2}X \right\rangle ^{1/2 } =( \frac{K_{B}T}{m})^{1/2} = ( \frac{4.115\cdot 10^{-21} j }{(28.00 amu)(1.661\cdot 10^{-27} kg amu^{-1} )})^{1/2}= 297 m s^{-1}\)

Here’s another good number to remember: typical molecular speeds in a room temperature gas are on the order of \(100 m s^{-1} \). We’ll return to this example of N2 at 298 K a few more times to help us stay familiar with these benchmark numbers.
