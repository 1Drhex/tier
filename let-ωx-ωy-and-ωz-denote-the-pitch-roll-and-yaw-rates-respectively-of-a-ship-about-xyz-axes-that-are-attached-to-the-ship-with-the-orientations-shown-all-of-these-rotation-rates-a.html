Guest Question.

Let \(ω_{x}, ω_{y}, \text{and} ω_{z}\) denote the pitch, roll, and yaw rates, respectively, of a ship about xyz axes that are attached to the ship with the orientations shown. All of these rotation rates are variable quantities. The origin of xyz coincides with the center of mass G of the ship. Consider an elevator car whose path perpendicularly intersects the centerline at a distance L forward from the center of mass. Let h(t) denote the height of the car above the centerline. The velocity and acceleration of the center of mass at this instant are \(\bar{v}_{G} \text {and} \bar{v}_{G} \). Determine the corresponding velocity and acceleration of the car.

Admin
Solution

This example brings to the fore an important feature of a body-fixed reference frame whose rotation about its own axes is known. We use \(x y z\) as the moving reference frame, as well as the global coordinate system. The given rotations are about body-fixed axes, so we have \(\bar{e}_{1}=\bar{i}, \bar{e}_{2}=\bar{j}, \bar{e}_{2}=\bar{k}\) corresponding to the rates \(\omega_{x}, \omega_{y}\), and \(\omega_{z}\), respectively. Thus, the angular velocity of the ship is

\(\bar{\omega}=\omega_{x} \bar{i}+\omega_{y} \bar{j}+\omega_{z} \bar{k}\).

Because the rotation directions are the unit vectors of \(x y z\), their angular velocity is \(\bar{\omega}\), that is, \(\bar{\Omega}_{1}=\bar{\Omega}_{2}=\bar{\Omega}_{3}=\bar{\omega} .\) The general description of the angular acceleration corresponding to variable rotation rates therefore is

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{\alpha} &=\dot{\omega}_{x} \bar{i}+\omega_{x}(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{i})+\dot{\omega}_{y} \bar{j}+\omega_{y}(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{j})+\dot{\omega}_{z} \bar{k}+\omega_{z}(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{k}) \\\\&=\dot{\omega}_{x} \bar{i}+\dot{\omega}_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\omega}_{z} \bar{k}+\bar{\omega} \times\left(\omega_{x} \bar{i}\right)+\bar{\omega} \times\left(\omega_{y} \bar{j}\right)+\bar{\omega} \times\left(\omega_{z} \bar{k}\right) \\\\&=\dot{\omega}_{x} \bar{i}+\dot{\omega}_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\omega}_{z} \bar{k}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{\omega}=\dot{\omega}_{x} \bar{i}+\dot{\omega}_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\omega}_{z} \bar{k}\end{aligned}\).

This result for \(\bar{α}\) indicates that the angular acceleration components are always the time derivatives of the angular velocity components, provided that those components are relative to body-fixed axes. This observation is a key aspect to the development of kinetics principles in Chapter 5.
The elevator follows a straight path relative to the ship, so the relative motion is

\(\bar{r}_{P / G}=L \bar{i}+h \bar{k}, \quad\left(\bar{v}_{P}\right)_{x y z}=h \bar{k}=u \bar{k}, \quad\left(\bar{a}_{P}\right)_{x y z}=\dot{u} \bar{k}\)

We have described all the terms in Eqs. (3.5.4), so we find the velocity to be

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{v}_{P}=\bar{v}_{O^{\prime}}+\left(\bar{v}_{P}\right)_{x y z}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{P / O^{\prime}} \\\\\bar{a}_{P}=\bar{a}_{O^{\prime}}+\left(\bar{a}_{P}\right)_{x y z}+\bar{\alpha} \times \bar{r}_{P / O^{\prime}}+\bar{\omega} \times\left(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{P / O^{\prime}}\right)+2 \bar{\omega} \times\left(\bar{v}_{P}\right)_{x y z}.\end{array} \) (3.5.4)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{v}_{P} &=\bar{v}_{G}+\left(\bar{v}_{P}\right)_{x y z}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{P / G} \\\\&=\bar{v}_{G}+\left(\omega_{y} h-\omega_{z} L\right) \bar{i}-\omega_{x} h \bar{j}+\left(u+\omega_{x} L\right) \bar{k},\end{aligned}\)

and the acceleration is

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{a}_{P}=& \bar{a}_{G}+\left(\bar{a}_{P}\right)_{x y z}+\bar{\alpha} \times \bar{r}_{P / G}+\bar{\omega} \times\left(\bar{\omega} \times \bar{r}_{P / G}\right)+2 \bar{\omega} \times\left(\bar{v}_{P}\right)_{x y z} \\\\=& \bar{a}_{G}+\dot{u} \bar{k}+\left(\dot{\omega}_{x} \bar{i}+\dot{\omega}_{y} \bar{j}+\dot{\omega}_{z} \bar{k}\right) \times(L \bar{j}+h \bar{k})+\left(\omega_{x} \bar{i}+\omega_{y} \bar{j}+\omega_{z} \bar{k}\right) \\\\& \times\left[\left(\omega_{y} h-\omega_{z} L\right) \bar{i}-\omega_{x} h \bar{j}+\omega_{x} L \bar{k}\right]+2\left(\omega_{x} \bar{i}+\omega_{y} \bar{j}+\omega_{z} \bar{k}\right) \times u \bar{k} \\\\=& \bar{a}_{G}+\left[\dot{\omega}_{y} h-\dot{\omega}_{z} L+\omega_{x} \omega_{y} L+\omega_{x} \omega_{z} h+2 \omega_{y} u\right] \bar{i}+\left[-\dot{\omega}_{x} h+\omega_{y} \omega_{z} h\right.\\\\&\left.-\left(\omega_{x}^{2}+\omega_{z}^{2}\right) L-2 \omega_{x} u\right] \bar{j}+\left[\dot{u}+\dot{\omega}_{x} L-\left(\omega_{x}^{2}+\omega_{y}^{2}\right) h+\omega_{y} \omega_{z} L\right] \bar{k}\end{aligned}\)

Some of the terms in the acceleration were foreseeable. The acceleration of the elevator relative to the ship is represented by the \(\dot{u}\) term, and the angular acceleration effects are contained in the \(\dot{\omega}_{x}, \dot{\omega}_{y}\), and \(\dot{\omega}_{z}\) terms. In the same vein, the \(\omega_{x}^{2}, \omega_{y}^{2}\), and \(\omega_{z}^{2}\) terms represent centripetal accelerations about the respective axes associated with each rotation being the only one present. The terms that are not intuitive are those containing products of rotation rates about different axes, as well as the Coriolis acceleration terms.
