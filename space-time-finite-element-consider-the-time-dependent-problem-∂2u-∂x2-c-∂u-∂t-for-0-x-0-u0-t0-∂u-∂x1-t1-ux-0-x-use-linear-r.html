Guest Question.

(Space-time finite element) Consider the time-dependent problem
\(\frac{\partial^{2} u}{\partial x^{2}}=c \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\) for 0 <x< 1, t> 0
\(u(0, t)=0\) , \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(1, t)=1\), \(u(x, 0)=x\)
Use linear rectangular elements in the (x, t)-plane to model the problem. Note that the finite-element model is given by \(\left[K^{e}\right]\left\{u^{e}\right\}=\left\{Q^{e}\right\}\), where
\(K_{i j}^{e}=\int_{0}^{\Delta t} \int_{x_{a}}^{x_{b}}\left(\frac{\partial \psi_{i}^{e}}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \psi_{j}^{e}}{\partial x}+c \psi_{i} \frac{\partial \psi_{j}}{\partial t}\right) d x d t\)
\(Q_{1}^{e}=\left.\left(-\int_{0}^{\Delta t} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} d t\right)\right|_{x=x_{a}}\), \(Q_{2}^{e}=\left.\left(\int_{0}^{\Delta t} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} d t\right)\right|_{x=x_{b}}\).

Admin
Solution

For one space-time element mesh, we have the equations

\(\frac{\Delta x}{12}\left[\begin{array}{cccc}-2 c+4 r & -c-4 r & c-2 r & 2 c+2 r \\-c-4 r & -2 c+4 r & 2 c+2 r & c-2 r \\-c-2 r & -2 c+2 r & 2 c+4 r & c-4 r \\-2 c+2 r & -c-2 r & c-4 r & 2 c+4 r\end{array}\right]\) \(\left\{\begin{array}{l}U_{1} \\U_{2} \\U_{3} \\U_{4}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}Q_{1} \\Q_{2} \\Q_{3} \\Q_{4}\end{array}\right\}\) (1)

The “boundary conditions” are: \(U_{1}=0\), \(U_{2}=\Delta x\) ,\(U_{4}=0\) , \(Q_{3}=\Delta t\) . Note that we have no condition given at \(t=\Delta t\) . This amounts to assuming that \(\partial u / \partial t=0\) .The value at the node 3 (i.e., \(U_{3}=u(\Delta x, \Delta t)\))can be determined easily from Eq. (1),

\(\frac{\Delta x}{12}\left[(-2 c+2 r) U_{2}+(2 c+4 r) U_{3}\right]=Q_{3}\)

or

\(\left[2 c+4 \frac{\Delta t}{(\Delta x)^{2}}\right] U_{3}=14 \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}+2 c \Delta x\)

For \(c=c_{0} r\), we have the result,

\(U_{3}=\frac{14+2 c_{0}}{4+2 c_{0}} \Delta x\)

The \(\alpha \)-family of approximation yields the equation

\(\left(\frac{c \Delta x}{6}\left[\begin{array}{ll}2 & 1 \\1 & 2\end{array}\right]+\frac{\alpha \Delta t}{\Delta x}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\right)\left\{\begin{array}{c}U_{1 n+1} \\U_{2 n+1}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

\(=\left(\frac{c \Delta x}{6}\left[\begin{array}{ll}2 & 1 \\1 & 2\end{array}\right]-\frac{(1-\alpha) \Delta t}{\Delta x}\left[\begin{array}{cc}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\right)\left\{\begin{array}{c}U_{1 n} \\U_{2 n}\end{array}\right\}+\Delta t\left\{\begin{array}{l}Q_{1} \\Q_{2}\end{array}\right\}\)

Using \(U_{1}=0, Q_{2}=1\) and \(U_{2}^{n+1}=\Delta x\) , we obtain

\(\left(c \frac{\Delta x}{3}+\alpha \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}\right) U_{2}^{n+1}=\left(-(1-\alpha) \frac{\Delta t}{\Delta x}+c \frac{\Delta x}{3}\right) \Delta x+\Delta t\)

For \(c=c_{0} r\), we have the result, \(U_{3}=\Delta x\).

While the two results differ quite a bit, it should not be taken seriously in viewof the coarse mesh taken and the special boundary and initial conditions used. Ingeneral, the space-time finite elements have a natural drawback in redefining theinitial-boundary value problem as an equivalent boundary value problem.
