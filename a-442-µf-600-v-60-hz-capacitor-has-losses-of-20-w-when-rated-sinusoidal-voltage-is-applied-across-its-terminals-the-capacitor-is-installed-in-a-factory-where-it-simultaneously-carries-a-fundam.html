Guest Question.

A 442 µF, 600 V, 60 Hz capacitor has losses of 20 W when rated sinusoidal voltage is applied across its terminals. The capacitor is installed in a factory where it simultaneously carries a fundamental current of 100 A, 60 Hz and a harmonic current of 80 A, 300 Hz. The harmonic current is 25° ahead of the fundamental. Because the peak is √2 times the effective value, the distorted current can be described by the following equation:

 

\(I=100 \sqrt{2} \sin \theta+80 \sqrt{2} \sin \left(5 \theta+25^{\circ}\right)\)

 

We wish to calculate

 

a. The effective value and THD of the current in the capacitor
b. The effective value and THD of the voltage across its teraminals
c. The approximate value of the losses

Admin
Solution

a. The effective value of the distorted current is

 

\(\begin{aligned}I &\left.=\sqrt{\left(100^{2}\right.}+80^{2}\right)=128 A \\THD &=I_{ H } / I_{ F }=80 A / 100 A =80 \%\end{aligned}\)

 

b. Reactance of the capacitor at 60 Hz

 

\(X_{60}=1 / 2 \pi f C=10^{6} /(2 \pi \times 60 \times 442)=6 \Omega\)

 

Reactance at 300 Hz

 

\(X_{300}=(60 Hz / 300 Hz ) \times 6 \Omega=1.2 \Omega\)

 

Fundamental voltage across the capacitor

 

\(E_{ F }=I X_{60}=100 \times 6=600 V\)

 

Harmonic voltage across the capacitor

 

\(E_{ H }=I X_{300}=80 \times 1.2=96 V\)

 

Total effective voltage across the capacitor

 

\(E=\sqrt{E_{ H }^{2}+E_{ F }^{2}}=\sqrt{600^{2}+96^{2}}=608 V\)

 

THD of voltage

 

\(THD =E_{ H } / E_{ F }=96 / 600=16 \%\)

 

Note that the THD of voltage (16%) is much less than the THD of current (80%).

 

The equation of the terminal voltage E is deduced from current I, while recalling that the fundamental and harmonic voltages are respectively 90° behind the corresponding currents:

 

\(\begin{aligned}E=& 600 \sqrt{2} \sin \left(\theta-90^{\circ}\right)+\\& 96 \sqrt{2} \sin \left(5 \theta+25^{\circ}-90^{\circ}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

from which

 

\(\begin{aligned}E=& 600 \sqrt{2} \sin \left(\theta-90^{\circ}\right)+\\& 96 \sqrt{2} \sin \left(5 \theta-65^{\circ}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

Fig. 19 shows the waveshape of the capacitor current and voltage. It is seen that the voltage is less distorted than the current. This is to be expected because the reactance at 300 Hz is 5 times less than that at 60 Hz.

 

c. Knowing that the harmonic and fundamental voltages can be treated separately, we can estimate the losses associated with each. We recall that the losses at 600 V, 60 Hz = 20 W.

 

The losses due to the 5th harmonic will be calculated on the basis of the 60 Hz losses. We assume they are proportional to the frequency and the square of the voltage. Consequently, the losses at 96 V, 300 Hz are

 

\(\begin{aligned}P_{300 Hz } &=20 W \times \frac{300 Hz }{60 Hz } \times\left(\frac{96 V }{600 V }\right)^{2} \\&=2.6 W\end{aligned}\)

 

Total losses are 20 W + 2.6 W = 22.6 W. The presence of the harmonic current has increased the losses by 13%.
