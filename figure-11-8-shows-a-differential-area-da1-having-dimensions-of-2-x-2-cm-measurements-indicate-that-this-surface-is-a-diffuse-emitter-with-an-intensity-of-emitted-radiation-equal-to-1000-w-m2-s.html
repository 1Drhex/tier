Guest Question.

Figure 11.8 shows a differential area \(dA_{1} \) having dimensions of 2 × 2 cm. Measurements indicate that this surface is a diffuse emitter with an intensity of emitted radiation equal to\( 1000 W/\left(m^{2}.sr \right)\) .A second differential area \(dA_{2}\) is located as shown. (a) Calculate the value of the solid angle subtended by \(dA_{2}\) with respect to \(dA_{1}\) .(b) What is the rate at which radiation emitted by \(dA_{1}\) is intercepted by \(dA_{2}\) ?(c) What is the irradiation associated with \(dA_{2}\) ? Both areas have the
same dimensions.

Admin
Solution

The area \(dA_{1}\) is emitting radiation in all directions, and it is a diffuse emitter. The area \(dA_{2}\) intercepts a small portion of the radiant energy.
Assumptions
1. Surfaces \(dA_{1}\) and \(dA_{2}\) can be treated as differential areas.
2. The system is at steady state.
a. The solid angle is calculated with Equation 11.7:

 

\(d \omega=\frac{dA_{n}}{r^{2}}=\frac{d A_{i} \cos \beta_{i}}{r^{2}}\)

The angle\( \beta\) is 0 because the surface normal of \(dA_{2}\) is directed at d \(A_{1}\). Substituting

\(d \omega_{2-1}=\frac{dA_{2} \cos 0}{r^{2}}=\frac{(0.02)(0.02)(1)}{(1)^{2}} \) \(d \omega_{2-1}=4.0 \times 10^{-4} {sr}\)

b. The intensity of radiation leaving \(dA_{1}\) in any direction is \(1000 {~W} /\left({m}^{2} \cdot {sr}\right)\). The amount intercepted by \(dA_{2}\) is given by

\(dq_{1-2}=I_{{el}} dA_{1} \cos \theta d \omega_{2-1}\)

Substituting

\(dq_{1-2}=1000(0.02)(0.02) \cos 30\left(4 \times 10^{-4}\right) \) \(dq_{1-2}=1.39 \times 10^{-4} {~W}\)

 

c. The irradiation associated with \(dA_{2}\) can be found by dividing the incident radiation by the receiver area:

\(dq_{i 2}^{\prime \prime}=\frac{d q_{1-2}}{d A_{2}}=\frac{1.39 \times 10^{-4}}{(0.02)(0.02)} \) \(dq_{i 2}^{\prime \prime}=3.46 \times 10^{-1} {~W} / {m}^{2}\)
