Guest Question.

Bearing Supports

A heavy door seals a furnace used to heat-treat metal parts. The door’s weight is W = 200 lb which acts through point G located at the center of the 18 in. by 18 in. door. Determine the force supported by cable AB and the reactions at the bearings at points C and D.

Admin
Solution

Road Map Our objective is to analyze equilibrium of the furnace door. The door is supported by a cable and two bearings, and appropriate modeling idealizations regarding the bearings will be needed to obtain a statically determinate problem. Both vector and scalar solutions are effective and both are illustrated.

Vector solution

Modeling The FBD of the furnace door is shown in Fig. 2 where we have assumed both bearings are perfectly aligned (or are self-aligning) so that the bearings have no moment reactions. If this assumption is not valid, then moment reactions are present at the bearings and this problem is statically indeterminate and cannot be solved using only the equations of static equilibrium.

Governing Equations In anticipation of summing moments about point D, the required position vectors* and force vectors are

\(\vec{r} _{DG} = \left(- 6\hat{\imath} + 5 \hat{\jmath} + 17 \hat{k} \right) in., \vec{W} = -200 \hat{\jmath} lb,\) (1)

\(\vec{r} _{DC} = 10 \hat{\jmath} in., \vec{C}= C_{x}\hat{\imath} + C_{z} \hat{k} ,\) (2)

\(\vec{r} _{DA} = \left(- 6\hat{\imath} + 14 \hat{\jmath} + 26 \hat{k} \right) in.,\) (3)
\(\vec{T} = T\frac{ 6\hat{\imath} + 20 \hat{\jmath} – 26 \hat{k}}{\sqrt{1112} } = T\left( 0\cdot 1799\hat{\imath} + 0\cdot 5998 \hat{\jmath} – 0\cdot 7797 \hat{k}\right)\) (4)

Summing moments† about point D gives

\( \sum{\vec{M} _{D} } = \vec{0} : \vec{r} _{DG} \times \vec{W} + \vec{r} _{DC} \times \vec{C} + \vec{r} _{DA} \times \vec{T} = \vec{0}\) (5)

\(\left(3400 in.\cdot lb\right) \hat{\imath} + \left(1200 in.\cdot lb\right) \hat{k} + C_{z} \left(10 in.\right) \hat{\imath}\)

\(– C_{x} \left(10 in.\right)\hat{k} + T\left[\left(- 26\cdot 51 in.\right) \hat{\imath} + \left(-6\cdot 119 in.\right) \hat{k} \right]= \vec{0}\) (6)

Because there are no \( \hat{\jmath}\) terms in Eq. (6), moment equilibrium about the y axis is automatically satisfied, regardless of the values of T and the reactions. Collecting \(\hat{\imath}\) and \(\hat{k}\) terms in Eq. (6) provides the following two equations, respectively

\(T\left(-26\cdot 51 in.\right)+ C_{z} \left(10 in.\right) + 3400 in. \cdot lb =0\) (7)

\(T\left(-6\cdot 119 in.\right)- C_{x} \left(10 in.\right) + 1200 in. \cdot lb =0\) (8)

Equations (7) and (8) contain three unknowns, thus additional equilibrium equations must be written before these unknowns can be determined. Equilibrium of forces requires

\(\sum{\vec{F} } = \vec{0} : \vec{W} + \vec{C} + \vec{D} + \vec{T} = \vec{0}\) (9)

Substituting for the force vectors in Eq. (9), where \(\vec{D} = D_{x} \hat{\imath} + D_{z} \hat{k}\), and collecting terms multiplying \(\hat{\imath}\), \(\hat{\jmath}\) and \(\hat{k}\) provide

\(\left(0\cdot 1799\right) T+ C_{x} + D_{x} = 0,\) (10)

\(\left(0\cdot 5998\right) T – 200 lb =0,\) (11)

\(\left(- 0\cdot 7797\right) T+ C_{z} + D_{z} = 0,\) (12)

Computation Equation (11) may immediately be solved for T . Then Eqs. (7) and (8) may be solved for \(C_{x}\) and \(C_{z}\), followed by solution of Eqs. (10) and (12) for \(D_{x}\) and \(D_{z}\). These solutions are

\( \Rightarrow T = 333\cdot 4 lb\)

 

\(C_{x} = -84\cdot 03 lb, C_{z} = 544\cdot 0 lb\)

\(D_{x} = 24\cdot 01 lb, D_{z} = -284\cdot 0 lb\) (13)

Scalar solution

Modeling The FBD is shown in Fig. 3 where we have assumed both bearings are perfectly aligned (or are self-aligning) so that the bearings have no moment reactions. For convenience, the cable force has been resolved into x, y, and z components.

Governing Equations & Computation The following strategy for writing and solving equilibrium equations is one of several that will allow for determination of the unknowns with minimal algebra:

\(\sum{F_{y} } = 0: \left(0\cdot 5998\right) T – 200 lb =0 \Rightarrow T = 333\cdot 4 lb,\) (14)

\(\sum{M_{Dx} } = 0: \left(-0\cdot 7797\right) T\left(14 in.\right) – \left(0\cdot 5998\right) T\left(26 in.\right)\)

\(+\left(200 lb\right) \left(17 in.\right) + C_{z} \left(10 in.\right) = 0\) (15)

\(\Rightarrow C_{z} = 544\cdot 0 lb\) (16)

\(\sum{M_{Dz} } = 0: -\left(0\cdot 5998\right) T\left(6 in.\right) – \left(0\cdot 1799\right) T\left(14 in.\right)\)

\(+\left(200 lb\right) \left(6 in.\right) – C_{x} \left(10 in.\right) = 0\) (17)

\(\Rightarrow C_{x} = – 84\cdot 03 lb\) (18)

\(\sum{F_{x} } = 0: \left(0\cdot 1799\right) T + C_{x} + D_{x} =0 \Rightarrow D_{x} = 24 \cdot 01 lb\) (19)

\(\sum{F_{z} } = 0: \left(- 0\cdot 7797\right) T + C_{z} + D_{z} =0 \Rightarrow D_{z} = – 284 \cdot 0 lb\) (20)

As expected, all solutions agree with those obtained using the vector approach.

Discussion & Verification We expect the cable to be in tension, and indeed our solution shows T >0. We should also verify that our solutions satisfy all of the equilibrium equations.

*Rather than \(\vec{r} _{DA}\), the vector \(\vec{r} _{DB}=34\hat{\jmath} \) in. could be used, and this would provide for easier evaluation of the cross product in Eq. (5).

†In Eq. (5), you may ﬁnd it convenient to evaluate \(\vec{r} _{DC}\times \vec{C} \) by inspection (i.e., scalar approach) rather than by formal evaluation of the cross product.
