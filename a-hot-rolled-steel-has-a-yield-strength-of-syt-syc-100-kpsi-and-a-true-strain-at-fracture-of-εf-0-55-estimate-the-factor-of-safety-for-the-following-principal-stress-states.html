Guest Question.

A hot-rolled steel has a yield strength of \(S_{y t}=S_{y c}=100\) kpsi and a true strain at fracture of \(\varepsilon_{f}=0.55\). Estimate the factor of safety for the following principal stress states:
\(\text { (a) } \sigma_{x}=70 kpsi , \sigma_{y}=70 kpsi , \tau_{x y}=0 kpsi\)

 

\(\text { (b) } \sigma_{x}=60 kpsi , \sigma_{y}=40 kpsi , \tau_{x y}=-15 kpsi\)

 

\(\text { (c) } \sigma_{x}=0 kpsi , \sigma_{y}=40 kpsi , \tau_{x y}=45 kpsi\)

 

\(\text { (d) } \sigma_{x}=-40 \text { kpsi, } \sigma_{y}=-60 \text { kpsi, } \tau_{x y}=15 \text { kpsi }\)

 

\(\text { (e) } \sigma_{1}=30 kpsi , \sigma_{2}=30 kpsi , \sigma_{3}=30 kpsi\)

 

Admin
Solution

Since \(\varepsilon_{f}>0.05 \text { and } S_{y t} \text { and } S_{y c} \text { a }\) are equal, the material is ductile and both the distortionenergy (DE) theory and maximum-shear-stress (MSS) theory apply. Both will be used for comparison. Note that cases a to d are plane stress states.

(a) Since there is no shear stress on this stress element, the normal stresses are equal to the principal stresses. The ordered principal stresses are \(\sigma_{A}=\sigma_{1}=70\)\(\sigma_{B}=\sigma_{2}=70, \sigma_{3}=0 \text { kpsi. }\)

 

DE From Eq. (5–13),

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left(\sigma_{A}^{2}-\sigma_{A} \sigma_{B}+\sigma_{B}^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}\) (5–13)

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left[70^{2}-70(70)+70^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}=70 kpsi\)

 

From Eq. (5–19),

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\frac{S_{y}}{n}\) (5–19)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{70}=1.43\)

 

MSS Noting that the two nonzero principal stresses are equal, \(\tau_{\max }\) will be from the largest Mohr’s circle, which will incorporate the third principal stress at zero. From Eq. (3–16),

 

\(\tau_{1 / 2}=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{2}}{2} \quad \tau_{2 / 3}=\frac{\sigma_{2}-\sigma_{3}}{2} \quad \tau_{1 / 3}=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}{2}\) (3–16)

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}{2}=\frac{70-0}{2}=35 kpsi\)

 

From Eq. (5–3),

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}{2}=\frac{S_{y}}{2 n}\) (5–3)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y} / 2}{\tau_{\max }}=\frac{100 / 2}{35}=1.43\)

 

(b) From Eq. (3–13), the nonzero principal stresses are

 

\(\sigma_{1}, \sigma_{2}=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (3–13)

 

\(\sigma_{A}, \sigma_{B}=\frac{60+40}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{60-40}{2}\right)^{2}+(-15)^{2}}=68.0,32.0 kpsi\)

 

The ordered principal stresses are \(\sigma_{A}=\sigma_{1}=68.0, \sigma_{B}=\sigma_{2}=32.0, \sigma_{3}=0 kpsi\)

 

DE \(\sigma^{\prime}=\left[68^{2}-68(32)+32^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}=59.0 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{59.0}=1.70\)

 

MSS Noting that the two nonzero principal stresses are both positive, \(\tau_{\max }\) will be from the largest Mohr’s circle which will incorporate the third principle stress at zero.From Eq. (3–16),

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}{2}=\frac{68.0-0}{2}=34.0 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y} / 2}{\tau_{\max }}=\frac{100 / 2}{34.0}=1.47\)

 

(c) This time, we shall obtain the factors of safety directly from the xy components
of stress.
DE From Eq. (5–15),

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left(\sigma_{x}^{2}-\sigma_{x} \sigma_{y}+\sigma_{y}^{2}+3 \tau_{x y}^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}\) (5–15)

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left(\sigma_{x}^{2}-\sigma_{x} \sigma_{y}+\sigma_{y}^{2}+3 \tau_{x y}^{2}\right)^{1 / 2}=\left[\left(40^{2}+3(45)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}=87.6 kpsi \right.\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{87.6}=1.14\)

 

MSS Taking care to note from a quick sketch of Mohr’s circle that one nonzero principal stress will be positive while the other one will be negative,\(\tau_{\text {max }}\) can be obtained from the extreme-value shear stress given by Eq. (3–14) without finding the
principal stresses.

 

\(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}=\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (3–14)

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x y}^{2}}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{0-40}{2}\right)^{2}+45^{2}}=49.2 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y} / 2}{\tau_{\max }}=\frac{100 / 2}{49.2}=1.02\)

 

For graphical comparison purposes later in this problem, the nonzero principal stresses can be obtained from Eq. (3–13) to be 69.2 kpsi and –29.2 kpsi.
(d) From Eq. (3–13), the nonzero principal stresses are

 

\(\sigma_{A}, \sigma_{B}=\frac{-40+(-60)}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{-40-(-60)}{2}\right)^{2}+(15)^{2}}=-32.0,-68.0 kpsi\)

 

The ordered principal stresses are \(\sigma_{1}=0, \sigma_{A}=\sigma_{2}=-32.0, \sigma_{B}=\sigma_{3}=-68.0 kpsi\)

 

DE \(\sigma^{\prime}=\left[(-32)^{2}-(-32)(-68)+(-68)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}=59.0 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{59.0}=1.70\)

 

MSS From Eq. (3–16),

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}{2}=\frac{0-(-68.0)}{2}=34.0 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y} / 2}{\tau_{\max }}=\frac{100 / 2}{34.0}=1.47\)

 

(e) The ordered principal stresses are \(\sigma_{1}=30, \sigma_{2}=30, \sigma_{3}=30 kpsi\)
DE From Eq. (5–12),

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left[\frac{\left(\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{2}\right)^{2}+\left(\sigma_{2}-\sigma_{3}\right)^{2}+\left(\sigma_{3}-\sigma_{1}\right)^{2}}{2}\right]^{1 / 2}\) (5–12)

 

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\left[\frac{(30-30)^{2}+(30-30)^{2}+(30-30)^{2}}{2}\right]^{1 / 2}=0 kpsi\)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma^{\prime}}=\frac{100}{0} \rightarrow \infty\)

 

MSS From Eq. (5–3),

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{S_{y}}{2 n} \quad \text { or } \quad \sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}=\frac{S_{y}}{n}\) (5–3)

 

\(n=\frac{S_{y}}{\sigma_{1}-\sigma_{3}}=\frac{100}{30-30} \rightarrow \infty\)

 

A tabular summary of the factors of safety is included for comparisons.

 

(e) (d) (c) (b) (a)
∞ 170 1.14 1.7 1.43 DE
∞ 1.47 1.02 1.47 1.43 MSS

 

Since the MSS theory is on or within the boundary of the DE theory, it will always predict a factor of safety equal to or less than the DE theory, as can be seen in the table. For each case, except case (e), the coordinates and load lines in the \(\sigma_{A}, \sigma_{B}\) plane are shown in Fig. 5–11. Case (e) is not plane stress. Note that the load line for case (a) is the only plane stress case given in which the two theories agree, thus giving the same factor of safety.
