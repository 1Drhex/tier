Guest Question.

A turbojet-powered trainer aircraft weighs 5000 Ib and is flying at h = 25,000 ft with 1000 Ib of fuel onboard. Its drag polar is \(C_{D}=0.018+0.095 C_{L}^{2}\), its wing area is 180 \(ft ^{2}\) , and the TSFC of its engines is 1.0/h at sea level. What is its maximum range and endurance to tanks dry at this altitude, and at what speed should the pilot initially fly to achieve each?

Admin
Solution

Maximum endurance is achieved at the speed for \( (L / D)_{\max }\). This speed can be determined by first calculating the required value of \(C_{L}\), then solving for the speed required to achieve \(L=W \text { at that } C_{L}:\)

\(C_{L}=\sqrt{\frac{C_{D_{0}}}{k}}=\sqrt{\frac{0.018}{0.095}}=0.435\)

 

\(L=W=C_{L} q S, \quad q=\frac{W}{C_{L} S}=\frac{5000 lb }{0.435\left(180 ft ^{2}\right)}=63.86 lb / ft ^{2}\)

 

Using \(\rho=0.001066 \text { at } h=25,000 ft\) obtained from the standard atmosphere table and the definition of q,

\(V_{\infty}=\sqrt{\frac{2 q}{\rho}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\left(63.86 lb / ft ^{2}\right)}{0.001066 slug / ft ^{3}}}=346.1 ft / s\)

for maximum endurance. Note that this is only the initial velocity for maximum endurance, and that as fuel is burned the velocity for best endurance will decrease. To calculate the maximum endurance time, it is first necessary to determine the magnitude of \((L / D)_{\max }\) Eq. (5.23):

\(\left(\frac{L}{D}\right)_{\max }=\left(\frac{C_{L}}{C_{D}}\right)_{\max }=\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{k C_{D_{0}}}}=\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{0.095(0.018)}}=12.1\)

The TSFC is also predicted using Eq. (5.16) with a = 1016.1 at h = 25,000 ft and \(a_{\text {sea level }}=1116.1 ft / s\) obtained from the standard atmosphere table

\(c_{t}=c_{t_{\text {sea level }}}\left(\frac{a}{a_{\text {sea level }}}\right)=1.0 / h \left(\frac{1016.1 ft / s }{1116.2 ft / s }\right)=0.91 / h\)

Then the endurance is calculated using Eq. (5.36) with \(W_{1}=5000 lb \text { and }\)

\(\begin{gathered}W_{2}=W_{1}-W_{f}=5000 lb -1000 lb =4000 lb \\E=\frac{1}{c_{t}} \frac{C_{L}}{C_{D}} \ln \left(\frac{W_{1}}{W_{2}}\right)=\frac{1}{0.91 / h }(12.1) \ln \left(\frac{5000 lb }{4000 lb }\right)=2.97 h\end{gathered}\)

Similarly, the velocity for maximum range is obtained by solving Eq. (5.39) for \(C_{L}\) and Eq. (4.3) for q:

\(\begin{gathered}C_{D_{0}}=3 k C_{L}^{2}, \quad C_{L}=\sqrt{\frac{C_{D_{0}}}{3 k}}=\sqrt{\frac{0.018}{3(0.095)}}=0.251 \\q=\frac{W}{C_{L} S}=\frac{5000 lb }{0.251\left(180 ft ^{2}\right)}=110.7 lb / ft ^{2} \\V_{\infty}=\sqrt{\frac{2 q}{\rho}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\left(110.7 lb / ft ^{2}\right)}{0.001066 slug / ft ^{3}}}=455.7 ft / s\end{gathered}\)

for maximum range. As with the velocity for maximum endurance, the velocity for best range will decrease as fuel is burned. The value calculated for \(C_{L}\) is now used to calculate \(C_{D}\) after which the maximum range is predicted using Eq. (5.38):

\(C_{D}=0.018+0.095 C_{L}^{2}=0.018+0.095(0.251)^{2}=0.024\)

 

\(R=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\rho S} \frac{2}{c_{t}}} \frac{C_{L}^{1 / 2}}{C_{D}}\left(W_{1}^{1 / 2}-W_{2}^{1 / 2}\right)\)

 

\(=\sqrt{\frac{2}{\left(0.001066 \operatorname{slug} / ft ^{3}\right)\left(180 ft ^{2}\right)}} \frac{2}{0.91 / h } \frac{(0.251)^{1 / 2}}{0.024}\left[(5000 lb )^{1 / 2}-(4000 lb )^{1 / 2}\right]\)

 

\(=\sqrt{10.4 \frac{ ft ^{2}}{ lb s ^{2}}}(2.197 h )(20.87)\left(7.465 lb ^{1 / 2}\right) /\left(1.69 \frac{ ft / s }{ kn }\right)=653.9 n mile\)

 

Note that the first term in Eq. (5.38) produces units of feet per second per squareroot pounds \(\left[ ft / s \cdot( lb )^{0.5}\right]\), whereas the second term has units of hours, and the desired answer is in nautical miles. It is necessary to divide by the factor 1.69 ft/s/kn to resolve this.
