Guest Question.

A catalyst particle is suspended in a gas stream of mixture A and B that is maintained at constant concentration \( c_{A, \infty}\). A radial cross section of the particle is provided in Figure. The core of the particle is nonporous and is coated with a catalyst. A highly porous layer with void fraction \( (\varepsilon) \) of 0.60 surrounds the catalyst coating. The catalyst coating catalyzes the heterogeneous reaction \( A \stackrel{k_{s}}{\longrightarrow} B \), where \( k_{s} \) is the surface reaction rate constant. Consider both a spherical and cylindrical particle, where \( r = R_{1} \) represents the radial position of the catalyst coating and \( r = R_{2} \) represents the outer radius of the porous layer. The length of the cylindrical particle is L. Reactant A in the surrounding fluid diffuses through the porous layer to the catalyst surface. At the catalyst surface of the particle, \( r = R_{1} \), the concentration of species A is \( C_{A s} \), and the reaction rate of species A at the catalyst surface is described by \( \left.N_{A} \right|_{r = R_{1}} = - k_{s} c_{A s}, \) with the negative sign indicating that the flux of A is opposite to the direction of increasing r.

Admin
Solution

Develop equations to predict the flux of species A at the catalyst surface ( \( r = R_{1} \) ), and then compare the flux for a reaction carried out at \( 300^{\circ} C \) and 1.0 atm with \( k_{s} = 2.0 cm / s , D_{A B} = 0.30 cm ^{2} / s \) and 50 mole% A maintained in the gas phase. The system control volume for mass transfer is the porous layer of the particle from \( r = R_{1} \) to \( r = R_{2} \) . The source for mass transfer of A is the surrounding fluid, and the sink for A is the reaction of A to product B at the catalyst surface. The assumptions common to both the spherical particle and the cylindrical particle are (1) constant source and sink for species A, leading to a steady-state process with \( \partial c_{A} / \partial t = 0 \), (2) one-dimensional flux of species A and B along coordinate r, (3) no homogeneous reaction of species A or B within the noncatalytic porous layer \( \left(R_{A} = 0\right) \), (4) the effective diffusion coefficient in the highly porous layer approximates \( D_{A e} \doteq \varepsilon^{2} D_{A B} \) where \( \varepsilon \) is the porosity of the porous layer, and (5) constant temperature (T) and total system pressure (P). In order to simplify the differential equation for mass transfer, we will use a spherical coordinate system for the spherical particle and a cylindrical coordinate system for the cylindrical particle. Based on assumptions 1-3 above, appropriate relationships, based on simplification of equations ( \( \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial t} = D_{A B} \left[ \frac{1}{r^{2} } \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left(r^{2} \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial r}\right) + \frac{1}{r^{2} \sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\sin \theta \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial \theta}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} \sin ^{2} \theta} \frac{\partial^{2} c_{A}}{\partial \phi^{2}}\right]\) ) and (\( \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial t}=\left[\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r N_{A, r}\right)+\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial N_{A, \theta}}{\partial \theta}+\frac{\partial N_{A, z}}{\partial z}\right]=R_{A} \)) are

 

\( sphere: \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} N_{A, r}\right) = 0 \quad\left(r^{2} N_{A, r} \text { constant along } r\right)\)

 

\( cylinder: \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r N_{A, r}\right) = 0 \quad\left(r N_{A, r} \text { constant along } r\right) \)

 

Equations ( \( \frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} N_{A, r}\right)=0 \quad\left(r^{2} N_{A, r} \text { constant along } r\right)\) ) and ( \( \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r N_{A, r}\right)=0\quad\left(r N_{A, r} \text { constant along } r\right) \) ) can also be obtained by a material balance on a differential volume element of the control volume, a “shell” bounded by radial position r and \( r + \Delta r \). For a sphere,

 

\( \left.4 \pi r^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r}(I N)-\left.4 \pi r^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r+\Delta r}(O U T)+0(G E N) = 0(A C C U M U L A T I O N)\)

 

dividing by \( 4 \pi^{2} \Delta r \), and rearranging yields

 

\( – \left.\left(\frac{\left.r^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r+\Delta r} – \left.r^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r}}{\Delta r}\right)\right|_{\lim \Delta r \rightarrow 0} = 0 \)

 

Finally, taking the limit as \( \Delta r \rightarrow 0 \) yields and rearrangement yields the differential equation for mass transfer of the spherical particle. Likewise, for a cylindrical partile,

 

\( \left.2 \pi r L N_{A, r}\right|_{r}(I N) – \left.2 \pi r L N_{A, r}\right|_{r+\Delta r}(O U T) + 0 (G E N) = 0 (A C C U M U L A T I O N)\)

 

dividing by \( 2 \pi L \Delta r \) and taking the limit as \( \Delta r \rightarrow 0 \) yields its differential equation for mass transfer.
The above equations show that the flux \( N_{A, r} \) is not constant along radial position r. However, the total mass-transfer rate \( W_{A} \), which is the product of flux and cross-sectional area for flux, is constant along r by continuity. Specifically, for a sphere,

\( W_{A} = 4 \pi r^{2} N_{A, r} = \left.4 \pi R_{1}^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r=R_{1}}=\left.4 \pi R_{2}^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r=R_{2}} \)

 

Likewise, for a cylinder,

\( W_{A} = 2 \pi r L N_{A, r} = \left.2 \pi R_{1} L N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{1}} = \left.2 \pi R_{2} L N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{2}} \)

 

In comparison, for equation ( \( \frac{d N_{A, z}}{d z} = 0 \) ), in rectilinear coordinates, \( N_{A, z} \) is constant along z, since the cross-sectional area for flux does not vary with position. Due to the reaction stoichiometry, note that the flux of B in the positive r-direction is equal to the flux in of A in the negative r-direction, so that \( N_{B, r} = – N_{A, r} \). Consequently, the flux equation in the r-direction is

 

\( N_{A, r} = – D_{A e} \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial r}+\frac{c_{A}}{c}\left(N_{A, r}+N_{B, r}\right) = – D_{A e} \frac{\partial c_{A}}{\partial r}=-D_{A e} \frac{d c_{A}}{d r} \)

 

The partial derivative \( \partial / \partial r \) is replaced within an ordinary derivative d/dr, since there is only one independent variable, r. Finally, two boundary conditions must be given for species A to completely specify the differential process model:

\( at r = R_{1} (0.3 cm ), \quad c_{A} = c_{A s} \)

 

\( at r = R_{2} (0.5 cm ), \quad c_{A} = c_{A, \infty} \)

 

Given the forms of the general differential equation for mass transfer, the differential flux equation can be integrated directly without the need to get an integral model for the concentration profile first. For the spherical catalyst particle,

 

\( W_{A} = \left.4 \pi R_{1}^{2} N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{1}} = 4 \pi r^{2} N_{A, r} = – 4 \pi r^{2} D_{A e} \frac{d c_{A}}{d r} \)

 

Separation of dependent variable \( C_{A} \) from independent variable r, and insertion of the boundary conditions as the limits of integration, yield

 

\( W_{A} \int_{R_{1}}^{R_{2}} \frac{1}{r^{2}} d r = – 4 \pi D_{A e} \int_{c_{A S}}^{c_{A, \infty}} d c_{A} \)

 

The final integral is

\( W_{A} = \frac{4 \pi D_{A e}}{1 / R_{2} – 1 / R_{1}}\left(c_{A, \infty}-c_{A s}\right) \)

 

or

\( \left.N_{A, r}\right|_{r=R_{1}} = \frac{D_{A e}\left(c_{A, \infty} – c_{A s}\right)}{R_{1}^{2}\left(1 / R_{2} – 1 / R_{1}\right)} \)

 

Likewise, for the cylindrical catalyst particle,

 

\( W_{A} = \left.2 \pi R_{2} L N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{1}} = 2 \pi r L N_{A, r} = – 2 \pi r L D_{A e} \frac{d c_{A}}{d r} \)

 

\( W_{A} \int_{R_{1}}^{R_{2}} \frac{1}{r} d r=-2 \pi L D_{A e} \int_{c_{A S}}^{c_{A_{\infty}}} d c_{A} \)

 

\( W_{A} = \frac{2 \pi L D_{A e}}{\ln \left(R_{1} / R_{2}\right)}\left(c_{A, \infty}-c_{A s}\right) \)

 

or
\( \left.N_{A}\right|_{r = R_{1}}=\frac{D_{A e}}{R_{1} \ln \left(R_{1} / R_{2}\right)}\left(c_{A, \infty} – c_{A s}\right) \)

 

At this point, the flux cannot be calculated, since \( C_{A s} \) is not known. However, since

 

\( c_{A s} = \frac{- \left.N_{A}\right|_{r = R_{1}}}{k_{s}} \)

 

it can be readily shown that can be \( C_{A s} \) eliminated. For the spherical particle,

 

\( \left.N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{1}} = \frac{- c_{A, \infty}}{\left(\frac{R_{1}^{2}\left(1 / R_{1}-1 / R_{2}\right)}{D_{A e}}+\frac{1}{k_{s}}\right)} \)

 

And for the cylindrical particle,

 

\( \left.N_{A}\right|_{r=R_{1}}=\frac{-c_{A_{\infty}}}{\left(\frac{R_{1} \ln \left(R_{2} / R_{1}\right)}{D_{A e}}+\frac{1}{k_{s}}\right)} \)

 

At the conditions of the process,

 

\( c_{A, \infty} = y_{A, \infty} c = \frac{y_{A, \infty} P}{R T} = \frac{(0.50)(1.0 atm )}{\left(\frac{82.06 cm ^{3} \cdot atm }{ gmole \cdot K }\right)(300+273) K }=1.06 \times 10^{-5} gmole / cm ^{3} \)

 

From Figure, for a random porous material where Knudsen diffusion effects are not important,

 

\( D_{A e} \doteq \varepsilon^{2} D_{A B} = (0.60)^{2}\left(0.30 cm ^{2} / s \right) = 0.108 cm ^{2} / s \)

 

The flux of A to the spherical particle at \( r = R_{1} \) is

 

\( \left.N_{A, r}\right|_{r=R_{1}}=\frac{- \left(1.06 \times 10^{-5} gmole / cm ^{3}\right)}{\left(\frac{(0.3 cm )^{2}(1 / 0.3 cm -1 / 0.5 cm )}{\left(0.108 cm ^{2} / s \right)}+\frac{1}{2.0 cm / s }\right)} = -6.60 \times 10^{-6} gmole / cm ^{2} \cdot s \)

 

with mole fraction of A at the catalytic surface, \( y_{A s} = \frac{- \left.N_{A, r}\right|_{r = R_{1}}}{k_{s} c} = 0.155 \).

The (–) indicates the direction of flux is opposite increasing radial position r. For comparison, the flux of A to the cylindrical particle at \( r = R_{1} \) is

 

\( \left.N_{A}\right|_{r = R_{1}}=\frac{- \left(1.06 \times 10^{-5} gmole / cm ^{3}\right)}{\left(\frac{(0.3 cm ) \ln (0.5 cm / 0.3 cm )}{0.108 cm ^{2} / s }+\frac{1}{2.0 cm / s }\right)} = – 6.78 \times 10^{-6} gmole / cm ^{2} \cdot s \)

 

with \( y_{A s} = 0.159 \)
It is interesting to note that as \( k_{s} \) increases, \( c_{A s} \) moves toward zero, which is the diffusion-limited condition.
