Guest Question.

Determine the slopes and deflections at points B and C of the cantilever beam shown in Fig. \( 6.5 (\mathrm{a})\) by the moment-area method.

Admin
Solution

Bending Moment Diagram. The bending moment diagram for the beam is shown in Fig. 6.5(b).

\( \boldsymbol{ M / E I } \) Diagram. As indicated in Fig. \(6.5\) (a), the values of the moment of inertia of the segments \(A B\) and \(B C\) of the beam are 6,000 in. \({ }^{4}\) and 3,000 in. \({ }^{4}\), respectively. Using \(I=I_{B C}=3,000\) in. \({ }^{4}\) as the reference moment of inertia, we express \(I_{A B}\) in terms of \(I\) as

\( I_{A B}=6,000=2(3,000)=2 I \)

which indicates that in order to obtain the \(M / E I\) diagram in terms of \(E I\), we must divide the bending moment diagram for segment \(A B\) by 2 , as shown in Fig. \(6.5\) (c).

Elastic Curve. The elastic curve for the beam is shown in Fig. \(6.5\) (d). Note that because the \(M / E I\) diagram is negative, the beam bends concave downward. Since the support at \(A\) is fixed, the slope at \(A\) is zero \(\left(\theta_{A}=0\right)\); that is, the tangent to the elastic curve at \(A\) is horizontal, as shown in the figure.

Slope at \( \boldsymbol{B} \). With the slope at \(A\) known, we can determine the slope at \(B\) by evaluating the change in slope \(\theta_{B A}\) between \(A\) and \(B\) (which is the angle between the tangents to the elastic curve at points \(A\) and \(B\), as shown in Fig. \(6.5\) (d)). According to the first moment-area theorem, \(\theta_{B A}=\) area of the \(M / E I\) diagram between \(A\) and \(B\). This area can be conveniently evaluated by dividing the \(M / E I\) diagram into triangular and rectangular parts, as shown in Fig. \(6.5(\mathrm{c}) .\) Thus,

\( \theta_{B A}=\frac{1}{E I}\left[(100)(15)+\frac{1}{2}(150)(15)\right]=\frac{2,625 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{2}}{E I} \)

From Fig. 6.5(d), we can see that because the tangent at \(A\) is horizontal (in the direction of the undeformed axis of the beam), the slope at \(B\left(\theta_{B}\right)\) is equal to the angle \(\theta_{B A}\) between the tangents at \(A\) and \(B\); that is,

\( \theta_{B}=\theta_{B A}=\frac{2,625 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{2}}{E I}=\frac{2,625(12)^{2} \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{in} .^{2}}{E I} \)

Substituting the numerical values of \(E=29,000 \mathrm{ksi}\) and \(I=3,000 \mathrm{in} .{ }^{4}\), we obtain

\( \begin{array}{l} \theta_{B}=\frac{2,625(12)^{2}}{(29,000)(3,000)} \mathrm{rad}=0.0043 \mathrm{rad} \\ \end{array} \)

\( \begin{array}{l} \theta_{B}=0.0043 \mathrm{rad} \end{array} \)

Deflection at \( \boldsymbol{B} \). From Fig. \(6.5(\mathrm{~d})\), it can be seen that the deflection of \(B\) with respect to the undeformed axis of the beam is equal to the tangential deviation of \(B\) from the tangent at \(A\); that is,

\( \Delta_{B}=\Delta_{B A} \)

According to the second moment-area theorem,

\( \begin{aligned} \Delta_{B A} &=\text { moment of the area of the } M / E I \text { diagram between } A \text { and } B \text { about } B \\ &=\frac{1}{E I}\left[(100)(15)(7.5)+\frac{1}{2}(150)(15)(10)\right]=\frac{22,500 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{3}}{E I} \end{aligned} \)

Therefore,

\( \begin{aligned} \Delta_{B} &=\Delta_{B A}=\frac{22,500 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{3}}{E I} \\ &=\frac{22,500(12)^{3}}{(29,000)(3,000)}=0.45 \mathrm{in} \\ \Delta_{B} &=0.45 \mathrm{in} . \downarrow \end{aligned} \)

Slope at \( \boldsymbol{C} \). From Fig. \(6.5(\mathrm{~d})\), we can see that

\( \theta_{C}=\theta_{C A} \)

where

\( \begin{aligned} \theta_{C A} &=\text { area of the } M / E I \text { diagram between } A \text { and } C \\ &=\frac{1}{E I}\left[(100)(15)+\frac{1}{2}(150)(15)+\frac{1}{2}(200)(10)\right]=\frac{3,625 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{2}}{E I} \end{aligned} \)

Therefore,

\( \begin{aligned} \theta_{C} &=\theta_{C A}=\frac{3,625 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{2}}{E I} \\ &=\frac{3,625(12)^{2}}{(29,000)(3,000)}=0.006 \mathrm{rad} \\ \end{aligned} \)

\( \begin{aligned} \theta_{C} &=0.006 \mathrm{rad} \end{aligned} \)

Deflection at \( \boldsymbol{C} \). It can be seen from Fig. \(6.5\) (d) that

\( \Delta_{C}=\Delta_{C A} \)

Where

\( \begin{aligned} \Delta_{C A} &=\text { moment of the area of the } M / E I \text { diagram between } A \text { and } C \text { about } C \\ &=\frac{1}{E I}\left[(100)(15)(7.5+10)+\frac{1}{2}(150)(15)(10+10)+\frac{1}{2}(200)(10)(6.67)\right] \\ &=\frac{55,420 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{3}}{E I} \end{aligned} \)

Therefore,

\( \begin{aligned} \Delta_{C} &=\Delta_{C A}=\frac{55,420 \mathrm{k}-\mathrm{ft}^{3}}{E I} \\ &=\frac{55,420(12)^{3}}{(29,000)(3,000)}=1.1 \mathrm{in} \\ \Delta_{C} &=1.1 \text { in. } \downarrow \end{aligned} \)
