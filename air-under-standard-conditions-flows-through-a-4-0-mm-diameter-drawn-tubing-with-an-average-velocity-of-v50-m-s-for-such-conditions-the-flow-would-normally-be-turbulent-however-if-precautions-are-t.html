Guest Question.

Air under standard conditions flows through a 4.0-mm-diameter drawn tubing with an average velocity of V = 50 \({m}/{s} \). For such conditions the flow would normally be turbulent. However, if precautions are taken to eliminate disturbances to the flow (the entrance to the tube is very smooth, the air is dust-free, the tube does not vibrate, etc.), it may be possible to maintain laminar flow.

FIND (a) Determine the pressure drop in a 0.1-m section of the tube if the flow is laminar.
(b) Repeat the calculations if the flow is turbulent.

Admin
Solution

Under standard temperature and pressure conditions the density and viscosity are \( \rho =1.23{kg}/{m^{3}}\) and \( \mu =1.79\times 10^{-5}{N\cdot s}/{m^{2}}\). Thus, the Reynolds number is

\( Re=\frac{\rho VD}{\mu }=\frac{(1.23{kg}/{m^{3}})(50{m}/{s})(0.004 m)}{1.79\times 10^{-5}{N\cdot s}/{m^{2}}}=13,700 \)

which would normally indicate turbulent flow.

(a) If the flow were laminar, then f = 64/Re = 64/13,700 = 0.00467 and the pressure drop in a 0.1-m-long horizontal section of the pipe would be

\( \Delta p=f\frac{l}{D}\frac{1}{2}\rho V^{2}=(0.00467)\frac{(0.1 m)}{(0.004 m)}\frac{1}{2}(1.23{kg}/{m^{3}})(50{m}/{s})^{2} \)

or

\(\Delta p\)=0.179 KPa

COMMENT Note that the same result is obtained from \(V=\frac{\pi R^{2}V_{c}}{2\pi R^{2}} =\frac{V_{c}}{2}=\frac{\Delta pD^{2}}{32\mu l} \).

\( \Delta p=\frac{32\mu l}{D^{2}}V=\frac{32(1.79\times 10^{-5}{N\cdot s}/{m^{2}})(0.1 m)(50{m}/{s})}{(0.004 m)^{2}} =179{N}/{m^{2}}\)

(b) If the flow were turbulent, then \( f=\phi (Re,{\epsilon }/{D})\) , where from Table 8.1, \(\epsilon =0.0015mm\) so that \( {\epsilon }/{D}={0.0015 mm}/{4.0 mm}=0.000375a\). From the Moody chart with \( Re=1.37\times 10^{4}\) and \( {\epsilon }/{D}= 0.000375 \) we obtain f = 0.028. Thus, the pressure drop in this case would be approximately

\(\Delta p=f\frac{l}{D}\frac{1}{2}\rho V^{2} =(0.028)\frac{(0.1 m)}{(0.004 m)}\frac{1}{2}(1.23 {kg}/{m^{3}}) (50{m}/{s})^{2}\)

or

\(\Delta p=1.076kPa\)

COMMENTS A considerable savings in effort to force the fluid through the pipe could be realized (0.179 kPa rather than 1.076 kPa) if the flow could be maintained as laminar flow at this Reynolds number. In general this is very difficult to do, although laminar flow in pipes has been maintained up to \( Re\approx 100,000\) in rare instances.

An alternate method to determine the friction factor for the turbulent flow would be to use the Colebrook formula, \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{f} } =-2.0\log (\frac{{\epsilon }/{D}}{3.7} +\frac{2.51}{Re\sqrt{f} } )\), Thus,

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{f} }=-2.0\log (\frac{{\epsilon }/{D}}{3.7}+\frac{2.51}{Re\sqrt{f} } ) =-2.0\log (\frac{0.000375}{3.7}+\frac{2.51}{1.37\times 10^{4}\sqrt{f} } )\)

or

\( \frac{1}{\sqrt{f} }=-2.0\log (1.01 \times 10^{-4}+\frac{1.83\times 10^{-4}}{\sqrt{f} } )\) (1)

By using a root-finding technique on a computer or calculator, the solution to Eq. 1 is determined to be f=0.0291 , in agreement (within the accuracy of reading the graph) with the Moody chart method of f = 0.028. Alternatively, the Haaland equation, \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{f} }=-1.8\log [(\frac{{\epsilon }/{D}}{3.7} )^{1.11}+\frac{6.9}{Re} \), could be used (without iteration) to obtain f = 0.0289.

Numerous other empirical formulas can be found in the literature (Ref. 4) for portions of the Moody chart. For example, an often-used equation, commonly referred to as the Blasius formula, for turbulent flow in smooth pipes (\({\epsilon }/{D}=0 \)) with \( Re\lt 10^{5}\) is

\(f=\frac{0.316}{Re^{{1}/{4}}} \)

For our case this gives

\(f=0.316(13,700)^{-0.25}=0.0292 \)

which is in agreement with the previous results. Note that the value of f is relatively insensitive to \({\epsilon }/{D}\) for this particular situation. Whether the tube was smooth glass (\({\epsilon }/{D}\)=0) or the drawn tubing (\({\epsilon }/{D}\)= 0.000375) would not make much difference in the pressure drop. For this flow, an increase in relative roughness by a factor of 30 to \({\epsilon }/{D}\)= 0.0113 (equivalent to a commercial steel surface; see Table 8.1) would give f = 0.043. This would represent an increase in pressure drop and head loss by a factor of 0.043/0.0291 = 1.48 compared with that for the original drawn tubing.
 The pressure drop of 1.076 kPa in a length of 0.1 m of pipe corresponds to a change in absolute pressure [assuming p = 101 kPa (abs) at x = 0] of approximately 1.076/101 = 0.0107, or about 1% Thus, the incompressible flow assumption on which the aforementioned calculations (and all of the formulas in this chapter) are based is reasonable. However, if the pipe were 2 m long the pressure drop would be 21.5 kPa, approximatel 20% of the original pressure. In this case the density would not be approximately constant along the pipe, and a compressible flow analysis would be needed.
