Guest Question.

EXPLORING THE BOHR MODEL

Find the kinetic, potential, and total energies of the hydrogen atom in the first excited level, and find the wavelength of the photon emitted in a transition from that level to the ground level.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

This problem uses the ideas of the Bohr model. We use simplified versions of Eqs. (39.12), (39.13), and (39.14) to find the energies of the atom, and Eq. (39.16), \(hc/λ = E_{nU} – E_{nL}\), to find the photon wavelength λ in a transition from \(n_U\) = 2 (the first excited level) to \(n_L\) = 1 (the ground level).

\(K_{n}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{n}^{2}=\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}^{2}} \frac{m e^{4}}{8 n^{2} h^{2}}\) (39.12)

\(U_{n}=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{e^{2}}{r_{n}}=-\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}^{2}} \frac{m e^{4}}{4 n^{2} h^{2}}\) (39.13)

\(E_{n}=K_{n}+U_{n}=-\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}^{2}} \frac{m e^{4}}{8 n^{2} h^{2}}\) (39.14)

\(E_{n}=-\frac{h c R}{n^{2}}, \text { where } R=\frac{m e^{4}}{8 \epsilon_{0}^{2} h^{3} c}\) (39.15)

\(\frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(\frac{1}{n_{\mathrm{L}}^{2}}-\frac{1}{n_{\mathrm{U}}^{2}}\right)\) (39.16)

EXECUTE:

We could evaluate Eqs. (39.12), (39.13), and (39.14) for the \(n\)th level by substituting the values of \(m, e, \epsilon_{0}\), and \(h\). But we can simplify the calculation by comparing with Eq. (39.15), which shows that the constant \(m e^{4} / 8 \epsilon_{0}^{2} h^{2}\) that appears in Eqs. (39.12), (39.13), and (39.14) is equal to \(hcR\):

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{m e^{4}}{8 \epsilon_{0}^{2} h^{2}}=& h c R \\=&\left(6.626 \times10^{-34} \mathrm{~J} \cdot \mathrm{s}\right)\left(2.998 \times 10^{8} \mathrm{~m} /\mathrm{s}\right) \\& \times\left(1.097 \times 10^{7} \mathrm{~m}^{-1}\right) \\=& 2.179\times 10^{-18} \mathrm{~J}=13.60 \mathrm{eV}\end{aligned}\)

This allows us to rewrite Eqs. (39.12), (39.13), and (39.14) as

\(K_{n}=\frac{13.60 \mathrm{eV}}{n^{2}} \quad U_{n}=\frac{-27.20 \mathrm{eV}}{n^{2}} \quad E_{n}=\frac{-13.60 \mathrm{eV}}{n^{2}}\)

For the first excited level (\(n = 2\)), we have \(K_2\) = 3.40 eV, \(U_2\) = -6.80 eV, and E2 = -3.40 eV. For the ground level (\(n = 1\)),
\(E_1\) = -13.60 eV. The energy of the emitted photon is then
\(E_2 – E_1 = -3.40\) eV – (-13.60 eV) = 10.20 eV, and

\(\begin{aligned}\lambda &=\frac{h c}{E_{2}-E_{1}}=\frac{\left(4.136 \times 10^{-15}\mathrm{eV} \cdot \mathrm{s}\right)\left(3.00 \times 10^{8} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\right)}{10.20 \mathrm{eV}} \\&=1.22 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{~m}=122 \mathrm{~nm}\end{aligned}\)

This is the wavelength of the Lyman-alpha (\(\left.\mathbf{L}_{\alpha}\right)\)) line, the longest-wavelength line in the Lyman series of ultraviolet lines in the hydrogen spectrum (see Fig. 39.24).

 

EVALUATE: The total mechanical energy for any level is negative and is equal to one-half the potential energy. We found the same energy relationship for Newtonian satellite orbits in Section 13.4.
The situations are similar because both the electrostatic and gravitational forces are inversely proportional to \(1/r^2\).
