Guest Question.

A TO 71 transistor with a height of 0.53 cm and a diameter of 0.44 cm is mounted on a circuit board, as shown in Fig. 15-52. The transistor is cooled by air flowing over it at a velocity of 90 m / min. If the air temperature is \( 65^{\circ} C\) and the transistor case temperature is not to exceed \( 95^{\circ} C\), determine the amount of power this transistor can dissipate safely.

 

 

Admin
Solution

SOLUTION A transistor mounted on a circuit board is cooled by air flowing over it. The power dissipated when its case temperature is \(90^{\circ} C\) is to be determined.

Assumptions 1 Steady operating conditions exist. 2 Air is an ideal gas. 3 Operation is at sea level and thus the atmospheric pressure is 1 atm.

Properties The properties of air at 1 atm pressure and the film temperature of

\( T_{f}=\left(T_{s}+T_{\text {fluid }}\right) / 2=(95+65) / 2=80^{\circ} C\) are

\( \rho =0.9994 kg / m ^{3} \)

 

\( c_{p} =1008 J / kg \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \)

 

\( Pr =0.7154\)

 

\( k=0.02953 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \)

 

\( \nu=2.097 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s\)

 

Analysis The transistor is cooled by forced convection through its cylindrical surface as well as its flat top and bottom surfaces. The characteristic length for flow over a cylinder is the diameter D=0.0044 m. Then the Reynolds number becomes

\( Re =\frac{V D}{\nu}=\frac{(90 / 60 m / s )(0.0044 m )}{2.097 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s }=315\)

which falls into the range 40 to 4000 . Using the corresponding relation from Table (15-2) for the Nusselt number, we obtain

\( Nu =0.683 Re ^{0.466} Pr ^{1 / 3}=0.683(315)^{0.466}(0.7154)^{1 / 3}=8.91\)

and

\( h=\frac{k}{D} Nu =\frac{0.02953 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C }{0.0044 m }(8.91)=59.8 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C\)

Also,

\( A_{\text {cyl }}=\pi D L=\pi(0.0044 m )(0.0053 m )=0.7326 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2}\)

Then the rate of heat transfer from the cylindrical surface becomes

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {cy1 }} =h A_{ cyl }\left(T_{s}-T_{\text {fuid }}\right) \)

 

\( =\left(59.8 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(0.7326 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2}\right)(95-65)^{\circ} C =0.131 W\)

 

We now repeat the calculations for the top and bottom surfaces of the transistor, which can be treated as flat plates of length L=0.0044 m in the flow direction (which is the diameter), and, using the proper relation from Table (15-2),

\( Re =\frac{V L}{v}=\frac{(90 / 60 m / s )(0.0044 m )}{2.097 \times 10^{-5} m ^{2} / s }=315 \)

 

\( Nu =0.664 Re ^{1 / 2} Pr ^{1 / 3}=0.664(315)^{1 / 2}(0.7154)^{1 / 3}=10.5\)

 

and

\( h=\frac{k}{D} Nu =\frac{0.02953 W / m \cdot{ }^{\circ} C }{0.0044 m }(10.5)=70.7 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C\)

Also,

\( A_{\text {flat }}=A_{\text {top }}+A_{\text {bottom }}=2 \times \frac{1}{4} \pi D^{2}=2 \times \frac{1}{4} \pi(0.0044 m )^{2}=0.3041 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2} \)

 

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {flat }}=h A_{\text {flat }}\left(T_{s}-T_{\text {fluid }}\right) =\left(70.7 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(0.3041 \times 10^{-4} m ^{2}\right)(95-65)^{\circ} C \)

 

\( =0.065 W\)

 

Therefore, the total rate of heat that can be dissipated from all surfaces of the transistor is

\( \dot{Q}_{\text {total }}=\dot{Q}_{\text {cy1 }}+\dot{Q}_{\text {flat }}=(0.131+0.065) W =0.196 W\)

which seems to be low. This value can be increased considerably by attaching a heat sink to the transistor to enhance the heat transfer surface area and thus heat transfer, or by increasing the air velocity, which will increase the heat transfer coefficient.
