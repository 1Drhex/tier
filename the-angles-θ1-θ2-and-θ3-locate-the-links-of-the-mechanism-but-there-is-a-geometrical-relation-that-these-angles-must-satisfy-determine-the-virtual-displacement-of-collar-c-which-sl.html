Guest Question.

The angles \(\theta_{1}, \theta_{2}\), and \(\theta_{3}\) locate the links of the mechanism, but there is a geometrical relation that these angles must satisfy. Determine the virtual displacement of collar \(C\), which slides along bar \(D E\) under each of the following conditions: (a) \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{2}\) are unconstrained generalized coordinates; (b) \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{3}\) are unconstrained generalized coordinates; and (c) \(\theta_{1}\) is driven by a motor such that it is a known function of time, and \(\theta_{2}\) is an unconstrained generalized coordinate; and \((\mathrm{d}) \theta_{3}\) is driven by a motor such that it is a known function of time, and \(\theta_{1}\) is an unconstrained generalized coordinate.

Admin
Solution

The purpose here is to illustrate the variety of conditions that can arise in a system and the significant effect that these conditions can have on the formulation of virtual displacement. In Part (a) we have \(q_{1}=\theta_{1}\) and \(q_{2}=\theta_{2} .\) These variables uniquely locate the collar, so the fact that \(\theta_{3}\) depends on the generalized coordinates is irrelevant to the evaluation of the virtual displacement of the collar. We follow the kinematical method by describing the velocity of the collar in terms of the generalized coordinates:

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{v}_{C} &=\bar{v}_{B}+\bar{\omega}_{B C} \times \bar{r}_{C / B}=\dot{\theta}_{1} \bar{k} \times \bar{r}_{B / A}+\dot{\theta}_{2} \bar{k} \times \bar{r}_{C / B} \\\\&=-\left(L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \bar{i}+\left(L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\right) \bar{j}.\end{aligned} (1) \)

We obtain the virtual displacement by replacing the rate variables with the corresponding virtual increments. It is useful to group the coefficients of \(\delta \theta_{1}\) and \(\delta \theta_{2}\), so we find

\(\begin{aligned}\delta \bar{r}_{C}=\left(-L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \sin \theta_{1} \bar{i}+L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \cos \theta_{1} \bar{j}\right) \delta \theta_{1}\\\\+\left(-L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \sin \theta_{2} \bar{i}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \theta_{2} \bar{j}\right) \delta \theta_{2}.\end{aligned} (2) \)

In Part (b) we have \(q_{1}=\theta_{1}\) and \(q_{2}=\theta_{3}\). We cannot describe the position of the collar \(C\) in terms of only the generalized coordinates without considering how \(\theta_{2}\) depends on \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{3}\). To that end we form the tangent of \(\theta_{3}\) in terms of horizontal and vertical distances constructed along bars \(A B\) and \(B C\). This gives

\(\tan \theta_{3}=\frac{L_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} \sin \theta_{2}}{L_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} \cos \theta_{2}-H},\)

which may be written as

\(\sin \theta_{2}=\tan \theta_{3} \cos \theta_{2}+\tan \theta_{3}\left(\frac{L_{1}}{L_{2}} \cos \theta_{1}-\frac{H}{L_{2}}\right)-\frac{L_{1}}{L_{2}} \sin \theta_{1}. (3)\)

We need to solve this relation for \(\theta_{2}\) in terms of \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{3}\), which we can do by using symbolic software or by using \(\sin \theta_{2} \equiv\left[1-\left(\cos \theta_{2}\right)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}\) to obtain a quadratic equation for \(\cos \theta_{2} .\) The specific solution is unimportant for the present purpose, so we merely denote it in functional form as

\(\theta_{2}=f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right). (4)\)

We proceed to the analysis of \(\bar{v}_{C}\), for which we recognize that we need to determine \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\) in terms of the generalized coordinates and velocities. We could obtain this result by differentiating Eq. (4), but the function is quite complicated. Instead we carry out a velocity analysis of the collar, based on its being constrained to move radially outward at an unknown rate \(u\) relative to bar \(D E .\) This means that \(\bar{v}_{C}=u \bar{e}_{E / D}+\bar{\omega}_{D E} \times \bar{r}_{C / D} .\) We describe \(\bar{r}_{C / D}\) in terms of \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{2}\) to avoid introducing the distance from pin \(D\) to the collar as another geometrical variable to eliminate. Comparing this viewpoint for \(\bar{v}_{C}\) with Eq. (1) gives

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{v}_{C}=& u\left(\cos \theta_{3} \bar{i}+\sin \theta_{3} \bar{j}\right)+\dot{\theta}_{3} \bar{k} \times\left[\left(L_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\right) \bar{i}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left(L_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \bar{j}\right] \\\\=&-\left(L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \sin \theta_{2}\right) \bar{i}+\left(L_{1} \dot{\theta}_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\right) \bar{j}.\end{aligned} \)

Matching like components in the preceding equation gives two simultaneous equations that may be solved for \(u\) and \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\) in terms of \(\theta_{1}, \theta_{2}, \theta_{3}, \dot{\theta}_{1}\), and \(\dot{\theta}_{3} .\) Equation (4) describes \(\theta_{2}\). Thus the result of this kinematical analysis has the form

\(\dot{\theta}_{2}=g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \dot{\theta}_{1}+g_{3}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \dot{\theta}_{3}. (5)\)

The detailed expressions are intricate, so we merely monitor how the analysis builds on them. Substitution of Eq. (5) into Eq. (1) gives \(\bar{v}_{C}\) in terms of the generalized velocities for this case:

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{v}_{C}=&\left\{-\left[L_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \sin \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{i}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left[L_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \cos \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{j}\right\} \dot{\theta}_{1} \\\\&+\left\{-L_{2} g_{3}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \sin \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right) \bar{i}+L_{2} g_{3}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \cos \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right) \bar{j}\right\} \dot{\theta}_{3}.\end{aligned} \)

To obtain the virtual displacement of the collar we replace the generalized velocities with the corresponding virtual increments, which gives

\(\begin{aligned}\delta \bar{r}_{C}=&\left\{-\left[L_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \sin \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{i}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left[L_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \cos \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{j}\right\} \delta \theta_{1} \\\\&+\left\{-L_{2} g_{3}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \sin \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right) \bar{i}+L_{2} g_{3}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \cos \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right) \bar{j}\right\} \delta \theta_{3} .\end{aligned} (6) \)

It is obvious that analyzing \(\delta \bar{r}_{C}\) with \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{3}\) as generalized coordinates is much more difficult than it is if \(\theta_{1}\) and \(\theta_{2}\) are the generalized coordinates. The same would be true for most other steps ultimately leading to the equations of motion. Thus it is wise to assess at the outset of a solution the difficulty of eliminating variables that are not generalized coordinates.

The situation in Part (c) is like that of Part (a), except that \(\theta_{1}\) is specified, rather than a generalized coordinate. Thus the terms in Eq. (1) that contain \(\dot{\theta}_{1}\) correspond to the \(\bar{v}_{P t}\) term in Eq. (7.3.8). In accord with the kinematical method, in Eq. (1) we replace \(\bar{v}_{C}\) with \(\delta \bar{r}_{C}\) and \(\dot{\theta}_{2}\) with \(\delta \theta_{2}\), and ignore the \(\dot{\theta}_{1}\) term. The resulting virtual displacement is

\(\begin{aligned} \bar{v}_{P} &=\sum_{j=1}^{N} \bar{v}_{P j}\left(q_{n}, t\right) \dot{q}_{j}+\bar{v}_{p t}\left(q_{n}, t\right), \\\\ \delta \bar{r}_{P} &=\sum_{j=1}^{N} \bar{v}_{P j}\left(q_{n}, t\right) \delta q_{j}, \\\\ \bar{v}_{P j} &=\frac{\partial \bar{r}_{P}}{\partial q_{j}}. \end{aligned} (7.3.8)\)

 

\(\delta \bar{r}_{C}=\left(-L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \sin \theta_{2} \bar{i}+L_{2} \dot{\theta}_{2} \cos \theta_{2} \bar{j}\right) \delta \theta_{2}.\)

This is the same as Eq. (2) when \(\delta \theta_{1}=0\), which is consistent with the definition of a virtual displacement as corresponding to fixed \(t\), so that \(\theta_{1}\) does not change.

Similar to the preceding case, the analysis for Part (d) may be performed by referring back to Part (b). Because \(\theta_{3}\) is not a generalized coordinate in this case, we set \(\delta \theta_{3}=0\) in Eq. (6), which reduces the virtual displacement to

\(\begin{aligned}\delta \bar{r}_{C}=&\left\{-\left[L_{1} \sin \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \sin \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{i}\right.\\\\&\left.+\left[L_{1} \cos \theta_{1}+L_{2} g_{1}\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right) \cos \left(f\left(\theta_{1}, \theta_{3}\right)\right)\right] \bar{j}\right\} \delta \theta_{1}.\end{aligned} \)

An overview of the cases considered here shows that eliminating constrained variables can lead to significant complications. An alternative not listed in the problem statement is to use all three angles as constrained generalized coordinates, rather than trying to eliminate one variable. In each case a constraint equation relating the angles, specifically Eq. (3) or its equivalent, would need to be enforced as a separate condition. Specification of the rotation of \(\theta_{1}\) or \(\theta_{3}\) as a function of time would lead to an additional constraint equation.
