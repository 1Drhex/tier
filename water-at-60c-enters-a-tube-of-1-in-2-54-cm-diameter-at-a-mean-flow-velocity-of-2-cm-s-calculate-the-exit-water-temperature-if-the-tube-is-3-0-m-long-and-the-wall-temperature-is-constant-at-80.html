Guest Question.

Water at \(60^{◦} C\) enters a tube of 1-in (2.54-cm) diameter at a mean flow velocity of 2 cm/s. Calculate the exit water temperature if the tube is 3.0 m long and the wall temperature is constant at \(80^{◦} C\).

Admin
Solution

We first evaluate the Reynolds number at the inlet bulk temperature to determine the flow regime. The properties of water at \(60^{◦} C\) are:

\(ρ= 985\; kg/m^{3} \enspace c_{p} =4.18 \; kJ/kg · ^{◦} C\)

 

\(μ = 4.71×10^{-4} kg/m· s\hspace{30 pt} [1.139 \> lb_{m} /h · ft]\)

 

\(k = 0.651\> W/m· ^{◦} C\qquad Pr=3.02\)

 

\(Re_{d}=\frac{ρu_{m} d}{μ} ==\frac{(985)(0.02)(0.0254)}{4.71×10^{-4} }=1062 \)

so the flow is laminar. Calculating the additional parameter, we have

\(Re_{d} \> Pr\frac{d}{L} = \frac{(1062)(3.02)(0.0254)}{3}=27.15>10 \)

so Equation (6-10)

\(\overline{Nu_{d} }=1.86(Re_{d}Pr)^{1/3} \left(\frac{d}{L} \right) ^{1/3} \left(\frac{μ}{μ_{w} } \right)^{0.14} \qquad [6-10] \)

is applicable. We do not yet know the mean bulk temperature to evaluate properties so we first make the calculation on the basis of 60◦C, determine an exit bulk temperature, and then make a second iteration to obtain a more precise value. When inlet and outlet conditions are designated with the subscripts 1 and 2, respectively, the energy balance becomes

\(q=hπdL=\left(T_{w}-\frac{T_{b1} +T_{b2} }{2} \right) =\dot{m}c_{p} (T_{b2}-T_{b1} ) \hspace{30 pt} [a]\)

At the wall temperature of \(80^{◦} C\) we have:

\( μ_{w} =3.55×10^{-4} kg/m· s \)

From Equation (6-10)

\(\overline{Nu_{d} }=1.86(Re_{d}Pr)^{1/3} \left(\frac{d}{L} \right) ^{1/3} \left(\frac{μ}{μ_{w} } \right)^{0.14} \qquad [6-10] \)

 

\( Nu_{d}=(1.86)\left[\frac{(1062)(3.02)(0.0254)}{3} \right]^{1/3}\left(\frac{4.71}{3.55} \right) ^{0.14} =5.816 \)

 

\( h=\frac{kNu_{d} }{d}=\frac{(0.651)(5.816)}{0.0254} =149.1 W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C \> [26.26 \; Btu/h · ft^{2} ·^{◦} F] \)

The mass flow rate is:

\( \dot{m}=ρ\frac{\pi d^{2} }{4} u_{m} =\frac{(985)π(0.0254)^{2} (0.02)}{4} =9.982×10^{-3 } kg/s \)

Inserting the value for h into Equation (a) along with ˙m and \(T_{b1}=60^{◦} C \) and \(T_{w}=80^{◦} C \)

 

\((149.1)π(0.0254)(3.0)(80-\frac{T_{b2}+60 }{2} ) =(9.982×10^{−3})(4180)(T_{b2}-60 )\qquad [b]\)

This equation can be solved to give

\(T_{b2} =71.98^{◦} C\)

Thus, we should go back and evaluate properties at

\(T_{b,mean} =\frac{71.98+60}{2}= 66^{◦} C\)

We obtain

\(ρ=982 \enspace kg/m^{3} \quad c_{p} =4185 \enspace J/kg ·^{◦} C\quad μ=4.36×10^{-4} kg/m· s\)

 

\(k =0.656\enspace W/m·^{◦} C\quad Pr=2.78\)

 

\(Re_{d}=\frac{(1062)(4.71)}{4.36} =1147 \)

 

\(Re\> Pr\frac{d}{L} =\frac{(1147)(2.78)(0.0254)}{3} =27.00 \)

 

\(Nu_{d}=(1.86)(27.00)^{1/3}\left(\frac{4.36}{3.55} \right)^{0.14} =5.743 \)

 

\(h=\frac{(0.656)(5.743)}{0.0254} =148.3\; W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C \)

 

We insert this value of h back into Equation (a) to obtain

\(T_{b2}=71.88 ^{◦} C \quad [161.4^{◦} F]\)

The iteration makes very little difference in this problem. If a large bulk-temperature difference had been encountered, the change in properties could have had a larger effect.
