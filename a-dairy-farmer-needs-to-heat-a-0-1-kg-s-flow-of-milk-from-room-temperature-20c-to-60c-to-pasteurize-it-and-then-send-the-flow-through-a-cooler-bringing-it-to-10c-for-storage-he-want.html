Guest Question.

A dairy farmer needs to heat a 0.1 kg/s flow of milk from room temperature 20°C to 60°C to pasteurize it and then send the flow through a cooler bringing it to 10°C for storage. He wants to by a heat pump to do this and finds one using R-134a with a high pressure of 3 MPa and a low pressure of 300 kPa. The farmer is very clever and uses the heat pump to heat the milk in a heat exchanger after which the milk flows through the evaporator heat exchanger to cool it. Find the power required to run the heat pump so it can do both the heating and the cooling assuming milk has the properties of water. Is there any excess heating or cooling capacity? What is the total rate of exergy destruction by running this system?

Admin
Solution

Do the refrigeration cycle.

State 1 (300 kPa, x = 1): \(h _{1}=398.69 kJ / kg , s _{1}=1.7259 kJ / kgK , T _{1}=0.56^{\circ} C\)

State 2 (3000 kPa, \(s = s _{1}\)): \(h _{2}=445.90 kJ / kg , T _{2}=95.5^{\circ} C\)

State 3 (3000 kPa, x = 0): \(h _{3}=335.225 kJ / kg , s _{3}=1.4186 kJ / kgK , T _{3}=86.2^{\circ} C\)

 

Evaporator: \(q _{ L }= h _{1}- h _{4}= h _{1}- h _{3}=398.69-335.225=63.465 kJ / kg\)

Condenser: \(q _{ H }= h _{2}- h _{3}=445.90-335.225=110.675 kJ / kg\)

Compressor: \(w _{ c }= h _{2}- h _{1}=445.90-398.69=47.21 kJ / kg\),

COP: \(\beta= q _{ L } / w _{ c }=63.465 / 47.21=1.344\)

 

Since we need to cool more than we need to heat, the heat pump needs to be sized to the cooling. From the definition of COP and the milk heat transfer required:

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{ Q }_{ L }=\dot{ m } C _{ p }\left( T _{2}- T _{3}\right)=0.1 \times 4.18(60-10)=20.9 kW =\beta \dot{ W } \\&\dot{ Q }_{ H \text { milk }}=\dot{ m } C _{ p }\left( T _{2}- T _{1}\right)=0.1 \times 4.18(60-20)=16.72 kW \\&\dot{ W }=\dot{ Q }_{ L } / \beta=20.9 kW / 1.344= 1 5 . 5 5 k W ; \quad \dot{ Q }_{ H }=\dot{ W }+\dot{ Q }_{ L }=36.45 kW \\&\dot{ Q }_{ H \text { extra }}=\dot{ Q }_{ H }-\dot{ Q }_{ H milk } =36.45-16.72= 1 9 . 7 3 kW\end{aligned}\)

 

For exergy we have work in and flow in with zero \(\left( T _{ o }\right)\), heat dumped into the ambient also assumed zero and then the cold flow out with a positive exergy.

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{\Phi}_{\text {flow out }} &=\dot{m}\left[ h _{3}- h _{1}- T _{o}\left( s _{3}- s _{1}\right)\right]=\dot{ m } C _{ p }\left[ T _{3}- T _{1}- T _{ o } \ln \left( T _{3} / T _{1}\right)\right] \\&=0.1 \times 4.18[-10-293 ln (283 / 293)]=0.073 kW\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\dot{\Phi}_{\text {destr }}=\dot{ W }-\dot{\Phi}_{\text {flow out }}=15.55-0.073= 1 5 . 4 8 ~ k W\)

 

If we take control volume total (as shown) then the s generation can be found

\(0=\dot{ m }\left( s _{1}- s _{3}\right)-\left(\dot{ Q }_{ H }-\dot{ Q }_{ H milk } \right) / T _{ o }+\dot{ S }_{ gen }\)

and then \(\dot{\Phi}_{\text {destr }}= T _{ o } \dot{ S }_{ gen }\) giving the same result.

Comment: Clearly the extra heat out at 86°C could be useful and the cycle can be run with a slightly lower high pressure as it only need to heat the milk to 60°C.
