Guest Question.

Let \( \vec{X}\) be a Gaussian random vector with

\(p_{ \vec{X}} (\vec{u}) = =\frac{1}{(2\pi )^{n/2} \left|K_{XX}\right|^{1/2} } exp (-\frac{1}{2} (\vec{u} -\vec{\mu _{X}})^{T} K_{XX}^{-1} (\vec{u} -\vec{\mu _{X}}))\)

and \(g(\vec{X}) \) be a nonlinear function of the \( n \) components of \( \vec{X} \). Show that

\(E [(\vec{X} -\vec{\mu _{X}}) g(\vec{X})] = K_{XX} E ([\frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{1}}, ... , \frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{n}} ]^{T})\)

for any \(g(.) \) function such that \(g(\vec{u}) p_{\vec{X}} (\vec{u}) \rightarrow 0 \) as the length of the \( \vec{u} \) vector tends to infinity.

Admin
Solution

We begin by considering the expected value of one component of the derivative vector:

\( E (\frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{j}} )= \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{…} \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{\frac{\partial g(\vec{u})}{\partial u_{j}}p_{\vec{X} }(\vec{u})du_{1} … du_{n}}\) \(= \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{…} \int_{-\infty }^{\infty }{g(\vec{u} )\frac{\partial }{\partial u_{j}}p_{\vec{X} }(\vec{u})du_{1} … du_{n}}\)

in which the second form has been obtained by integration by parts, taking advantage of the fact that \(g(\vec{u}) p_{\vec{X}} (\vec{u}) \rightarrow 0 \) at the limits of the integral. Expanding the exponent in the probability density function as

\( (\vec{u} -\vec{\mu } _{X})^{T} K_{XX}^{-1}(\vec{u} -\vec{\mu } _{X})=\sum\limits_{l=1}^{n}{(u_{j}-\mu _{X_{j} })[K_{XX}^{-1}]_{jl} (u_{l}-\mu _{X_{l}})}\)

now gives

\( \frac{\partial }{\partial u_{j}}p_{\vec{X}}(\vec{u}) =-\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}{(u_{k}-\mu _{X_{k}})[K_{XX}^{-1}]_{jk} p_{\vec{X}}(\vec{u}) }\)

and substitution yields

\( E(\frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{j}} ) =\sum\limits_{k=1}^{n}{E(X_{k}-\mu _{X_{k}})[K_{XX}^{-1}]_{jk} }\)

This, though, is exactly the \(j\)th row of the vector expression

\( E([\frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{j}}, … , \frac{\partial g(\vec{X})}{\partial X_{n}}]^{T} ) = K_{XX}^{-1} E[(\vec{X} – \vec{\mu }_{X})g(\vec{X} )]\)

Multiplying this expression by \( K_{XX} \) provides the desired result.

The expression derived here will be used in Chapter 10 in the process of “equivalent” linearization of nonlinear dynamics problems. It was apparently first used in this context by Atalik and Utku (1976), but it was presented earlier by Kazakov (1965). Falsone and Rundo Sotera (2003) have pointed out that, in addition to its use in nonlinear dynamics, the expression can also be used to obtain recursive relationships for the moments of Gaussian random variables. First, let us consider central moments by letting

\( g(\vec{X})=(X_{1} -\mu _{X_{1}})^{\alpha _{1}}…(X_{n} -\mu _{X_{n}})^{\alpha _{n}}\)

The formula then gives

\( E[(X_{j} -\mu _{X_{j}})g(\vec{X} )]= \sum\limits_{l=1}^{n}{\alpha _{l}K_{X_{j}X_{l}}E(\frac{g(\vec{X} )}{(X_{l}-\mu _{X_{l}})} )}\)

which allows computation of higher-order moment expressions from lower-order moment expressions. In particular, the expression has \( (X_{j}- \mu _{X_{j}}) \) to the power \( \alpha _{j}+1 \) on the left-hand side and each term on the right-hand side is either to its original power or to a lower power. Repeated use of this relationship allows computation of any order central moments from lower order moments, such as mean and covariance. For the special case of \( n =1 \) we have

\( E[(X -\mu _{X})^{\alpha +1}]= \alpha \sigma _{X}^{2} E[(X -\mu _{X})^{\alpha -1}]\)

For example,

\( E[(X -\mu _{X})^{4}]= 3 \sigma _{X}^{2} E[(X -\mu _{X})^{2}] =3\sigma _{X}^{4}\)

and

\( E[(X -\mu _{X})^{6}]= 5 \sigma _{X}^{4} E[(X -\mu _{X})^{2}] =15\sigma _{X}^{6}\)

These relationships agree exactly with those in . Furthermore, given the fact that the central moment of order unity is zero, the recursive relationship also shows that all odd central moments are zero, as found in . This recursive relationship can also be extended to noncentral moments by choosing the nonlinear function as \( g(\vec{X})= X_{1}^{\alpha _{1}} … X_{n}^{\alpha _{n}} \). This gives

\( E[(X_{j} -\mu _{X_{j}})g(\vec{X})]= \sum\limits_{l=1}^{n}{\alpha _{l} K_{X_{j}X_{l}}E(g(\vec{X}) / X_{l})}\)

or

\( E[(X_{j} g(\vec{X}))]= \mu _{X_{j}} E[g(\vec{X})]+ \sum\limits_{l=1}^{n}{\alpha _{l} K_{X_{j}X_{l}}E(g(\vec{X}) / X_{l})}\)

For the special case of \( n=1 \) we have

\( E(X^{\alpha +1})=\mu _{X}E(X^{\alpha })+\alpha \sigma _{X}^{2}E(X^{\alpha -1})\)

For example,

\( E(X^{2})=\mu _{X}E(X^{1})+ \sigma _{X}^{2}E(X^{0})=\mu _{X}^{2}+\sigma _{X}^{2}\)

 

\( E(X^{3})=\mu _{X}E(X^{2})+ 2 \sigma _{X}^{2}E(X^{1})=\mu _{X}^{3}+ 3 \mu_{X} \sigma _{X}^{2}\)

 

\( E(X^{4})=\mu _{X}E(X^{3})+3 \sigma _{X}^{2}E(X^{2})=\mu _{X}^{4}+6 \mu _{X}^{2} \sigma _{X}^{2} +3 \sigma _{X}^{4}\)

and so forth. Clearly those recursive relationships can be very useful when higher order moments are needed for Gaussian random variables.
