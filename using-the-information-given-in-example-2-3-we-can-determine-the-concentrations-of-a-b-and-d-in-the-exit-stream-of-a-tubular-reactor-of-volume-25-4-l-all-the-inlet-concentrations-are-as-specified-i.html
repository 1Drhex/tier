Guest Question.

Using the information given in , we can determine the concentrations of A, B, and D in the exit stream of a tubular reactor of volume 25.4 L . All the inlet concentrations are as specified in and the flow is the sum \(q_{AF}+q_{BF}\), as given in the example.

Admin
Solution

The model equations for the tubular reactor become the same as those for the batch reactor, with \(\tau\) = z/v substituted for t:

\(\frac{dC_{A} }{d\tau } =-KC_{A}C_{B},\)
\(\frac{dC_{B} }{d\tau } =-KC_{A}C_{B},\)
\(\frac{dC_{D} }{d\tau } =2KC_{A}C_{B}.\)

As was shown in previous sections the following relationships are obtained for the
species concentrations:

\(C_{A}-C_{AF} =C_{B}-C_{BF} ,\)
\(C_{AF} -C_{A}=\frac{1}{2}C_{D}.\)

(Here we are denoting the concentration at \(\tau\) = 0 as \(C_{AF}\) , rather than \(C_{Ai}\) , as for the batch system.) Because \(C_{AF}\) and \(C_{BF}\) are equal in this example, \(C_{A} =C_{B}\) and \(C_{A}\) is
readily obtained by substitution into the first equation:

\(\frac{dC_{A} }{d\tau } =-KC^{2}_{A}\).

Integration of this equation yields exactly the same result as that given by the equation in Table 2.2, with \(\tau\) substituted for t:

\(\frac{1}{C_{A} } =\frac{1}{C_{AF} }+K\tau .\)

Here \(C_{AF}\) represents the inlet concentration of A in the combined stream containing both A and B. To maintain the same molar feed rate as in , the feed concentrations must be one-half those in the individual feed streams, so

\(C_{AF}=\frac{11}{2}\) = 5.5 g-mol/L,

\(C_{BF}=\frac{11}{2}\) = 5.5 g-mol/L.

The exit of the reactor is at \(\tau\) =\(\Theta =\pi D^{2} L/4q\) = V/q. For q = 0.2, \(\Theta \) =127. Thus, \(C_{A} \) ,the concentration of A in the exit stream from the tubular reactor, is

\(\frac{1}{C_{A} }=\frac{1}{5.5}+6.05\times 10^{-4} \times 127\) ,

\(C_{A}\) = 3.84 g-mol/L.

We see that, for the same residence time, \(\Theta = 127 min\), we obtain a higher conversion of A in the tubular reactor than in the CFSTR. A series of calculations can be performed to yield Table 2.5, which compares the tubular reactor results with the CFSTR results contained in Table 2.4. It is clear that the tubular configuration is more efficient in that a smaller reactor is always required than that necessary to produce the same conversion in a CFSTR. The fact that a smaller reactor is required does not necessarily mean that it will be less expensive to construct, and in fact there are many situations in which a CFSTR is less expensive.
