Guest Question.

The mechanism shown in Figure 14.9 is used to lower and retract the landing gear on small airplanes. The wheel assembly link weighs 100 lb, with a center of gravity as shown. The radius of gyration of the assembly, relative to the center of gravity, has been experimentally determined as 1.2 ft. The motor link is rotating counterclockwise at 3 rad/s and accelerating at 10 rad/\(s^2\). For mass property estimation, the motor crank will weigh approximately 15 lb and will be 2 ft long, 1 ft wide, and 0.25 ft thick. The connecting link is estimated to weigh 20 lb and can be modeled as a 3.5-ft slender rod. Determine all forces acting on the joints of all links and the torque required to drive the motor link. 1.Draw a Kinematic Diagram and Identify the Degrees of Freedom 2.Decide on a Method to Achieve the Required Motor Torque 3.Determine the Velocity of Points B and C 4.Calculate Acceleration Components 5.Construct an Acceleration Diagram 6.Measure the Acceleration of the Center of Gravity of All Links 7.Calculate Mass Properties 8.Calculate the Inertial Force 9.Calculate the Inertial Torque 10.Sketch Free-Body Diagrams of the Mechanism Links 11.Generate Equilibrium Equations for Link 4 12.Generate Equilibrium Equations for Link 3 13.Generate Equilibrium Equations for Link 2 14.Solve the Equilibrium Equations.

Admin
Solution

1: This mechanism is the common four-bar linkage, having a single degree of freedom. A kinematic diagram is given in Figure 14.10a.

2: Because all links have significant weight, the acceleration of the center of gravity of all links must be determined. Such acceleration analysis has been extensively. Once the accelerations have been established, the subsequent inertial forces and torques can be calculated. Finally, free-body diagrams and the corresponding equations can be used to determine the required torque.

3: This type of analysis was extensively discussed in the earlier chapters of the book. The 1.77-ft crank is rotating at 3 rad/s. The velocity of point B is

\(V_B = ω_2 r_{AB}\) = (3 rad/s)(1.77 ft) = 5.31 ft/s 60° ∠

The direction of \(V_B\) is perpendicular to link 2 and consistent with the direction of \(ω_2\), up and to the left. Using CAD, a vector can be drawn to scale, from the velocity diagram origin, to represent this velocity.

The relative velocity equation for points B and C can be written as

\(V_C = V_B + > V_{C/B}\)

The vector diagram is constructed in Figure 14.10b. Scaling the vector magnitudes from the diagram,

\(V_C\) = 5.00 ft/s 30.7° ∠

\(V_{C/B}\) = 2.63 ft/s 51.4° ∠

4: The next step is to construct an acceleration diagram, which includes points B and C. Calculating the magnitudes of the known accelerations,

\(A^n_B=\frac{(V_B)^2}{r_{AB}}=\frac{(5.31\ ft/s)^2}{1.77\ ft}=15.93\ ft/s^2\ 30^\circ \angle\) (directed toward the center of rotation, point A)

\(A^t_B = a_2 r_{AB}\) = (10rad/\(s^2\))(1.77 ft) = 17.70 ft/\(s^2\) 60° ∠ (perpendicular to link 2, consistent with \(α_2\) )

\(A^n_{C/B}=\frac{(V_{C/B})^2}{r_{BC}}=\frac{(2.63\ ft/s)^2}{3.0\ ft}=2.30\ ft/s^2\ 38.6^\circ \angle\) (directed from C toward B, measured from CAD)

\(A^n_C=\frac{(V_C)^2}{r_{CD}}=\frac{(500\ ft/s^2)}{2.33\ ft}=10.72\ ft/s^2\ 59.3^\circ \angle\) (directed from C toward D, measured from CAD)

5: The relative acceleration equation for points B and C can be written as

\(A^n_C + > A^t_C = A^n_B + > A^t_B + > A^n_{C/B} + > A^t_{C/B}\)

The acceleration polygon is constructed and shown in Figure 14.10c. Notice that the concept of the acceleration image, was used to determine the acceleration of the center of gravity of the three moving links.

6: Scaling the vector magnitudes from the diagram,

\(A^t_{C/B} = 12.28 ft/s^2\ 51.4° ∠\)

\(A^t_C = 11.60 ft/s^2\ 30.6° ∠\)

\(A_{g2} = 11.91 ft/s^2\ 78.0° ∠\)

\(A_{g3} = 19.21 ft/s^2\ 89.4° ∠\)

\(A_{g4} = 20.32 ft/s^2\ 73.4° ∠\)

The angular accelerations of the links can then be determined.

\(a_3=\frac{a^t_{C/B}}{r_{BC}}=\frac{12.28\ ft/s^2}{3.0\ ft}=4.1\ rad/s^2,counterclockwise\)

\(a_4=\frac{a^t_C}{r_{CD}}=\frac{11.60\ ft/s^2}{2.33\ ft} =5.0\ rad/s^2,counterclockwise\)

7: The motor crank can be considered a rectangular block. From Table 14.2, the mass moment of inertia, at the center of mass, relative to an axis normal to the broad side of the link is

\(I_{g2}=\frac{1}{12}[m(W^2+l^2)]=\frac{1}{12}(\frac{15\ lb}{32.2\ ft/s^2} )[(2\ ft)^2+(1\ ft)^2]=0.194\ lb\ ft\ s^2\)

The connecting arm can be considered a slender rod. From Table 14.2, the mass moment of inertia at the center of mass relative to an axis normal to the length of the link is

\(I_{g3}=\frac{1}{12}[ml^2]=\frac{1}{12}(\frac{20\ lb}{32.2\ ft/s^2} )(3.5\ ft)^2=0.634\ lb\ ft\ s^2\)

The radius of gyration of the wheel assembly has been experimentally determined. From equation \(I = mk^2\), the mass moment of inertia at the center of mass relative to an axis normal to the length of the assembly is

\(I_{g4}=ml^2=(\frac{100\ lb}{32.2\ ft/s^2} )(1.2\ ft)^2=4.472\ lb\ ft\ s^2\)

8: For the three moving links, the inertial force is computed by combining equations \(F^i_g = -> mA_g\) and W = mg.

\(F^i_{g\ 2}-\gt m_2A_{g2}=\frac{W_2}{g}(-\gt A_{g\ 2})\)

\(=\frac{(15\ lb)}{32.2\ ft/s^2}(11.91\ ft/s^2)=5.55\ lb\ 78.0^\circ \angle\)

\(F^i_{g\ 3}=-\gt m_3A_{g3}=\frac{W_3}{g}(-\gt A_{g3})\)

\(=\frac{(20\ lb)}{32.2\ ft/s^2}(19.21\ ft/s^2)=11.93\ lb\ 89.4^\circ \angle\)

\(F^i_{g\ 4}=-\gt m_4A_{g4}=\frac{W_4}{g}(-\gt A_{g4})\)

\(=\frac{(100\ lb)}{32.2\ ft/s^2}(20.32\ ft/s^2)=63.11\ lb\ 73.4^\circ \angle\)

9: For the three moving links, the inertial torque is computed with equation \(T^i_g=-\gt I_g\alpha \).

\(T^i_{g2}=-\gt I_{g\ 2}\alpha _2=(0.194\ lb\ ft\ s^2)(10\ rad/s^2)\)

\(=1.94\ ft\ lb,cw\)

\(T^i_{g\ 3}=-\gt I_{g\ 3}\alpha _3=(0.634\ lb\ ft\ s^2)(4.1\ rad/s^2)\)

\(=2.60\ ft\ lb,cw\)

\(T^i_{g\ 4}=-\gt I_{g\ 4}\alpha _4=(4.472\ lb\ ft\ s^2)(5\ rad/s^2)\)

\(=22.36\ ft\ lb,cw\)

10: Because the weight of all links is to be included in the analysis, there are no two-force members. Thus, all contact forces at the joints are general and are represented by their orthogonal components. The free-body diagram of link 4 is shown in Figure 14.10d. The free-body diagram of link 3 is shown in Figure 14.10e. Of course, Newton’s third law, declaring that \(F_{34} and F_{43}\) have the same magnitude and opposing directions, still applies. Finally, the free-body diagram of link 2 is shown in Figure 14.10f. Because each link has more than three unknown forces, the equilibrium equations from all links will need to be solved simultaneously.

11: The following dynamic equilibrium equations are generated from the free-body diagram of link 4 (Figure 14.10d).

\(\overset{+}{\rightarrow }\ \ \ \ \Sigma F^x+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^x_{41} – F^x_{43} – F^i_{g4}\) cos 73.4° = 0

\(F^x_{41} – F^x_{43}\) – 18.03 lb = 0

\(+\uparrow \ \ \ \ \Sigma F^y+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^y_{41} – F^y_{43} – W_4 – F^i_{g4}\) sin 73.4° = 0

\(F^y_{41} – F^y_{43}\) – 160.48 lb = 0

\(+\Lsh \ \ \ \ \Sigma M_D+\gt T^i_g=0:\)

\(– F^x_{43}\)[2.33 ft (sin 59.4°)] – \(F^y_{43}\)[2.33 ft (cos 59.4°)] – \(W_4\)[3.0 ft(cos 59.4°)]

\(– F^i_{g4}\) [cos (73.4° – 30.6°)] [3.0 ft] – \(T^i_{g4}\) = 0

Substituting values gives

\(– 2.000F^x_{43} – 1.186F^y_{43}\) – 313.98 ft lb = 0

12: The following dynamic equilibrium equations are generated from the free-body diagram of link 3 (Figure 14.10e).

\(\overset{+}{\rightarrow }\ \ \ \ \Sigma F^x+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^x_{34} – F^x_{32} – F^i_{g\ 3}\) cos 89.4° = 0

\(F^x_{34} – F^x_{32}\) – 0.13 lb = 0

\(+\uparrow \ \ \ \ \Sigma F^y+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^y_{34} – F^y_{32} – W_3 – F^i_{g3}\) sin 89.4° = 0

\(F^y_{34} – F^y_{32}\) – 31.93 lb = 0

\(+\Lsh \ \ \ \ \Sigma M_B+\gt T^i_g=0:\)

\(F^x_{34}\)[3.0 ft (cos 38.6°)] – \(F^y_{34}\)[3.0 ft (sin 38.6°)](2.33 ft) + \(W_3\)[1.5 ft(cos 38.6°)]

\(+ F^i_{g\ 3}\) [cos (38.6° – 0.6°)] [1.5 ft] – \(T^i_{g\ 3}\) = 0

Substituting values gives

\(2.344F^x_{34} – 1.872F^y_{34}\) + 34.95 ft lb = 0

13: The following dynamic equilibrium equations are generated from the free-body diagram of link 2 (Figure 14.10f).

\(\overset{+}{\rightarrow }\ \ \ \ \Sigma F^x+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^x_{23} + F^x_{21} + F^i_{g\ 2}\) cos 78° = 0

\(F^x_{23} + F^y_{21}\) + 1.15 lb = 0

\(+\uparrow \ \ \ \ \Sigma F^y+\gt F^i_g=0:\)

\(F^y_{23} + F^y_{21} – W_2 – F^i_{g2}\) sin 78° = 0

\(F^y_{23} + F^y_{21}\) – 20.43 lb = 0

\(+\Lsh \ \ \ \ \Sigma M_A+\gt T^i_g=0:\)

\(T_{21}+F^x_{23}\)[1.77 ft (sin 30°)] + \(F^y_{23}\)[1.77 ft (cos 30°)] – \(W_2\)[0.89 ft(cos 30°)]

\(F^i_{g\ 2}\) [sin (78° – 30°)] [0.89 ft] – \(T^i_{g\ 2}\) = 0

Substituting values gives

\(T_{21} + 0.885 F^x_{23} + 1.533F^y_{23}\) – 17.17 ft lb = 0

14: A total of nine equilibrium equations have been generated. As previously stated, Newton’s third law stipulates that the following magnitudes are equal.

\(F^x_{43} = F^x_{34}\ \ \ \ \ F^y_{43}=F^y_{34}\)

\(F^x_{23} = F^x_{32}\ \ \ \ \ F^y_{23}=F^y_{23}\)

Therefore, nine unknown quantities remain. Solving the nine equilibrium equations, simultaneously, gives the following results:

\(F^x_{41}\) = -78.41 lb = 78.41 lb ←

\(F^y_{41}\) = +58.38 lb = 58.38 lb ↑

\(F^x_{43}\) = -96.44 lb = 96.44 lb → and \(F^x_{34}\) = 96.44 lb ←

\(F^y_{43}\) = -102.09 lb = 102.09 lb ↑ and \( F^y_{34}\) = 102.09 lb ↓

\(F^x_{32}\) = -96.32 lb = 96.32 lb → and \( F^x_{23}\) = 96.32 lb ←

\(F^y_{32}\) = -134.03 lb = 134.03 lb ↑ and \( F^y_{23}\) = 134.03 lb ↓

\(F^x_{21}\) = + 95.17 lb = 95.17 lb →

\(F^y_{21}\) = + 154.46 lb = 154.46 lb ↑

\(T_{21}\) = + 307.88 ft lb = 307.88 ft lb,cw
