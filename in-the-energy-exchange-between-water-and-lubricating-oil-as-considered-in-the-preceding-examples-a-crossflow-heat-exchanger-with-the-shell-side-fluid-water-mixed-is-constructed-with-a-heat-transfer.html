Guest Question.

In the energy exchange between water and lubricating oil as considered in the preceding examples, a crossflow heat exchanger with the shell-side fluid (water) mixed is constructed with a heat-transfer area of \( 1.53 m^{2} \). A new pump is attached to the water supply line, enabling the water flow rate to be increased to 1000 kg/h. What will be the exit temperatures of the water and oil for the new operating conditions?

Admin
Solution

If the \( \Delta T_{lm} \) method were used in this problem, a trial-and-error method would be necessary as \( \Delta T_{lm} \), Y, and F are all dependent on one or both exit stream temperatures. The NTU method is thus a bit simpler. Using the NTU method, it is first necessary to calculate the capacity coefficients:

 

\( C_{oil} = \left ( 0.5 kg/s \right )\left ( 2090 J/kg \cdot K \right ) = 1045 J/s \cdot K \)

 

\( C_{w} = \left ( 1000 kg/h \right )\left ( h/ 3600 s \right ) \left ( 4177 J/kg \cdot K \right ) = 1160 J/kg \cdot K \)

 

Oil is now the “minimum” fluid. With \( C_{oil} = C_{min} \) we have

 

\( NTU = \frac{UA}{C_{min}} = \frac{\left ( 250 W/m^{2} \cdot K \right ) \left ( 1.53 m^{2} \right )}{1045 J/s \cdot K} = 0.366 \)

 

and, from Figure, the effectiveness is

\( \varepsilon \cong 0.29 \)

 

Using equation ( \( q = \varepsilon C_{min} \left ( T_{H in} – T_{c in} \right )\) ) we may evaluate the heat-transfer rate as

 

\( q = \left ( 0.29 \right )\left ( 1045 J/s \cdot K \right )\left ( 95 K \right ) = 28.8 K \)

 

which is an increase of over 10%. This value may now be used in equations ( \( dq = \left ( \dot{m c_{p}} \right ) _{H} dT_{H} = C_{H} dT_{H}\) ) and ( \( dq = \left ( \dot{m} c_{p} \right ) _{H} dT_{H} = C_{H} dT_{H}\) ) to yield the required answers.

\( T_{oil out} = 375 – 28.8 kW/ \left ( 1045 W/K \right ) = 347.4 K \)

 

\( T_{w out} = 280 + 28.8 kW/ \left ( 1160 W/K \right ) = 304.8 K \)
