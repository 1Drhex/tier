Guest Question.

Use the standard values of chemical potentials and temperature coefficients for water and ice— and the idea of chemical equilibrium—to find an approximation to the melting point of \(H_{2}O\) (values in Table 6.2).

Isotope \(M_{0} / kg/mole\) \(μ_{0} / kG\)
\(^{1}H\) \(0.00100783\) \(0.0905788 · 10^{12} \)
\(^{4}He\) \(0.0040026\) \(0.359736 · 10^{12}\)
\(^{90}Sr\) \(0.08990774\) \(8.080505 · 10^{12}\)
\(^{90}Y\) \(0.08990716\) \(8.080453 · 10^{12}\)
\(^{90}Zr\) \(0.0899047\) \(8.080231 · 10^{12}\)
Admin
Solution

Since the transformation of water to ice or ice to water can go either way at the melting point, the chemical potential of the phases must be equal at that point:

\(\mu_{i c e}\left(T_{\text {melt }}\right)=\mu_{\text {water }}\left(T_{\text {melt }}\right)\)

 

We express the chemical potentials of both substances in terms of standard potentials and the temperature coefficients (according to Equ.(6.24)):

\(\mu_{i c e}^{0}+\alpha_{\mu, i c e}\left(T_{m e l t}-T_{0}\right)=\mu_{\text {water }}^{0}+\alpha_{\mu, \text { water }}\left(T_{\text {melt }}-T_{0}\right)\)

 

Solving the equation and inserting values from Table 6.2 leads to a value of

\(\begin{aligned} T_{m e l t} &=\frac{\mu_{\text {water }}^{0}-\mu_{i c e}^{0}+T_{0}\left(\alpha_{\mu, i c e}-\alpha_{\mu, \text { water }}\right)}{\alpha_{\mu, i c e}-\alpha_{\mu, \text { water }}} \\ &=\frac{-237180-(-236590)+298.15(-44.8-(-69.9))}{-44.8-(-69.9)} \mathrm{K}=274.6 \mathrm{~K} \end{aligned}\)

 

This is equal to about \(1.5°C\). Considering that this is just a linear approximation, the result is quite acceptable.

\(\mu\left(T, P_{\text {ref }}\right)=\mu\left(T_{\text {ref }}, P_{\text {ref }}\right)+\alpha_{\mu}\left(T-T_{\text {ref }}\right) \quad\quad\) (6.24)
