Guest Question.

(a) Plug s = 0 , s = 1 , s = 2, and s = 3 into Kramers’ relation (Equation 7.113) to obtain formulas for \( \left\langle r^{-1}\right\rangle,\langle r\rangle,\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle, \text { and }\left\langle r^{3}\right\rangle \). Note that you could continue indefinitely, to find any positive power.

\( \frac{s+1}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{s}\right\rangle-(2 s+1) a \left\langle r^{s-1}\right\rangle +\frac{s}{4}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-s^{2}\right] a^{2}\left\langle r^{s-2}\right\rangle=0 \) , (7.113)

(b) In the other direction, however, you hit a snag. Put in s = -1 , and show that all you get is a relation between \( \left\langle r^{-2}\right\rangle \text { and }\left\langle r^{-3}\right\rangle \).

(c) But if you can get \( \left\langle r^{-2}\right\rangle \) by some other means, you can apply the Kramers relation to obtain the rest of the negative powers. Use Equation 7.57 (which is derived in) to determine \( \left\langle r^{-3}\right\rangle \) ,and check your answer against Equation 7.66.

\( \left\langle\frac{1}{r^{2}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{(\ell+1 / 2) n^{3} a^{2}} \) (7.57).

\( \left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1 / 2)(\ell+1) n^{3} a^{3}} \) (7.66).

Admin
Solution

(a)

\( \frac{1}{n^{2}}\langle 1\rangle-a\left\langle\frac{1}{r}\right\rangle+0=0 \Rightarrow\left\langle\frac{1}{r}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{n^{2} a} \).

\( \frac{2}{n^{2}}\langle r\rangle-3 a\langle 1\rangle+\frac{1}{4}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-1\right] a^{2}\left\langle\frac{1}{r}\right\rangle=0 \Rightarrow \frac{2}{n^{2}}\langle r\rangle=3 a-\ell(\ell+1) a^{2} \frac{1}{n^{2} a}=\frac{a}{n^{2}}\left[3 n^{2}-\ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

\( \langle r\rangle=\frac{a}{2}\left[3 n^{2}-\ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

\( \frac{3}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle-5 a\langle r\rangle+\frac{1}{2}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-4\right] a^{2}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{3}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle=5 a \frac{a}{2}\left[3 n^{2}-\ell(\ell+1)\right]-\frac{a^{2}}{2}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-4\right] \).

\( \frac{3}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle=\frac{a^{2}}{2}\left[15 n^{2}-5 \ell(\ell+1)-4 \ell(\ell+1)-1+4\right]=\frac{a^{2}}{2}\left[15 n^{2}-9 \ell(\ell+1)+3\right] \).

\( =\frac{3 a^{2}}{2}\left[5 n^{2}-3 \ell(\ell+1)+1\right] ; \quad\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle=\frac{n^{2} a^{2}}{2}\left[5 n^{2}-3 \ell(\ell+1)+1\right] \).

\( \frac{4}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{3}\right\rangle-7 a\left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle+\frac{3}{4}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-9\right] a^{2}\langle r\rangle=0 \Longrightarrow \)

\( \frac{4}{n^{2}}\left\langle r^{3}\right\rangle=7 a \frac{n^{2} a^{2}}{2}\left[5 n^{2}-3 \ell(\ell+1)+1\right]-\frac{3}{4}[4 \ell(\ell+1)-8] a^{2} \frac{a}{2}\left[3 n^{2}-\ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

\( =\frac{a^{3}}{2}\left\{35 n^{4}-21 \ell(\ell+1) n^{2}+7 n^{2}-[3 \ell(\ell+1)-6]\left[3 n^{2}-\ell(\ell+1)\right]\right\} \).

\( =\frac{a^{3}}{2}\left[35 n^{4}-21 \ell(\ell+1) n^{2}+7 n^{2}-9 \ell(\ell+1) n^{2}+3 \ell^{2}(\ell+1)^{2}+18 n^{2}-6 \ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

\( =\frac{a^{3}}{2}\left[35 n^{4}+25 n^{2}-30 \ell(\ell+1) n^{2}+3 \ell^{2}(\ell+1)^{2}-6 \ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

\( \left\langle r^{3}\right\rangle=\frac{n^{2} a^{3}}{8}\left[35 n^{4}+25 n^{2}-30 \ell(\ell+1) n^{2}+3 \ell^{2}(\ell+1)^{2}-6 \ell(\ell+1)\right] \).

(b)

\( 0+a\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{2}}\right\rangle-\frac{1}{4}\left[(2 \ell+1)^{2}-1\right] a^{2}\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=0 \Rightarrow\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{2}}\right\rangle=a \ell(\ell+1)\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle \).

(c)

\( a \ell(\ell+1)\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{(\ell+1 / 2) n^{3} a^{2}} \Rightarrow\left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1 / 2)(\ell+1) n^{3} a^{3}} \) Agrees with Eq. 7.66.

\( \left\langle\frac{1}{r^{3}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\ell(\ell+1 / 2)(\ell+1) n^{3} a^{3}} \) (7.66).

Notice a curious fact about these results: for a given n,

\( \langle r\rangle \text { decreases with increasing } \ell \text {, whereas }\langle 1 / r\rangle \text { is constant } \);

\( \left\langle r^{2}\right\rangle \text { decreases with increasing } \ell, \text { but so does }\left\langle 1 / r^{2}\right\rangle \);

\( \left\langle r^{3}\right\rangle \text { decreases with increasing } \ell, \text { and so does }\left\langle 1 / r^{3}\right\rangle \).

Off hand, you might suppose that if \( \left\langle r^{s}\right\rangle \text { decreases, }\left\langle 1 / r^{s}\right\rangle \) should increase, but this is not the case. What
happens (as Paul Muzikar points out) is that the electron density is more sharply peaked as \( \ell \) increases; for small \( \ell \) it is both closer in and farther out than it is for large \( \ell \).
