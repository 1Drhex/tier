Guest Question.

Water at 60 °F flows from the basement to the second floor through the 0.75-in. (0.0625-ft)-diameter copper pipe (a drawn tubing) at a rate of Q = 12.0 gal/min = 0.0267 \({ft^{3}}/{s}\) and exits through a faucet of diameter 0.50 in. as shown in Fig. E8.6a.
FIND Determine the pressure at point (1) if
(a) all losses are neglected,
(b) the only losses included are major losses, or
(c) all losses are included.

Admin
Solution

Since the fluid velocity in the pipe is given by \( V_{1}={Q}/{A_{1}}={Q}/{({\pi D^{2}}/{4})}={(0.0267{ft^{3}}/{s})}/{[{\pi (0.0625 ft)^{2}}/{4}]}=8.70{ft}/{s}\) and the fluid properties are \( \rho =1.94{slugs}/{ft^{3}}\) and \(\mu =2.34\times 10^{-5}{Ib\cdot s}/{ft^{2}}\) (see Table B.1), it follows that

\(Re={\rho VD}/{\mu }={(1.94{slugs}/{ft^{3}})(8.70{ft}/{s})(0.0625ft)}/{(2.34\times 10^{-5}{Ib\cdot s}/{ft^{2}})}=45,000\)

. Thus, the flow is turbulent. The governing equation for case (a), (b), or (c) is the energy equation as given by \(\frac{p_{out}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{out}\overline{V}^{2}_{out} }{2}+gz_{out} =\frac{p_{in}}{\rho } +\frac{\alpha _{in}\overline{V}^{2}_{in} }{2}+gz_{in}+w_{shaft net in}-loss\)

\(\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma } +\alpha _{1}\frac{V^{2}_{1} }{2g}+z_{1} =\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma } +\alpha _{2}\frac{V^{2}_{2} }{2g}+z_{2}+h_{L}\)

where \(z_{1}=0, z_{2}=20ft,p_{2}=0\) (free jet), \(\gamma =\rho g=62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}}\), and the outlet velocity is \(V_{2}={Q}/{A_{2}}={(0.0267{ft^{3}}/{s})}/{[{\pi ({0.50}/{12})^{2}ft^{2}}/{4}]}=19.6{ft}/{s}\). We assume that the kinetic energy coefficients \(\alpha _{1}\) and \(\alpha _{2}\) are unity. This is reasonable because turbulent velocity profiles are nearly uniform across the pipe (see Section 5.3.4). Thus,

\(p_{1}=\gamma z_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\rho (V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}) +\gamma h_{L}\) (1)

where the head loss is different for each of the three cases.

(a) If all losses are neglected (\(h_{L}=0\) ), Eq. 1 gives

\(p_{1}=(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})(20ft)+\frac{1.94{slugs}/{ft^{3}}}{2}[(19.6{ft}/{s})^{2}-(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}] =(1248+299){Ib}/{ft^{2}}=1547{Ib}/{ft^{2}}\)

or

\(p_{1}=10.7psi\)

COMMENT Note that for this pressure drop, the amount due to elevation change (the hydrostatic effect) is \(\gamma (z_{2}-z_{1})=8.67psi\) and the amount due to the increase in kinetic energy is \( {\rho (V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2})}/{2}=2.07psi\).

( b) If the only losses included are the major losses, the head loss is

\(h_{L}=f\frac{l}{D}\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2g} \)

From Table 8.1 the roughness for a 0.75-in.-diameter copper pipe (drawn tubing) is \(\epsilon =0.000005ft\) so that \({\epsilon }/{D}=8\times 10^{-5}\). With this \({\epsilon }/{D}\) and the calculated Reynolds number (Re = 45,000), the value of f is obtained from the Moody chart as f = 0.0215. Note that the Colebrook equation ( \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{f} } =-2.0\log (\frac{{\epsilon }/{D}}{3.7} +\frac{2.51}{Re\sqrt{f} } )\)) would give the same value of f. Hence, with the total length of the pipe as l = (15 + 5 + 10 + 10 + 20) ft = 60 ft and the elevation and kinetic energy portions the same as for part (a), Eq. 1 gives

\( p_{1}=\gamma z_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\rho (V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}) +\rho f\frac{l}{D} \frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2} =(1248 +299){Ib}/{ft^{2}}+(1.94{slugs}/{ft^{3}})(0.0215)(\frac{60ft}{0.0625ft} )\frac{(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2}=(1248+299+1515){Ib}/{ft^{2}}=3062 {Ib}/{ft^{2}}\)

or

\(p_{1}=21.3psi\)

COMMENT Of this pressure drop, the amount due to pipe friction is approximately (21.3 – 10.7) psi = 10.6 psi.

(c) If major and minor losses are included, Eq. 1 becomes

\(p_{1}=\gamma z_{2}+\frac{1}{2}\rho (V_{2}^{2}-V_{1}^{2}) +f\gamma \frac{l}{D}\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2g}+\sum{\rho K_{L}\frac{V^{2}}{2} } \)

or

\(p_{1}=21.3psi+\sum{\rho K_{L}\frac{V^{2}}{2} } \) (2)

where the 21.3-psi contribution is due to elevation change, kinetic energy change, and major losses [part (b)] and the last term represents the sum of all of the minor losses. The loss coefficients of the components (\(K_{L}=1.5 \) for each elbow and \(K_{L}= 10\) for the wide-open globe valve) are given in Table 8.2 (except for the loss coefficient of the faucet, which is given in Fig. E8.6a as \(K_{L}\)=2). Thus,

\(\sum{\rho K_{L}\frac{V^{2}}{2} } =(1.94{slugs}/{ft^{3}})\frac{(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2}[10+4(1.5)+2] =1321{Ib}/{ft^{2}}\)

or

\(\sum{\rho K_{L}\frac{V^{2}}{2} } =9.17psi\) (3)

COMMENTS Note that we did not include an entrance or exit loss because points (1) and (2) are located within the fluid streams, not within an attaching reservoir where the kinetic energy is zero. Thus, by combining Eqs. 2 and 3 we obtain the entire pressure drop as

\(p_{1}=(21.3+9.17)psi=30.5psi\)

This pressure drop calculated by including all losses should be the most realistic answer of the three cases considered.

More detailed calculations will show that the pressure distribution along the pipe is as illustrated in Fig. E8.6b for cases (a) and (c)—neglecting all losses or including all losses.

Note that not all of the pressure drop, \(p_{1}-p_{2}\), is a “pressure loss.” The pressure change due to the elevation and velocity changes is completely reversible. The portion due to the major and minor losses is irreversible.

This flow can be illustrated in terms of the energy line and hydraulic grade line concepts introduced in Section 3.7. As is shown in Fig. E8.6c, for case (a) there are no losses and the energy line (EL) is horizontal, one velocity head (\({V^{2}}/{2g} \)) above the hydraulic grade line (HGL), which is one pressure head (\( \gamma z\)) above the pipe itself. For case (c) the energy line is not horizontal. Each bit of friction in the pipe or loss in a component reduces the available energy, thereby lowering the energy line. Thus, for case (a) the total head remains constant throughout the flow with a value of

\(H=\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma } +\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2g}+z_{1}=\frac{(1547{Ib}/{ft^{2}})}{(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})} +\frac{(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2(32.2{ft}/{s^{2}})} +0= 26.0ft=\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma } +\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2g}+z_{2}=\frac{p_{3}}{\gamma } +\frac{V_{3}^{2}}{2g}+z_{3}=…\)

For case (c) the energy line starts at

\(H_{1}=\frac{p_{1}}{\gamma }+\frac{V_{1}^{2}}{2g}+z_{1}=\frac{(30.5\times 144){Ib}/{ft^{2}}}{(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})} +\frac{(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2(32.2{ft}/{s^{2}})} +0=71.6ft\)

and falls to a final value of

\(H_{2}=\frac{p_{2}}{\gamma }+\frac{V_{2}^{2}}{2g}+z_{2}=0+\frac{(19.6{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2(32.2{ft}/{s^{2}})}+20 ft=26.0ft \)

The elevation of the energy line can be calculated at any point along the pipe. For example, at point (7), 50 ft from point (1),

\(H_{7}=\frac{p_{7}}{\gamma }+\frac{V_{7}^{2}}{2g}+z_{7}=\frac{(9.93\times 144){Ib}/{ft^{2}}}{(62.4{Ib}/{ft^{3}})} +\frac{(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2(32.2 {ft}/{s^{2}})}+20ft=44.1ft \)

The head loss per foot of pipe is the same all along the pipe. That is,

\(\frac{h_{L}}{l}=f\frac{V^{2}}{2gD} =\frac{0.0215(8.70{ft}/{s})^{2}}{2(32.2{ft}/{s^{2}})(0.0625ft)} =0.404{ft}/{ft}\)

Thus, the energy line is a set of straight-line segments of the same slope separated by steps whose height equals the head loss of the minor component at that location. As is seen from Fig. E8.6c, the globe valve produces the largest of all the minor losses.
