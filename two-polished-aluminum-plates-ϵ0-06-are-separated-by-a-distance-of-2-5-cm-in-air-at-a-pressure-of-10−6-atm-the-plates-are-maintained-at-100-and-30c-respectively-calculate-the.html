Guest Question.

Two polished-aluminum plates \((\epsilon =0.06)\) are separated by a distance of 2.5 cm in air at a pressure of \(10^{−6}\) atm. The plates are maintained at 100 and \(30^{◦} C \), respectively. Calculate the conduction heat transfer through the air gap. Compare this with the radiation heat transfer and the conduction for air at normal atmospheric pressure.

Admin
Solution

We first calculate the mean free path to determine if low-density effects are important. From Equation (7-70)

\( λ=2.27×10^{−5}\frac{T}{p}\enspace meters \hspace{30 pt} [7-70] \)

, at an average temperature of \(65^{◦} C=338 \> K\),

\(\lambda =\frac{(2.27×10^{-5} )(338)}{(1.0132×10^{+5} )(10^{−6} )}=0.0757\> m=7.57\; cm \enspace \qquad [0.248 \enspace ft] \)

Since the plate spacing is only 2.5 cm, we should expect low-density effects to be important. Evaluating properties at the mean air temperature of \(65^{◦} C\), we have

\( k =0.0291\; W/m· ^{◦} C \hspace{30 pt} [0.0168 \enspace Btu/h · ft · ^{◦} F] \)

 

\( γ =1.40 \qquad Pr =0.7 \qquad α≈0.9 \qquad from\quad Table \enspace 7-4 \)

Combining Equation (7-75)

\( T_{y=0} −T_{w} =\frac{2−α}{α} \frac{2\gamma }{\gamma +1}\frac{\lambda }{Pr} \frac{∂T}{∂y}]_{y=0} \hspace{30 pt} [7-75] \)

with the central-temperature-gradient relation gives

\( \Delta T=\frac{2−α}{α} \frac{2\gamma }{\gamma +1}\frac{\lambda }{Pr} \frac{T_{1} −T_{2} −2\Delta T}{L} \)

Inserting the appropriate properties gives

\( \Delta T=\frac{2-0.9}{0.9} \frac{2.8}{2.4} \frac{0.0757}{0.7}\frac{100−30−2\Delta T}{0.025}=32.38^{◦} C \qquad [58.3^{◦} F] \)

The conduction heat transfer is thus

\(\frac{q}{A}=k\; \frac{T_{1} −T_{2} −2\Delta T}{L}= \frac{(0.0291)(70−64.76)}{0.025} = 6.099\; W/m^{2}\qquad [1.93\enspace Btu/h · ft^{2} ] \)

At normal atmospheric pressure the conduction would be

\(\frac{q}{A}=k\; \frac{T_{1} −T_{2} }{L}= 81.48 \; W/m^{2} \qquad [25.8\quad Btu/h · ft^{2} ] \)

The radiation heat transfer is calculated with Equation (8-42)

\( \frac{q}{A}=\frac{σ(T ^{4} _{1} −T ^{4} _{2} )}{1/\epsilon 1 +1/\epsilon 2 −1} \hspace{30 pt} [8-42] \)

 

, taking \(\epsilon _{1} =\epsilon _{2} =0.06\) for polished aluminum:

\( (\frac{q}{A} )_{rad} =\frac{σ(T ^{4} _{1} −T ^{4} _{2} )}{2/\epsilon −1} =\frac{(5.669×10^{−8} )(393^{4} −303^{4} )}{2/0.06−1}= 27.05 \enspace W/m^{2} \qquad [8.57\enspace Btu/h · ft^{2} ] \)

Thus, at the low-density condition the radiation heat transfer is almost 5 times as large as the
conduction, even with highly polished surfaces.
