Guest Question.

Find the \( H(\omega)\) harmonic transfer matrix for the 2DF system of with

\( m=\begin{pmatrix} 1000 & 0 \\ 0 & 500 \end{pmatrix}kg \), \( k=\begin{pmatrix} 10^{6} & -5\times 10^{5} \\ -5\times 10^{5} & -5\times 10^{5} \end{pmatrix} \frac{N}{m}\), \( c=\begin{pmatrix} 1000 & -500 \\ -500 & 500 \end{pmatrix} \frac{N.s}{m}\)

and investigate the pole locations for \( H(\omega)\).

Admin
Solution

From the results in and 8.5, we have

\( \theta =\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1.414 & -1.414 \end{pmatrix}\), \( \lambda =\begin{pmatrix} 292.9 & 0 \\ 0 & 1707. \end{pmatrix} (rad/s)^{2}\)

and this choice of \( \theta \) gives

\( \hat{m}=\begin{pmatrix} 2000 & 0 \\ 0 & 2000 \end{pmatrix}kg\), \( \beta =\begin{pmatrix} 0.2929 & 0 \\ 0 & 1.707 \end{pmatrix} rad/s \)

Thus, we can obtain the harmonic transfer matrix from Eq. 8.41

\( H(\omega )=\theta \vec{H} (\omega )\theta ^{T}=\theta \vec{m} ^{-1}[\lambda -\omega ^{2}I+i\omega \beta ]^{-1}\theta ^{T}\)

as

\( H(\omega )=\theta \vec{m}^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} 292.9-\omega ^{2}+0.2929i\omega & 0 \\ 0 & 1707-\omega ^{2}+1.707i\omega \end{pmatrix}^{-1} \theta ^{T}\)

The components are

\( H_{11}(\omega )=\frac{H_{22}(\omega )}{2} =\frac{5\times 10^{-4}}{292.9-\omega ^{2}+0.2929i\omega } +\frac{5\times 10^{-4}}{1707-\omega ^{2}+1.707i\omega }\)

and

\( H_{12}(\omega )=H_{21}(\omega ) =\frac{7.07\times 10^{-4}} {292.9-\omega ^{2}+0.2929i\omega } +\frac{7.071\times 10^{-4}} {1707.- \omega ^{2} +1.707i \omega } \)

Alternatively, one can use Eq. 8.42

\( H(\omega )=[k -\omega ^{2}m+i\omega c ]^{-1}\)

without eigenanalysis to find

\( H(\omega )=\begin{pmatrix} 10^{6}-1000\omega ^{2}+1000i\omega & -0.5(10^{6}+1000i\omega) \\ -0.5(10^{6}+1000i\omega) & 0.5(10^{6}-1000\omega ^{2}+1000i\omega ) \end{pmatrix} ^{-1} \) \( =\frac{1}{D} \begin{pmatrix} 1000-\omega ^{2}+i\omega & 1000+i\omega \\ 1000+i\omega & 2(1000-\omega ^{2}+i\omega ) \end{pmatrix} \)

in which the denominator is \( D=1000(\omega ^{4}-2i\omega ^{3} -2000.5 \omega ^{2}+1000i \omega +5\times 10^{5})\), or \( D= 1000 (292.9 -\omega^{2} +0.2929i\omega ) (1707-\omega ^{2}+1.707i\omega )\) . The reader can confirm that the two versions of \( H(\omega)\) are identical.

Recall that one could choose to evaluate the \( h(t)\) impulse response function matrix by using the inverse Fourier transform of \( H(\omega) \). To do this, one would probably use the calculus of residues, giving the integral as a sum of terms coming from the poles of \( H(\omega) \); that is, from the values of complex co at which \( H(\omega) \) is infinite. Obviously these pole locations are the solutions of D=0. The four pole locations can be found by solving the two complex quadratic equations \( \omega ^{2}-0.2929i \omega -292.9=0 \) and \(\omega ^{2}-1.707i\omega -1707=0 \). The solutions are \( \pm 17.11+0.1465i \) and \( \pm 41.31+0.8535i \). These values are a particular example of the general relationship that the poles of \( H(\omega) \) are located at \( \pm \omega _{j}[1-(\zeta _{j})^{2}]^{1/2}+i\zeta _{j}\omega _{j} \). It is observed that the real values of the pole locations are the damped natural frequencies of the system, and the imaginary values are one-half the \( \beta _{j} \) modal damping values. The fact that the imaginary parts are always positive is necessary in order that the system satisfy the causality condition of \( h(t)=0 \) for \( t \lt 0 \).
