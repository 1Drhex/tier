Guest Question.

uppose J(r) is constant in time but ρ(r, t) is not—conditions that might prevail, for instance, during the charging of a capacitor.

(a) Show that the charge density at any particular point is a linear function of time:

\(\rho( r , t)=\rho( r , 0)+\dot{\rho}( r , 0) t\) ,

where \(\dot{\rho}( r , 0)\) is the time derivative of ρ at t = 0. [Hint: Use the continuity equation.]

This is not an electrostatic or magnetostatic configuratio\(n ;^{34}\) nevertheless, rather surprisingly, both Coulomb’s law (Eq. 2.8) and the Biot-Savart law (Eq. 5.42) hold, as you can confirm by showing that they satisfy Maxwell’s equations. In particular:

\(E ( r )=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho\left( r ^{\prime}\right)}{ᴫ^{2}} \hat{ ᴫ } d \tau^{\prime}\) (2.8)

\(B ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ K \left( r ^{\prime}\right) \times \hat{ ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}} d a^{\prime}\) and \(B ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( r ^{\prime}\right) \times \hat{ ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}} d \tau^{\prime}\) (5.42)

\({ }^{34} S\)ome authors would regard this as magnetostatic, since B is independent of t. For them, the BiotSavart law is a general rule of magnetostatics, but ∇ · J = 0 and \(\nabla \times B =\mu_{0} J\) apply only under the additional assumption that ρ is constant. In such a formulation, Maxwell’s displacement term can (in this very special case) be derived from the Biot-Savart law, by the method of part (b). See D. F. Bartlett, Am. J. Phys. 58, 1168 (1990); D. J. Griffiths and M. A. Heald, Am. J. Phys. 59, 111 (1991).

(b) Show that

\(B ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( r ^{\prime}\right) \times \hat{ ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}} d \tau^{\prime}\)

obeys Ampère’s law with Maxwell’s displacement current term.

Admin
Solution

(a) The continuity equation says \(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}=-\nabla \cdot J\) . Here the right side is independent of t, so we can integrate: \(\rho(t)=(- \nabla \cdot J ) t+\) constant. The “constant” may be a function of r—it’s only constant with respect to t. So, putting in the r dependence explicitly, and noting that \(\nabla \cdot J =-\dot{\rho}( r , 0), \rho( r , t)=\dot{\rho}( r , 0) t+\rho( r , 0)\) . qed

(b) Suppose \(E =\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho \hat{ ᴫ }}{ ᴫ ^{2}} d \tau\) and \(B =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \times \hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ ^{2}} d \tau\) . We want to show that \( \nabla \cdot B =0, \nabla \times B =\mu_{0} J +\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial E }{\partial t} ; \nabla \cdot E =\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}} \rho, \text { and } \nabla \times E =-\frac{\partial B }{\partial t}\) , provided that J is independent of t.

We know from Ch. 2 that Coulomb’s law \(\left( E =\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho \hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}} d \tau\right)\) satisfies \(\nabla \cdot E =\frac{1}{\epsilon_{0}} \rho \text { and } \nabla \times E = 0\) .

Since B is constant (in time), the \(\nabla \cdot E \text { and } \nabla \times E\) equations are satisfied. From Chapter 5 (specifically, Eqs. 5.47- 5.50) we know that the Biot-Savart law satisfies \(\nabla \cdot B =0\) . It remains only to check \(\nabla \times B\) . The argument in Sect. 5.3.2 carries through until the equation following Eq. 5.54, where I invoked \(\nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J =0\) . In its place we now put \(\nabla ^{\prime} \cdot J =-\dot{\rho}\) :

\(B ( r )=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{ J \left( r ^{\prime}\right) \times \hat{ ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}} d \tau^{\prime}\) (5.47)

∇ · B = 0 (5.50)

\(-( J \cdot \nabla) \frac{\hat{ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}=\left( J \cdot \nabla^{\prime }\right) \frac{\hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}\) (5.54)

\(\nabla \times B =\mu_{0} J -\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \underbrace{( J \cdot \nabla ) \frac{\hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}}_{\left(- J \cdot \nabla ^{\prime}\right) \frac{\hat{ ᴫ}}{ᴫ^{2}}} d \tau \quad(\text { Eq. 5.54) } \quad \text { (Eqs. 5.51-5.53) }\)

 

\(\nabla \times B =\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \nabla \times\left( J \times \frac{\hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}\right) d \tau^{\prime}\) (5.51)

\(\nabla \cdot\left(\frac{\hat{ ᴫ}}{ ᴫ^{2}}\right)=4 \pi \delta^{3}( ᴫ)\) (5.53)

Integration by parts yields two terms, one of which becomes a surface integral, and goes to zero. The other is

\(\frac{ ᴫ}{ ᴫ^{3}} \nabla^{\prime} \cdot J =\frac{\hat{ ᴫ}}{ ᴫ^{2}}(-\dot{\rho})\) . So:

\(\nabla \times B =\mu_{0} J -\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{\hat{ ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{2}}(-\dot{\rho}) d \tau=\mu_{0} J +\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left\{\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int \frac{\rho \hat{ᴫ }}{ᴫ^{3}} d \tau\right\}=\mu_{0} J +\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial E }{\partial t}\) . qed
