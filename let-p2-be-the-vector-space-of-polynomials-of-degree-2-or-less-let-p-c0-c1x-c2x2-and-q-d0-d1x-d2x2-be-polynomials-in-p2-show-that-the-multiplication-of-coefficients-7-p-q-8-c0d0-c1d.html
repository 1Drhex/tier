Guest Question.

Let \(P_{2}\) be the vector space of polynomials of degree 2 or less. Let

\(\mathbf{p}=c_{0}+c_{1} x+c_{2} x^{2} \text { and } \mathbf{q}=d_{0}+d_{1} x+d_{2} x^{2}\)

be polynomials in \(P_{2}\). Show that the multiplication of coefficients:

\(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=c_{0} d_{0}+c_{1} d_{1}+c_{2} d_{2}\)

defines an inner product on \(P_{2}\).

[For example, if \(\mathbf{p}=2+3 x+5 x^{2}\) and \(\mathrm{q}=4-x+7 x^{2}\) then

\(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=(2 \times 4)+(3 \times(-1))+(5 \times 7)=40]\)
Admin
Solution

How do we show \(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=c_{0} d_{0}+c_{1} d_{1}+c_{2} d_{2}\) is an inner product on \(P_{2} ?\)

Checking all four axioms of Definition (4.1).

(i) We need to check \(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}\rangle\) holds for the multiplication of coefficients:

\(\begin{aligned}\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle &=c_{0} d_{0}+c_{1} d_{1}+c_{2} d_{2} \\&=d_{0} c_{0}+d_{1} c_{1}+d_{2} c_{2} \quad\left[\begin{array}{l}\text { remember, the order of multiplication } \\\text { does not matter }\end{array}\right] \\&=\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}\rangle\end{aligned}\)

(ii) We need to check \(\langle\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{r}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle:\)

Adding the two quadratics \(\mathbf{p}=c_{0}+c_{1} x+c_{2} x^{2} \quad\) and \(\quad \mathbf{q}=d_{0}+d_{1} x+d_{2} x^{2}\) gives:

\(\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}=\left(c_{0}+d_{0}\right)+\left(c_{1}+d_{1}\right) x+\left(c_{2}+d_{2}\right) x^{2}\)

Let \(\mathbf{r}=e_{0}+e_{1} x+e_{2} x^{2}\). Finding the inner product of \(\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}\) and \(\mathbf{r}\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\langle\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle&=\left\langle\left(c_{0}+d_{0}\right)+\left(c_{1}+d_{1}\right) x+\left(c_{2}+d_{2}\right) x^{2}, e_{0}+e_{1} x+e_{2} x^{2}\right\rangle \\&=\left(c_{0}+d_{0}\right) e_{0}+\left(c_{1}+d_{1}\right) e_{1}+\left(c_{2}+d_{2}\right) e_{2} \quad[\text { multiplying coefficients }] \\&=c_{0} e_{0}+d_{0} e_{0}+c_{1} e_{1}+d_{1} e_{1}+c_{2} e_{2}+d_{2} e_{2}\end{aligned}\)

Evaluating the addition of the other inner products:

\(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{r}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle=\underbrace{c_{0} e_{0}+c_{1} e_{1}+c_{2} e_{2}}_{=\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{r}\rangle}+\underbrace{d_{0} e_{0}+d_{1} e_{1}+d_{2} e_{2}}_{=\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle} \\ \begin{array}{ll}=c_{0} e_{0}+d_{0} e_{0}+c_{1} e_{1}+d_{1} e_{1}+c_{2} e_{2}+d_{2} e_{2} & {[\text { rearranging }]} \\ =\langle\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle & {[\text { from above }]}\end{array}\)

Hence part (ii) is satisfied \(-\langle\mathbf{p}+\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{r}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}\rangle\).

(iii) We need to check \(\langle k \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=k\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle \quad[\) taking out the scalar \(k]\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\langle k \mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle &=\left\langle k\left(c_{0}+c_{1} x+c_{2} x^{2}\right), d_{0}+d_{1} x+d_{2} x^{2}\right\rangle \\ &=\left\langle k c_{0}+k c_{1} x+k c_{2} x^{2}, d_{0}+d_{1} x+d_{2} x^{2}\right\rangle \quad[\text { opening brackets }] \\ &=k c_{0} d_{0}+k c_{1} d_{1}+k c_{2} d_{2} \quad[\text { multiplying coefficients }] \\ &=k\left(c_{0} d_{0}+c_{1} d_{1}+c_{2} d_{2}\right) \quad[\text { taking out a common factor }] \\ &=k\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle \end{aligned}\)

Hence part (iii) is satisfied.

(iv) We need to show \(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}\rangle \geq 0\) and that we have \(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}\rangle=0 \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{p}=\mathbf{O}\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}\rangle &=\left\langle c_{0}+c_{1} x+c_{2} x^{2}, c_{0}+c_{1} x+c_{2} x^{2}\right\rangle \\&=c_{0} c_{0}+c_{1} c_{1}+c_{2} c_{2}=\left(c_{0}\right)^{2}+\left(c_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(c_{2}\right)^{2} \geq 0\end{aligned}\)

Also

\(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{p}\rangle=\left(c_{0}\right)^{2}+\left(c_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(c_{2}\right)^{2}=0 \Leftrightarrow c_{0}=c_{1}=c_{2}=0\)

If \(c_{0}=c_{1}=c_{2}=0\) then \(\mathrm{p}=\mathbf{O}\)

All four axioms are satisfied, therefore multiplication of coefficients:

\(\langle\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}\rangle=c_{0} d_{0}+c_{1} d_{1}+c_{2} d_{2}\)

forms an inner product on \(P_{2}\)

Definition (4.1): An inner product on a real vector space \(V\) is an operation which assigns to each pair of vectors, \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\), a unique real number \(\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle\) which satisfies the following axioms for all vectors \(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) in \(V\) and all scalars \(k\).

(i) \(\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}\rangle \quad\) [commutative law]

(ii) \(\langle\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}\rangle+\langle\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}\rangle \quad\) [distributive law \(]\)

(iii) \(\langle k \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle=k\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle \quad[\) taking out the scalar \(k]\)

(iv) \(\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u}\rangle \geq 0\) and we have \(\langle\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u}\rangle=0 \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{O}\) between the same vectors is zero or positive.]
