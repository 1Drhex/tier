Guest Question.

A sinusoidal voltage \(V_{d}(t)=V_{m} \sin (\omega t)\) is applied across the deflecting plates of a cathode-ray tube where \(V_{m}\) and \( \omega\) are the amplitude and frequency of the applied potential. The transit time between the plates is \( \tau \) . The length of the line on the screen is A. If A0 is the line length when the transit time is negligible compared with the period of the applied voltage, show that

\( A=A_{0}\frac{\sin (\omega \tau /2)}{(\omega \tau )} \)

Admin
Solution

Consider the coordinate system as shown in Fig. 1.4. Since the electric field is in the –Y direction, the force equation are given as

\(f_{y}=m\frac{d^2y}{dt^2}=\frac{eV_{d}(t)}{d} =\frac{eV_{m}\sin (\omega t)}{d} \) and fx = fz = 0

Subjecting to the initial conditions y = 0 and \(v_{y}=\frac{dy}{dt} =0\) t = 0 , the displacement equation of the electron in the Y direction is given by

\(y=\frac{eV_{m}}{dm\omega } \left\lgroup t-\frac{\sin (\omega t)}{\omega } \right\rgroup \)

Since no force is acting on the electron along the X or Z direction, with the initial conditions vx = v0x and vz = 0 at t = 0, the displacements along the X and Y directions are given as

x = v0x t and z = 0

Substituting \(t=\frac{x}{v_{ox}} \) in the equation of y, the path of the electron is given as

\(y=\frac{eV_{m}}{dm\omega } \left\lgroup \frac{x}{v_{ox}} -\frac{\sin (\frac{\omega x}{v_{ox}} )}{\omega } \right\rgroup \)

Now, for x > 1, the path becomes a straight line MP′ (as shown in Fig.1.4) with slope

\(\tan \theta =\frac{dy}{dx}|_{x=1}=\frac{eV_{m}}{dm\omega v_{ox}}\left\lgroup1-\sin \frac{\omega 1}{v_{ox}} \right\rgroup \)

\(=\frac{eV_{m}\tau }{dm\omega 1} (1-\cos (\omega \tau )) \)

where \( \tau =\frac{1}{v_{ox}} \) is the transit time.

Now, the total deflection line-length PP′ on the screen is given by

\( A=\left\lgroup L-\frac{1}{2} \right\rgroup \tan \theta +y_{1} =\left\lgroup\frac{eV_{m}\tau ^2\left\lgroup L-\frac{1}{2} \right\rgroup }{dm\omega l} \right\rgroup \sin \left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2} \right\rgroup \frac{\sin \left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2}\right\rgroup }{\left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2}\right\rgroup }+y_{1} \)

where y1 is the displacement of the electron from the \({X}\) axis at \({x} = l\) which is written as

\(y=y_{1}=\frac{eV_{m}}{dm\omega }\left\lgroup\tau -\frac{\sin (\omega \tau )}{\omega } \right\rgroup \)

\(= \frac{eV_{m}}{dm}\left\{\frac{\tau }{\omega }-\frac{1}{\omega ^2}\left\lgroup\omega \tau -\frac{(\omega \tau )^3}{3!}+ \frac{(\omega \tau )^5}{5!}- ….. \right\rgroup \right\} \)

\(= \frac{eV_{m}}{dm} \left\lgroup \frac{(\omega \tau )\tau ^2}{3!}-\frac{(\omega \tau )^3\tau ^2}{5!}+…..\right\rgroup \)

Since \(\omega \tau =2\pi \left\lgroup\frac{\tau }{T}\right\rgroup \) where T is the period of the applied voltage, y1 = 0 for \(\tau\). However, for higher frequencies of the applied voltage, where T is very small but \(\tau\) is comparable with T resulting in the finite value of \(\omega\tau\), y1 again becomes very small because of the very small values of \(\tau\) . Thus, we may say that for any finite frequency of the applied voltage, y1 can always be neglected as compared to the total deflection of the beam on the screen. Therefore, the total deflection line-length on the screen may be approximately written as

\(A \approx \left\lgroup\frac{eV_{m}\tau ^2 \left\lgroup L-\frac{1}{2} \right\rgroup } {dm\omega l} \right\rgroup \sin \left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2} \right\rgroup \frac{\sin \left\lgroup \frac{\omega \tau }{2} \right\rgroup }{\left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2}\right\rgroup } \)

For, t << T

\(\frac{\sin \left\lgroup \frac{\omega \tau }{2} \right\rgroup }{\left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2}\right\rgroup } \) ≈ 1

hence the line-length A0 can be given by

\(A_{0} \approx \left\lgroup\frac{eV_{m}\tau ^2 \left\lgroup L-\frac{1}{2} \right\rgroup } {dm\omega l} \right\rgroup \sin \left\lgroup\frac{\omega \tau }{2} \right\rgroup \)

Now, the line-length on the screen A for all values of \(\tau\) and T may be expressed in the form of the desired result as

\(A = A_{0}=\frac{\sin (\omega \tau /2)}{\omega \tau /2} \)
