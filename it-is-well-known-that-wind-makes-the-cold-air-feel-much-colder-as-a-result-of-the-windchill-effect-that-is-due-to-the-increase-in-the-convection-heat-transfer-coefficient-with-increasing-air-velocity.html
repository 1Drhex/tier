Guest Question.

It is well known that wind makes the cold air feel much colder as a result of the windchill effect that is due to the increase in the convection heat transfer coefficient with increasing air velocity. The windchill effect is usually expressed in terms of the windchill factor, which is the difference between the actual air temperature and the equivalent calm-air temperature. For example, a windchill factor of 20°C for an actual air temperature of 5°C means that the windy air at 5°C feels as cold as the still air at 15°C. In other words, a person will lose as much heat to air at 5°C with a windchill factor of 20°C as he or she would in calm air at 15°C. For heat transfer purposes, a standing man can be modeled as a 30-cm-diameter, 170-cm-long vertical cylinder with both the top and bottom surfaces insulated and with the side surface at an average temperature of 34°C. For a convection heat transfer coefficient of \( 15 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \), determine the rate of heat loss from this man by convection in still air at 20°C. What would your answer be if the convection heat transfer coefficient is increased to \( 50 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \) as a result of winds? What is the windchill factor in this case?

Admin
Solution

A standing man is subjected to high winds and thus high convection coefficients. The rate of heat loss from this man by convection in still air at 20°C, in windy air, and the wind-chill factor are to be determined.

Assumptions 1 A standing man can be modeled as a 30-cm diameter, 170-cm long vertical cylinder with both the top and bottom surfaces insulated. 2 The exposed surface temperature of the person and the convection heat transfer coefficient is constant and uniform. 3 Heat loss by radiation is negligible.

Analysis The heat transfer surface area of the person is

\( A_{s}=\pi D L=\pi(0.3 m )(1.70 m ) = 1.60 m ^{2} \)

 

The rate of heat loss from this man by convection in still air is

\( Q_{\text {still air }}=h A_{s} \Delta T=\left(15 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(1.60 m ^{2}\right)(34-20)^{\circ} C = 3 3 6 W\)

 

In windy air it would be

\( Q_{\text {windy air }}=h A_{s} \Delta T=\left(50 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(1.60 m ^{2}\right)(34-20){ }^{\circ} C = 1 1 2 0 W \)

 

To lose heat at this rate in still air, the air temperature must be

\( 1120 W =\left(h A_{s} \Delta T\right)_{\text {still air }}=\left(15 W / m ^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} C \right)\left(1.60 m ^{2}\right)\left(34-T_{\text {effective }}\right)^{\circ} C\)

 

which gives

\( T_{\text {effective }}=-12.7^{\circ} C \)

 

That is, the windy air at 20°C feels as cold as still air at -12.7°C as a result of the wind-chill effect. Therefore, the wind-chill factor in this case is

\( F_{\text {wind-chill }}=20-(-12.7)=32.7^{\circ} C\)
