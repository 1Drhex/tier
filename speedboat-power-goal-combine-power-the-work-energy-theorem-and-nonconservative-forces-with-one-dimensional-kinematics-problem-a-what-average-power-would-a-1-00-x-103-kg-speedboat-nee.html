Guest Question.

Speedboat Power

GOAL Combine power, the work–energy theorem, and nonconservative forces with one-dimensional kinematics.

PROBLEM (a) What average power would a \(1.00 \times 10^{3}- kg\) speedboat need to go from rest to 20.0 m/s in 5.00 s, assuming the water exerts a constant drag force of magnitude \(f_{d}=5.00 \times 10^{2} N\) and the acceleration is constant. (b) Find an expression for the instantaneous power in terms of the drag force \(f_{d}\), the mass m, acceleration a, and time t.

STRATEGY The power is provided by the engine, which creates a nonconservative force. Use the work–energy theorem together with the work done by the engine, \(W_{engine}\), and the work done by the drag force, \(W_{drag}\), on the left-hand side. Use one-dimensional kinematics to find the acceleration and then the displacement \(\Delta x\). Solve the work–energy theorem for \(W_{engine}\), and divide by the elapsed time to get the average power. For part (b), use Newton’s second law to obtain an example for \(F_{E}\), and then substitute into the definition of instantaneous power.

Admin
Solution

(a) Write the work–energy theorem: \(W_{ net }=\Delta K E=\frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}-\frac{1}{2} m v_{i}^{2}\)

 

Fill in the two work terms and take \(v_{i}=0\) : (1) \(W_{\text {engine }}+W_{\text {drag }}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}\)

 

To get the displacement \(\Delta x\), first find the acceleration using the velocity equation of kinematics: \(\begin{aligned}v_{f} &=a t+v_{i} \rightarrow v_{f}=a t \\20.0 m / s &=a(5.00 s ) \quad \rightarrow \quad a=4.00 m / s ^{2}\end{aligned}\)

 

Substitute a into the time-independent kinematics equation and solve for \(\Delta x\) : \(\begin{aligned}v_{f}^{2}-v_{i}^{2} &=2 a \Delta x \\(20.0 m / s )^{2}-0^{2} &=2\left(4.00 m / s ^{2}\right) \Delta x \\\Delta x &=50.0 m\end{aligned}\)

 

Now that we know \(\Delta x\), we can find the mechanical energy lost due to the drag force: \(W_{\text {drag }}=-f_{d} \Delta x=-\left(5.00 \times 10^{2} N \right)(50.0 m )=-2.50 \times 10^{4} J\)

 

Solve equation (1) for \(W_{engine}\) : \(\begin{aligned}W_{\text {engine }} &=\frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}-W_{\text {drag }} \\&=\frac{1}{2}\left(1.00 \times 10^{3} kg \right)(20.0 m / s )^{2}-\left(-2.50 \times 10^{4} J \right) \\W_{\text {engine }} &=2.25 \times 10^{5} J\end{aligned}\)

 

Compute the average power: \(\bar{P}=\frac{W_{\text {engine }}}{\Delta t}=\frac{2.25 \times 10^{5} J }{5.00 s }=4.50 \times 10^{4} W =60.3 hp\)

 

(b) Find a symbolic expression for the instantaneous power.

Use Newton’s second law: \(m a=F_{E}-f_{d}\)

 

Solve for the force exerted by the engine, \(F_{E}\): \(F_{E}=m a+f_{d}\)

 

Substitute the expression for \(F_{E} \text { and } v=a t\) into Equation 5.25 to obtain the instantaneous power: \(\begin{aligned}&P=F_{E} v=\left(m a+f_{d}\right)(a t) \\&P=\left(m a^{2}+a f_{d}\right) t\end{aligned}\)

 

\(P=F v\) [5.25]

 

REMARKS In fact, drag forces generally get larger with increasing speed.
