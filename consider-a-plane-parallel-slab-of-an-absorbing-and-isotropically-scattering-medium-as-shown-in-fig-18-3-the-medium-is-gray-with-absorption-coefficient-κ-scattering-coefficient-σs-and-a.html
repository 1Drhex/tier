Guest Question.

Consider a plane-parallel slab of an absorbing and isotropically scattering medium as shown in Fig. 18-3. The medium is gray (with absorption coefficient κ, scattering coefficient \(σ_{s}\), and a refractive index of n = 1), cold (i.e., essentially nonemitting) and of constant thickness L, At the top (z = 0) the layer is bounded by a nonparticipating gas (n=1) and is exposed to solar radiation impinging at \(θ_{0}\) off-normal. At the bottom of the layer (z = L) the medium is bounded by a cold black surface.
Determine radiative heat flux (and its divergence) as functions of depth.

Admin
Solution

Since both media have the same index of refraction the interface reflectivity is ρ =0 and \(\widehat{s}_{c}=\widehat{s}_{0}\) equation (18.11) \(I_{c}(r,\widehat{s})=[1-\rho (r_{w})]q_{0}(r_{w})\delta [\widehat{s}-\widehat{s}_{c}(r_{w})]e^{-\tau_{c} }\) we find immediately

\(I_{c}(\tau ,\widehat{s} ) =q_{s}e^{-\tau /\mu _{0}}\delta (\widehat{s}-\widehat{s}_{0})\)

as well as

\(S_{c}(\tau )=\frac{G_{c}}{4\pi }(\tau ) =\frac{q_{s}}{4\pi }e^{-\tau /\mu _{0}} ,\)
\(q_{c}(\tau )=q_{s}e^{-\tau/\mu _{0} }\widehat{s}_{0}.\)

The heat flux vector due to collimated irradiation has two components, one in the direction of τ, the other in a direction normal to it (in the plane formed by \(\widehat{s}_{0}\) and the surface normal). Thus, the overall problem is two-dimensional. However, inspection of the source term in equation (18.12) \(\frac{1}{\beta }\widehat{s} \cdot \triangledown I_{d}(r,\widehat{s} )=\widehat{s} \cdot \triangledown _{T}I_{d}(r,\widehat{s} )= -I_{d}(r,\widehat{s} )+\frac{w}{4\pi }\int_{4\pi }^{}{}I_{d}(r,\widehat{s}^{\prime } )\Phi (\widehat{s} ,\widehat{s} ^{\prime })d\Omega ^{\prime } +(1-w)I_{b}(r)+wS_{c}(r,\widehat{s} ),\) shows that the source is isotropic, as are the boundary conditions for equation (18.12). Therefore, \({I}_{d}\) can depend only on distance perpendicular to the surfaces τ , and on polar angle. Thus,

\(\mu \frac{dI_{d}}{d\tau } +I_{d}=w\left[\frac{1}{4\pi }\int_{4\pi }^{}{} I_{d}d\Omega ^{\prime }+ S_{c} \right]=\frac{w}{4\pi }[G_{d}(\tau )+G_{c}(\tau )]\)

The boundary conditions are

\(\tau =0: I(0,\widehat{s} )=q_{s}\delta (\widehat{s}-\widehat{s}_{0}), 0\leq \theta \lt \frac{\pi }{2} ,\)
\(\tau =_{L}: I(\tau _{L},\widehat{s} )=0 , \frac{\pi }{2}\lt \theta \leq \pi ,\)

or, after subtracting the coUimated component,

\(\tau =0: I_{d}(0,\mu )=0, 0\lt \mu \leq 1,\)
\(\tau =_{L}: I_{d}(\tau ,\mu )=0, -1\leq \mu \lt 0.\)

Therefore, with \(S=(w/4\pi )(G_{d}+G_{c})\), the solution for \({I}_{d}\) is, from equation (13.21) \(G(\tau )=2\pi \left\{\int_{0}^{1}{I_{1}(\mu )e^{-\tau /\mu }d\mu }+\int_{0}^{1}{}I_{2}(-\mu )e^{-(\tau_{L}-\tau ) /\mu }d\mu + \int_{0}^{1}{}\left[\int_{0}^{\tau }{}S(\tau ^{\prime },\mu ) e^{-(\tau-\tau^{\prime } ) /\mu }d\tau ^{\prime } +\int_{\tau }^{\tau _{L}}{} S(\tau ^{\prime },-\mu ) e^{-(\tau^{\prime }-\tau ) /\mu }d\tau ^{\prime } \right]\frac{d\mu }{\mu } \right\}\) ,

\(G_{d}(\tau )=2\pi \int_{0}^{\tau _{L}}{}\frac{w}{4\pi } (G_{d}+G_{c})(\tau ^{\prime })E_{1}(\left|\tau -^{\prime }\right| )d\tau ^{\prime }\).

This integral equation must be solved numerically in the same fashion as equation (13.41) \(\Phi _{b}(\tau )=\frac{1}{2}\left[E_{2}(\tau )+\int_{0}^{\tau _{L}}{}\Phi _{b}(\tau ^{\prime })E_{1}(\left|\tau -\tau ^{\prime }\right| )d \tau ^{\prime }\right]\) . Once the function Φ(τ) has been determined, the diffuse component of the heat flux is found from equation (13.22) \(q(\tau )=\int_{0}^{2\pi }{}\int_{0}^{\pi }{}I(\tau ,\theta )\cos \theta \sin \theta d\theta d\psi =2\pi \int_{-1}^{+1}{}I(\tau ,\mu )\mu d\mu\)
\(= 2\pi \left\{\int_{0}^{1}{I_{1}(\mu )e^{-\tau /\mu }\mu d\mu }-\int_{0}^{1}{}I_{2}(-\mu )e^{-(\tau_{L}-\tau ) /\mu }\mu d\mu + \int_{0}^{1}{}\left[\int_{0}^{\tau }{}S(\tau ^{\prime },\mu ) e^{-(\tau-\tau^{\prime } ) /\mu }d\tau ^{\prime } -\int_{\tau }^{\tau _{L}}{} S(\tau ^{\prime },-\mu ) e^{-(\tau^{\prime }-\tau ) /\mu }d\tau ^{\prime } \right]d\mu \right\}\) as

\(q_{d}(\tau )=q_{d}(\tau )\widehat{k} =2\pi \int_{-1}^{1}{I_{d}\mu d\mu \widehat{k} } ,\)

\(q_{d}(\tau )=2\pi \left\{\int_{0}^{\tau }{}\frac{w}{4\pi }(G_{d}+G_{c})(\tau ^{\prime })E_{2}(\tau -\tau ^{\prime })d \tau ^{\prime }-\int_{\tau }^{\tau _{L}}{} \frac{w}{4\pi }(G_{d}+G_{c})(\tau ^{\prime })E_{2}(\tau -\tau ^{\prime })d \tau ^{\prime } \right\}\)

Finally, the total heat flux in the r-direction, on a nondimensional basis, is

\(\Psi =\frac{q_{c}\cdot \widehat{k}+ q_{d} }{q_{s}}=\mu _{o} e^{-\tau /\mu _{0}}+\frac{w}{2}\left[\int_{0}^{\tau }{\Phi (\tau ^{\prime })E_{2}(\tau -\tau ^{\prime })d\tau ^{\prime } }- \int_{\tau }^{\tau _{L}}{}\Phi (\tau ^{\prime })E_{2}(\tau ^{\prime }-\tau )d\tau ^{\prime } \right] .\)

The divergence of the radiative heat flux is found from equation (9.53) \(\triangledown \cdot q_{\eta }=\kappa _{\eta }\left(4\pi I_{b\eta }-\int_{4\pi }^{}{}I_{\eta }d\Omega \right) =\kappa _{\eta }(4\pi I_{b\eta }-G_{\eta })\) as

\(\triangledown _{T}\cdot q= (1-w)(4\pi I_{b}-G)=-(1-w)(G_{c}+G_{d})\),

or in nondimensional form

\(\frac{1}{q_{s}}\triangledown _{T}\cdot q=-(1-w)\Phi\)

Some results for a purely scattering medium (w = 1) are shown in Fig. 18-4. For that case we find \(\triangledown \cdot q\) = 0 and, since the tangential component of \(q_{c}\) . does not depend on tangential direction, \(q\cdot \widehat{k}\) = const and Ψ = const. Thus, evaluating the heat flux at τ = 0, we get

\(\Psi (w=1)=\mu _{o}-\frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{\tau _{L}}{}\Phi (\tau ^{\prime }) E_{2}(\tau ^{\prime })d\tau ^{\prime }\).
