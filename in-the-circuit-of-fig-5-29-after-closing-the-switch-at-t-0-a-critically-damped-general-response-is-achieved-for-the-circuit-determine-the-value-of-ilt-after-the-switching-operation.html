Guest Question.

In the circuit of Fig. 5.29, after closing the switch at t = 0, a critically damped general response is achieved for the circuit. Determine the value of \(I_{L}(t)\) after the switching operation.

Admin
Solution

6.29. Based on the given information, the circuit will be in critically damped condition if the switch is closed (t > 0).
Therefore, the delta (discriminant) of the characteristic equation of the circuit shown in Fig. 6.29.2 must be zero (Δ = 0).
Now, we need to determine the differential equation of the circuit.

KVL in the left-side mesh:

\(V_{L}(t)-V_{C}(t)=0\Rightarrow V_{C}(t)=V_{L}(t)\) …….(1)

The voltage-current relation of the capacitor:

\(I_{C}(t)=C\frac{d}{dt}V_{C}(t)=10^{-6}\frac{d}{dt}V_{C}(t)\)

Using (1)\(\Rightarrow I_{C}(t)=10^{-6}\frac{d}{dt}V_{C}(t)\)…….(2)

The current-voltage relation of the inductor:

\(V_{L}(t)=L\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t)=4×\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t)\) ……(3)

KCL in the supernode:

\(I_{C}(t)+I_{L}(t)+\frac{1}{R}V_{L}(t)=0\) …….(4)

Applying (2) in (4):

\(10^{-6}\frac{d}{dt}V_{C}(t)+I_{L}(t)+\frac{1}{R}V_{L}(t)=0\)

Using (3)\(\Rightarrow 10^{-6}\frac{d}{dt}(4×\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t) )+I_{L}(t)+\frac{1}{R}(4×\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t) )=0\)

\(\Rightarrow 4×10^{-6}×\frac{d²}{dt²}I_{L}(t)+I_{L}(t)+\frac{4}{R}\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t)=0\)

\(\Rightarrow \frac{d²}{dt²}I_{L}(t)+\frac{10^{6}}{R}\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t)+\frac{10^{6}}{4}I_{L}(t)=0\) …….(6)

Therefore, the characteristic equation of the circuit is:

\(s²+\frac{10^{6}}{R}s+\frac{10^{6}}{4}=\)0 …….(7)

\(Δ=0\Rightarrow (\frac{10^{6}}{R})²-4(1)(\frac{10^{6}}{4})² =0 \Rightarrow \frac{10^{6}}{R}²=10^{6}\Rightarrow \frac{10^{6}}{R}=±10^{3} \Rightarrow R=±1000Ω \)……..(8)

In (8), just the positive value of the resistor, that is, R = 1000 Ω, is acceptable.
Now, we can analyze the circuit for t = \(0^{-}\), shown in Fig. 6.29.3.
Using the current division formula:

\(I_{L}(0^{-})=\frac{1000}{1000+250}×0.5\) = 0.4 A ……..(9)

Using Ohm’s law for the 250 Ω resistor:

\(V_{L}(0^{-})=I_{L}(0^{-})×250=0.4×250 \)= 100 V ……..(10)

Due to the current continuity of the inductor in lack of a power source with an infinite size (e.g., impulse power source):

\(I_{L}(0^{+}) = I_{L}(0^{-})=\)0.4 A ……..(11)

Due to the voltage continuity of the capacitor in lack of a power source with an infinite size (e.g., impulse power source):

\(V_{C}(0^{+})=V_{C}(0^{-})=\) 100 V …….(12)

Now, we need to come back to the main problem (analyzing the circuit for t > 0), shown in Fig. 6.29.4. Solving (7) and (8):

\(s²+\frac{10^{6}}{1000}s+\frac{10^{6}}{4}=0\Rightarrow s²+1000s+250000=0\Rightarrow (s+500)^{2}=0\)

\(\Rightarrow\) s= 500 , -500 ……..(13)

Therefore, the general form of the current of the inductor is:

\(I_{L}(t)=(A+Bt)e^{-500t}u(t) \)……..(14)

Now, we need to determine the value of its parameters (“A” and “B”):
Applying (11) in (14):

\(0.4=(A+B×0^{+})e^{-500×0^{+}}\Rightarrow A=0.4\) ……..(15)

As can be seen in Fig. 6.29.4, the inductor and the capacitor are in parallel and by using (12):

\(V_{L}(0^{+})=V_{C}(0^{+})=\) 100 V ……..(16)

Using (3) and (16) for t = \(0^{+}\) :

100= \(4\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(0^{+})\Rightarrow \frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(0^{+})\) = 25 A/sec ………(17)

Calculating the first derivative of (14):

\(\frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(t)=\frac{d}{dt}(A+Bt)e^{-500t}=Be^{-500t}-500e^{-500t}(A+Bt)\)

for t= \(0^{+}\Rightarrow \frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(0^{+})= Be^{-500×0^{+}}-500e^{-500×0^{+}}(A+B×0^{+})=B-500A\)

Using (15)\(\Rightarrow \frac{d}{dt}I_{L}(0^{+})=\) B – 500 × 0.4= B – 200 ……..(18)

Solving (17) and (18):

25 = B – 200\(\Rightarrow \) B= 225 …….(19)

Applying (15) and (19) in (14):

\(I_{L}(t)= ( 0.4+225t) e^{-500t} u(t) \)
