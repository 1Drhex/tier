Guest Question.

Consider intrinsic germanium at room temperature (300°K). By what percent does the conduc- tivity increase per degree rise in temperature?

Admin
Solution

Substituting \(n=p=n_{i}=\sqrt{A_{0}}T^{3/2} exp \left\lgroup-\frac{E_{GO}}{2kT} \right\rgroup \) from Eq. (4.3) , \(n^2_{i}=A_{0}T^3exp(-E_{GO}/kT) \) , in Eq. (4.2), \(\sigma =(n\mu _{n}+p\mu _{p})e \) , we can write the expression for conductivity of any intrinsic semiconductor as

\(\sigma =\left\{e(\mu _{n}+\mu _{P})\sqrt{A_{0}} \right\} T^{3/2}exp\left\lgroup-\frac{E_{GO}}{2kT}\right\rgroup \)

Taking logarithm of both sides and differentiating with respect to T, we can get

\(\frac{\left\lgroup\frac{d\sigma }{\sigma } \right\rgroup }{dT}=\frac{3}{2T}+\frac{E_{GO}}{2kT^2} \)

since \(\left\{e(\mu _{n}+\mu _{p})\sqrt{A_{0}} \right\} \) is a constant.

Putting T = 300°K, EGO = 0.785 eV (i.e. forbidden or band-gap energy of germanium at 0°K) and k = 8.62 × \(10^{−5}\) eV/°K in the above relation, we can get

\(\frac{\left\lgroup\frac{d\sigma }{\sigma } \right\rgroup }{dT}=\frac{3}{2\times 300^{\circ }K}+\frac{0.785eV}{2\times 8.62\times 10^{-5}(eV/^{\circ }K)(300^{\circ }K)^2} \)

= 0.0556/°K

which shows that the conductivity of the intrinsic germanium increases by 5.56% per degree (Kelvin) rise in temperature.
