Guest Question.

Suppose a worker’s wage is w and the marginal income tax rate, \(\nu\), is constant, so \(\underline{\omega } = (1 - \nu)ω\) is the worker’s after-tax wage, and the worker supplies
\(H([1 - \nu]ω) = H(\underline{\omega } )\) hours of work. What is the effect of an increase in \(\nu\)on the tax revenue collected? Show that the change in tax revenue in response to an increase in \(\nu\)depends on a direct effect of a higher tax rate and the labor supply response to a higher tax rate. For what elasticity of supply of labor does tax revenue rise if \(\nu\)falls?

Admin
Solution

1. Determine the government’s tax revenue. The government’s tax revenue, T, is v times a worker’s earnings (the wage, ω, times the hours worked):

\(T = \nu \omega H([1-\nu ]\omega )=\nu \omega H(\underline{\omega })\) (5.12)

2. By differentiating Equation 5.12 with respect to \(\nu\), show how the tax revenue changes as the tax rate increases. Using the product rule and the chain rule, the
change in T as v increases is

\(\frac{dT}{d\nu}= \omega H(\underline{\omega })+\nu \omega \frac{dH}{d\underline{\omega } }\frac{d\underline{\omega }}{d\nu } = \omega H(\underline{\omega }) -\nu \omega ^{2} \frac{dH}{d\underline{\omega } }\) (5.13)

3. Show that \(dT/d\nu\) reflects both a direct tax effect and labor supply response.Equation 5.13 shows that a change in the tax rate has two effects. First, the government collects more revenue because of the higher tax rate: A one-unit increase in v causes the tax revenue to increase by \(\omega H(\underline{\omega })\), the amount that the worker earns. Second, the change in the tax alters the hours worked. As the rate goes up, before-tax labor earnings, \(\omega H(\underline{\omega })\), decrease if the labor supply is upward sloping, \(dH/d\underline{\omega } >0\), which reduces the tax revenue by \(vw^{2}dH/d\underline{\omega }\).

4. Determine a condition under which tax revenue rises when the tax rate falls. According to Equation 5.13, for the tax revenue to rise when the tax rate decreases (or to decrease when the tax rate increases), we need
\(dT/d\nu = \omega H(\underline{\omega }) – vw^{2}dH/d\underline{\omega }\)<0. Using algebra, we can rewrite this condition as

\(\frac{1}{\nu } \lt \frac{dH}{d\underline{\omega }} \frac{\omega }{H(\underline{\omega })}\) (5.14)

5. Express the condition in Equation 5.14 in terms of the elasticity of supply of labor. If we multiply both sides of Equation 5.14 by \((1 – \nu)\), we obtain the condition that

\(\frac{1-\nu }{\nu} \lt \frac{dH}{d\underline{\omega }} \frac{(1-\nu )\omega }{H(\underline{\omega })}= \frac{dH}{d\underline{\omega }} \frac{\underline{\omega } }{H(\underline{\omega })}=\eta\) (5.15)

where \( η= [dH/d\underline{\omega }][\underline{\omega }/H(\underline{\omega })]\) is the elasticity of supply of work hours with respect to after-tax wages, \(\underline{\omega }\).

Comment: Thus, for the tax revenue the government collects to rise from a small decrease in the tax rate, the elasticity of supply of labor must be greater than \(({1-\nu })/{\nu}\). In the United States in 2018, a single person with taxable income between $38,701 and $82,500 had a marginal tax rate of \(\nu\)= 22%. For a small decrease in this rate to raise the tax revenue collected, such a person’s had to be greater than 0.78/0.22 ≈ 3.5, which is not likely. In the past, before the Kennedy-era tax cuts, the top U.S. marginal tax rate was \(\nu\)= 91%. For this rate, the condition is met if the elasticity of supply is greater than about 0.11.
