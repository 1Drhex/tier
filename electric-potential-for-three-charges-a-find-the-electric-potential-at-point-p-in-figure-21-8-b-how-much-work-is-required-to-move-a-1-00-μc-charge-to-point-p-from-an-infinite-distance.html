Guest Question.

Electric Potential for Three Charges

(a) Find the electric potential at point P in Figure 21-8.

(b) How much work is required to move a \(+1.00 \mu C\) charge to point P from an infinite distance?

Admin
Solution

(a) According to the superposition principle, we can find the electric potential at point P due to each of the charges separately, and then simply add the three individual potentials algebraically. We use Equation 21-10 with the appropriate charge and the distance from each charge to point P. Distances \(r_{1} \text { and } r_{3}\) are both \(\sqrt{2.00} m\) :

 

\(V=\frac{Q}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r}\) {21-10}

 

\(V=\frac{q_{1}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r_{1}}+\frac{q_{2}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r_{2}}+\frac{q_{3}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r_{3}}\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left(\frac{-4.00 \times 10^{-6} C }{\sqrt{2.00 m }}+\frac{+8.00 \times 10^{-6} C }{1.00 m }\right.\) \(\left.+\frac{-4.00 \times 10^{-6} C }{\sqrt{2.00 m }}\right)\)

 

\(=\frac{1 C / m }{4 \pi \times 8.85 \times 10^{-12} C ^{2} /\left( N \cdot m ^{2}\right)}\) \(\times\left(-2.828 \times 10^{-6}+8.00 \times 10^{-6}-2.828 \times 10^{-6}\right)\)

 

\(=+2.11 \times 10^{4} V\)

 

(b) When the \(+1.00 \mu C\) charge is infinitely far away, it will have an electric potential of zero, so the potential difference between that location and point P is +21.1 kV. Since the electric potential is the electrical potential energy per unit charge, the electrical potential energy when the \(1.00 \mu C\) charge is at point P is

 

\(\left(+1.00 \times 10^{-6} C \right)\left(+2.11 \times 10^{4} V \right)=+0.0211 J\)

 

Therefore, the work required to move the charge from infinitely far away to point P is 0.0211 J, or 21.1 mJ.

 

Making sense of the result

 

Although there are equal amounts of positive and negative charge in the initial configuration, the net electric potential is positive because point P is closer to the positive charge than to the negative charges. You may be surprised by how large a potential difference is produced by a small fraction of a coulomb of charge. Remember from Chapter 19 that a coulomb is a huge amount of charge, with most charging situations involving \(\mu C\) or less. In part (b), we are moving a positive charge to a region with a more positive electric potential; therefore, the work required is positive.
