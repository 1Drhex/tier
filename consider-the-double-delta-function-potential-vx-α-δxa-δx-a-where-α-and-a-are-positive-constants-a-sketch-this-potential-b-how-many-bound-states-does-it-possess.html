Guest Question.

Consider the double delta-function potential

V(x) = – α [δ(x+a) + δ(x-a)],

where α and a are positive constants.

(a) Sketch this potential.

(b) How many bound states does it possess? Find the allowed energies, for \( α=\hbar ^2/ma \) and for \( α=\hbar ^2/4ma\) , and sketch the wave functions.

(c) What are the bound state energies in the limiting cases (i) a→0 and (ii) a→ ∞ (holding α fixed)? Explain why your answers are reasonable, by comparison with the single delta-function well.

(b) From the solutions are even or odd. Look first for even solutions:

\( \psi(x)= \begin{cases}A e^{-\kappa x} & (x>a) \\ B\left(e^{\kappa x}+e^{-\kappa x}\right) & (-a<x<a) \\ A e^{\kappa x} & (x<-a)\end{cases} \).

Continuity at a : \( A e^{-\kappa a}=B\left(e^{\kappa a}+e^{-\kappa a}\right) \), or \( A=B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}+1\right) \).

Discontinuous derivative at a; \( \Delta \frac{d \psi}{d x}=-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2}} \psi(a) \):

\( -\kappa A e^{-\kappa a}-B\left(\kappa e^{\kappa a}-\kappa e^{-\kappa a}\right)=-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2}} A e^{-\kappa a} \Rightarrow A+B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}-1\right)=\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa} A \); or

\( B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}-1\right)=A\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right)=B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}+1\right) \mid\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right) \Rightarrow e^{2 \kappa a}-1=e^{2 \kappa a}\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right)+\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1 \).

\( 1=\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1+\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa} e^{-2 \kappa a} \), \( \frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa}{m \alpha}=1+e^{-2 \kappa a} \) , or \( e^{-2 \kappa a}=\frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa}{m \alpha}-1 \).

This is a transcendental equation for κ (and hence for E). I’ll solve it graphically: Let \( z \equiv 2 \kappa a, c \equiv \frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 a m \alpha} \) so \( e^{-z}=c z-1 \) . Plot both sides and look for intersections:

From the graph, noting that c and z are both positive, we see that there is one (and only one) solution.

(for even ψ). If \( \alpha=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m a} \) so c=1, the calculator gives z = 1.278, so \( \kappa^{2}=-\frac{2 m E}{\hbar^{2}}=\frac{z^{2}}{(2 a)^{2}} \Rightarrow E = -\frac{(1.278)^{2}}{8}\left(\frac{\hbar^{2}}{m a^{2}}\right)=-0.204\left(\frac{\hbar^{2}}{m a^{2}}\right) \).

Now look for odd solutions:

\( \psi(x)= \begin{cases}A e^{-\kappa x} & (x>a) \\ B\left(e^{\kappa x}-e^{-\kappa x}\right) & (-a<x<a) \\ -A e^{\kappa x} & (x<-a)\end{cases} \).

Continuity at a : \( A e^{-\kappa a}=B\left(e^{\kappa a}-e^{-\kappa a}\right) \), or \( A=B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}-1\right) \).

Discontinuity in \( \psi^{\prime}:-\kappa A e^{-\kappa a}-B\left(\kappa e^{\kappa a}+\kappa e^{-\kappa a}\right)=-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2}} A e^{-\kappa a} \Rightarrow B\left(e^{2 \kappa a}+1\right)=A\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right) \).

\( e^{2 \kappa a}+1=\left(e^{2 \kappa a}-1\right)\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right)=e^{2 \kappa a}\left(\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1\right)-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}+1 \).

\( 1=\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa}-1-\frac{2 m \alpha}{\hbar^{2} \kappa} e^{-2 \kappa a} \); \( \frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa}{m \alpha}=1-e^{-2 \kappa a} \), \( e^{-2 \kappa a}=1-\frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa}{m \alpha} \) or \( e^{-z}=1-c z \).

This time there may or may not be a solution. Both graphs have their y-intercepts at 1, but if c is too large (α too small), there may be no intersection (solid line), whereas if c is smaller (dashed line) there will be. (Note that z = 0 ⇒ κ = 0 is not a solution, since ψ is then non-normalizable.) The slope of \( e^{-z} \) (at z = 0) is -1; the slope of (1 – cz) is -c. So there is an odd solution ⇔ c < 1, or \( \alpha>\hbar^{2} / 2 m a \).

Conclusion: One bound state if \( \alpha \leq \hbar^{2} / 2 ma \); two if \( \alpha>\hbar^{2} / 2 ma \).

\( \alpha=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{m a} \Rightarrow c=\frac{1}{2} \cdot\left\{\begin{array}{l} \text { Even: } e^{-z}=\frac{1}{2} z-1 \Rightarrow z=2.21772, \\ \text { Odd: } e^{-z}=1-\frac{1}{2} z \Rightarrow z=1.59362 . \end{array}\right. \).

\( E=-0.615\left(\hbar^{2} / m a^{2}\right) ; E=-0.317\left(\hbar^{2} / m a^{2}\right) \).

\( \alpha=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{4 m a} \Rightarrow c=2 \), Only even: \( e^{-z} = 2z – 1 ) z = 0.738835 \); \( E=-0.0682\left(\hbar^{2} / m a^{2}\right) \) .

(c) (i) There is one bound state (even); c is huge, so z is small, so \( e^{-z} \approx 1=c z-1 \), which means z = 2/c, \( 2 \kappa a=2\left(2 a m \alpha / \hbar^{2}\right) \Rightarrow \kappa=\left(2 m \alpha / \hbar^{2}\right) \).

\( E=-\frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa^{2}}{2 m}= -\frac{2 m \alpha^{2}}{\hbar^{2}} \).

This makes sense: the two delta-functions coincide, so there is really just one delta-function, with \strength” 2α. Putting this into Equation 2.130 we recover the answer in the box.

\( E=-\frac{\hbar^{2} \kappa^{2}}{2 m}=-\frac{m \alpha^{2}}{2 \hbar^{2}}\) (2.130).

(ii) There are two bound states, one even and one odd; c is small, so z is huge, and \( e^{-z} \approx 0 \). For the even case, \( 0=c z-1 \Rightarrow z=1 / c \Rightarrow \kappa=\left(m \alpha / \hbar^{2}\right) \). For the odd case, 0 = 1 – cz, which leads to the same result: the two states are degenerate, each with energy \( -\frac{m \alpha^{2}}{2 \hbar^{2}} \). Any linear combination of the two will be an eigenstate (with the same energy); the sum (properly normalized) would represent a particle in the delta-function out at large positive x, and the difference would be a particle in the delta-function at large negative x (the other-distant-delta-function becomes irrelevant), so it makes sense that we get two states, each with the energy of a particle in a single delta-function well.
