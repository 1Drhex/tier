Guest Question.

For the circuit of Fig. 8.34, find i(t) for \(t=\infty, 3^{-}, 3^{+}\), and 100 μs after the source changes value.

Admin
Solution

Long after any transients have died out (t →∞), the circuit is a simple dc circuit driven by a 12 V voltage source. The inductor appears as a short circuit, so

 

\(i(\infty)=\frac{12}{1000}=12\) mA

 

What is meant by \(i\left(3^{-}\right)\)? This is simply a notational convenience to indicate the instant before the voltage source changes value. For t < 3, \(u(t-3)=0 . \text { Thus, } i\left(3^{-}\right)=0\) as well.

At \(t=3^{+}\), the forcing function 12u(t − 3) = 12 V. However, since the inductor current cannot change in zero time, \(i\left(3^{+}\right)=i\left(3^{-}\right)=0\).

The most straightforward approach to analyzing the circuit for t > 3 s is to rewrite Eq. [25] as

 

\(i=\left(\frac{V_{0}}{R}-\frac{V_{0}}{R} e^{-R t / L}\right) u(t)\) [25]

 

\(i\left(t^{\prime}\right)=\left(\frac{V_{0}}{R}-\frac{V_{0}}{R} e^{-R t^{\prime} / L}\right) u\left(t^{\prime}\right)\)

 

and note that this equation applies to our circuit as well if we shift the time axis such that

 

\(t^{\prime}=t-3\)

 

Therefore, with \(V_{0} / R=12 mA \text { and } R / L=20,000 s ^{-1}\),

 

\(i(t-3)=\left(12-12 e^{-20,000(t-3)}\right) u(t-3)\) mA [26]

 

which can be written more simply as

 

\(i(t)=\left(12-12 e^{-20,000(t-3)}\right) u(t-3)\) mA [27]

 

since the unit-step function forces a zero value for t < 3, as required. Substituting t = 3.0001 s into Eq. [26] or [27], we find that i = 10.38 mA at a time 100 μs after the source changes value.
