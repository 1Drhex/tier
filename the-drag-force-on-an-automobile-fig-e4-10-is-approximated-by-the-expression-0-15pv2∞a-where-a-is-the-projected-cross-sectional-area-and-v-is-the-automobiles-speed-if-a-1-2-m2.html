Guest Question.

The drag force on an automobile (Fig. E4.10) is approximated by the expression \(0.15 \rho V_{\infty}^{2} A\), where \(A\) is the projected cross-sectional area and \(V_{\infty}\) is the automobile’s speed. If \(A=1.2 \mathrm{~m}^{2}\), calculate the efficiency \(\eta\) of the engine if the rate of fuel consumption \(f\) (the gas mileage) is \(15 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{L}\) and the automobile travels at \(90 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{h}\). Assume that the fuel releases \(44000 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{kg}\) during combustion. Neglect the energy lost due to the exhaust gases and coolant and assume that the only resistance to motion is the drag force. Use \(\rho_{\text {air }}=1.12 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{m}^{3}\) and \(\rho_{\text {fuel }}=0.68 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{L}\).

Admin
Solution

If the car is taken as the moving control volume (note that the control volume is fixed), as shown, we can simplify the energy equation (Eq.4.5.3 in combination with 4.5.11) to

\(\dot{Q}-\dot{W}=\frac{d}{d t} \int_{\mathrm{c} . \mathrm{v}} e \rho d V+\int_{\mathrm{c} . \mathrm{s}} \rho e \mathbf{V} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} d A (4.5.3)\)

\(\dot{W}=\int_{\mathrm{c} . \mathrm{s}} p \hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{V} d A+\dot{W}_{S}+\dot{W}_{\text {shear }}+\dot{W}_{I} (4.5.11)\)

\( \dot{Q} – \dot{W_{I}} = o \)

since all other terms are negligible; there is no velocity crossing the control volume, so \(\mathbf{V} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}=0\) (neglect the energy of the exhaust gases); there is no shear or shaft work; the energy of the c.v. remains constant. The energy input \( \dot{Q} \) which accomplishes useful work is \(\eta\) times the energy released during combustion; that is,

\(\dot{Q}=\dot{m}_{f} \times 44000 \eta \mathrm{kJ} / \mathrm{s}\)

where \(\dot{m}_{f}\) is the mass flux of the fuel. The mass flux of fuel is determined knowing the rate of fuel consumption \( \dot{f} \) and the density of fuel as \(0.68 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{L}\), as follows:

\(\dot{f}=\frac{\text { distance }}{\text { volume }}=\frac{V_{\infty} \times \text { time }}{Q \times \text { time }}=\frac{V_{\infty}}{\dot{m}_{f} / \rho_{f}}=\frac{\rho_{f} V_{\infty}}{\dot{m}_{f}}\)

with \(V_{\infty}=90000 / 3600=25 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\), we have, using \(\dot{f}=15 \times 1000 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{L}\),

\(\begin{aligned}15 \times 1000 &=\frac{0.68 \times 25}{\dot{m}_{f}} \\\therefore \dot{m}_{f} &=0.001133 \mathrm{~kg} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

The inertial work-rate term is

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{W}_{I} &=V_{\infty} \times \mathrm{drag} \\&=0.15 \rho V_{\infty}^{3} A=0.15 \times 1.12 \times 25^{3} \times 1.2=3150 \mathrm{~J} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

Equating \(\dot{Q} =\dot{W}_{I}\), we have

\(\begin{aligned}44000 \eta \times 0.001133 &=3.15 \\\therefore \eta &=0.0632 \text { or } 6.32 \% \end{aligned}\)

This is obviously a very low percentage, perhaps surprisingly low to the reader. Very little power (3.15 kJ/s = 4.22 hp) is actually needed to propel the automobile at 90 km/h. The relatively large engine, needed primarily for acceleration, is quite inefficient
when simply propelling the automobile.

Note the importance of using a stationary reference frame. The reference frame attached to the automobile is an inertial reference frame since it is moving at constant velocity. Yet the energy equation demands a stationary reference frame allowing the energy required by the drag force to be properly included.
