Guest Question.

An irreversible reaction takes place in a CSTR containing spherical particles catalyst of 24mm diameter. Pure reactant A is introduced at pressure of 1 atm and constant temperature of 300◦C with a space time of 4000 (g×s/\(m^{3})\). Conversion was measured with time as shown in the table (Fogler, 2000). Calculate the deactivation rate with and without diffusion, knowing the additional data:
\(D_{e}=2\times 10^{-8} m^{2}/s; S_{g}=30 m^{2}/kg; _{s}=1500 g/m^{3}\)

Admin
Solution

Let us first consider the case in the absence of diffusion effects:
We start from Equation 19.12 with \(\Phi _{1} = 1\) and take the natural log of both sides of the equation:

\(\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}}= \frac{3\cdot\acute{k}\cdot S_{g}\cdot \tau _{CSTR} }{\Phi _{1}} e^{(-k_{d}t/2)}\) (19.12)

\(\ln (\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}})=\ln (k'.S_{g}.\tau _{CSTR})-k_{d}t\)

Therefore, the slope provides \(k_{d}=0.256 \, h^{-1}\) and the intercept allows calculating \(In(\acute{k}S_{g}\tau _{CSTR} )=1.096\)

Then, we calculate the value of \(\acute{k} \) using \(S_{g}=30 \, m^{2}/g\) and \(\tau =4000(g \, s/m^{3})\)

\(\acute{k}=2.493*10^{-5}m^{3}/(m^{2}s)\)

We calculate the Thiele modulus to verify if there are diffusion effects in the data above.

\(\Phi _{1}=R\sqrt{\frac{\acute{k}\cdot S_{g}\rho _{s} }D_{e}{} } \) \(\Phi _{1}=2.4*10^{-3}\sqrt{\frac{2.493*10^{-5}*30*1500}{2.0*10^{-8}} } =18\)

We note strong diffusion effects.

Therefore, now we admit the rate with mass transfer limitations.
We start from Equation 19.12

\(\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}}=\frac{3\cdot \acute{k}\cdot S_{g} \cdot \tau _{CSTR}}{\Phi _{1}} e^{-(k_{d}t/2)}\)

Table 19.1 Conversion as a function of time

t(h) 0 2 4 6
\(X_{A}\) 0.75 0.64 0.52 0.39

Taking the natural log of both sides of the equation, we obtain:

\(In(\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}} )=In(k^{*})-\frac{k_{d}}{2}t \)

Using the experimental data of Table 19.1, we obtain the same graph. Thus,

\(k_{d}=0.52 \, h^{-1}\) \(In \, k^{*}=1.096\) \(k^{*}=3.0\)

But:

\(k*=\frac{3\cdot \acute{k}\cdot S_{g}\cdot \tau _{CSTR} }{\Phi _{1}} \)

And:

\(\Phi _{1}=R\sqrt{\frac{k\cdot S_{g}\rho _{s}}{D_{e}} } \)

Consequently:

\(\acute{k} =\frac{R^{2}\cdot \rho _{s}}{S_{g}\cdot \tau ^{2}\cdot D_{e}} =9*10^{-4}(m^{3}/m^{2}/s)\)

Therefore, the new value of the constant \(\acute{k} \) is:

\(k"=2.7*10^{2}m^{3}/(kg \, s)\) \(\acute{k} =9*10^{-4}(m^{3}/m^{2}s)\)

Then we calculate again the Thiele modulus using the data above.

\(\Phi _{1}=R\sqrt{\frac{\acute{k}\cdot S_{g}\rho _{s}}{D_{e}} } \) \(\Phi _{1}=2.4*10^{-3}\sqrt{\frac{9*10^{-4}*30*1500}{2.0*10^{-8}} } =108\)

Thus, the correct value of the constant is:

\(\acute{k} =1.5*10^{-4}(m^{3}/m^{2}/s)\)

And we can calculate the effectiveness factor, i.e.:

\(\eta =\frac{3}{\Phi _{1}}=\frac{3}{108} =0.009\)

Depending on the activity, the Thiele modulus changes as Equation 19.9:

\(\Phi _{1d}=R\sqrt{\frac{\acute{k}\cdot a\cdot S_{g}\rho _{s} }{D_{e}} }=108\sqrt{a} \)

And the effectiveness factor will be:

\(\eta =\frac{3}{\Phi _{1d}} =\frac{3}{108\sqrt{a} } \)

Finally, the conversion varies with time and activity will be:

In the absence of mass transfer limitations:

\(\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}}=\acute{k}\cdot S\cdot \tau _{CSTR}\, e^{-(0.256t)} =2.99 \, e^{-(0.256t)} \)

In the presence of mass transfer limitations:

\(\frac{X_A}{1-X_{A}} =\frac{3\cdot \acute{k}\cdot S_{g}\cdot \tau _{CSTR}\cdot }{\Phi _{1}} =0.5\sqrt{e^{-0.52t}} \)

Therefore,

\(\frac{X_{A}}{1-X_{A}}=0.5\sqrt{e^{-0.52t}} \)

To verify the data, we have Figure 19.7.
