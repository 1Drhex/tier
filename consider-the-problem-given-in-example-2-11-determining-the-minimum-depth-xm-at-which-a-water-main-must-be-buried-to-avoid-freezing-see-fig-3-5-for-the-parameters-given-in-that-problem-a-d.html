Guest Question.

Consider the problem given in —determining the minimum depth\( x_m\) at which a water main must be buried to avoid freezing (see Fig. 3.5). For the parameters given in that problem, a depth of 0.68 m was required. Determine xm by solving the explicit difference equation. In the soil was initially at a uniform temperature of 20°C and we assumed that under the worst conditions anticipated it would be subjected to a surface temperature of -15°C for a period of 60 days. We used the following properties for soil (at 300 K):

ρ = 2050 kg/\(m^3\) k = 0.52 W/m K c = 1840 J/kg K

\(∝ = 0.138 × 10^{- 6} m^2/s\)

Solution

We need to implement Eq. (3.14) in a computer program.

\(T_{i,m+1}= T_{i,m}+\frac{\Delta t}{pc\Delta x} \left\{\frac{K}{\Delta x }(T_{i+1,m} -2T_{i,m} + T_{i-1,m})+\dot{q}_ {G, i, m}\Delta x \right\}\) (3.14)

Figure 3.5 shows the relationship between depth and nodes. Since there is no heat generation(\(\dot{q}_ G =0\)),the equation simplifies to

\(T_{i,m+1}= T_{i,m} +Fo(T_{i+1,m} – 2T_{i,m} + T_{i-1,m} )\)

where \(Fo\) is the Fourier number, \(Fo = \Delta t\alpha /\Delta x ^2\) , as discussed earlier. Let us create a one-dimensional array to contain the known temperatures at time step m and a similar array for the unknown temperature at time step m+1:

\(T_{i,old} ≡ T_{i,m}\) \(T_{i,new} ≡ T_{i,m + 1}\)

We now have an equation of the form

\(T_{i,new} = T_{i,old} + Fo(T_{i+1,old} – 2T_{i,old} + T_{i-1,old})\)

which is valid for all nodes except the boundary nodes. For the boundary nodes we have\( T_{1,old} = T_{1,new} = -15°C\) and \(T_{N,old} = T_{N,new} = 20°C\).

We must now decide to what depth the calculation should extend and the size of the control volumes. At a very large depth we would expect to see no temperature change for the entire 60 days. Let us select a maximum depth of 6 m and check our solution to ensure that no temperature change is seen at 6 m depth. If the solution shows that the temperature has changed significantly at 6 m, we will need to allow the calculation to extend to a greater depth.

We are free to select the control volume size, \(\Delta x\) , but remember that accuracy improves as \(\Delta x\) decreases. As far as the time step is concerned, we must follow the constraint given in Eq. (3.15).

\(\Delta t\lt \frac{\Delta x ^2}{2\alpha }\)

As the time step decreases, we expect greater accuracy. A further constraint is that we want the calculation to end at exactly 60 days, so we want 60 days to be an integer multiple of Δt.
