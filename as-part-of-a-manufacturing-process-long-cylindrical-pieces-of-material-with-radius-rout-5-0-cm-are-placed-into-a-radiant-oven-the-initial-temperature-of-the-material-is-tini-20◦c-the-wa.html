Guest Question.

As part of a manufacturing process, long cylindrical pieces of material with radius \(r_{out}=5.0cm\) are placed into a radiant oven. The initial temperature of the material is \(T_{ini}=20^{\circ}C\). The walls of the oven are maintained at a temperature of \(T_{wall}=750^{\circ}\) and the oven is evacuated so that the outer edge of the cylinder is exposed only to radiation heat transfer. The emissivity of the surface of the cylinder is \(\varepsilon=0.95\). The material is considered to be completely processed when the temperature everywhere is at least \(T_{p}=250^{\circ}C\). The properties of the material are k=1.4W/m-K, \(\rho=2500kg/m^{3}\), c= 700 J/kg-K.

a) Is a lumped capacitance model appropriate for this problem?

b) How long will the processing require? Use an effective, radiation heat transfer coefficient for this calculation.

c) What is the minimum amount of energy per unit length that is required to process the material? That is, how much energy would be required to bring the material to a uniform temperature of \(T_{p}\)?

d) How much energy is actually required per unit length of material?

e) The efficiency of the process (η) is defined as the ratio of the minimum possible energy transfer required to process the material (from part (b)) to the actual energy transfer (from part (c)). Plot the efficiency of the process as a function of the radius of the material for various values of \(T_{wall}\).

Solution

a) The known inputs are entered in EES:

“EXAMPLE 3.5-1: Material in a Radiant Oven”

$UnitSystem SI MASS RAD PA K J
$TABSTOPS 0.2 0.4 0.6 0.8 3.5 in

“Inputs”
r_out=5.0 [cm]∗convert(cm,m) “radius”
T_ini=converttemp(C,K,20[C]) “initial temperature”
T_wall=converttemp(C,K,750[C]) “wall temperature”
e=0.95 “emissivity”
T_p=converttemp(C,K,250[C]) “processing temperature”
k=1.4 [W/m-K] “conductivity”
rho=2500 [kg/mˆ3] “density”
c=700 [J/kg-K] “specific heat capacity”
alpha=k/(rho∗c) “thermal diffusivity”

The effective heat transfer coefficient associated with radiation (\(\bar h_{rad},\) discussed in Section 1.2.6) is:

\( \overline{h}_{rad}=\sigma \epsilon (T^{2}_{wall}+T^{2}_{s})(T_{wall}+T_{s}) \)

where \(T_{s}\) is is the surface temperature, which is not known but will certainly not be less then \(T_{ini}\) and will likely not be much higher than \(T_{p}\). An average of these values is used to evaluate the radiation heat transfer coefficient.

T_s=(T_ini+T_p)/2 “average temperature used for surface temp.”
h_bar_rad=sigma# ∗e∗(T_wallˆ2+T_sˆ2)∗(T_wall+T_s)
“effective heat transfer coefficient due to radiation”

Because \(T_{wall}\) is so much higher than \(T_{s}\), the value of \(\bar h_{rad}\) is not affected significantly by the choice of \(T_{s}\). Using \(\bar h_{rad}\), it is possible to calculate the Biot number:

\(B~ i=\frac{\bar{h}_{\text {rad }} r_{\text {out }}}{k}\)
Bi=h_bar_rad∗r_out/k “Biot number”

which provides a Biot number of 3.3. Therefore, a lumped capacitance model is not valid.

b) The dimensionless temperature difference at which the processing is complete can be calculated using Eq. (3-258):

\(\tilde{\theta}=\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{i n i}-T_{\infty}}\) (3-258)

 

\(\tilde{\theta}_{p}=\frac{T_{p}-T_{w a l l}}{T_{i n i}-T_{w a l l}}\)
theta_hat_p=(T_p-T_wall)/(T_ini-T_wall) “dimensionless temperature for processing”

The function cylinder_ND implements the exact solution for a cylinder, Eq. (3-255)

\( \widetilde{\theta }(\widetilde{r},Fo ) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{\infty }{C_{i}BesselJ(0,\zeta _{i}\widetilde{r} )\exp [-\zeta _{i}^{2}Fo]} \)

and provides \(\tilde{\theta}\) given \(\tilde{r}\), Fo, Bi. In this case, we know \(\tilde{\theta}=\tilde{\theta}_{p} \text { at } \tilde{r}=0\) (the center of the cylinder, which will be the lowest temperature part of the material), and the value of Bi is also known. We would like to solve for the corresponding value of Fo (and therefore time). EES will solve this implicit equation in order to determine Fo:

theta_hat_p=cylinder_T_ND(0, Fo, Bi)
“implements the dimensionless solution to a cylinder”

Initially you are likely to obtain an error in EES related to the value of Fo being negative. This error condition can be easily overcome by setting appropriate limits (e.g., 0.001 to 1000) on the value of Fo in the Variable Information window. The Fourier number is used to calculate the processing time \(\left\lgroup T_{process}\right\rgroup\), according to Eq. (3-257):

\(F~ o=\frac{t~ \alpha}{r_{o u t}^{2}}\) (3-257)

 

\(t_{\text {process }}=F o \frac{r_{\text {out }}^{2}}{\alpha}\)
t_process=Fo∗r _outˆ2/alpha “processing time”

which leads to a processing time of 678 s.

c) An energy balance on the material shows that the minimum amount of energy required to bring the material to a uniform temperature \(\left\lgroup Q_{min}\right\rgroup\) is:

\(Q_{\min }=\pi ~r_{\text {out }}^{2}~ L~ \rho \text { C }\left(T_{p}-T_{\text {ini }}\right)\)
L=1 [m] “per unit length of material”
Q_min=pi∗r _outˆ2∗L∗rho∗c∗(T_p-T_ini) “minimum amount of energy transfer required”

which leads to \(Q_{min}=3.16MJ\).

d) The dimensionless energy transfer to the cylinder \(\left\lgroup\tilde{Q}\right\rgroup\) is obtained using the cylinder_Q_ND function in EES:

Q_hat=cylinder_Q_ND(Fo, Bi) “dimensionless heat transfer”

The dimensionless energy transfer is defined according to Eq. (3-239)

\(\tilde{Q}=\frac{Q}{Q_{\max }}\) (3-239)

 

\(\tilde{Q}=\frac{Q}{Q_{\max }}\)

where \(Q_{max}\) is the energy transfer that occurs if the process is continued until the cylinder reaches equilibrium with the wall:

\(Q_{\max }=\rho ~c ~L~ \pi~ r_{\text {out }}^{2}\left(T_{\text {wall }}-T_{\text {ini }}\right)\)
Q_max=pi∗r_outˆ2∗L∗rho∗c∗(T_wall-T_ini)
“maximum amount of energy transfer that could occur”
Q=Q_max∗Q_hat “actual energy transfer”

which leads to Q = 5.62 MJ.

e) The efficiency is defined as:

\(\eta=\frac{Q_{\min }}{Q}\)
eta=Q_min/Q “Process efficiency”

The efficiency is computed over a range of radii, \(r_{out}\), using a parametric table at several values of \(T_{wall}\) and the results are shown in Figure 1.

Notice that the efficiency improves with reduced radius because the temperature gradients within the material are reduced and so the amount of energy wasted in ‘overheating’ the material toward the outer radius of the cylinder is reduced. Also, reducing the oven temperature tends to improve the efficiency for the same reason, but at the expense of increased processing time. The most efficient process is associated with processing a very small amount of material (with small radius) very slowly (at low oven temperature); this is a typical result: very efficient processes are not usually very practical.
