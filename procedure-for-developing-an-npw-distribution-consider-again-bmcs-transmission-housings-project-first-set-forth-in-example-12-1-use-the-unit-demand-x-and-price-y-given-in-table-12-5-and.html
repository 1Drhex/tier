Solution

Given: Probability distributions for \(X\) and Y.
Find: NPW probability distribution.

Using the generalized cash flow approach discussed in Chapter 10, we can summarize the after-tax cash flow for the BMC’s transmission housings project as functions of random variables \(X\) and Y. As shown in Table 12.10, we can compute the PW of cash inflows as

PW(15%) = 0.6\(X\)Y(P/A, 15%, 5) + $44,490
= 2.0113\(X\)Y + $44,490

The PW of cash outflows is

PW(15%) = $125,000 + (9\(X\) + $6,000)(P/A, 15%, 5)
= 30.1694\(X\) + $145,113

Thus, the NPW is

PW(15%) = 2.0113\(X\)(Y – $15) – $100,623

If the product demand \(X\) and the unit price Y are independent random variables, then PW(15%) also will be a random variable. To determine the NPW distribution, we need to consider all the combinations of possible outcomes\(^{6}\). The first possibility is the event in which \(X\) = 1,600 and Y = $48. Since \(X\) and \(Y\) are considered to be independent random variables, the probability of this joint event is

P(\(X\) = 1,600, Y = $48) = P(\(X\) = 1,600)P(Y = $48)
= (0.20)(0.30)
= 0.06

With these values as input, we compute the possible NPW outcome as

PW(15%) = 2.0113\(X\)(Y – $15) – $100,623
= 2.0113(1,600)($48 – $15) – $100,623
= $5,574

Eight other outcomes are possible; they are summarized with their joint probabilities in Table 12.11 and depicted in Figure 12.8.

The NPW probability distribution in Table 12.11 indicates that the project’s NPW varies between $5,574 and $82,808, but that no loss occurs under any of the circumstances examined. On the one hand, from the cumulative distribution, we further observe that there is a 0.38 probability that the project would realize an NPW less than that forecast for the base case ($40,168). On the other hand, there is a 0.32 probability that the NPW will be greater than this value. Certainly, the probability distribution

We have developed a probability distribution for the NPW by considering random cash flows. As we observed, a probability distribution helps us to see what the data imply in terms of project risk. Now we can learn how to summarize the probabilistic information—the mean and the variance.

• We obtain the variance of the NPW distribution, assuming independence between \(X\) and Y and using Eq. (12.2)

\(Var\left[X\right] =\sigma ^{2}_{X} =\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{J}{\left(x_{j}-\mu \right) ^{2}\left(p_{j} \right) }\)

as shown in Table 12.13. We could obtain the same result more easily by using Eq. (12.4).

\(Var\left[X\right] = \sum{p_{j}x^{2}_{j} } – \left(\sum{p_{j}x_{j} } \right)^{2}\)

\(= E\left[X^{2} \right] – \left(E\left[X \right]\right) ^{2}\)

Comments: We can obtain the mean and variance of the NPW analytically by using the properties of the product of random variables. Let W = \(XY\), where \(X\) and \(Y\) are random variables with known means and variances \(\left(\mu _{X}, \sigma ^{2}_{X} \right)\) and \(\left(\mu _{Y}, \sigma ^{2}_{Y} \right)\) respectively. If \(X\) and Y are independent of each other, then

\(E\left(W\right) = E\left(XY\right) =\mu _{X}\mu _{Y}\) (12.9)

and

\(Var \left(W\right) = Var \left(XY\right) = \mu ^{2}_{X} \sigma ^{2}_{Y} + \mu ^{2}_{Y} \sigma ^{2}_{X}+\sigma ^{2}_{X} \sigma ^{2}_{Y}\) (12.10)

The expected net present worth is then

\(E\left[PW\left(15\%\right) \right] = 2.0113E\left(XY\right) – \left(2.0113\right) \left(15\right) E\left(X\right) – 100,623\)

= 2.0113(2,000)(50) – (2.0113)(15)(2,000) – 100,623
= $40,168

Now let Z = Y – 15. Then \(E\left(Z\right) =E\left(Y\right) -15 =\mu _{Y} -15 = 50 – 15 = 35 and Var \left[Z\right] = Var \left[Y\right] = \sigma ^{2}_{Y} = 3\) . So
