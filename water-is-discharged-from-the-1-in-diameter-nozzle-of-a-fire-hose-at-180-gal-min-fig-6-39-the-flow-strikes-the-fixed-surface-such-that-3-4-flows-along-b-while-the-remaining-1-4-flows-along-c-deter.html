Guest Question.

Water is discharged from the 1-in.diameter nozzle of a fire hose at 180 gal/min, Fig. 6-39. The flow strikes the fixed surface such that 3/4 flows along B, while the remaining 1/4 flows along C. Determine the x and y components of force exerted on the surface.

Admin
Solution

Fluid Description. This is a case of steady flow. We consider the water as an ideal fluid, for which \(\gamma _w= 62.4 lb/ft^3.\)

 

Control Volume and Free-Body Diagram. We will select a fixed control volume that contains the water from the nozzle and on the surface, Fig. 6-3a. The pressure distributed over the surface creates resultant horizontal and vertical reaction components,\( F_x, and F_y. \) on the closed control surface. We will neglect the weight of the water within the control volume. Also, because the (sage) pressure on all the open control surfaces is zero, no force is acting on these surfaces, If we apply the Bernoulli equation to a point on the fluid stream along A, and another point on the fluid stream along B (or C). neglecting elevation changes between these points, and realizing that the (gage) pressure is zero, then \( p/\gamma + V^2/2g +z= const.\) shows that the speed of the water onto and along the fixed surface is the same everywhere. That is, \(V_A = V_B V_C \) The surface only changes the direction of the velocity

Linear Momentum. The velocity can be determined from the flow at A, because here the cross-sectional area is known.

 

\(Q_A = \frac{180 gal }{1 min} \left(\frac{1 min}{60 s} \right) \left(\frac{1 ft^3}{7.48 gal} \right) = 0.4011 ft^3/s\)

 

\(V_A =V_B = V_C= \frac{Q_A}{A_A} =\frac{0.4011 ft^3/s}{\pi \left(\frac{0.5}{12} ft\right)^2 } \)

 

For this case of steady flow, the linear momentum equation becomes

 

\(\sum{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_{cv} V \rho dV + \int_{cs} V \rho V \cdot dA \)

 

\(\sum{F} = 0 + V_B(\rho V_B A_B) + V_C(\rho V_CA_C) + V_a (-\rho V_AA_a)\)

 

Notice that the last term is negative because the flow is into the control volume. Because O-VA.then

 

\(\sum{F} = \rho (Q_BV_B + Q_CV_C – Q_AV_A)\)

 

Now when we resolve this equation in the x and y directions, only the velocities. V. will have components. The flows, O = VA, are scalar quantities. Thus,

 

\(\xmapsto{+} \sum{F_x} = \rho (Q_BV_{Bx} + Q_CV_{Cx} – Q_AV_{Ax})\)

 

\( – \sum{F} = \frac{62.4 Ib/ft^3}{32.2 ft/s^2}\left[0 + \frac{1}{4} (0.4011 ft^3/s)[(73.53 ft/s) \cos 60^\circ ] – (0.4011 ft^3/s)[(73.53 ft/s) \cos 45^\circ ] \right] \)

 

\(F_x = -33.27 Ib = 33.3 Ib \longleftarrow \)

 

\(+ \uparrow \sum{F_y} = \rho \left[Q_B(-V_{By}) + Q_CV_{Cy} – Q_A(-V_{Ay})\right] \)

 

\(F_y = \frac{62.4 Ib/ft^3}{32.2 ft/s^2} \left[\frac{3}{4}(0.4011 ft^3/s) (-73.53 ft/s) + \frac{1}{4}(0.4011 ft^3/s) [(-73.53 ft/s) \sin 60^\circ ] – (0.4011 ft^3/s) [(-73.53 ft/s) \sin 45^\circ ]\right] \)

 

\(F_y = 9.923 Ib\uparrow \)

Ans These results produce a resultant force of 34.7 lb that acts on the water. The equal but opposite dynamic force acts on the surface. Note that if the nozzle is directed perpendicular to a flat surface, a similar calculation shows that the force is 57.2 lb. This can become quite dangerous if the stream is directed at someone!
