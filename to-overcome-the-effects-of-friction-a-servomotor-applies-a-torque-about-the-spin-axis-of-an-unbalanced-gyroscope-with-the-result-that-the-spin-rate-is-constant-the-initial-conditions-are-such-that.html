Guest Question.

To overcome the effects of friction, a servomotor applies a torque about the spin axis of an unbalanced gyroscope, with the result that the spin rate is constant. The initial conditions are such that the initial precession rate \(\dot{\psi}^{*}\) and nutation angle \(\theta^{*}\) correspond to steady precession. Determine whether the action of the servomotor can cause the gyroscope to be unstable to small disturbances.

Admin
Solution

The idea here is to develop the ability to analyze nonideal effects for gyroscopes. The primary difference between the present system and a free gyroscope is that there are only two degrees of freedom, because the spin rate \(\dot{\phi}\) is constrained, rather than the spin momentum \(\beta_{\phi}\) being constant. We commence to derive the equations of motion for the servogyroscope by using the energies in Eqs. (10.2.2) and \((10.2 .3)\) :

\(T=\frac{1}{2}\left[I^{\prime} \dot{\psi}^{2}(\sin \theta)^{2}+I^{\prime} \dot{\theta}^{2}+I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi})^{2}\right] (10.2.2)\), \(V=m g L \cos \theta (10.2.3)\)

The precession angle is an ignorable generalized coordinate, because only its derivative appears in \(T\). The corresponding conservation of momentum equation is identical to the first of Eqs. (10.2.4):

\(\begin{array}{c}p_{\psi}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\psi}}=I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}) \cos \theta+I^{\prime} \dot{\psi}(\sin \theta)^{2} \equiv I^{\prime} \beta_{\psi}, \\\\p_{\phi}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\phi}}=I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}) \equiv I^{\prime} \beta_{\phi},\end{array} (10.2.4)\)

 

\(\beta_{\psi}=\dot{\psi}\left[\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}(\cos \theta)^{2}+(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi} \cos \theta. (1)\)

We may find the actual value of \(\beta_{\psi}\) by substituting \(\dot{\psi}^{*}\) and \(\theta^{*}\). Equation (10.2.6) is the Lagrange equation for \(\theta\) corresponding to arbitrary dependence of \(\psi\) and \(\phi .\) It may be written as

\(I^{\prime} \ddot{\theta}-(\dot{\psi} \sin \theta)\left[I^{\prime} \dot{\psi} \cos \theta-I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi})\right]-m g L \sin \theta=0. (10.2.6)\)

 

\(\begin{array}{c}\ddot{\theta}+g(\psi, \theta)=0, \\\\g(\psi, \theta)=\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right) \dot{\psi}^{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta+\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \psi \phi-m g L\right) \sin \theta.\end{array} (2)\)

Because the initial conditions are those appropriate to a steady precession, we have

\(g\left(\psi^{*}, \theta^{*}\right)=0. (3)\)

We assume that the configuration of interest is not a sleeping top, so \(\sin \theta^{*} \neq 0\). Satisfaction of Eq. (3) therefore requires

\(\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right)\left(\dot{\psi}^{*}\right)^{2} \cos \theta^{*}+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\psi}^{*} \dot{\phi}-m g L=0, (4)\)

which matches the expression established in for the free gyroscope. Although the angular motion in steady precession is identical to that of a free gyroscope, the stability situation is different. Constancy of \(\beta_{\psi}\) now requires that any fluctuation in the nutation angle be compensated solely by a change in the precession rate. We consider the deviations from the nominal steady-state condition by setting \(\dot{\psi}=\dot{\psi}^{*}+\varepsilon \dot{\psi}_{1}\) and \(\theta=\theta^{*}+\varepsilon \theta_{1}\), where \(\varepsilon \ll 1\) and \(\dot{\psi}_{1}\) and \(\theta_{1}\) are taken to have unit magnitude. We substitute these expressions into the basic equations of motion, Eqs. (1) and (2), both of which are expanded in Taylor series in powers of \(\varepsilon\). The results are

\(\begin{aligned} \beta_{\psi}=\dot{\psi}^{*}\left[\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}\left(\cos \theta^{*}\right)^{2}+\left(\sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}\right]+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi} \cos \theta^{*}+\varepsilon\left\{\dot{\psi}_{1}\left[\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}\left(\cos \theta^{*}\right)^{2}+(\sin \theta *)^{2}\right]\right.\\\\-\left.\left[2 \dot{\psi}^{*}\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right)\left(\sin \theta^{*} \cos \theta^{*}\right)+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi} \cos \theta^{*}\right] \theta_{1}\right\}, \\\\ g \left(\psi^{*}, \theta^{*}\right)+\varepsilon\left[\ddot{\theta}_{1}+\left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \dot{\psi}}\right)^{*} \psi_{1}+\left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \theta}\right)^{*} \theta_{1}\right]=0 .\end{aligned}\)

The zero-order terms in each of the preceding equations convey no new information because \(\dot{\psi}^{*}\) and \(\theta^{*}\) satisfy Eq. (3) and also give the value of \(\beta_{\psi}\) in Eq. (1). The first-order terms in the preceding therefore must satisfy

\(\dot{\psi}_{1}\left[\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}(\cos \theta)^{2}+(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]-\left[\dot{\psi}^{*}\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right) \sin \left(2 \theta^{*}\right)+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi} \sin \theta^{*}\right] \theta_{1}=0, (5)\)

whereas the first-order terms obtained from the energy conservation equation (2) require that

\(\ddot{\theta}_{1}+\left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \dot{\psi}}\right)^{*} \psi_{1}+\left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \theta}\right)^{*} \theta_{1}=0 . (6)\)

Both derivatives are marked by an asterisk to remind us that they are evaluated at the steady precession state. We solve Eq. (5) for \(\dot{\psi}_{1}\), and substitute that expression into Eq. (6). The result has the form

\(\ddot{\theta}_{1}+K \theta_{1}=0 .\)

The constant \(K\), which is obtained by differentiating the expression for \(g\) in Eqs. (2) and then substituting the aforementioned expression for \(\dot{\psi}_{1}\), is

\(\begin{aligned}K=&\left[\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right)\left(\dot{\psi}^{*}\right)^{2} \cos \left(2 \theta^{*}\right)+\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi} \dot{\psi}^{*}-\frac{\gamma}{2}\right) \cos \theta^{*}\right] \\\\&+\frac{I^{\prime}\left(\sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}}{I\left(\cos \theta^{*}\right)^{2}+I^{\prime}\left(\sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}}\left[2\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right) \dot{\psi}^{*} \cos \theta^{*}+\frac{I}{I^{\prime}} \dot{\phi}\right]^{2} .\end{aligned} \)

To simplify this further, we employ Eq. (4) to eliminate \(\dot{\phi}\). The result after many manipulations is

\(K=\frac{I^{\prime}\left(\sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}}{\left(\dot{\psi}^{*}\right)^{2}\left[I\left(\cos \theta^{*}\right)^{2}+I^{\prime}\left(\sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}\right]}\left\{\frac{\gamma^{2}}{4}+\left(\frac{I}{I^{\prime}}-1\right)\left(\dot{\psi}^{*}\right)^{2}\left[\gamma \cos \theta^{*}-\left(\dot{\psi}^{*}\right)^{2}\right]\right\}. (7)\)

The solutions of Eq. (7) are sinusoidal when \(K>0\), whereas one grows exponentially if \(K<0 .\) Thus, as was true for the free gyroscope, whose stability was described by Eqs. (10.3.5), \(K<0\) indicates situations in which the servo-driven gyroscope is unstable to small disturbances of the nutation angle. Suppose we are interested in evaluating whether a specific design corresponding to specified values of \(\dot{\phi}\), \(\gamma\), and \(I / I^{\prime}\) is unstable in some range of \(\theta^{*}\). To address this question we would consider Eq. (4) to be a quadratic equation for the steady precession rate \(\dot{\psi}^{*}\). We would substitute the smaller root, corresponding to slow precession, into Eq. (7), which would yield an expression for \(K\) as a function of \(\theta^{*}\). Scanning this function over \(0<\theta^{*}<\pi\) would reveal whether \(K\) is negative in some range of \(\theta^{*}\). As an example of such a computation, consider \(I / I^{\prime}=1.5\) and \(\gamma=20 ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}^{2} .\) Setting \(\dot{\phi}>3.366\) \(~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\) leads to stability for any \(\theta^{*}\), whereas \(\dot{\phi}=2 ~\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\) leads to stability only for \(0<\theta^{*}<97.09^{\circ}\).

\(\begin{array}{c}\ddot{\xi}+K \xi=0, \\\\K=\beta_{\phi}^{2}+\left(\dot{\psi}^{*} \sin \theta^{*}\right)^{2}-2 \gamma \cos \theta^{*}\end{array} (10.3.5\))

The possibility that controlling the spin rate can lead to instability could have been anticipated on the basis of physical arguments. The free gyroscope is a conservative system. In contrast, the servo-driven gyroscope is not, because the servomotor does work to hold the spin rate constant. The energy provided to the system from this source can drive the nutational motion away from steady precession. However, most situations of practical interest are like the preceding numerical example, in that the spin rate below which the gyroscope would lose stability is sufficiently low to be of no concern.
