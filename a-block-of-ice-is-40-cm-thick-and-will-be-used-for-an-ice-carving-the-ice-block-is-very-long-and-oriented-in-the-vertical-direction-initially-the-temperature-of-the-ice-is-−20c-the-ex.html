Guest Question.

A block of ice is 40 cm thick and will be used for an ice carving. The ice block is very long and oriented in the vertical direction. Initially the temperature of the ice is −20°C. The exposed surfaces are in contact with air whose temperature is 25°C. Determine the time required for the center to reach −10°C, and the temperature profile of the ice at that instant. Use the following properties for ice: \(k = 2.22 W/(m\cdot K)\),\(\rho _{s}=913\, kg/m^{3}\), and \(c=1.83\, kJ/(kg\cdot K)\). Assume \(\bar{h_{c}}=10\, W/(m^{2}\cdot K)\).

Admin
Solution

Heat is transferred to the exposed side by convection, and throughout the ice by conduction. Properties of the ice will change with temperature. We assume that the ice can be modeled using the infinite plate solution by making suitable assumptions. Now the infinite plate solution will have a temperature profile that is symmetric about its centerline. Therefore, a plane of symmetry is treated the same mathematically as an insulated surface. Thus, the half thickness of the infinite plate problem (1/2 × 2L) equals the actual thickness of the ice in this example.

 

Assumptions

 

1. The convection coefficient is constant over the exposed side of the wall.
2. Material properties are constant.
3. One-dimensional heat fl ow exists in the ice and the infinite plate solution charts apply.

 

The first step in this (or any unsteady) problem is to calculate the Biot number to determine if a lumped capacity analysis can be used. For the ice of this problem, we have

 

\(Bi=\frac{\bar{h_{c}}L}{k}\)

 

where L is the wall thickness. Substituting

 

\(Bi=\frac{10(0.4)}{2.22}=1.8\)

 

Because this value is greater than 0.1, the lumped capacity approach will not give an accurate result. We can, however, use Figure 4.6 to obtain a solution. Note that to use that figure, we must identify a wall thickness 2L. Recall that for this problem, we are modeling a wall whose thickness is 2(0.2 m), and whose temperature profile is symmetric about its centerline. Therefore, L = 0.2 m. The reciprocal of the Biot number (required to use Figure 4.6) is

 

\(\frac{1}{Bi}=\frac{k}{\bar{h_{c}}L}=\frac{2.22}{10(0.2)}=1.11\)

 

The dimensionless temperature is \((T_{c} − T_{\infty })/(T_{i} − T_{\infty })\) where \(T_{c}\) is the temperature at the plate centerline, which, in this problem due to symmetry, is at the surface of the insulated side. We therefore write

 

\(\frac{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}=\frac{-10-25}{-20-25}=0.778\)

 

Using the value for 1/Bi with this ratio of temperature differences, Figure 4.6a gives

 

\(Fo=\frac{\alpha t}{L^{2}}\simeq 0.5\)

 

It is difficult to read the graph accurately in the region where this result is obtained. Nevertheless, we calculate the time using the Fourier number

 

\(t=\frac{L^{2}Fo}{\alpha }\)

 

The thermal diffusivity is calculated from the given properties as

 

\(\alpha =\frac{k}{\rho _{s}c}=\frac{2.22}{913(1830)}=1.33\times 10^{-6}m^{2}/s\)

 

The time then is

 

\(t=\frac{(0.2)^{2}(0.5)}{1.33\times 10^{-6}}\)

 

\(t=12042\, s=3.3hr\)

 

To determine the temperature profile, we use Figure 4.6b. At a value of 1/Bi = 1.1, we read the values in the first two columns of Table 4.1. Note the subscripts on the temperature difference ratios in the second column. We use the values with the dimensionless centerline temperature we calculated earlier (0.778). So, for example, at x/L = 0.4, we find for the dimensionless temperature.

 

\(\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}=\frac{T-T_{\infty}}{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}\cdot \frac{T_{c}-T_{\infty}}{T_{i}-T_{\infty}}=0.95(0.778)=0.74\)

 

Results of calculations for this step appear in column 3 of Table 4.1. Using the previously obtained value, the temperature then is

 

\(T=0.74(T_{i}-T_{\infty})+T_{\infty }=0.74(-20-25)+25=-8.3^{\circ}C\)

 

Results of these calculations appear in column 4 of Table 4.1. The temperatures in this column correspond to locations within the ice, and these are calculated using the data in column 1. At x/L = 0.4, we find

 

\(x=0.4(0.2)=0.08\, m= 8\,cm\)

 

The locations are given in the column 5 of Table 4.1. A graph of temperature versus distance is provided in Figure 4.9. We see that the ice surface temperature on the exposed side is 0.5°C and the temperature on the insulated side is −10°C. Had we used the lumped-capacitance approach, the result would have predicted that the wall temperature is a constant, and Figure 4.9 shows that it is not. Admittedly these results are approximate, due primarily to inaccuracies associated with reading the graphs.
