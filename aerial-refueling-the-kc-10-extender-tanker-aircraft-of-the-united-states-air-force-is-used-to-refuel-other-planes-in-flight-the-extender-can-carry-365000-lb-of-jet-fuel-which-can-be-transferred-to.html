Aerial Refueling

The KC-10 Extender tanker aircraft of the United States Air Force is used to refuel other planes in flight. The Extender can carry 365,000 lb of jet fuel, which can be transferred to another aircraft through a boom that temporarily connects the two planes. (a) Express the mass of the fuel in the units of slugs and lbm. (b) Express the mass and weight of the fuel in SI units.
We will calculate the mass min terms of the fuel’s weight wand the gravitational acceleration \(g=32.2 ft / s ^{2}\). With wexpressed in pounds and ghaving the units of \(ft / s ^{2}\), the expression w = mgis dimensionally consistent when mass has the units of slugs. We will then convert from slugs to lbm using the conversion factor 1 slug =32.174 lbm from Equation (3.5). In part (b), we convert the fuel’s mass in the USCS to the SI using the conversion factor 1 slug =14.59 kg from Table 3.6.

Solution

(a) We first determine the mass of the fuel in the units of slugs:

 

\(\begin{aligned}m &=\frac{3.65 \times 10^{5} lb }{32.2 ft / s ^{2}} \leftarrow\left[m=\frac{w}{g}\right] \\&=1.134 \times 10^{4} \frac{ lb \cdot s ^{2}}{ ft } \\&=1.134 \times 10^{4} \text { slugs }\end{aligned}\)

 

In the last step, we used the definition \(1 \text { slug }=1\left( lb \cdot s ^{2}\right) / ft \text {. }\) object that weighs 1 lb has a mass of 1 lbm, the mass of the fuel can alternatively be expressed as 365,000 lbm.

 

(b) We convert the mass quantity \(1.134 \times 10^{4}\) slugs into the SI unit kg:

 

\(\begin{aligned}m &=\left(1.134 \times 10^{4} \text { slugs }\right)\left(14.59 \frac{ kg }{\text { slug }}\right) \\&=1.655 \times 10^{5} \text { (sluggs) }\left(\frac{ kg }{\text { slug }}\right) \\&=1.655 \times 10^{5} kg\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the numerical value for mass has a large power-of-ten exponent, an SI prefix from Table 3.3 should be applied. We first write \(m=165.5 \times 10^{3}\) kg so that the exponent is a multiple of 3. Since the “kilo” prefix already implies a factor of \(10^{3} g , m=165.5 \times 10^{6} g \text { or } 165.5 Mg \text {, }\) where “M” denotes the prefix “mega.” The fuel’s weight in the SI is

 

\(\begin{aligned}w &=\left(1.655 \times 10^{5} kg \right)\left(9.81 \frac{ m }{ s ^{2}}\right) \leftarrow[w=m g] \\&=1.62 \times 10^{6} \frac{ kg \cdot m }{ s ^{2}} \\&=1.62 \times 10^{6} N\end{aligned}\)

 

Because this quantity also has a large power-of-ten exponent, we use the SI prefix “M” to condense the factor of 1 million. The fuel weighs 1.62 MN.
