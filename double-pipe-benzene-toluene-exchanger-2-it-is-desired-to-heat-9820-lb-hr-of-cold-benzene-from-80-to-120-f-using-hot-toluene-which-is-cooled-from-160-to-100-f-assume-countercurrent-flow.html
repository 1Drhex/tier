Guest Question.

Double Pipe Benzene-Toluene Exchanger [2]. It is desired to heat 9820 lb/hr of cold benzene from 80 to 120 °F using hot toluene which is cooled from 160 to 100 °F. Assume countercurrent flow. The specific gravities at 68 °F are 0.88 and 0.87, respectively. The other fluid properties can be found either in the Appendix of this book or in Perry’s Chemical Engineers’ Handbook [5]. A fouling factor of 0.001 is provided for each stream, and the allowable pressure drop on each stream is 10.0 psi. A number of 20-ft hairpins of 2- by 1¼in IPS pipe are available. How many hairpins are required?

Admin
Solution

(1) Heat Balance:

Q = \(\dot{M}\)CΔT = \(\dot{m}\)cΔt

Cold Benzene,

\(t_{avg}=\frac{80+120}{2}=100°F\)

c = 0.425 Btu/(lb· °F) at 100°F

Q = (9820)(0.425)(120 – 80) = 167,000 Btu/hr

Hot Toluene,

\(T_{avg}=\frac{160+100}{2}=130°F\)

C = 0.44 Btu/(lb· °F) at 130°F

\(\dot{M}=\frac{167,000}{(0.44)(160-100)}=6330\ lb/hr\)

(2) LMTD, Δ\(T_{LM}\):

\(LMTD=\frac{\Delta T_{b}-\Delta T_{a}}{ln(\Delta T_{b}/\Delta T_{a})}=\frac{20}{ln(20/40)}=28.8^{\circ}F\)

Proceed now to the inner pipe. A check of Table 6.4 indicates that the flow area of the inner pipe is greater than that of the annulus. Place the larger stream (benzene) in the inner pipe.
