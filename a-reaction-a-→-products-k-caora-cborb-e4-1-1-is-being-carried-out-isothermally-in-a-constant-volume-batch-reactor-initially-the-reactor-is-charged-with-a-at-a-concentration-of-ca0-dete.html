Guest Question.

A reaction,

\(A \rightarrow \text { products } \quad k C_{ A }^{ O _{ RA }} C_{ B }^{ O _{ RB }}\) (E4.1.1)

is being carried out isothermally in a constant volume batch reactor. Initially, the reactor is charged with A at a concentration of \(C_{A 0}\). Determine the concentration of A in the reactor as a function of time.

Admin
Solution

To start, let us draw a sketch to illustrate the problem as shown in Fig. E4.1.1. Mole balance of reactant A inside the reactor leads to

\( \overset{\overset{0}{\nwarrow } }{In} – \overset{\overset{0}{ \nearrow } }{Out}+Generation=Accumulation\)

\(r_{A} V=\frac{d n_{A}}{d t}.\) (E4.1.2)

Since the volume is constant (constant volume batch reactor), Eq. (E4.1.2) \(r_{A} V=\frac{d n_{A}}{d t}.\) is reduced to

\(r_{A}=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). (E4.1.3)

Applying the rate law,

\(KC _{ A }^{ O _{ RA }} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }}\). (E4.1.4)

Substituting the rate expression, Eq. (E4.1.4) \(KC _{ A }^{ O _{ RA }} C _{ B }^{ O _{ RB }}\). , into the reduced mole balance Eq. (E4.1.3) \(r_{A}=\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}\). , one obtains

\(\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}=r_{A}=-k C_{A}^{O_{R A}}\). (E4.1.5)

Separation of variables,

\(\frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}}}=-k d t.\) (E4.1.6)

And integrate:

\(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}}}=-\int_{0}^{t} k d t.\) (E4.1.7)

If \(O_{ R A }\) =1, that is for a first order reaction, we have

\(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}}=-\int_{0}^{t} k d t\), (E4.1.8)

which can be integrated to yield

\(\left.\ln C_{A}\right|_{C_{A 0}} ^{C_{A}}=-\left.k t\right|_{C_{A 0}} ^{C_{A}}\). (E4.1.9)

Thus

\(\ln C_{A}-\ln C_{A 0}=-k t.\) (E4.1.10)

Eq. (E4.1.10) \(\ln C_{A}-\ln C_{A 0}=-k t.\) can be rearranged to yield

\(C_{A}=C_{A 0} \exp (-k t).\) (E4.1.11)

That is, the concentration of A decreases exponentially in a batch reactor for a first order reaction. If \(O_{ RA } \neq 1\), Eq. (E4.1.7) \(\int_{C_{A 0}}^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}^{O_{R A}}}=-\int_{0}^{t} k d t.\) is integrated to yield

\(\left.\frac{C_{A}^{1-O_{R A}}}{1-O_{R A}}\right|_{C_{A 0}} ^{C_{A}}=-\left.k t\right|_{C_{A 0}} ^{C_{A}} .\) (E4.1.12)

Thus

\(C_{A}^{1-O_{R A}}-C_{A 0}^{1-O_{R A}}=-\left(1-O_{R A}\right) k t.\) (E4.1.13)

Eq. (E4.1.13) \(C_{A}^{1-O_{R A}}-C_{A 0}^{1-O_{R A}}=-\left(1-O_{R A}\right) k t.\) can be rearranged to yield

\(C_{A}=\left[C_{A 0}^{1-O_{R A}}-\left(1-O_{R A}\right) k t\right]^{\frac{1}{1-O_{R A}}}\). (E4.1.14)

That is, the concentration of A decreases following a hyperbolic path for an \(O_{ RA } \text { th }\) order reaction.

Fig. E4.1.2 shows the plots for the concentration of A as a function of time. One can observe that the concentration drops down quicker for a lower order reaction than a higher order reaction. This is due to the fact that at low concentrations, higher order reactions are slower than lower order reactions.
