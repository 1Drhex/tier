Guest Question.

Consider a particle of mass m moving freely between x = 0 and x = a inside an infinite square well potential.

(a) Calculate the expectation values \(〈\hat{X}〉_{n}, 〈\hat{P}〉_{n},〈\hat{X}^{2}〉_{n}\) and \(〈\hat{P}^{2}〉_{n}\) and compare them with their classical counterparts.

(b) Calculate the uncertainties product \(\Delta x_{n}\Delta p_{n}\) .

(c) Use the result of (b) to estimate the zero-point energy.

Admin
Solution

(a) Since \(\psi _{n}(x)=\sqrt{2/a}\sin(n\pi x/a)\) and since it is a real function, we have \(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}|\psi _{n} 〉=0 \) because for any real function \(\phi (x)\) the integral \( 〈\hat{P}〉=-i\hbar ∫\phi ^{*}(x)(d\phi (x)dx)dx\) is imaginary and this contradicts the fact that \( 〈\hat{P}〉\) has to be real. On the other hand, the expectation values of \(\hat{X},\hat{X}^{2}\) and \(\hat{P}^{2}\) are

\(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}|\psi _{n} 〉=\int_{0}^{a} \psi ^{*}_{n} x\psi _{n}(x)dx=\frac{2}{a}x\sin ^{2}\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a} \right) dx\)

 

\(=\frac{1}{a} \int_{0}^{a}x\left[1-\cos \left(\frac{2n\pi x}{a} \right) \right] dx=\frac{a}{2}\) (4.206)

 

\(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉=\frac{2}{a} \int_{0}^{a} x^{2} \sin ^{2} \left(\frac{n\pi x}{a} \right)dx=\frac{1}{a} \int_{0} ^{a}x^{2}\left[1-\cos \left(\frac{2n\pi x}{a} \right) \right]dx\)

 

\(=\frac{a^{2}}{3}- \frac{1}{a} \int_{0}^{a}x^{2}\cos \left(\frac {2n\pi x}{a} \right)dx\)

 

\(=\frac{a^{2}}{3}-\frac{1}{2n\pi }x^{2}\sin \left(\frac{2n\pi x}{a} \right)\mid ^{x=a}_{x=0} +\frac{1}{n\pi }\int_{0}^{a}x\sin \left(\frac{2n\pi x}{a} \right)dx\)

 

\(=\frac{a^{2}}{3}-\frac{a^{2}}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}}\), (4.207)

 

\(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉=-\hbar ^{2}\int_{0}^{a}\psi ^{*}_{n}(x)\frac{d^{2}\psi _{n}(x)}{dx^{2}}dx=\frac{n^{2}\pi ^{2} \hbar ^{2}}{a^{2}}\int_{0}^{a}\left|\psi _{n}(x)\right| ^{2}dx=\frac {n^{2}\pi ^{2}\hbar ^{2}}{a^{2}}\). (4.208)

In deriving the previous three expressions, we have used integrations by parts. Since \(E_{n} =n^{2}\pi ^{2}\hbar ^{2}/(2ma^{2})\) , we may write

\(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉=\frac{n^{2}\pi ^{2}\hbar ^{2}}{a^{2}}=2mE_{n}\). (4.409)

To calculate the classical average values \(x_{a\upsilon},p_{a\upsilon},x^{2}_{a\upsilon},p^{2}_{a\upsilon}\), it is easy first to infer that \(p_{a\upsilon}=0\) and \(p^{2}_{a\upsilon}=2mE\), since the particle moves to the right with constant momentum \(p=m_{\upsilon }\) and to the left with \(p=-m_{\upsilon }\). As the particle moves at constant speed, we have \(x=\upsilon t\), hence

\(x_{a\upsilon}=\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}x(t)dt= \frac{\upsilon }{T} \int_{0}^{T}tdt=\upsilon \frac{T}{2}=\frac{a}{2}\), (4.210)

 

\(x^{2}_{a\upsilon}=\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}x^{2}(t)dt=\frac {\upsilon ^{2} }{T} \int_{0}^{T}t^{2}dt=\frac{1}{3} \upsilon ^{2} T^{2} =\frac{a^{2} }{3} \), (4.211)

where T is \(half^{5}\) of the period of the motion, with \(a=\upsilon T\).

We conclude that, while the average classical and quantum expressions for x, p and \(p^{2}\) are identical, a comparison of (4.207) and (4.211) yields

\(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉=\frac{d^{2} }{3}-\frac {a^{2}}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}}=x^{2}_{a\upsilon}-\frac{a^{2}}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}}\), (4.212)

so that in the limit of large quantum numbers, the quantum expression \(〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉\) matches with its classical counterpart \(x^{2}_{a\upsilon}: \lim_{n \longrightarrow \infty } 〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉=a^{2}/3= x^{2}_{a\upsilon}\).

(b) The position and the momentum uncertainties can be calculated from (4.206) to (4.208):

\(\Delta x_{n}=\sqrt{〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉-〈\psi _{n}|\hat{X}|\psi _{n} 〉^{2} } =\sqrt{\frac{a^{2}}{3}-\frac{a^{2}}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}}-\frac{a^{2}}{4}}=a\sqrt{\frac{1}{12}-\frac{1}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}} }\), (4.213)

 

\(\Delta p_{n}=\sqrt{〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉-〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}|\psi _{n} 〉^{2} } =\sqrt{〈\psi _{n}|\hat{P}^{2}|\psi _{n} 〉} =\frac{n\pi \hbar }{a}\), (4.214)

hence

\(\Delta x_{n}\Delta p_{n}=n\pi \hbar \sqrt{\frac{1}{12}-\frac{1}{2n^{2}\pi ^{2}}}\). (4.215)

(c) Equation (4.214) shows that the momentum uncertainty for the ground state is not zero, but

\(\Delta p_{1}=\frac{\pi \hbar }{a}\). (4.216)

This leads to a nonzero kinetic energy. Therefore, the lowest value of the particle’s kinetic energy is of the order of \(E_{\min } \thicksim (\Delta p_{1})^{2}/(2m)\thicksim \pi ^{2}\hbar ^{2}/(2m a^{2} )\). This value, which is in full agreement with the ground state energy, \(E_{1}=\pi ^{2}\hbar ^{2}/(2ma^{2} )\), is the zero-point energy of the particle.
