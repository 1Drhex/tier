CAPILLARY EFFECT: USE OF DIMENSIONAL MATRIX

When a small tube is dipped into a pool of liquid, surface tension causes a meniscus to form at the free surface, which is elevated or depressed depending on the contact angle at the liquid-solid-gas interface. Experiments indicate that the magnitude of this capillary effect, \(Δh\), is a function of the tube diameter, \(D\), liquid specific weight, \(γ\), and surface tension, \(σ\). Determine the number of independent \(Π\) parameters that can be formed and obtain a set.

Admin
Solution

Given: \(Δh = f(D, γ, σ)\)
Find:

(a) Number of independent \(Π\) parameters.
(b) One set of \(Π\) parameters.
Solution:
(Circled numbers refer to steps in the procedure for determining dimensionless \(Π\) parameters.)
(1) \(Δh\quad D \quad γ \quad σ \quad n = 4\) dimensional parameters
(2) Choose primary dimensions (use both \(M, L, t\) and \(F, L, t\) dimensions to illustrate the problem in determining \(m\)).

(3)

(a) \(M, L, t\)

\(\begin{array}{llll}\Delta h & D & \gamma & \sigma\end{array}\) \(L \quad L \quad \frac{M}{L^{2} t^{2}} \quad \frac{M}{t^{2}}\)

\(r=3\) primary dimensions

 (b) \(F, L, t\)

\(\begin{array}{llll}\Delta h & D & \gamma &\sigma\end{array}\) \(L \quad L \quad \frac{F}{L^{3}} \quad \frac{F}{L}\)

\(r=2\) primary dimensions

Thus for each set of primary dimensions we ask, “Is m equal to r?” Let us check each dimensional matrix to find out.
The dimensional matrices are

\(\begin{array}{c|ccrr} & \Delta h & D & \gamma & \sigma \\\hline M & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\L & 1 & 1 & -2 & 0 \\t & 0 & 0 & -2 & -2\end{array}\) \(\begin{array}{c|ccrr} & \Delta h & D & \gamma & \sigma \\\hline F & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\L & 1 & 1 & -3 & -1 \end{array}\)

The rank of a matrix is equal to the order of its largest nonzero determinant.

\(\begin{aligned}&\left|\begin{array}{rrr}0 & 1 & 1 \\1 & -2 & 0 \\0 & -2 & -2\end{array}\right|=0-(1)(-2)+(1)(-2)=0\end{aligned}\)

\(\left|\begin{array}{rr}-2 & 0 \\-2 & -2\end{array}\right|=4 \neq 0 \quad \begin{array}{r}\therefore m=2\\m\neq r\end{array}\)

 \(\begin{aligned}&\left|\begin{array}{rr}1 & 1 \\-3 & -1\end{array}\right|=-1+3=2 \neq 0\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\therefore m=2\\m= r\)

(4)

\(m = 2\). Choose \(D, γ\) as repeating parameters. \(m = 2\). Choose \(D, γ\) as repeating parameters.

(5)

\(n – m = 2\) dimensionless groups will result. \(n – m = 2\) dimensionless groups will result.

 

\(\begin{gathered}\Pi_{1}=D^{a} \gamma^{b} \Delta h \quad \text { and } \\(L)^{a}\left(\frac{M}{L^{2} t^{2}}\right)^{b}(L)=M^{0} L^{0} t^{0} \\\left.\begin{array}{c}M: \quad b+0=0 \\L: \quad a-2 b+1=0 \\t: -2 b+0=0\end{array}\right\} \quad\quad \begin{array}{l}b=0 \\a=-1\end{array} \\\text { Therefore, } \Pi_{1}=\frac{\Delta h}{D} \\\Pi_{2}=D^{c} \gamma^{d} \sigma \text { and } \\(L)^{c}\left(\frac{M}{L^{2} t^{2}}\right)^{d} \frac{M}{t^{2}}=M^{0} L^{0} t^{0} \\\left.\begin{array}{c}M: \quad d+1=0 \\L: \quad c-2 d=0 \\t: -2 d-2=0\end{array}\right\} \quad\quad\begin{array}{l}d=-1 \\c=-2\end{array}\\\text { Therefore, } \Pi_{2}=\frac{\sigma}{D^{2} \gamma}\end{gathered}\) \(\begin{aligned}&\Pi_{1}=D^{e} \gamma^{f} \Delta h \text { and }\\&\qquad(L)^{e}\left(\frac{F}{L^{3}}\right)^{f} L=F^{0} L^{0} t^{0}\end{aligned}\)

\(\left.\begin{array}{ll}F: & f=0 \\L: & e-3 f+1=0\end{array}\right\} \quad e=-1\)

 

Therefore, \(\Pi_{1}=\frac{\Delta h}{D}\)
\(\Pi_{2}=D^{g} \gamma^{h} \sigma\) and
\((L)^{g}\left(\frac{F}{L^{3}}\right)^{h} \frac{F}{L}=F^{0} L^{0} t^{0}\)

 

\(\left.\begin{array}{ll}F: & h+1=0 \\L: & g-3 h-1=0\end{array}\right\} \quad \begin{aligned}&h=-1\\&g=-2\end{aligned}\)

Therefore, \(\Pi_{2}=\frac{\sigma}{D^{2} \gamma}\)

(6)

Check, using \(F, L, t\) dimensions

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\Pi_{1}\right]=\left[\frac{\Delta h}{D}\right] \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{L}{L}=1} \\&{\left[\Pi_{2}\right]=\left[\frac{\sigma}{D^{2} \gamma}\right] \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{F}{L} \frac{1}{L^{2}} \frac{L^{3}}{F}=1}\end{aligned}\)

 Check, using \(M, L, t\) dimensions

\(\begin{aligned}&{\left[\Pi_{1}\right]=\left[\frac{\Delta h}{D}\right] \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{L}{L}=1} \\&{\left[\Pi_{2}\right]=\left[\frac{\sigma}{D^{2} \gamma}\right] \quad \text { and } \quad \frac{M}{L} \frac{1}{L^{2}} \frac{L^{2}t^2}{M}=1}\end{aligned}\)

 

Therefore, both systems of dimensions yield the same dimensionless Π parameters. The predicted functional relationship is

\(\Pi_{1}=f\left(\Pi_{2}\right) \quad \text { or } \quad \frac{\Delta h}{D}=f\left(\frac{\sigma}{D^{2} \gamma}\right)\)

 

Notes:

  • This result is reasonable on physical grounds. The fluid is static; we would not expect time to be an important dimension.
  • We analyzed this problem in , where we found that Δh = 4σ cos(θ)/ρgD (θ is the contact angle). Hence Δh/D is directly proportional to σ/D\(^2\)γ.
  • The purpose of this problem is to illustrate use of the dimensional matrix to determine the required number of repeating parameters.
