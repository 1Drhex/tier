Guest Question.

In a microwave oven operating at a frequency of \(10_{9} \)Hz (one gigahertz), water is heated in two paper cups. As shown in Figure, in one cup the water used is distilled and in the other it has 0.5% (on molal basis) of ordinary salt (NaCl). The power setting on the microwave oven is chosen for \(\bar{(e_{e}^{2})}^{1/2}=10^{2} V/m \). For the \(\rho c_{p}\) of water and cup use \(4×10^{6} J/m^{3}- ^{\circ }C\) and \(4 × 10^{5} J/m^{3}- ^{\circ} C\). Assume that no heat is transferred between the water and its surroundings and also the cup and its surroundings. Also assume that the electric field is uniform within the cup.

(a) For both cases (i.e., with and without salt), estimate the rate of increase in the temperature of the water and the cup.
(b) For each of the cups, does the temperature of the water rise faster or slower than that of the cup? Use the dielectric loss factor at a temperature near \( 90^{\circ} C\).

Admin
Solution

We begin by writing the integral-volume energy equation for the water and the cup, assuming a uniform temperature and a uniform energy conversion for
each medium, i.e., \(T_{\omega }\) and \(T_{c}\) , and \((\dot{S} _{e,m})_{\omega } \) and \((\dot{S} _{e,m})_{c }\). These energy equations are

\( Q\mid _{A,\omega }=0=-V_{\omega }\frac{d}{dt}(\rho c_{p})_{\omega }T_{\omega }+V_{\omega }(\dot{s}_{e,m} )_{\omega } \) energy equation for water

\( Q\mid _{A,c }=0=-V_{c }\frac{d}{dt}(\rho c_{p})_{c }T_{c }+V_{c}(\dot{s}_{e,m} )_{c } \) energy equation for cup.

The energy conversion terms are the volumetric microwave heating rates given by \(\frac{\dot{S}_{e,m} }{V}= \dot{s}_{e,m}=2\pi f\epsilon _{ec}\epsilon _{o}\overline{e_{e}^{2}}, \overline{e_{e}^{2}}= \overline{e_{e}\cdot e_{e}} \). These would become

\( (\dot{s}_{e,m})_{\omega }= 2\pi f\epsilon _{ec,\omega }\epsilon _{o}\overline{e_{e}^{2}} \) volumetric energy conversion for water

\((\dot{s}_{e,m})_{c }= 2\pi f\left\langle\epsilon _{ec}\right\rangle_{c} \epsilon _{o}\overline{e_{e}^{2}} \) volumetric energy conversion for cup.

Using these in the above energy equations, we solve for the time rate of change in temperature and have (for constant \( \rho c_{p}\) )

\( \frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}=\frac{(\dot{s}_{e,m} )_{\omega }}{(\rho c_{p})_{\omega }} \) rate of rise in temperature of water

\( \frac{dT_{c }}{dt}=\frac{(\dot{s}_{e,m} )_{c }}{(\rho c_{p})_{c }} \) rate of rise in temperature of cup

or

\( \frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}=\frac{2\pi f\epsilon _{ec,\omega }\epsilon _{0} \overline{e_{e}^{2}} }{(\rho c_{p})_{\omega }} \) and \( \frac{dT_{c }}{dt}=\frac{2\pi f\left\langle\epsilon _{ec }\right\rangle_{c} \epsilon _{0} \overline{e_{e}^{2}} }{(\rho c_{p})_{c }} \)

(a) For \(f = 10^{9}\) Hz, we will use the values for \(\epsilon _{ec , \omega}\) and\( \left\langle\epsilon _{ec }\right\rangle_{c} \) from Table . Note that for the paper we have the dielectric loss factor at \(T = 82^{\circ }C\) and for the distilled water at \(T = 95^{\circ }C\) and for the distilled water with 0.5% (in molal basis) salt at \(T = 25^{\circ }C\). We use these as the only available properties, noting the lack of consistency in temperatures used for properties.

\( \left\langle\epsilon _{ec }\right\rangle_{c}=0.216 \) paper, Table

\( (\epsilon _{ec,\omega })_{no salt} =0.364\) distilled water, Table

\( (\epsilon _{ec,\omega })_{salt}=269 \) distilled water with salt, Table

Substituting the numerical values in these equations, for the time rate of change in temperatures, we have

\(\frac{dT_{c }}{dt}=\frac{2\pi \times 10^{9}(1/s)\times 0.216\times 8.8542\times 10^{-12}(A^{2}-s^{2}/N-m^{2})\times (10^{2})^{2}(V/m)^{2} }{4\times 10^{5}(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)} \)

 

\( \frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{no salt}=\frac{2\pi \times 10^{9}(1/s)\times 0.364\times 8.8542\times 10^{-12}(A^{2}-s^{2}/N-m^{2})\times (10^{2})^{2}(V/m)^{2} }{4\times 10^{6}(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)} \)

 

\(\frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{salt}=\frac{2\pi \times 10^{9}(1/s)\times 269\times 8.8542\times 10^{-12}(A^{2}-s^{2}/N-m^{2})\times (10^{2})^{2}(V/m)^{2} }{4\times 10^{6}(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)} \)

In reviewing the units, note that

\( \frac{(1/s)(A^{2}-s^{2}/N-m^{2})(V^{2}/m^{2}) }{(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)} =\frac{(A^{2}-V^{2}-s^{2})(1/N-m^{4}-s) }{(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)} = \frac{(W^{2}-s^{2})(1/J-m^{3}-s) }{(J/m^{3}-^{\circ }C)}=\frac{(J^{2}-^{\circ }C)}{(J^{2}-s)}=(^{\circ }C/s) \)

where we have used

(V-A)=(W), (J)=(W-s), and (J)=(N-m)

Carrying on the arithmetic, we have

\( \frac{dT_{c}}{dt}=3.00\times 10^{-4\circ }C/s \)

 

\( \frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{no salt}=5.06\times 10^{-5\circ }C/s \)

 

\( \frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{salt}=0.374^{\circ }C/s \)

(b) From these numerical results, by comparing the rates of temperature rise for the case of distilled water without salt, we have

\(\frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{no salt}\lt \frac{dT_{c }}{dt} \)

i.e., the cup heats up faster. For the case of added salt

\(\frac{dT_{\omega }}{dt}\mid _{salt}\gt \frac{dT_{c }}{dt} \)

i.e., the salt water heats up faster.
