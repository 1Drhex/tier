Guest Question.

Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^{3} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{2}\) be the linear transformation defined by

\( T\left[\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \\ z \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} x-2 y \\ x+y-3 z \end{array}\right] \)

and let \(\mathcal{B}=\left\{\mathbf{e}_{1}, \mathbf{e}_{2}, \mathbf{e}_{3}\right\}\) and \(\mathcal{C}=\left\{\mathbf{e}_{2}, \mathrm{e}_{1}\right\}\) be bases for \(\mathbb{R}^{3}\) and \(\mathbb{R}^{2}\), respectively. Find the matrix of \(T\) with respect to \(\mathcal{B}\) and \(\mathcal{C}\) and verify Theorem 6.26 for \(\mathbf{v}=\left[\begin{array}{r}1 \\ 3 \\ -2\end{array}\right]\)

Admin
Solution

First, we compute

\( T\left(\mathbf{e}_{1}\right)=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right], \quad T\left(\mathbf{e}_{2}\right)=\left[\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right], \quad T\left(\mathbf{e}_{3}\right)=\left[\begin{array}{r} 0 \\ -3 \end{array}\right] \)

Next, we need their coordinate vectors with respect to \(\mathcal{C} .\) Since

\( \left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]=\mathbf{e}_{2}+\mathbf{e}_{1}, \quad\left[\begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]=\mathbf{e}_{2}-2 \mathbf{e}_{1}, \quad\left[\begin{array}{r} 0 \\ -3 \end{array}\right]=-3 \mathbf{e}_{2}+0 \mathbf{e}_{1} \)

we have

\( \left[T\left(\mathbf{e}_{1}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}=\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right], \quad\left[T\left(\mathbf{e}_{2}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}=\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -2 \end{array}\right], \quad\left[T\left(\mathbf{e}_{3}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}=\left[\begin{array}{r} -3 \\ 0 \end{array}\right] \)

Therefore, the matrix of \(T\) with respect to \(\mathcal{B}\) and \(\mathcal{C}\) is

\( \begin{aligned} A &=[T]_{C \leftarrow B}=\left[\left[T\left(\mathrm{e}_{1}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}\left[T\left(\mathrm{e}_{2}\right)\right]_{c}\left[T\left(\mathrm{e}_{3}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}\right] \\ &=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 1 & -3 \\ 1 & -2 & 0 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned} \)

To verify Theorem \(6.26\) for \(\mathbf{v}\), we first compute

\( T(\mathbf{v})=T\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} -5 \\ 10 \end{array}\right] \)

Then

\( \begin{gathered} {[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}}=\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{array}\right]} \\and {[T(\mathbf{v})]_{\mathcal{C}}=\left[\begin{array}{c} -5 \\ 10 \end{array}\right]_{c}=\left[\begin{array}{r} 10 \\ -5 \end{array}\right]} \end{gathered} \)

(Check these.) Using all of these facts, we confirm that

\( A[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}}=\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 1 & -3 \\ 1 & -2 & 0 \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{r} 1 \\ 3 \\ -2 \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} 10 \\ -5 \end{array}\right]=[T(\mathbf{v})]_{\mathcal{C}} \)

Theorem 6.26 Let \(V\) and \(W\) be two finite-dimensional vector spaces with bases \(\mathcal{B}\) and \(\mathcal{C}\), respectively, where \(\mathcal{B}=\left\{\mathrm{v}_{1}, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{n}\right\}\). If \(T: V \rightarrow W\) is a linear transformation, then the \(m \times n\) matrix \(A\) defined by

\(A=\left[\left[T\left(\mathbf{v}_{1}\right)\right]_{C}\left|\left[T\left(\mathbf{v}_{2}\right)\right]_{\mathcal{C}}\right| \cdots \mid\left[T\left(\mathbf{v}_{n}\right)\right]_{c}\right]\)

satisfies

\(A[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}}=[T(\mathbf{v})]_{c}\)

for every vector \(\mathrm{v}\) in \(V\).
