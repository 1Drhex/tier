Guest Question.

For the circuit of Fig. 2.13 calculate (a) the current drawn from the source, (b) the p.d. across each resistor, (c) the current through each resistor, and (d) the power dissipated by the 5立 resistor.

Admin
Solution

The first step in the solution is to label the diagram clearly with letters at the junctions and identifying p.d.s and branch currents. This shown in Fig. 2.14 .

\(\begin{aligned}(a) R_{A B} &=\frac{R_{1} R_{2}}{R_{1}+R_{2}} \text { ohm }=\frac{4 \times 6}{4+6}=2.4 \Omega \\R_{B C} &=5 \Omega \\\frac{1}{R_{C D}} &=\frac{1}{R_{4}}+\frac{1}{R_{5}}+\frac{1}{R_{6}}=\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{8} \\&=\frac{8+4+3}{24}=\frac{15}{24} \mathrm{~S} \\R_{C D} &=\frac{24}{15}=1.6 \Omega \\R &=R_{A B}+R_{B C}+R_{C D} \text { ohm } \\R &=2.4+5+1.6=9 \Omega \\I &=\frac{E}{R} \text { amp }=\frac{18}{9} \\I &=2 \mathrm{~A}\end{aligned}\)

(b) The circuit has been reduced to its series equivalent as shown in Fig. 2.15. Using this equivalent circuit it is now a simple matter to calculate the p.d. across each section of the circuit.

\(\begin{aligned}&V_{A B}=I R_{A B} \text { volt }=2 \times 2.4 \\&V_{A B}=4.8 \mathrm{~V}\end{aligned}\)

(this p.d. is common to both \(R_{1}\) and \(R_{2}\) )

\(\begin{aligned}&V_{B C}=I R_{B C} \text { volt }=2 \times 5 \\&V_{B C}=10 \mathrm{~V} \\&V_{C D}=I R_{C D} \text { volt }=2 \times 1.6 \\&V_{C D}=3.2 \mathrm{~V}\end{aligned}\)

(this p.d. is common to \(R_{4}, R_{5}\) and \(R_{6}\) )

(c) \(I_{1}=\frac{V_{A B}}{R_{1}}=\frac{4.8}{4}\) or \(\quad I_{1}=\frac{R_{2}}{R_{1}+R_{2}} \times I=\frac{6}{10} \times 2\)
\(I_{1}=1.2 \mathrm{~A}\) \(\quad I_{1}=1.2 \mathrm{~A}\)
\(I_{2}=\frac{V_{A B}}{R_{2}}=\frac{4.8}{6}\) or \(\quad I_{2}=\frac{R_{1}}{R_{1}+R_{2}} \times I=\frac{4}{10} \times 2\)
\(I_{2}=0.8 \mathrm{~A}\) \(\quad I_{2}=0.8 \mathrm{~A}\)
\(I_{3}=I=2\) A
\(I_{4}=\frac{V_{C D}}{R_{4}}=\frac{3.2}{3}\) or \(\quad \frac{1}{R_{C D}}=\frac{1}{R_{4}}+\frac{1}{R_{5}}+\frac{1}{R_{6}}\)
\(I_{4}=1.067 \mathrm{~A}\) \(\quad=\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{8}\)
\(I_{5}=\frac{V_{C D}}{R_{5}}=\frac{3.2}{6} \quad=\frac{8+4+3}{24}=\frac{15}{24}\)
\(I_{5}=0.533\) A \(\quad\) so \(I_{4}=\frac{8}{15} \times 2\)
\(I_{6}=\frac{V_{C D}}{R_{6}}=\frac{3.2}{8} \quad I_{4}=1.067 \mathrm{~A}\)
\(I_{6}=0.4 \mathrm{~A}\) \(\quad I_{5}=\frac{4}{15} \times 2\)
\(I_{5}=0.533\) A
\(I_{6}=\frac{3}{15} \times 2\)
\(I_{6}=0.4 \mathrm{~A}\)

Notice that the p.d. method is an easier and less cumbersomeone than current division when more than two resistors are connected in parallel.
(d) \(P_{3}=I_{3}^{2} R_{3}\) watt or \(V_{B C} I_{3}\) watt or \(\frac{V_{B C}^{2}}{R_{3}}\) watt and using the first of these alternative equation:

\(\begin{aligned}&P_{3}=2^{2} \times 5 \\&P_{3}=20 \mathrm{~W}\end{aligned}\)

It is left to the reader to confirm that the other two power equations above yield the same answer.
