Guest Question.

Find h for the bilateral resistive circuit drawn in Fig. 17.24.

Admin
Solution

With the output short-circuited \(\left( V _{2}=0\right)\), the application of a 1 A source at the input \(\left( I _{1}=1 A \right)\) produces an input voltage of 3.4 V \(\left( V _{1}=3.4 V \right)\); hence, \(h _{11}=3.4 \Omega\). Under these same conditions, the output current is easily obtained by current division: \(I _{2}=-0.4\) A; thus, \(h _{21}=-0.4\).

The remaining two parameters are obtained with the input opencircuited \(\left( I _{1}=0\right)\). We apply 1 V to the output terminals \(\left( V _{2}=1 V \right)\).

The response at the input terminals is 0.4 V \(\left( V _{1}=0.4 V \right)\), and thus \(h _{12}=0.4\). The current delivered by this source at the output terminals is 0.1 A \(\left( I _{2}=0.1 A \right)\), and therefore \(h _{22}=0.1 S\).

We therefore have \(h =\left[\begin{array}{cl}3.4 \Omega & 0.4 \\-0.4 & 0.1 S\end{array}\right]\). It is a consequence of the reciprocity theorem that \(h _{12}=- h _{21}\) for a bilateral network.
