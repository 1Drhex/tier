Guest Question.

ELECTRIC AND MAGNETIC FIELD OF A LASER BEAM

A carbon dioxide laser emits a sinusoidal electromagnetic wave that travels in vacuum in the negative x-direction. The wavelength is 10.6 mm (in the infrared; see Fig. 32.4) and the \(\vec E\) field is parallel to the z-axis, with \(E_{max}\) = 1.5 MV/m. Write vector equations for \(\vec E\) and \(\vec B\) as functions of time and position.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

Equations (32.19) describe a wave traveling in the negative x-direction with \(\vec E\) along the y-axis—that is, a wave that is linearly polarized along the y-axis. By contrast, the wave in this example is linearly polarized along the z-axis. At points where \(\vec E\) is in the positive z-direction, \(\vec B\) must be in the positive y-direction for the vector product \(\vec E\) × \(\vec B\) to be in the negative x-direction (the direction of propagation). Figure 32.15 shows a wave that satisfies these requirements.

\(\begin{aligned}\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}}(x, t) &=\hat{\jmath} E_{\max } \cos (k x+\omega t) \\\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}(x, t) &=-\hat{\boldsymbol{k}} B_{\max } \cos(kx+\omega t)\end{aligned}\) (32.19)

EXECUTE:

A possible pair of wave functions that describe the wave shown in Fig. 32.15 are

\(\begin{aligned}&\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}}(x, t)=\hat{\boldsymbol{k}} E_{\max } \cos(k x+\omega t) \\&\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}(x, t)=\hat{\jmath} B_{\max } \cos (kx+\omega t)\end{aligned}\)

The plus sign in the arguments of the cosine functions indicates
that the wave is propagating in the negative x-direction, as it
should. Faraday’s law requires that \(E_{max} = cB_{max}\) [Eq. (32.18)], so

\(B_{\max }=\frac{E_{\max }}{c}=\frac{1.5 \times 10^{6} \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m}}{3.0 \times 10^{8} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}}=5.0 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~T}\)

(Recall that 1 V = 1 Wb/s and 1 Wb/\(m^2\) = 1 T.)
We have λ = 10.6 × \(10^{-6}\) m, so the wave number and angular frequency are

\(\begin{aligned}k &=\frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}=\frac{2 \pi \mathrm{rad}}{10.6 \times 10^{-6}\mathrm{~m}}=5.93 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m} \\\omega &=c k=\left(3.00 \times10^{8} \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\right)\left(5.93 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{rad} /\mathrm{m}\right) \\&=1.78 \times 10^{14} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\end{aligned}\)

Substituting these values into the above wave functions, we get

\(\begin{aligned}\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{E}}(x, t)=& \hat{\boldsymbol{k}}\left(1.5 \times10^{6} \mathrm{~V} / \mathrm{m}\right) \\& \times \cos \left[\left(5.93 \times 10^{5}\mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m}\right) x+\left(1.78 \times 10^{14} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\right) t\right] \\\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}(x, t)=& \hat{\jmath}\left(5.0 \times 10^{-3}\mathrm{~T}\right) \\& \times \cos \left[\left(5.93 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{m}\right) x+\left(1.78 \times 10^{14} \mathrm{rad} / \mathrm{s}\right) t\right]\end{aligned}\)

 

EVALUATE: As we expect, the magnitude Bmax in teslas is much smaller than the magnitude Emax in volts per meter. To check the directions of \(\vec E\) and \(\vec B\), note that \(\vec E\) × \(\vec B\) is in the direction of \(\hat{k} \times \hat{\jmath}=-\hat{\imath}\). This is as it should be for a wave that propagates in the negative x-direction.

Our expressions for \(\vec E (x,t)\) and \(\vec B (x,t)\) are not the only possible solutions. We could always add a phase angle f to the arguments of the cosine function, so that \(k x+\omega t\) would become \(k x+\omega t+\phi\) To determine the value of \(\phi\) we would need to know \(\vec E\) and \(\vec B\) either as functions of \(x\) at a given time \(t\) or as functions of \(t\) at a given coordinate \(x\). However, the statement of the problem doesn’t include this information.
