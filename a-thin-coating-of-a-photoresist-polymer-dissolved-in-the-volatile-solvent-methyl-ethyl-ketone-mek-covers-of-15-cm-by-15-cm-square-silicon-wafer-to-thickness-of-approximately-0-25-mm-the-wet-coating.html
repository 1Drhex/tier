Guest Question.

A thin coating of a photoresist polymer dissolved in the volatile solvent methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) covers of 15 cm by 15 cm square silicon wafer to thickness of approximately 0.25 mm. The wet coating contains 30 wt% polymer solid and 70 wt% solvent, and the total mass loading of the coating is 0.50 g. The solvent can be considered to be in a liquid state. The solvent will be evaporated from the polymer surface by flowing air parallel to the surface of the wafer, as shown in Figure. The solvent vapor must transfer from the coating surface through the hydrodynamic boundary layer (dotted line in Figure) formed by the air flow over the surface. During the solvent evaporation process, a heating plate beneath the wafer maintains the coating at a uniform temperature of \( 27^{\circ} C \), and the vapor pressure exerted by the solvent at this temperature is constant at 99.4 mm Hg. The flowing air is also at \( 27^{\circ} C \) and 1.0 atm total system pressure. The rate of solvent evaporation is limited by the convective masstransfer process. The flow of air is high enough that relative to the rate of solvent transfer, the concentration of solvent vapor in the bulk gas \( \left(c_{A, \infty}\right) \) is very small and can be assumed to be near zero. Furthermore, any resistances associated with the molecular diffusion of the solvent through the very thin polymer coating can be neglected.

Admin
Solution

Experimental tests demonstrated that the solvent in the polymer coating evaporated after 45 s following exposure to the flowing air stream. Based on the assumptions above, what is an estimate of convective mass-transfer coefficient needed for this process?

First, the molar flux of solvent (MEK, species A, molecular weight 72 g/gmole) from the coating will be estimated from the test result. The source of mass transfer is the solvent in the polymer coating, and the sink is the surrounding air stream. The convective mass-transfer process will be assumed to be steady-state so the solvent evaporation rate is constant up until all the solvent in the wet polymer coating is evaporated. A process material balance on the solvent (A) as moles A/time is

( rate of solvent transfer from surface to flowing airsteam ) = ( rate of change of solvent loading within polymer coating )

 

\( – N_{A} S = \frac{d m_{A}}{d t} \)

 

where \( m_{A} \) is the molar amount of solvent remaining in the coating, and S is the cross-sectional area for mass-transfer flux. Since the convective flux \( N_{A} \) is constant with time, integration from the initial time \( \left(t = 0, m_{A} = m_{A o}\right)\) to the final time when all the solvent has evaporated \( \left(t=t_{f}, m_{A} = 0\right) \) gives

 

\( – N_{A} S \int_{0}^{t_{f}} d t = \int_{m_{\Lambda o}}^{0} d m_{A}\)

 

or

\( N_{A} = \frac{m_{A o}}{t_{f} S} = \frac{4.86 \times 10^{-3} gmole MEK }{(45 s )(15 cm )^{2}} = 4.80 \times 10^{-7} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{2} \cdot s } \)

 

where \( m_{A o} \) is determined by

 

\( m_{A o} = 0.50 g \text { coating } \frac{0.70 g MEK }{ g \text { coating }} \cdot \frac{1 gmole MEK }{72 g MEK } = 4.86 \times 10^{-3} gmole MEK \)

 

The convective mass-transfer flux \( N_{A} \) is defined by

 

\( N_{A} = k_{c}\left(c_{A s} – c_{A, \infty}\right) \)

 

Since the flux \( N_{A} \) is known, the convective mass-transfer coefficient required to achieve \( N_{A} \) is

 

\( k_{c} = \frac{N_{A}}{\left(c_{A s} – c_{A, \infty}\right)} = \frac{4.86 \times 10^{-7} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{2} s }}{5.31 \times 10^{-6} \frac{ gmole }{ cm ^{3}}-0} = 0.090 cm / s \)

 

We will see in this chapter that \( k_{c} \) will be a function of the molecular diffusion coefficient in the fluid phase, the properties of the flowing fluid, and the fluid velocity.
