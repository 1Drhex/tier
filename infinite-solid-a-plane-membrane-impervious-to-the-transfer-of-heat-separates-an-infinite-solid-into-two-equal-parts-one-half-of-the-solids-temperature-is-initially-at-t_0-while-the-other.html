Guest Question.

Infinite Solid. A plane membrane, impervious to the transfer of heat, separates an infinite solid into two equal parts. One half of the solid’s temperature is initially at \(T_0\) while the other half is at zero. At time t = 0, the membrane is removed and the solids are brought into direct contact with each other. Calculate the temperature in the solid as a function of position and time.

Admin
Solution

The initial temperature profile in the system is shown in Figure 2.7.

The PDE describing this system can easily be shown to be:

\(\frac{∂T}{∂t}=a\frac{∂^2T}{∂y^2}\) where T = T(y,t)

The BC and IC are:

BC(1): T = finite at y = \(\infty\) , for all t and approaching \(T_0\) /2 as t→ \(\infty\)

BC(2): T = finite at y = \(-\infty\) , for all t and approaching \(T_0\) /2 as t→ \(\infty\)

IC: T = \(T_0\) at t = 0, for \(-\infty\)≤ y ≤ 0

T = 0 at t = 0, for 0 ≤ y ≤ + \(\infty\)

The above equation can be solved by using Laplace transforms or Fourier integrals. Theodore provides the details of the solution [2].

\(T=\frac{T_0}{2}[1-erf\left\lgroup\frac{y}{\sqrt{4at} } \right\rgroup ]\)

The error function is tabulated in most advanced mathematics texts [6–8]. A plot of erf(z) against z is presented in Figure 2.8. The reader should note that the notation z used in the error function is a generic variable. Note that this function has the properties:

erf (\(\infty\)) = 1.0, erf (0) = 0, erf(\(-\infty)\) = -1.0

and,

erf(-z) = -erf (z)

It can also be evaluated numerically by expanding \(e^{-ξ2}\) in a power series:

\(e^{-\xi 2}=1-\xi ^2+\frac{1}{2}\xi ^4-…\)

The RHS of this equation can then integrated term by term to give:

\(erf(z)=\frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi } }\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty }\frac{(-1)^n\ z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\)

This power series converges for all z, although very slowly for large z. The reader can now verify that Equation \(\alpha \left[\frac{1}{r}\frac{∂}{∂r}\left\lgroup r\frac{∂T}{∂r} \right\rgroup+\frac{1}{r^2}\frac{∂^2T}{r^2∂\phi ^2}+\frac{∂^2T}{∂z^2} \right]+\frac{A}{\rho C_p}=\frac{∂T}{∂t}\) is a solution to Equation \(\alpha \left[\frac{∂^2T}{∂x^2}+\frac{∂^2T}{∂y^2}+\frac{∂^2T}{∂z^2} \right]+\frac{A}{\rho C_p}=\frac{∂T}{∂t} \). A check of the BC and/or IC yields:

BC: at t = \(\infty\) and any y, \( T=\frac{T_0}{2}\)

IC: at t = 0 and y > 0, \(T=\frac{T_0}{2}[1-erf(\infty )] \)

\(=\frac{T_0}{2}[1-1]\)

=0

at t = 0 and y < 0, \(T=\frac{T_0}{2}[1-erf(-\infty )]\)

\(=\frac{T_0}{2}[1-(-1)]\)

\(=T_0\)
