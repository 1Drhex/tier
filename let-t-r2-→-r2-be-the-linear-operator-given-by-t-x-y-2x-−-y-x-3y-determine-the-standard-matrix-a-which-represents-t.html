Guest Question.

Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^{2} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{2}\) be the linear operator given by

\(T\left(\left[\begin{array}{l}x \\y\end{array}\right]\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l}2 x-y \\x+3 y\end{array}\right)\)

Determine the standard matrix A which represents \(T\).

Admin
Solution

What is the standard basis for \(\mathbb{R}^{2} ?\)

\(\mathrm{e}_{1}=(10)^{T}\) is the unit vector in the \(x\) direction and \(\mathrm{e}_{2}=(01)^{T}\) is the unit vector in the \(y\) direction. Applying the given linear operator to these basis (axes) vectors \(\left\{\mathrm{e}_{1}, \mathrm{e}_{2}\right\}\) yields:

\( \begin{aligned} &T\left(\mathbf{e}_{1}\right)=T\left(\left[\begin{array}{l} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 2(1)-0 \\ 1+3(0) \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 2 \\ 1 \end{array}\right) \quad\left[\text { using } T\left(\left[\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right]\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} 2 x-y \\ x+3 y \end{array}\right)\right] \\ &T\left(\mathrm{e}_{2}\right)=T\left(\left[\begin{array}{l} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 2(0)-1 \\ 0+3(1) \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right) \quad\left[\mathrm{using} T\left(\left[\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right]\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} 2 x-y \\ x+3 y \end{array}\right)\right] \end{aligned} \)

Note, \(T\left(\mathrm{e}_{1}\right)\) gives the \(x\) coefficients and \(T\left(\mathrm{e}_{2}\right)\) gives the \(y\) coefficients, because \(\mathrm{e}_{1}\) and \(\mathrm{e}_{2}\) are the unit vectors in these directions respectively.

What is the standard matrix A equal to in this case?

By the above Proposition (5.29) we have \(\mathbf{A}=\left(\begin{array}{ll}T\left(\mathrm{e}_{1}\right) & T\left(\mathbf{e}_{2}\right)\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{rr}2 & -1 \\ 1 & 3\end{array}\right)\).

Note that we have the same matrix as in which represents the given transformation \(T\). We have \(T(\mathbf{u})=\) Au where \(\mathbf{u}=(x y)^{T}\)

Proposition (5.29). Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^{n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m}\) be a linear transformation and \(\left\{\mathbf{e}_{1}, \mathbf{e}_{2}, \mathbf{e}_{3}, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_{n}\right\}\) be the standard basis for \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\). If

\( T\left(\mathbf{e}_{1}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} c_{11} \\ c_{21} \\ \vdots \\ c_{m 1} \end{array}\right), \quad T\left(\mathbf{e}_{2}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} c_{12} \\ c_{22} \\ \vdots \\ c_{m 2} \end{array}\right), \cdots \text { and } T\left(\mathbf{e}_{n}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{c} c_{1 n} \\ c_{2 n} \\ \vdots \\ c_{m n} \end{array}\right) \)

then we can write the transformation \(T(\mathbf{u})\) as

\( T(\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{A u} \)

where \(\mathbf{A}=\left(\begin{array}{llll}T\left(\mathbf{e}_{1}\right) & T\left(\mathbf{e}_{2}\right) & \cdots & T\left(\mathbf{e}_{n}\right)\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{ccc}c_{11} & c_{12} & c_{1 n} \\ c_{21} & c_{22} & c_{2 n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ c_{m 1} & c_{m 2} & c_{m n}\end{array}\right)\) and \(\mathbf{u}\) is in \(\mathbb{R}^{n}\).
