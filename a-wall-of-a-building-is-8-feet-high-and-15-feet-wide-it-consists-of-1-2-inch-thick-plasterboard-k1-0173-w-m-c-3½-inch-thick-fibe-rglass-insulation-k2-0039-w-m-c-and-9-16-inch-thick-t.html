Guest Question.

A wall of a building is 8 feet high and 15 feet wide. It consists of 1/2 inch thick plasterboard \(\left(k_{1}=0.173 W / m C \right)\), 3½ inch thick fibe rglass insulation \(\left(k_{2}=0.039 W / m C \right)\), and 9/16 inch thick T-111 siding \(\left(k_{3}=0.115 W / m C \right)\). The air inside the house is quiet at 72 F. Outside the house, the air is at 35 F, and it is blowing horizontally across the siding at 10 miles per hour. What is the rate of heat transfer through the wall?

Admin
Solution

Preliminary: There is natural convection at the inside surface of the wall and forced convection on the outside surface.We will assume that radiation is insignificant. The heat transfer is steady state. Composite walls were considered in Chapter 3. We will use the resistance concept from that chapter to solve the problem. Eqs. (10.2), (10.4), (10.6) will be used for the properties of air. These equations are for air temperatures being in Kelvin degrees.

The applicable equation is Eq. (3.18):

 

\(q=\frac{\Delta T_{\text {overall }}}{\sum R}\) (3.18)

 

\(k( W / mK ) \text { and } T( K ): k=4.629 \times 10^{-12} T^{3}-2.899 \times 10^{-8} T^{2}+9.041 \times 10^{-5} T+0.001009\) (10.2)

 

\(v\left( m ^{2} / s \right) \text { and } T( K ): v=-1.725 \times 10^{-14} T^{3}+1.027 \times 10^{-10} T^{2}+3.725 \times 10^{-8} T-4.196 \times 10^{-6}\) (10.4)

 

\(T( K ): Pr =2.916 \times 10^{-13} T^{4}-1.148 \times 10^{-9} T^{3}+1.675 \times 10^{-6} T^{2}-1.021 \times 10^{-3} T+0.9126\) (10.6)

 

For this problem, the overall temperature difference is the difference between the inside and outside air temperatures. There are five resistances: three conductive resistances for the wall layers and two convective resistances for the inner and outer surfaces of the wall. For a layer, the resistance is \(\frac{L}{k A}\), where L is the thickness of the layer, k is the conductivity of the layer, and A is the cross-sectional area through which the heat flows. For the convection, the resistance is \(\frac{1}{h A}\), where h is the convective coefficient. Hence, for this problem, the heat flow through the wall is, from Eq. (3.18),

 

\(q=\frac{T_{\infty_{i}}-T_{\infty_{o}}}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{1} A}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{1}}{k_{1} A}\right)_{\text {plaster }}+\left(\frac{L_{2}}{k_{2} A}\right)_{\text {fiberglass }}+\left(\frac{L_{3}}{k_{3} A}\right)_{T-111}+\left(\frac{1}{h_{4} A}\right)}\) (10.41)

 

\(L_{1}=0.5 \text { inch } \times\left(\frac{0.0254 m }{\text { inch }}\right)=0.0127 m . \text { Similarly, } L_{2}=0.0889 m \text { and } L_{3}=0.01429 m\)

 

\(T_{\infty_{i}}=72 F =(5 / 9)(72-32)=22.22 C \quad T_{\infty_{o}}=35 F =(5 / 9)(35-32)=1.67 C\)

 

Rearranging Eq. (10.41) and putting in values, we have

 

\(\frac{q}{A}=\frac{20.55}{2.477+\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{h_{4}}\right)}\) (10.42)

 

Looking at Eq. (10.42), we see that we need convective coefficients \(h_{1} \text { and } h_{4}\) to determine the heat flow through the wall. These convective coefficients depend on their respective film temperatures. Hence, we need to determine \(T_{1} \text { and } T_{4}\). There is no need to determine interface temperatures \(T_{2} \text { and } T_{3}\). The resistance concept will be used to obtain equations for the
surface temperatures as follows:

 

\(\text { For } T_{1} \text { we have } \frac{q}{A}=\frac{T_{\infty_{i}}-T_{1}}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)}=\frac{T_{1}-T_{\infty_{o}}}{\left(\frac{L_{1}}{k_{1}}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{2}}{k_{2}}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{3}}{k_{3}}\right)+\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)}\) (10.43)

 

\(\text { For } T_{4} \text { we have } \frac{q}{A}=\frac{T_{\infty_{i}}-T_{4}}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{1}}{k_{1}}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{2}}{k_{2}}\right)+\left(\frac{L_{3}}{k_{3}}\right)}=\frac{T_{4}-T_{\infty_{o}}}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{4}}\right)}\) (10.44)

 

Putting values into Eq. (10.43), we get

 

\(\frac{q}{A}=\frac{22.22-T_{1}}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)}=\frac{T_{1}-1.67}{2.477+\left(\frac{1}{h_{4}}\right)}\) (10.45)

 

Putting values into Eq. (10.44), we get

 

\(\frac{q}{A}=\frac{22.22-T_{4}}{2.477+\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)}=\frac{T_{4}-1.67}{\left(\frac{1}{h_{4}}\right)}\) (10.46)

 

The convective coefficients \(h_{1} \text { and } h_{4}\) are functions of \(T_{1} \text { and } T_{4}\), respectively. Eqs. (10.45) and (10.46) are solved simultaneously for \(T_{1} \text { and } T_{4}\). In doing so, the values of \(h_{1} \text { and } h_{4}\) are obtained. The rate of heat transfer through the wall may then be determined from any one of several of the above equations.

An appropriate equation for the forced convection coefficient \(h_{4}\) on the outside surface of the wall is Equation (6.92).

 

\(h=\left(0.037 \operatorname{Re}_{L}^{0.8}-871\right) \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}(k / L)\) (6.92)

 

\(h=\left(0.037 \operatorname{Re}_{L}^{0.8}-871\right) \operatorname{Pr}^{1 / 3}(k / L)\) (10.47)

 

The characteristic length L is the length of the wall in the direction of flow. We have L = 15 feet = 4.572 m.

The Reynolds number in Eq. (10.47) is \(\operatorname{Re}_{L}=\frac{u_{\infty} L}{v}\), where y is the kinematic viscosity of air at the film temperature \(\left(T_{4}+T_{\infty_{o}}\right) / 2\). The other properties of air in the equation are also taken at the film temperature. The free-stream velocity is \(u_{\infty}=10 \text { miles per hour }=4.47\) m/s.

An appropriate equation for the natural convection coefficient \(h_{1}\) at the inside surface of the wall is Eq. (7.11).

 

\(Nu =\frac{h L}{k}=\left\{0.825+\frac{0.387 Ra _{L}^{1 / 6}}{\left[1+(0.492 / Pr )^{9 / 16}\right]^{8 / 27}}\right\}^{2}\) (7.11)

 

\(Nu =\frac{h L}{k}=\left\{0.825+\frac{0.387 Ra _{L}^{1 / 6}}{\left[1+(0.492 / Pr )^{9 / 16}\right]^{8 / 27}}\right\}^{2}\) (10.48)

 

The characteristic length is the height of the wall, L = 8 feet = 2.438 m. Eq. (10.48) is for a surface having both laminar and turbulent boundary layers. The results of the analysis will confirm that this is the case for this problem.

As mentioned above, there are two unknowns in this problem: \(T_{1} \text { and } T_{4}\). Once these are obtained, the heat flow through the wall can be calculated. We will use Excel’s Solver program for the solution.

From Eqs. (10.45) and (10.46), we create two functions that must be zero for the solution. These functions are

 

\(f_{1}=h_{1}\left(22.22-T_{1}\right)-\frac{T_{1}-1.67}{2.477+\left(\frac{1}{h_{4}}\right)}=0\) (10.49)

 

\(f_{2}=\frac{22.22-T_{4}}{2.477+\left(\frac{1}{h_{1}}\right)}-h_{4}\left(T_{4}-1.67\right)=0\) (10.50)

 

Solver needs a single criterion for solution. Our criterion is \(f_{1}^{2}+f_{2}^{2}=0\). If this is zero, then both functions will be zero. We give initial guesses for \(T_{1} \text { and } T_{4}\): Solver performs iterations on these values until \(f_{1}^{2}+f_{2}^{2}=0\).
