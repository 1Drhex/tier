Guest Question.

A schematic of a rocket with its ejected fuel is shown in Figure 2.10. (a) Derive the equation of motion (for velocity). (b) Solve for the relation between rocket mass,m, and velocity, ν.

Admin
Solution

(a) The mass of the rocket decreases by the quantity of ejected fuel that is burned and ejected. We wish to obtain the equation of motion of the rocket, which includes a term reflecting the variable mass. Let m (t) be the mass of the rocket and the fuel inside at
time t, and \(m_{i}\) be the initial mass before ignition. Consider the rocket-fuel system at two time instances Δt apart. Assume that initially, at time t, the rocket and all its fuel have mass m with velocity v. (Since the rocket travels in one direction in this example, vectors Figure 2.10: Schematic of rocket in flight. The external force is the thrust imparted by ejected combusted fuel. There are also other external forces such as aerodynamic and thermal loads. The absolute speed of the ejected fuel is u and the absolute speed of the rocket is v .

are shown as scalars.) At a later time t+Δt, a quantity of fuel Δm has been ejected. Denoting this loss of mass as a negative quantity,9 the rocket mass is m + Δm. The rocket is traveling at an increased velocity, v + Δv. The ejected fuel has mass −Δm and is traveling at −u. The velocity of the ejected fuel can also be written as \(u = v_{rel} − v\) to the left. It is reasonable to expect that \(|u| \geqslant|v|\)

Consider next the momentum of the total system. Recalling Newton’s second law of motion in momentum form,

\(F=\frac{d p}{d t}=\lim _{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}\) (2.13)

where p is the momentum and F are external forces such as due to gravity, aerodynamic drag, etc. During time interval Δt, the change in momentum is

\(\Delta p=p_{f}-p_{i}\) (2.14)

that is, the final minus the initial momenta, where, from the previous discussion,

\(\begin{aligned}p_{i} &=m v \\p_{f} &=(m+\Delta m)(v+\Delta v)+(-\Delta m)(-u) \\&=(m+\Delta m)(v+\Delta v)+\left(v_{r e l}-v\right) \Delta m\end{aligned}\)

Equation 2.14 then becomes

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta p &=(m+\Delta m)(v+\Delta v)+\left(v_{r e l}-v\right) \Delta m-m v \\&=m \Delta v+\Delta m \Delta v+v_{r e l} \Delta m\end{aligned}\)

From Equation 2.13,

\(\begin{aligned}F &=\lim _{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \\&=\lim _{\Delta t \rightarrow 0}\left[m \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}+v_{r e l}\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}+\Delta v \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t}\right] \\&=m \frac{d v}{d t}+v_{r e l} \frac{d m}{d t}\end{aligned}\) (2.15)

since Δv → 0 as Δt → 0. The effect of the variable mass is reflected in the second term, which is \(v_{rel} dm/dt\) an extra term in the equation of motion. Equation 2.15 can be written as

\(m \frac{d v}{d t}=F-v_{r e l} \frac{d m}{d t}\) (2.16)

In this form, the second term on the right-hand side represents an equivalent force acting on the system due to the movement of mass. For a rocket, this term is called
the thrust. To maximize the thrust, designers attempt to make each term,\(v_{rel}\) and dm/dt , as large as possible.\(^{10}\) Equation 2.16 can be solved for the relation between mass and velocity.

(b) It is assumed that the only force acting on the rocket is the thrust. Other forces act on the rocket structure, such as aerodynamic forces, and they cannot be ignored
in an actual design, but we do so here in order to show an analytical solution. Making fewer simplifying assumptions would mean that a computational method would be needed to obtain the solution. Equation 2.16 becomes

\(m \frac{d v}{d t}=-v_{r e l} \frac{d m}{d t}\) (2.17)

The negative sign indicates that as the mass of the rocket decreases, the velocity of the rocket increases. Equation 2.17 can be rewritten as

\(d v=-v_{r e l} \frac{d m}{m}\) (2.18)

during which some fuel is remaining. Integrating both sides of Equation 2.18, we find

\(\begin{aligned}\int_{v_{i}}^{v} d v &=-v_{r e l} \int_{m_{i}}^{m} \frac{d m}{m} \\v-v_{i} &=-v_{r e l} \ln \left(\frac{m}{m_{i}}\right)\end{aligned}\)

where \(m_{i}\) is the combined mass of the rocket and the fuel when the velocity is \(v_{i}\), and m is the combined mass when the velocity is v. If the rocket starts from rest, \(v_{i} = 0\) and

\(m(v)=m_{i} e^{-v / v_{r a l}}\)

is the relation between the combined mass of the rocket and the remaining fuel, m, and the velocity, v. This equation can be used to estimate the mass of fuel needed
to attain a certain velocity. Figure 2.11 shows the plot of the remaining mass as a function of the velocity of the rocket. For example, for the propellant mass fraction of

\(u_{\circ} \frac{d m}{d t} \simeq A_{\circ} \Delta P\)

0.8 (that is, the 80% of the total initial mass is fuel), the fuel runs out when the velocity is

\(\begin{aligned}0.2 m_{i} &=m_{i} e^{-v / v_{r s l}} \\v / v_{r e l} &=1.609 \end{aligned}\)

or 1.6 times the relative fuel ejection velocity. The relative fuel ejection velocity is also called the effective exhaust velocity and is a characteristic of a rocket.
