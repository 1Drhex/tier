Guest Question.

Reconsider , i.e., the heat loss from an isothermal slab at T = 1000 K, but here consisting of a 90% \(N_{2}\) – 10% \(CO_{2}\) (by volume) binary gas mixture. Calculate the heat loss from this slab using the FSK and SLW methods, employing both the HITEMP database and the correlation of Denison and Webb.

Admin
Solution

For a homogeneous medium without scattering the FSK’s radiative transfer equation, equation (19.115) \(\frac{dI_{g}}{ds}= k \left(I_{b}(T) – I_{g}\right) -\sigma _{s}\left\lgroup I_{g} -\frac{1}{4\pi }\int_{4\pi }^{}{}I_{g}(\widehat{s}^{\prime } ) \Phi (\widehat{s}, \widehat{s}^{\prime } )d\Omega ^{\prime } \right\rgroup\) , reduces to

\(\frac{dI_{g}}{ds}= k(I_{bm}-I_{g}),\) (19.132)

subject to the cold-wall boundary condition of \(I_{g}\) = 0 at both walls. Since the walls are cold no a-function is needed and only one k-distribution must be obtained, either from the HITEMP database using the Fortran program fskdist from Appendix F, or from the Denison and Webb correlation using subroutine fskdco2. Both distributions are shown in Fig. 19-12. The solution to the spectral equation (19.132) is, as in

\(q_{g}(L)=1-E_{3}(kL)\),

and the total, nondimensional heat flux becomes,

\(\Psi =\frac{q}{\sigma T^{4}}=\int_{0}^{1}{}[1-E_{3}(kL)]dg\simeq \sum\limits_{n=1}^{N}{w_{n}}\left[1-E_{3}\left(k(g_{n})L\right) \right] ,\)

where the integral is evaluated through an N-point numerical quadrature (with quadrature points \(g_{n}\) and weights \(w_{n}\)). Usually \(N \simeq 10\) gives accuracies better than 1% if the quadrature scheme is chosen wisely. Since k-values generally vary over several orders of magnitude, and since the larger k-values tend to have a larger impact on the solution, quadrature points should move closer together for larger values of k and g. The Gauss-Lobatto quadrature scheme [97] has probably been the most popular for this purpose. Another scheme, the m-th order Gaussian quadrature of moments [97] is somewhat more flexible, because, depending on the order m, the spacing of quadrature points can be controlled.
We will here use an 8-point, 3rd order Gaussian quadrature of moments, whose quadrature points have been obtained from a Numerical Recipes [98] routine and are included in Fig. 19-12. Results are shown in Fig. 19-13 and are seen to virtually coincide with line-by-line results: for a homogeneous medium the FSK methods are exact and any discrepancies are due to quadrature errors (for the LBL as well as the FSK quadratures). Using the k-distribution correlation of Denison and Webb gives very respectable results, slightly overpredicting the LBL data, with a maximum error of about 10% (at small L). For large L the correlation would be expected to underpredict the heat loss, since the fit underpredicts small k-values (see Fig. 19-12).

For the SLW approach four g-ranges have been chosen, namely ranges (based on the k-distribution correlation) with k < \(10^{-5} cm^{-1}\) , \(10^{-5} cm^{-1}\) < k < \(10^{-3} cm^{-1}\) , \(10^{-3} cm^{-1}\) < k < \(10^{-1} cm^{-1}\) , and k > \(10^{-1} cm^{-1}\) (same for both k-distributions); The k-values are then calculated from equation (19.126) \(\overline{k}_{i}(T)= \sqrt{k(T,g_{i-1})k(T,g_{i})}\) , resulting in almost identical values for the HITEMP and correlation distributions, except for the first and the last values (with \(k_{HITEMP} > k_{Correlation}\) ); see Fig. 19-12. Consequently, both SLW simulations yield almost identical results. The SLW results oscillate slightly around the LBL data, indicating that the accuracy of the SLW method depends mostly on a wise choice of g-ranges (with different optima for different slab widths L).
