Guest Question.

The Microbot robot of Fig. 10.22 described in is to be guided such that: (1) tool point O6 follows the straight line path given by

\(R _{O_{6}}(t)=(4.0+1.6 t) \hat{ i }_{1}+(3.0+1.2 t) \hat{ j }_{1}+2.0 \hat{ k }_{1} \text { in }\)

with t varying from 0.0 to 5.0 s; and (2) the orientation of the end effector is to remain constant with \(\hat{ i }_{6}=\hat{ k }_{1} \text { (vertical) and } \hat{ k }_{6}\) radially outward from the base of the robot. Find expressions for how each of the joint actuators must be driven, as functions of time, to achieve this motion.

Admin
Solution

From the problem requirements stated, we can express the required time history of the tool coordinate system by the transformation matrix

 

\(T_{1,6}(t)=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}0 & 0.600 & 0.800 & 4.0+1.6 t in \\0 & -0.800 & 0.600 & 3.0+1.2 t in \\1 & 0 & 0 & 2.0 in \\0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\) (1)

 

We can also multiply out the matrix description for T16 from Eq. (10.36); this gives

\(T_{1, n+1}=T_{1,2} T_{2,3} \ldots T_{n, n+1}\) (10.36)

 

\(T_{1,6}=\left[\begin{array}{cccc}\cos \phi_{1} \cos \beta \cos \phi_{5} – \sin \phi_{1} \sin \phi_{5} & -\cos \phi_{1} \cos \beta \sin \phi_{5} + \sin \phi_{1} \cos \phi_{5} & \cos \phi_{1} \sin \beta & 7 \cos \phi_{1} \cos \phi_{2} +7 \cos \phi_{1} \cos \left(\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}\right) + 3.8 \cos \phi_{1} \sin \beta \\\sin \phi_{1} \cos \beta \cos \phi_{5} – \cos \phi_{1} \sin \phi_{5} & -\sin \phi_{1} \cos \beta \sin \phi_{5} – \cos \phi_{1} \cos \phi_{5} & \sin \phi_{1} \sin \beta & 7 \sin \phi_{1} \cos \phi_{2} +7 \sin \phi_{1} \sin \left(\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}\right) + 3.8 \sin \phi_{1} \sin \beta \\\sin \beta \cos \phi_{5} & -\sin \beta \sin \phi_{5} & -\cos \beta & 7 \sin \phi_{2} +7 \sin \left(\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}\right) – 3.8 \cos \beta \\0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right],\) (2)

 

where, to save space, we have adopted the definition

 

\(\beta=\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}+\phi_{4}\) (3)

 

Now, to achieve the problem requirements, we must find expressions for the actuator variables Φi that ensure that the elements of Eq. (2) are equal to those of Eq. (1) for all values of time; this is our required constraint condition. Equating the ratios of the elements of the second row, third column with the elements of the first row, third column of Eqs. (2) and (1) gives

 

\(\tan \phi_{1}=\frac{0.600}{0.800}=0.750\)

 

Therefore, the first joint variable is

 

\(\phi_{1}=\tan ^{-1} 0.750=36.87^{\circ}\)

 

Similarly, from the ratios of the elements of the third row, second column with the elements of the third row, first column we find

 

\(\begin{aligned}\tan \phi_{5} &=0 \\\phi_{5} &=0^{\circ} \text { or } \pm 180^{\circ}\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the problem gives no preference for the orientation of the end effector in the horizontal plane, we are free to choose

 

\(\phi_{5}=0^{\circ}\)

 

Next, from the third row, third column elements, we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}\cos \beta &=0 \\\beta &=\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}+\phi_{4}=\pm 90^{\circ}\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the orientation of the end effector in the horizontal plane is not specified, we arbitrarily choose

 

\(\beta=\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}+\phi_{4}=90^{\circ}\) (4)

 

Turning now to the elements of the fourth column, and simplifying according to already known results, we obtain two more independent equations:

 

\(7 \cos \phi_{2}+7 \cos\left(\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}\right)=1.2+2 t\) (5)

 

\(7 \sin \phi_{2}+7 \sin \left(\phi_{2}+\phi_{3}\right)=-5.68\) (6)

 

Squaring and adding these two equations gives

 

\(98+98 \cos \phi_{3}=33.7024+4.8 t+4 t^{2}\) (7)

 

Therefore, a solution for Φ3 is

\(\phi_{3}=\cos ^{-1}\left(-0.65610+0.04898 t+0.040816 t^{2}\right)\) (8)

 

Since this form admits to multiple values, we take the smallest solution with Φ3 ≤ 0 so that the elbow is kept above the path. At time t = 0, for example, the value of Φ3 is \(\phi_{3}=\cos ^{-1}(-0.65610)=-131.00^{\circ}\).

Knowing the solution for Φ3, we now rewrite Eqs. (5) and (6) as follows:

\(\sin \phi_{2}=\frac{-5.68}{14}-\frac{(1.2+2 t) \sin \phi_{3}}{14\left(1+\cos \phi_{3}\right)}\) (9)

 

\(\cos \phi_{2}=\frac{(1.2+2 t)}{14}-\frac{5.68 \sin \phi_{3}}{14\left(1+\cos \phi_{3}\right)}\) (10)

 

Since the third actuator variable Φ3 now has a known solution, either of these equations can be used to find Φ2. Actually, both should be evaluated to ensure that the proper quadrant is found. These two equations can be numerically evaluated at any value of time, and they are considered solutions even though they are not explicit, closed-form functions of time. Similarly, from Eq. (4), we now find the fourth joint variable:

 

\(\phi_{4}=90^{\circ}-\phi_{2}-\phi_{3}\) (11)

Graphs of the five actuator variables versus time are shown in Fig. 10.23. Note that for the path being followed, the actuator variables follow very smooth but nonlinear curves. Thus, it appears that the actuator velocities and accelerations will not be zero but should fall within very reasonable limits.
