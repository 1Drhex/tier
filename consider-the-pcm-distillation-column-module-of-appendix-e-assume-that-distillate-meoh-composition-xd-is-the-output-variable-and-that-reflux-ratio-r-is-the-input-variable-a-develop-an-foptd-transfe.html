Guest Question.

Consider the PCM distillation column module of Appendix E. Assume that distillate \(MeOH\) composition \(x_{D}\) is the output variable and that reflux ratio \(R\) is the input variable.

(a) Develop an FOPTD transfer function model from response data for a step change in \(R\) at \(t=10\) min from \(1.75\) to 2.0. Summarize your calculated model parameters in a table and briefly describe the method used to calculate them.

(b) Repeat (a) for an SOPTD model.

(c) Plot the actual \(x_{D}\) response and the two model responses for the \(R\) step change of part (a).

(d) Are the two models reasonably accurate? Which model is better? Justify your answer by considering the least squares index for the prediction errors,

\(S=\sum\limits_{k=1}^{N}[y(k)-\hat{y}(k)]^{2}\)

where \(N\) is the number of data points.

Admin
Solution

(a) Fit a FOPTD model to the column step response data:

Let \(y=\) distillate \(MeOH\) composition, \(x_{ D }\)

\(u=\text { reflux ratio, } R\)

The step response data is shown in Fig. S7.18a with the step change in \(u\) from \(1.75\) to \(2.0\) occurring at \(t=3950 s\). By inspection of the noisy data, the time delay is \(\theta \approx 50 s\).

The following information can be obtained from the step response data:

\(x_{D}(0)=0.85, \quad x_{D}(\infty)=0.88\)

thus

\(\Delta x_{D}=0.88-0.85=0.03\)

and

\(K=\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u}=\frac{0.03}{0.25}=0.12\)

Also,

\(y_{63.2}=0.85+(0.632)(0.03)=0.869\)

From the figure, \(\tau=t_{63.2}-t(0)=5050-3950-50 \approx 1050 s\). Thus, one estimate of the time constant is \(\tau=1050 s\). A second estimate can be obtained from the settling time, \(t_{ s } \approx 7600-3950=3650 s\). Thus, \(\tau \approx t_{ s / 4}=912 s\). Averaging these two estimates gives:

\(\tau_{\text {ave }}=\frac{1050+912}{2}=981 s\)

Thus the identified transfer function is,

\(\frac{x_{D}(s)}{R(s)}=\frac{0.12 e^{-50 s}}{981 s+1}\)

(b) SOPTD model:

Use Smith’s method:

\(\begin{aligned}&y_{20}=0.85+(0.2)(0.03)=0.856 \\&y_{60}=0.85+(0.6)(0.03)=0.868\end{aligned}\)

From the step response data:

\(\begin{aligned}&t_{20} \approx 4280-3950-50=280 s \\&t_{60} \approx 4900-3950-50=900 s\end{aligned}\)

Thus,

\(\frac{t_{20}}{t_{60}}=\frac{280}{900}=0.31\)

From Fig. 7.7:

\(\frac{t_{60}}{\tau}=4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tau=\frac{t_{60}}{4}=225 s\)

and

\(\zeta=2.0\)

The SOPTD model can be written as:

\(\frac{x_{D}(s)}{R(s)}=\frac{K e^{-\theta s}}{\tau^{2} s^{2}+2 \zeta t s+1}=\frac{0.12 e^{-50 s}}{4 \times 10^{4} s^{2}+800 s+1}\)

which can be factored using \((5-45)\) and \((5-46)\) :

\(\tau_{2}=\frac{\tau}{\zeta+\sqrt{\zeta^{2}-1}} \quad(\zeta \geq 1)\) (5-45)

\(Y(s)=\frac{K M}{s\left(\tau^{2} s^{2}+2 \zeta \tau s+1\right)}\) (5-46)

\(\frac{x_{D}(s)}{R(s)}==\frac{0.12 e^{-50 s}}{(769 s+1)(54 s+1)}\)

d) Discussion

The model comparisons in Fig. S7.18b indicate that both models are reasonably accurate. However, the second-order model is more accurate as indicated visually and by its slightly lower S value:

\(\text{First order model:}\quad S=9.014 \times 10^{-4}\)

\(\text{Second-order model:} \quad S=9.012 \times 10^{-4}\)
