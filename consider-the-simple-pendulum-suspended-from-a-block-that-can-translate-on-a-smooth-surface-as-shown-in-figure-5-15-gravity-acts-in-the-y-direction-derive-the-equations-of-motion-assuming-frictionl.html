Guest Question.

Consider the simple pendulum suspended from a block that can translate on a smooth surface, as shown in Figure 5.15. Gravity acts in the \(y\) direction. Derive the equations of motion, assuming frictionless contact between the block and the surface, and a frictionless pivot for the pendulum.

Admin
Solution

In this example we demonstrate the procedure based on Equations 5.21 and 5.22. The kinetic energy of each mass is derived and appropriate derivatives are set equal to the respective generalized force, resulting
in one equation

\(\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left[\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}_{k}}\right)-\frac{\partial T}{\partial q_{k}}\right] \delta q_{k}=\delta W\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\delta W &=\sum_{i=1}^{N} F _{i} \cdot \delta r _{i} \\&=\sum_{i=1}^{N} F _{i} \cdot \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{\partial r _{i}}{\partial q_{k}} \delta q_{k} \\&=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} F _{i} \cdot \frac{\partial r _{i}}{\partial q_{k}}\right) \delta q_{k} \\&=\sum_{k=1}^{n} Q_{k} \delta q_{k},\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\left[\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}_{k}}\right)-\frac{\partial T}{\partial q_{k}}\right]=Q_{k}\)

for each generalized coordinate \(k\). To find the generalized forces, we will evaluate the virtual work done in each generalized coordinate.
The kinetic energy of the two-mass system is given by

\(T=\frac{1}{2} m_{1} \dot{x}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m_{2}\left(\dot{x}_{2}^{2}+\dot{y}_{2}^{2}\right)\)

The kinematic relations are

\(\begin{aligned}&x_{2}=x_{1}+r \sin \theta \\&y_{2}=r \cos \theta\end{aligned}\)

which are constraint equations. In deriving the equations of motion, we will select \(x_{1}\) and θ as the two generalized coordinates

Differentiating Equations 5.24 and 5.25, substituting and eliminating \(\dot{x}_{2}\) and \(\dot{y}_{2}\) from the expression for kinetic energy, we find

\(T=\frac{1}{2} m_{1} \dot{x}_{1}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} m_{2}\left(\dot{x}_{1}^{2}+2 r \dot{x}_{1} \dot{\theta} \cos \theta+r^{2} \dot{\theta}^{2}\right)\)

The following derivatives are needed in Equation 5.23. For the equation of motion in generalized coordinate \(x_{1}\),

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}_{1}} &=m_{1} \dot{x}_{1}+m_{2} \dot{x}_{1}+m_{2} r \dot{\theta} \cos \theta \\\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{x}_{1}}\right) &=m_{1} \ddot{x}_{1}+m_{2} \ddot{x}_{1}+m_{2} r\left(\ddot{\theta} \cos \theta-\dot{\theta}^{2} \sin \theta\right) \\\frac{\partial T}{\partial x_{1}} &=0\end{aligned}\)

Substituting these into Equation 5.23 and equating to the generalized force in the \(x_{1}\) direction,

\(\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{x}_{1}+m_{2} r\left(\ddot{\theta} \cos \theta-\dot{\theta}^{2} \sin \theta\right)=F_{x_{1}}\)

This is the equation of motion in the \(x_{1}\) direction. The expression for \(F_{x_{1}}\) will be evaluated after the equation of motion in the θ direction is derived, for which the following derivatives are needed,

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}} &=m_{2} r \dot{x}_{1} \cos \theta+m_{2} r^{2} \dot{\theta} \\\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}}\right) &=m_{2} r\left(\ddot{x}_{1} \cos \theta-\dot{x}_{1} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta\right)+m_{2} r^{2} \ddot{\theta} \\\frac{\partial T}{\partial \theta} &=-m_{2} r \dot{x}_{1} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta\end{aligned}\)

Substituting and equating to the generalized force in the θ direction gives the second equation of motion,

\(m_{2} r \ddot{x}_{1} \cos \theta+m_{2} r^{2} \ddot{\theta}=F_{\theta}\)

Next, we evaluate the virtual work performed by the generalized forces in the \(x_{1}\) and θ directions. The general virtual work expression is

\(\delta W=F_{x_{1}} \delta x_{1}+F_{\theta} \delta \theta\)

where \(\delta x _{1}\) and\(\delta \theta\) are independent. Since there is no friction between the block and the surface upon which it rests, and there are no other forces in the \(x_{1}\) direction, \(F_{x_{1}}\) = 0.

To evaluate \(F_{\theta}\) it is helpful to sketch the virtual displacement \(\delta \theta\), as shown in an exaggerated view in Figure 5.16.

The virtual work is performed in raising the pendulum mass a vertical distance \(\delta h\), which is related to \(\delta \theta\) by \(\delta h=r \delta \theta \sin \theta\). The virtual work done by gravity is \(F_{\theta} \delta \theta\),
or

\(-m_{2} g r \sin \theta \delta \theta\)

where the negative sign signifies that the work is performed in raising the mass against the gravitational force. Thus, the generalized force in the θ direction, \(F_{\theta}\) , is the moment,

\(F_{\theta}=-m_{2} g r \sin \theta\)

The equation of motion in the θ direction is

\(m_{2} r \ddot{x}_{1} \cos \theta+m_{2} r^{2} \ddot{\theta}=-m_{2} g r \sin \theta .\)

The coupled equations of motion are then

\(\begin{array}{r}\left(m_{1}+m_{2}\right) \ddot{x}_{1}+m_{2} r\left(\ddot{\theta} \cos \theta-\dot{\theta}^{2} \sin \theta\right)=0 \\m_{2} r \ddot{x}_{1} \cos \theta+m_{2} r^{2} \ddot{\theta}+m_{2} g r \sin \theta=0 .\end{array}\)

In Chapter 6 these simultaneous equations will be linearized and solved. Had other external forces existed, theywould have been included in \(F_{x_{1}}\) or \(F_{\theta}\) and appeared on the right-hand sides of the equations of motion.
Dissipation forces such as damping or friction, if present, are part of the virtual work expression and thus part of the generalized forces in the equations of motion. This is discussed next.
