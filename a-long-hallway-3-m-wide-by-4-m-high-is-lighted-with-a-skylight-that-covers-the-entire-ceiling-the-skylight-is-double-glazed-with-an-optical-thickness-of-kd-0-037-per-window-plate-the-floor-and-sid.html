Guest Question.

A long hallway 3 m wide by 4 m high is lighted with a skylight that covers the entire ceiling. The skylight is double-glazed with an optical thickness of \(\kappa d\) = 0.037 per window plate. The floor and sides of the hallway may be assumed to be gray and diffuse with ε= 0.2. The outside of the skylight is exposed to a clear sky, so that diffuse visible light in the amount of \(q_{sky}\)= 20,000 lm/\(m^{2}\) is incident on the skylight. Direct sunshine also falls on the skylight in the amount of \(q_{sun}\)= 80,000 lm/\(m^{2}\) (normal to the rays). For simplicity assume that the sun angle is \(\)θ_{s}\(\) = 36.87° as indicated in Fig. 6-15. Determine the amount of light incident on a point in the lower right-hand comer (also indicated in the figure) if (a) the skylight is clear, (b) the skylight is diffusing (with the same transmittance and reflectance).

Admin
Solution

From Fig. 3-32 for double glazing and\(\kappa d = 0.037\) we find a hemispherical transmittance (i.e., directionally averaged) of \(\tau \simeq 0.70\) , while for solar incidence with θ = 36.87° we have \(\tau_{\theta } \simeq 0.75\). The hemispherical reflectance of the skylight may be estimated by assuming that the reflectance is the same as the one of a nonabsorbing glass. Then, from Fig. 3-31 \(\rho _{1} =\rho ^{s}_{1} =1-\tau (\kappa d=0)\simeq 1-0.75=0.25.\)
From equation (6.38) \(\epsilon _{a} (r)= \epsilon +\tau =1-\rho ,<br /> \epsilon _{a} E_{b,a} (r)= \epsilon E_{b} + \tau ^{d} q_{oc} + \tau q_{od}.\) we find \(\epsilon _{1,a} = 1 – \rho _{1} = 0.75\) and, for a clear skylight, \(\epsilon _{1,a} E_{b1,a} =0+0+\tau q_{sky}\) since \(\tau^{d}=0\) , and since there is no luminous emission from the window (or from any of the other walls, for that matter). Because of the special sun angle, direct sunshine falls only onto surface \(A_{2}\), filling the entire wall,\(H^{s}_{o2} = \tau _{\theta } q_{sun}\sin \theta _{s}\)
To determine the illumination at the point in the comer, we need to calculate the local irradiation H (in terms of lumens). This calculation, in turn, requires knowledge of the radiosity for all the surfaces of the hallway (for the sky fight it is already known as \(J_{1} =\epsilon _{1,a} E_{b1,a} = \tau q_{sky}\) , since \(\rho ^{d}_{1} = 0)\). To this purpose we shall approximate the hallway as a four-surface enclosure for which we shall calculate the average radiosities. Based on these radiosities we may then calculate the local irradiafion for a point from equation (6.15) \(H(r)=\int_{A}^{}{J(r^{\prime} ) d F^{s}_{dA-dA^{\prime} } } + H^{s}_{o}(r)\) . While equation (6.22) \(\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{\left[\delta _{ij} – (1- \rho ^{s}_{j}) F^{s}_{i-j} \right] } E_{bj}= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{\left\lgroup\frac{\delta _{ij}}{\epsilon _{j} } -\frac{ \rho ^{d}_{j}}{\epsilon _{j}} F^{s}_{i-j} \right\rgroup } q_{j} + H^{s}_{oi} , i = 1,2,…N\) is most suitable for heat transfer calculafions, we shall use equation (6.19)[ \(j_{i} = \epsilon _{i} E_{bi} + \rho ^{d}_{i} \left\lgroup\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{j_{i} F^{s}_{i-j} + H^{s}_{oi} } \right\rgroup ,i = 1,2,…,N\) for this example since radiosities are more useful in lighting calculations. Therefore, for i = 2,3, and4,

\(J_{2}=\rho _{2} (J_{1}F^{s}_{2-1} + J_{2}F^{s}_{2-2}+ J_{3}F^{s}_{2-3} + J_{4}F^{s}_{2-4} ) + H^{s}_{o2},\)
\(J_{3}=\rho _{3} (J_{1}F^{s}_{3-1} + J_{2}F^{s}_{3-2}+ J_{3}F^{s}_{3-3} + J_{4}F^{s}_{3-4} ),\)
\(J_{4}=\rho _{4} (J_{1}F^{s}_{4-1} + J_{2}F^{s}_{4-2}+ J_{3}F^{s}_{4-3} + J_{4}F^{s}_{4-4} ).\)

The necessary view factors are readily calculated from the crossed-strings method:

\( F^{s}_{2-1} = F_{2-1} =\frac{3+4-5}{2\times 4} = 0.25 , F^{s}_{2-2}=0\)
\(F^{s}_{2-3}=F_{2-3} + \rho _{1} F_{2(1)-3} = 0.25 + 0.25 \times \frac{8+5 – (4+\sqrt{73} )}{2\times 4} = 0.25 (1+0.05700)= 0.26425,\)
\(F^{s}_{2-4}= F_{2-4} + \rho _{1} F_{2(1)-4} 0.5+0.25\times \frac{3+\sqrt{73} -2 \times 5 }{2\times 4} = 0.5 + 0.25 \times 0.19300 = 0.54825,\)
\(F^{s}_{3-1} = F_{3-1} = \frac{2\times 5 -2\times 4}{2\times 3} = 0.33333,\)
\(F^{s}_{3-2}= \frac{A_{2} }{A_{3}} F^{s}_{2-3}=\frac{4}{3}\times 0.26425 = 0.35233,\)
\(F^{s}_{3-3}= \rho _{1} F_{3(1)-3} = 0.25\times \frac{2\times \sqrt{73}-2\times 8 }{2\times 3} = 0.25\times 0.18133 = 0.04533 ,\)
\(F^{s}_{3-4} = F^{s}_{3-2} = 0.35233,\)
\(F^{s}_{4-1}= F^{s}_{2-1}=0.2500, F^{s}_{4-2}F^{s}_{2-4} = 0.54825,\)
\(F^{s}_{4-3} F^{s}_{2-3}= 0.26425, F^{s}_{4-4} =0.\)

Therefore, after normalization with \(J_{i}\) = \(J_{i}/J_{1}\) and H = \( H^{s}_{o2} / J_{1}\) and with \(\rho _{2} = \rho _{3} = \rho _{4} = 1 – 0.2 = 0.8\)

\(J_{2}= 0.8 (0.25 + 0+ 0.26425J_{3} + 0.54825 J_{4}) + H,\)
\(J_{3} = 0.8(0.33333+0.35233J_{2} + 0.04533J_{3}+ 0.35233J_{4}),\)
\(J_{4} = 0.8 (0.25 + 0.54825J_{2} 0.26425J_{3}+0),\)

or

\(J_{2}-0.21140J_{3} – 0.43860J_{4} = H +0.2,\)
\(-0.28186J_{2} + 0.96374J_{3} – 0.28186J_{4} = 0.26667,\)
\(-0.43860J_{2} – 0.21140J_{3} +J_{4} = 0.2 .\)

Omitting the details of solving these three simultaneous equations, we find

\(J_{2}= 1.48978H + 0.59051,\)
\(J_{3}= 0.66812H + 0.62211,\)
\(J_{4}= 0.79466H + 0.59051.\)

The irradiation onto the comer point is, from equation (6.15) \(H(r)=\int_{A}^{}{J(r^{\prime} ) d F^{s}_{dA-dA^{\prime} } } + H^{s}_{o}(r)\)

\(H_{p} = \sum\limits_{j=1}^{4}{j_{i} F^{s}_{p-j}} = j_{1} \left(F^{s}_{p-1}+ J_{2}F^{s}_{p-2}+ J_{3}F^{s}_{p-3} + J_{4}F^{s}_{p-4}\right),\)

where the view factors may be determined from Configurations 10 and 11 in Appendix D (with \(b\rightarrow \infty\) , and multiplying by 2 since the strip tends to infinity in both directions):

\(F^{s}_{p-1}= F_{p-1} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{a} {\sqrt{a^{2} +c^{2}} }=\frac{1}{2}\times \frac{3}{5} = 0.3,\)
\(F^{s}_{p-2}=F_{p-2} + \rho _{1} F_{p(1)-2} = F_{p-2} + \rho _{1} \left[F_{p(1)-2+2(1)} – F_{p(1)-2(1)}\right] ,\)
\(F_{p-2}= \frac{1}{2}\left(1-\frac{c} {\sqrt{a^{2} +c^{2}} }\right) = \frac{1}{2} \left(1-\frac{3}{5} \right) = 0.2,\)
\(F_{p(1)-2(1)}= F_{p-2} = 0.2 , F_{p(1)-2+2(1)}= \frac{1}{2} \left(1- \frac{3}{\sqrt{73} } \right) = 0.32444,\)
\(F^{s}_{p-2}= 0.2 +0.25 \times (0.32444-0.2)=0.23111,\)
\(F^{s}_{p-3}= \rho _{1} F_{p(1)-3}= 0.25\times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{3}{\sqrt{73}} = 0.04389 , F^{s}_{p-4}= 0.5.\)

Therefore,

\(H_{p} = \frac{H_{p}}{J_{1}} = 0.3+0.23111\times (1.48978H + 0.59051)\)
\(+ 0.04389 \times (0.66812H+0.62211)+0.5\times (0.79466H+0.59051)\)
\(=0.77096H + 0.75903.\)

Finally, for a clear window,\(J_{1} =\tau _{1}q_{sky} = 0.7\times 20,000=14,000lx and H^{s}_{o2}= \tau _{\theta } q_{sun}\sin 36.87° = 0.75\times 80.000\times 36,000 lx\), and

\(H_{p} = 0.77096 \times 36,000+0.75903\times 14,000 = 38,381 lx\)

On the other hand, if the window has a diffusing transmittance \(\tau =\tau ^{d} =0.7\) , then \(H^{s}_{o2} = 0\) and, from equation (6.37) \(J(r)= \epsilon E_{b} + \tau ^{d}q_{ oc} + \tau q_{ od} + \rho ^{d} H\) , \(J_{1} =\tau(q_{sky}+q_{sun} \cos 36.87° )=0.7\times (20,000+80,000\times 0.8)=58,800 lx\). This results in

\(H_{p}= 0.75903\times 58,800 = 44,631 lx\)

For a diffusing window the light is more evenly distributed throughout the hallway, resulting in higher illumination at point p.
