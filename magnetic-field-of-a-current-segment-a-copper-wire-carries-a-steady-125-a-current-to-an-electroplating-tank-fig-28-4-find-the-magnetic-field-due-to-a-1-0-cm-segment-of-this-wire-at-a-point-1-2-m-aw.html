Guest Question.

MAGNETIC FIELD OF A CURRENT SEGMENT

A copper wire carries a steady 125-A current to an electroplating tank (Fig. 28.4). Find the magnetic field due to a 1.0-cm segment of this wire at a point 1.2 m away from it, if the point is (a) point \(P_1\), straight out to the side of the segment, and (b) point \(P_2\), in the xy-plane and on a line at 30° to the segment.

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

Although Eqs. (28.5) and (28.6) apply only to infinitesimal current elements, we may use either of them here because the segment length is much less than the distance to the field point. The current element is shown in red in Fig. 28.4 and points in the -x-direction (the direction of the current), so \(d \vec{l}=\) \(d l(-\hat{\imath})\). The unit vector \(\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}\) for each field point is directed from the current element toward that point: \(\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}\) is in the +y-direction for point \(P_1\) and at an angle of 30° above the -x-direction for point \(P_2\).

\(d B=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{I d l \sin \phi}{r^{2}}\) (28.5)

\(d \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{I d \overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{l}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{r^{2}}\) (28.6)

EXECUTE:

(a) At point \(P_{1}, \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}=\hat{\boldsymbol{j}}\), so

\(\begin{aligned}\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}} &=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{I d\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{l}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{r^{2}}=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi}\frac{I d l(-\hat{\boldsymbol{\imath}}) \times \hat{\jmath}}{r^{2}}=-\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi}\frac{I d l}{r^{2}} \hat{\boldsymbol{k}} \\&=-\left(10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} /\mathrm{A}\right) \frac{(125 \mathrm{~A})\left(1.0 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~m}\right)}{(1.2\mathrm{~m})^{2}} \hat{\boldsymbol{k}} \\&=-\left(8.7 \times 10^{-8} \mathrm{~T}\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{k}}\end{aligned}\)

The direction of \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}\) at \(P_1\) is into the xy-plane of Fig. 28.4.
(b) At \(P_2\), the unit vector is \(\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}=\left(-\cos 30^{\circ}\right) \hat{\imath}+\left(\sin 30^{\circ}\right) \hat{\jmath}\).
From Eq. (28.6),

\(\begin{aligned}\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}} &=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \frac{I d\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{l}} \times \hat{\boldsymbol{r}}}{r^{2}}=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi}\frac{I d l(-\hat{\imath}) \times\left(-\cos 30^{\circ} \hat{\imath}+\sin 30^{\circ}\hat{\jmath}\right)}{r^{2}} \\&=-\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi} \frac{d l \sin 30^{\circ}}{r^{2}}\hat{\boldsymbol{k}} \\&=-\left(10^{-7} \mathrm{~T} \cdot \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{A}\right)\frac{(125 \mathrm{~A})\left(1.0 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~m}\right)\left(\sin 30^{\circ}\right)}{(1.2 \mathrm{~m})^{2}} \hat{\boldsymbol{k}} \\&=-\left(4.3 \times 10^{-8}\mathrm{~T}\right) \hat{\boldsymbol{k}}\end{aligned}\)

The direction of \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}\) at \(P_2\) is also into the xy-plane of Fig. 28.4.

 

EVALUATE: We can check our results for the direction of \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{B}}\) by comparing them with Fig. 28.3. The xy-plane in Fig. 28.4 corresponds to the beige plane in Fig. 28.3, but here the direction of the current and hence of \(d \vec{l}\) is the reverse of that shown in Fig. 28.3.
Hence the direction of the magnetic field is reversed as well. Hence the field at points in the xy-plane in Fig. 28.4 must point into, not out of, that plane. This is just what we concluded above.
