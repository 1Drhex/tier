Guest Question.

The offset slider-crank linkage in the posture shown in Fig. 3.8a is driven by slider 4 at a constant speed of VC = 10 m/s to the left. Determine the angular velocities of links 2 and 3 and the velocity of point D.

Admin
Solution

The loop-closure equation for the offset slider-crank linkage, in complex polar form, is

\(j r_{1}+r_{2} e^{j \theta_{2}}+r_{3} e^{j \theta_{3}}-r_{4}=0 .\) (1)

The two scalar equations are

r2 cos θ2 +r3 cos θ3 −r4 = 0,

r1 +r2 sin θ2 +r3 sin θ3 = 0. (2)

Since the input is the linear displacement of link 4, then differentiating Eqs. (2) with respect to r4 gives

\(-r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} \theta_{2}^{\prime}-r_{3} \sin \theta_{3} \theta_{3}^{\prime}=1\),

 

\(r_{2} \cos \theta_{2} \theta_{2}^{\prime}+r_{3} \cos \theta_{3} \theta_{3}^{\prime}=0\) (3)

where

 

\(\theta_{2}^{\prime}=\frac{d \theta_{2}}{d r_{4}} \quad \text { and } \quad \theta_{3}^{\prime}=\frac{d \theta_{3}}{d r_{4}}\) (4)

 

are the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 2 and 3. Symbolic equations for the first-order kinematic coefficients are obtained here using Cramer’s rule. The first step is to write Eqs. (3) in matrix form; that is,

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}-r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} & -r_{3} \sin \theta_{3} \\r_{2} \cos \theta_{2} & r_{3} \cos \theta_{3}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{l}\theta_{2}^{\prime}\\\theta_{3}^{\prime}\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{l}1 \\0\end{array}\right]\) (5)

 

The determinant of the (2×2) coefficient matrix in Eq. (5) is written as

Δ = r2 r3 sin (θ3 −θ2). (6)

Note that the determinant is zero when (a) θ2 = θ3 or when \(\text { (b) }\theta_{2}=\theta_{3} \pm 180^{\circ}\) —that is, when links 2 and 3 are fully extended or aligned on top of each other.

From Eq. (5), the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 2 and 3 can be written as

\(\theta_{2}^{\prime}=\frac{\cos \theta_{3}}{r_{2} \sin \left(\theta_{3}-\theta_{2}\right)} \quad \text { and } \quad \theta_{3}^{\prime}=\frac{-\cos \theta_{2}}{r_{3} \sin \left(\theta_{3}-\theta_{2}\right)} .\) (7)

 

For the given link dimensions and the given input position r4 = 164 mm, the angular positions of links 2 and 3 are θ2 = 45° and θ3 = 337°, respectively. Substituting these values into Eqs. (7), the first-order kinematic coefficients of links 2 and 3 are

\(\theta_{2}^{\prime}=-19.856 rad / m \text { and } \theta_{3}^{\prime}=+5.447 rad / m\) (8)

where the negative sign shows that link 2 rotates clockwise while the positive sign shows that link 3 rotates counterclockwise for positive input motion of link 4 (to the right).

The angular velocities of links 2 and 3 can be written from the chain rule as

\(\omega_{2}=\theta_{2}^{\prime} \dot{r}_{4} \quad \text { and } \quad \omega_{3}=\theta_{3}^{\prime} \dot{r}_{4},\) (9)

where the velocity of the slider is \(\dot{r}_{4}=V_{C}=-10 m / s \text {. }\) Substituting Eqs. (8) and the velocity of the slider into Eqs. (9), the angular velocities of the two links are

ω2 = 198.56 rad/s ccw and ω3 = −54.47 rad/s (cw)

These answers agree closely with the results obtained from the velocity polygon method in (ω2 = 200 rad/s ccw and ω3 = 53.6 rad/s cw) and are more accurate. The position of point D with respect to ground pivot A (Fig. 3.8a) can be written as

\(\overset{??}{r_{D}} = \overset{\sqrt{}I}{r_{2}} + \overset{\sqrt{}\sqrt{}}{r_{DB}}.\) (10)

The x and y components of this vector equation are

xD = r2 cos θ2 +rDB cos(θ3 −β), (11a)

yD = r2 sin θ2 +rDB sin(θ3 −β), (11b)

where β = (RDE/REB) = tan-1 (50 mm/80 mm) = 32.01º.

Differentiating Eqs. (11) with respect to input variable r4 , the first-order kinematic coefficients for point D are

\(x_{D}^{\prime}=-r_{2} \sin \theta_{2} \theta_{2}^{\prime}-r_{D B} \sin \left(\theta_{3}-\beta\right) \theta_{3}^{\prime}\) (12a)

 

\(y_{D}^{\prime}=r_{2} \cos \theta_{2} \theta_{2}^{\prime}+r_{D B} \cos \left(\theta_{3}-\beta\right) \theta_{3}^{\prime}\) (12b)

 

Substituting the known values into these equations, the first-order kinematic coefficients are

\(x_{D}^{\prime}=1.123 m / m \quad \text { and } \quad y_{D}^{\prime}=-0.407 m / m \text {. }\) (13)

The velocity of point D can be written as

\(V _{D}=\left(x_{D}^{\prime} \hat{ i }+y_{D}^{\prime} \hat{ j }\right) \dot{r}_{4}\) (14)

Substituting Eqs. (13) and the input velocity \(\dot{r}_{4}=-10 m / s\) into this equation, the velocity of point D is

\(V _{D}=-11.23 \hat{ i }+4.07 \hat{ j } m / s =11.94 m / s \angle 160.08^{\circ}\) (15)

Therefore, the instantaneous speed of point D agrees closely with the result obtained from the velocity polygon method in (VD = 12 m/s).
