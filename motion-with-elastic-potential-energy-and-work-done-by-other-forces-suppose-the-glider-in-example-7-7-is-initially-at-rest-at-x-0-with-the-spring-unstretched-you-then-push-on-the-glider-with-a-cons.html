Guest Question.

MOTION WITH ELASTIC POTENTIAL ENERGY AND WORK DONE BY OTHER FORCES Suppose the glider in is initially at rest at x = 0, with the spring unstretched. You then push on the glider with a constant force \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\) (magnitude 0.610 N) in the +x-direction. What is the glider’s velocity when it has moved to x = 0.100 m?

Admin
Solution

IDENTIFY and SET UP:

Although the force \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\) you apply is constant, the spring force isn’t, so the acceleration of the glider won’t be constant. Total mechanical energy is not conserved because of the work done by force \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\), so \(W_{other}\) in Eq. (7.14) (\(K_{1}+U_{1}+W_{\text {other }}=K_{2}+U_{2}\)) is not zero.
As in , we ignore gravitational potential energy because the glider’s height doesn’t change. Hence we again have \(U=U_{\mathrm{el}}=\frac{1}{2} k x^{2}\). This time, we let point 1 be at \(x_1\) = 0, where the velocity is \(v_{1x}\) = 0, and let point 2 be at x = 0.100 m. The glider’s displacement is then \(\Delta x=x_{2}-x_{1}=0\) m. Our target variable is \(v_{2x}\), the velocity at point 2.

EXECUTE:

Force \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\) is constant and in the same direction as the displacement, so the work done by this force is F∆x. Then the energy quantities are

\(K_1\), \(U_{1}=\frac{1}{2} k x_{1}^{2}=0\), \(K_{2}=\frac{1}{2} m v_{2 x}^{2}\)

\(U_{2}=\frac{1}{2} k x_{2}^{2}=\frac{1}{2}(5.00 \mathrm{~N} / \mathrm{m})(0.100 \mathrm{~m})^{2}=0.0250 \mathrm{~J}\)

 

\(W_{\text {other }}=F \Delta x=(0.610 \mathrm{~N})(0.100 \mathrm{~m})=0.0610 \mathrm{~J}\)

The initial total mechanical energy is zero; the work done by \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\) increases the total mechanical energy to 0.0610 J, of which \(U_2\) = 0.0250 J is elastic potential energy. The remainder is kinetic energy. From Eq. (7.14) (\(K_{1}+U_{1}+W_{\text {other }}=K_{2}+U_{2}\)),

\(K_{1}+U_{1}+W_{\text {other }}=K_{2}+U_{2}\)

 

\(K_{2}=K_{1}+U_{1}+W_{\text {other }}-U_{2}\) = 0 + 0 + 0.0610 J – 0.0250 J = 0.0360 J

\(v_{2 x}=\sqrt{\frac{2 K_{2}}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(0.0360 \mathrm{~J})}{0.200 \mathrm{~kg}}}=0.60 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

We choose the positive square root because the glider is moving in the +x-direction.

 

EVALUATE: What would be different if we disconnected the glider from the spring? Then only \(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{F}}\) would do work, there would be zero elastic potential energy at all times, and Eq. (7.14) would give us

\(K_{2}=K_{1}+W_{\text {other }}=0+0.0610 \mathrm{~J}\)

 

\(v_{2 x}=\sqrt{\frac{2 K_{2}}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{2(0.0610 \mathrm{~J})}{0.200 \mathrm{~kg}}}=0.78 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}\)

Our answer \(v_{2x}\) = 0.60 m/s is less than 0.78 m/s because the spring does negative work on the glider as it stretches (see Fig. 7.13b).
If you stop pushing on the glider when it reaches x = 0.100 m, only the spring force does work on it thereafter. Hence for x > 0.100 m, the total mechanical energy E = K + U = 0.0610 J is constant. As the spring continues to stretch, the glider slows down and the kinetic energy K decreases as the potential energy increases. The glider comes to rest at some point x = \(x_3\), \(U=U_{\mathrm{el}}=\frac{1}{2} k x_{3}^{2}\) equals the total mechanical energy 0.0610 J.
Can you show that \(x_3\) = 0.156 m? (It moves an additional 0.056 m after you stop pushing.) If there is no friction, will the glider remain at rest?
