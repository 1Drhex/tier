Guest Question.

An infinite wire runs along the z axis; it carries a current I (z) that is a function of z (but not of t), and a charge density λ(t) that is a function of t (but not of z).

(a) By examining the charge flowing into a segment dz in a time dt, show that dλ/dt = –d I/dz. If we stipulate that λ(0) = 0 and I (0) = 0, show that λ(t) = kt, I (z) = –kz, where k is a constant.

b) Assume for a moment that the process is quasistatic, so the fields are given by Eqs. 2.9 and 5.38. Show that these are in fact the exact fields, by confirming that all four of Maxwell’s equations are satisfied. (First do it in differential form, for the region s > 0, then in integral form for the appropriate Gaussian cylinder/Amperian loop straddling the axis.)

\(E=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{2 \lambda}{z}\) (2.9)

\(B=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi s}\) (5.38)

Admin
Solution

(a) The charge flowing into dz in time dt is

\(d q=I(z) d t-I(z+d z) d t=-\frac{d I}{d t} d z d t=\lambda(t+d t) d z-\lambda(t) d z=\frac{d \lambda}{d t} d t d z \Rightarrow \frac{d \lambda}{d t}=-\frac{d I}{d z}\) .

Since the left side is a function only of t, and the right side is a function only of z, they must both be constant; call it k:

\(\frac{d \lambda}{d t}=k \Rightarrow \lambda(t)=k t+C_{1} ; \quad \frac{d I}{d z}=-k \Rightarrow I(z)=-k z+C_{2}\) .

\(\text { If } \lambda(0)=0 \text { and } I(0)=0 \text { the constants } C_{1} \text { and } C_{2} \text { must both be zero: } \lambda(t)=k t, I(z)=-k z\) .

(b) In the quasistatic approximation, \(E =\frac{\lambda}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} s} \hat{ s }=\frac{k t}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} s} \hat{ s } ; \quad B =\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi s} \hat{ \phi }=-\frac{\mu_{0} k z}{2 \pi s} \hat{ \phi }\) .

\(\nabla \cdot E =\frac{1}{s} \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\left(s \frac{k t}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} s}\right)=0\)

 

\(\nabla \cdot B =\frac{1}{s} \frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\left(-\frac{\mu_{0} k z}{2 \pi s}\right)=0\)

 

\(\nabla \times E =\frac{\partial E}{\partial z} \hat{ \phi }-\frac{1}{s} \frac{\partial E}{\partial \phi} \hat{ z }= 0 =-\frac{\partial B }{\partial t}\)

 

\(\nabla \times B =-\frac{\partial B}{\partial z} \hat{ \phi }+\frac{1}{s} \frac{\partial(s B)}{\partial s} \hat{ z }=\frac{\mu_{0} k}{2 \pi s} \hat{ z }=\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{\partial E }{\partial t} \)

For a gaussian cylinder of radius s about the z axis, at height z and with width dz:

\(\oint E \cdot d a =\frac{k t}{2 \pi \epsilon_{0} s}(2 \pi s) d z=\frac{k t}{\epsilon_{0}} d z=\frac{\lambda d z}{\epsilon_{0}}=\frac{Q_{ enc }}{\epsilon_{0}}\) . \(\oint B \cdot d a =0 \) .

For a circular amperian loop of radius s about the z axis, at height z:

\(\oint E \cdot d l =0=-\frac{d}{d t} \int B \cdot d a\) , \(\oint B \cdot d l =-\frac{\mu_{0} k z}{2 \pi s}(2 \pi s)=\mu_{0} I=\mu_{0} I_{ enc }+\mu_{0} \epsilon_{0} \frac{d}{d t} \int E \cdot d a\) .

(Note that \(\int B \cdot d a \text { and } \int E\) · da are zero through this loop.)
