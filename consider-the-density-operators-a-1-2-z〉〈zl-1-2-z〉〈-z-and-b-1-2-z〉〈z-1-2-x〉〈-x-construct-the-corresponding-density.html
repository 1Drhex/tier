Guest Question.

Consider the density operators (a) \(\frac{1}{2} |+z〉〈+z| + \frac{1}{2} |-z〉〈-z| and (b) \frac{1}{2} |+z〉〈+z| + \frac{1}{2} |-x〉〈-x|\). Construct the corresponding density matrices in the \(S_{z}\) basis and show that these are density operators for mixed states. Calculate \(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle\) for each state.

We will see that the density operator in (a) can be used to characterize the unpolarized ensemble of silver atoms that exit the oven in the original Stem-Gerlach experiment (see Section 1.1), whereas the density operator in (b) might characterize an ensemble that is generated by a 50-50 mix of atoms that exit an SGz device with \(S_{z}=\hbar /2\) and an SGx device with \(S_{x}= -\hbar /2 \), as illustrated in Fig. 5 .11.

Admin
Solution

(a) \( \hat{\rho }=\frac{1}{2}|+z〉〈+z|+\frac{1}\){2}〉〈-z|\underset{S_{z} basis }{\longrightarrow } \frac{1}{2}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)

Note that

\( \hat{\rho } ^{2}\underset{S_{z} basis }{\longrightarrow } \frac{1}{4}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\)

 

and thus tr \(\hat{\rho } ^{2}=\frac{1}{2}\) signifying a mixed state.

\(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle=tr(\hat{S} _{x}\hat{\rho } )=tr\left[\frac{\hbar }{2}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)\frac{1}{2}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right) \right]=tr\frac{\hbar }{4}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right) =0\)

 

which is what we would expect for an ensemble of particles half of which have \(S_{z}=\hslash /2\) and half of which have \(S_{z}=-\hslash /2\)

(b) \(\hat{\rho }=\frac{1}{2}|+z〉〈+z|+\frac{1}{2}-x〉〈-x|\)

 

\( =\frac{1}{2}|+z〉〈+z|+\frac{1}{4}(|+z〉-|z〉)(〈+z|-〈-z|)\)

 

Therefore

\(\hat{\rho }\underset{S_{z}basis }{\longrightarrow }\frac{1}{2}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)+\frac{1}{4}\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)=\frac{1}{4}\left(\begin{matrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\)

 

Consequently,

\( \hat{\rho } ^{2}\underset{S_{z} basis }{\longrightarrow }\frac{1}{8}\left(\begin{matrix} 5 & -2 \\ -2 & 1 \end{matrix} \right)\)

 

for which the trace is \(\frac{5}{8}+\frac{1}{8}=\frac{3}{4}\), again signifying a mixed state.

\(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle=tr(\hat{S} _{x}\hat{P} )=tr\left[\frac{\hbar }{2}\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\frac{1}{4}\left(\begin{matrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{matrix} \right) \right]\)

 

\( =tr\frac{\hbar }{8}\left(\begin{matrix} -1 & 1 \\ 3 & -1 \end{matrix} \right)=-\frac{\hbar }{4}\)

 

which is what you would expect for an ensemble of particles for which
half are in the state |+z〉, which has \(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle\)=0, and half are in the state |-x〉, which has \(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle =-\hbar /2\).

Caution: Although we have argued that our results for \(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle\) are consistent with an ensemble of spin \(-\frac{1}{2}\) particles with a certain fraction of the particles in one pure state and a certain fraction in another pure state, you cannot infer from the form of the density operator for a mixed state that the ensemble is indeed in this particular mixture of pure states. For example, take the density operator

\( \hat{\rho }=\frac{1}{2}|+x〉〈+x|+\frac{1}{2}〉|-x〉〈-x|\)

Expressing this operator in terms of the |+z〉-|-z〉 basis states, we see that

\(\hat{P}=\frac{1}{4}(|+z〉+|-z〉)(〈+z|+〈-z|)+\frac{1}{4}(|+z〉-|-z〉)(〈+z|-〈-z|)\)

 

\( =\frac{1}{2}|+ z〉〈+z|+\frac{1}{2}|-z〉-〈-z|\)

namely, the same density operator that we examined in part (a) and
characterized as an ensemble with half the particles having \(\left\langle S_{z} \right\rangle =-\hbar /2\)

and half having \(\left\langle S_{z} \right\rangle =-\hbar /2\) . Now we see that we could just as easily characterize this state as a mixture with half the particles having

\(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle =\hbar /2\) and half having \(\left\langle S_{x} \right\rangle =-\hbar /2\) It can also be shown (see Problem
5.19) that the density operator \(\frac{1}{2}|+z〉〈+z|+\frac{1}{2}|-z〉〈-z|\) is the same as the density operator

\(\hat{P}=\frac{1}{2}|+n〉〈+n|+\frac{1}{2}|-n〉〈-n|\)

Thus this mixed state can be viewed as having half the particles with \(S_{n}=\hbar /2\) and half with \(S_{n}=-\hbar /2 \) for any direction n. Consequently, the density operator \(\frac{1}{2}| +z〉〈 +z| +\frac{1}{2}|-z〉〈-z|\) characterizes a completely unpolarized collection of spin\(-\frac{1}{2}\)particles.

This example illustrates an important point. Seemingly different mixed states that correspond to the same density operator are really the same quantum state, since the expectation value of every observable is the same for each of these ensembles.
