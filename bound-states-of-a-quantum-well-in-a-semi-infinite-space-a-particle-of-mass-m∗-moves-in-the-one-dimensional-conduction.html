Guest Question.

Bound states of a quantum well in a semi-infinite space A particle of mass \(m^{\ast} \) moves in the one-dimensional conduction band energy profile shown in Figure 3.6. Find the energy of the bound states and their corresponding wave functions. Compare your results to those for a potential well of the form \(E_{c} \left ( z \right ) =- \Delta E_{c}\) (3.142)

for \(z < \left |W \right | \) and \(E_{c} \left ( z \right ) =0 \) otherwise. Determine the number of bound states as a function of the well parameters \(\Delta E_{c} \) and W.

Admin
Solution

The Schrodinger equation for this problem is

\(\phi " \left ( z \right ) =- \alpha ^{2}\phi \left ( z \right ) \) for 0 < z < W (3.143)

\(\phi " \left ( z \right )= \beta ^{2}\phi \left ( z \right ) \) for z > W , and (3.144)

\(\phi \left ( z \right )=0 \) for z < 0 , (3.145)

where the following quantities were introduced:

\(\alpha = \left [ \frac{2m^{\ast}}{h ^{2}} \left ( E – \Delta E_{c} \right )\right ] ^{1/2} \) (3.146)

\(\beta = \left (\frac{2m^{\ast} E}{h ^{2}} \right )^{1/2} \) (3.147)

which are both real for bound states. Since \(\phi \left ( z=0 \right ) = 0 \), bound state solutions with \(− \Delta E_{c} < E<0 \) are of the form \(\phi \left ( z \right ) = A \sin \left (\alpha z \right ) \) for 0 ≤ z ≤ W (3.148)

and

\(\phi \left ( z \right ) = B e^{- \beta z} \) for z ≥ W. (3.149)
Enforcing the continuity of \(\phi \left ( z \right ) \) and \(\phi \left ( z \right ) \) at z = W, we get the following two equations:

\(B = A e^{\beta W}\sin \left (\alpha W \right ) \) (3.150)

and

\(\beta B = – A e^{\beta W} \alpha \cos \left (\alpha W \right ) \) (3.151)

Introducing the variables \(\xi = \alpha W \) and \(\eta = \beta W, \) we can combine and write the last two equations as

\(\begin{bmatrix} e^{\eta } \sin\xi & -1 \\ \alpha e^{\eta } \cos \xi & \beta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} A\\ B \end{bmatrix} =0 \) (3.152)

The requirement that non-trivial solutions exist for A and B dictates that the determinant of the 2×2 matrix vanishes, which results in the following relation:

\(\xi \cot \xi = – \eta \) (3.153)

In addition, using Equations (3.146) and (3.147), it is found that the two variables \(\xi \) and \(\eta \)must also satisfy the relation
\(\xi^{2} +\eta^{2} =2 m^{\ast} W^{2} \Delta E_{c} /h^{2} \) (3.154)

The bound state solutions can be obtained by solving the last two equations simul- taneously for \(\eta \) and \(\xi. \) One approach for accomplishing this is to plot the last two equations in the \((\xi,\eta) \) plane. The intersection(s) of the circle given by Equation (3.154) and the different branches associated with Equation (3.153) correspond to the bound state solutions. The larger the value of \(W^{2}\Delta E_{c} \), the larger will be the radius of the circle, leading to more possible bound states.

If \(\Delta E_{c}< \frac{\pi^{2} h^{2}}{ 8 m^{\ast} W^{2}} \) , there is no bound state.

If \(\frac{\pi^{2} h^{2}}{ 8 m^{\ast} W^{2}}<\Delta E_{c}< \frac{\pi^{2} h^{2}}{ 2m^{\ast} W^{2}} \) , there will be one bound state only. It is easy to show that the number of bound states is the largest integer less than

\(\frac{2}{\pi} \left (\frac{2m^{\ast} W^{2} \Delta E_{c}}{h^{2}}\right ) \) (3.155)

The solutions discussed above are identical to the odd solutions of the potential energy profile given by

\( E_{c} \left ( z \right )= − \Delta E_{c}\) for −W 0 with \(\phi \left ( 0 \right ) = 0. \)
