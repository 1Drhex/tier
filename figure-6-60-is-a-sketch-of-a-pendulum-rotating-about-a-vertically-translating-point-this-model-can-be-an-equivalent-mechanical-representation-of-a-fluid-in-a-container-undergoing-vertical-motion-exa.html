Guest Question.

Figure 6.60 is a sketch of a pendulum rotating about a vertically translating point. This model can be an equivalent mechanical representation of a fluid in a container undergoing vertical motion. Examples include liquid fuel in a rocket, or stores of liquid in a tank on the ground excited by an earthquake. Derive the system equations of motion and discuss system behavior.

Admin
Solution

Solution: This problem seems no different than many earlier problems. However, its equation of motion has a unique feature that demonstrates a behavior that is new. The governing equations are derived using Lagrange’s equation for each generalized coordinate, \(y\) and θ. We assume that the container is rigid and only vertical motion is significant.
The kinetic energy can be expressed as

 

\(\begin{aligned}T &=\frac{1}{2} m\left[(L \dot{\theta} \cos \theta)^{2}+(\dot{y}+L \dot{\theta} \sin \theta)^{2}\right] \\&=\frac{1}{2} m\left[L^{2} \dot{\theta}^{2}+2 L \dot{y} \dot{\theta} \sin \theta+\dot{y}^{2}\right],\end{aligned}\)

 

where Figure 6.61 shows the velocity components. The total potential energy equals the sum due to vertical displacement \(y\) and rotation θ,

 

\(V=m g[L(1-\cos \theta)+y]\)

 

where \(V\) is measured positive from position θ = 0.

 

The base motion results in a transmitted force, \(F(t)\), on the pendulum pivot in the vertical direction \(y\). Since there is no force in the θ direction, the virtual work simplifies, as follows,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\delta W &=F_{\theta} \delta \theta+F_{y} \delta y \\&=0 \cdot \delta \theta+F \delta y\end{aligned}\)

 

We can now evaluate Lagrange’s equation for each generalized coordinate. For the θ coordinate,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}} &=\frac{1}{2} m\left[2 L^{2} \dot{\theta}+2 L \dot{y} \sin \theta\right] \\\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}}\right) &=\frac{1}{2} m\left[2 L^{2} \ddot{\theta}+2 L \ddot{y} \sin \theta+2 L \dot{y} \dot{\theta} \cos \theta\right] \\\frac{\partial T}{\partial \theta} &=\frac{1}{2} m[2 L \dot{\theta} \dot{y} \cos \theta] \\\frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta} &=m g L \sin \theta .\end{aligned}\)

 

Substituting these into Lagrange’s equation leads to

 

\(m L^{2} \ddot{\theta}+m L \ddot{y} \sin \theta+m g L \sin \theta=0\)

 

which can be simplified to

 

\(L \ddot{\theta}+(\ddot{y}+g) \sin \theta=0\) (6.154)

 

For the \(y\) coordinate,

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}} &=m[\dot{y}+L \dot{\theta} \sin \theta] \\\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{y}}\right) &=m\left[\ddot{y}+L \ddot{\theta} \sin \theta+L \dot{\theta}^{2} \cos \theta\right] \\\frac{\partial T}{\partial y} &=0 \\\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} &=m g .\end{aligned}\)

 

The resulting equation of motion is

 

\(\ddot{y}+L \ddot{\theta} \sin \theta+L \dot{\theta}^{2} \cos \theta+g=\frac{F}{m} .\) (6.155)

 

Next, we look for the stability of motion about the equilibrium position θ = 0. In the region about small θ, Equation 6.154 can be rewritten as

 

\(\ddot{\theta}+\frac{1}{L}(\ddot{y}+g) \theta=0\) (6.156)

 

which shows the coupling between the generalized coordinates. For small θ, Equation 6.155 becomes

 

\(\ddot{y}+L \ddot{\theta} \theta+L \dot{\theta}^{2}+g=\frac{F}{m},\) (6.157)

 

which is simplified but still nonlinear. If θ and \(\dot{\theta}\) are small, we can neglect higher order terms such as \(\dot{\theta}^{2}\) and \(\ddot{\theta} \theta\). In this case, Equation 6.157 is linearized to

 

\(\ddot{y}+g=\frac{F}{m},\) (6.158)

 

and is decoupled from the θ motion.

If we assume that the support motion is harmonic, \(y(t)=A \cos \omega t\) we have an example of the base motion of a fluid filled container. Substituting the second derivative of \(y(t)\) into Equation 6.158, we find that the force required to create the base motion is

 

\(F(t)=-m A \omega^{2} \cos \omega t+m g\)

 

Equation 6.156 then becomes

 

\(\ddot{\theta}+\frac{1}{L}\left(g-A \omega^{2} \cos \omega t\right) \theta=0\) (6.159)

 

which is an equation with a time-varying coefficient of θ. The term \(\left(g-A \omega^{2} \cos \omega t\right)\) is a harmonic function that can have negative or positive values depending on the values of \(A\) and ω.

If\(g>A \omega^{2} \text { then } g>A \omega^{2} \cos \omega t\) and the coefficient of θ is always positive with a response \(θ(t)\) that is always stable. If \(g<A \omega^{2}\), we obtain solutions with coefficients of the form \(\exp (+\omega T)\) that grow without bound. Equations of this type, known as the Mathieu equation, are discussed further in Section 11.5.
