Guest Question.

Using Eqs. 2.27 and 2.30, find the potential at a distance z above the center of the charge distributions in Fig. 2.34. In each case, compute E = −∇V, and compare your answers with , , and , respectively. Suppose that we changed the right-hand charge in Fig. 2.34a to −q; what then is the potential at P? What field does that suggest? Compare your answer to , and explain carefully any discrepancy.

\(V(r) =\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\frac{q_i}{\eta _i}\) (2.27)

\(V =\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\int{\frac{\lambda \left(r^\prime \right) }{\eta } }dl^\prime and V = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\int{}\frac{\sigma \left(r^\prime \right) }{\eta }da^\prime\) (2.30)

Admin
Solution

(a) \(V=\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\frac{2q}{\sqrt{z^2+\left(\frac{d}{2} \right)^2 } } \) .

(b) \(V=\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\int_{-L}^{L}{} \frac{\lambda dx}{\sqrt{z^2+x^2} } =\frac{\lambda }{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\ln \left(x+\sqrt{x^2+x^2} \right) \mid ^{L}_{-L} \)

=\(\frac{\lambda }{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\ln \left[\frac{L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2} }{-L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2} } \right]=\frac{\lambda }{2\pi \varepsilon _0}\ln \left(\frac{L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2} }{z} \right) \) .

(c) \(V =\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\int_{0}^{R}{}\frac{\sigma 2\pi rdr}{\sqrt{r^2+z^2} }=\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0} 2\pi \sigma \left(\sqrt{r^2+z^2} \right)\mid ^{R}_{0}=\frac{\sigma }{2\varepsilon _0}\left(\sqrt{R^2+z^2}-z \right) \) .

In each case, by symmetry \(\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}=0. \therefore E=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat{z} \) .

(a) \(E = −\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}2q\left(-\frac{1}{2} \right)\frac{2z}{\left(z^2+\left(\frac{d}{2} \right)^2 \right)^{3/2} }\hat{z}=\frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\frac{2qz}{\left(z^2+\left(\frac{d}{2} \right)^2 \right)^{3/2} } \hat{z} \) (agrees with ).

(b) \(E = −\frac{\lambda }{4\pi \varepsilon _0} \left\{\frac{1}{\left(L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2} \right) }\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2} } 2z-\frac{1}{\left(-L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2} \right) } \frac{1}{2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2} }2z \right\}\hat{z} \)

= \(-\frac{\lambda }{4\pi \varepsilon _0}\frac{z}{\sqrt{z^2+L^2} } \left\{\frac{-L+\sqrt{z^2+L^2}-L-\sqrt{z^2+L^2} }{\left(z^2+L^2\right)-L^2 } \right\} \hat{z} = \frac{2L\lambda }{4\pi \varepsilon _0} \frac{1}{z\sqrt{z^2+L^2} } \hat{z} \) (agrees with ).

(c) \(E=-\frac{\sigma }{2\varepsilon _0} \left\{\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{\sqrt{R^2+z^2} }2z-1 \right\} \hat{z}=\frac{\sigma }{2\varepsilon _0}\left[1-\frac{z}{\sqrt{R^2+z^2} } \right]\hat{z} \) (agrees with )

If the right-hand charge in (a) is −q, then V = 0 , which, naively, suggests E = −∇V = 0, in contradiction with the answer to . The point is that we only know V on the z axis, and from this we cannot hope to compute\(E_x=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} or E_y=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} \) . That was OK in part (a), because we knew from symmetry that \(E_x = E_y = 0\). But now E points in the x direction, so knowing V on the z axis is insufficient to determine E.
