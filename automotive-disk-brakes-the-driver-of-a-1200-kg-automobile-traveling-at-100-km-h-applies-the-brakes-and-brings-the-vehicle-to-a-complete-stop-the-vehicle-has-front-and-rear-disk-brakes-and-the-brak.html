Guest Question.

Automotive Disk Brakes The driver of a 1200 kg automobile traveling at 100 km/h applies the brakes and brings the vehicle to a complete stop. The vehicle has front and rear disk brakes, and the braking system is balanced so that the front set provides 75% of the total braking capacity. Through friction between the brake pads and the rotors, the brakes convert the automobile’s kinetic energy into heat (Figure 7.10). If the two front 7 kg brake rotors are initially at a temperature of 25 ºC, how hot are they after the vehicle stops? The specific heat of cast iron is c 50.43 kJ/(kg ? ºC).

As the vehicle comes to a stop, a portion of its initial kinetic energy is lost through air drag, the rolling resistance of the tires, and the wear of the brake pads, but we will neglect those extraneous factors at the first level of approximation. The automobile’s kinetic energy [Equation (7.3)] decreases as work is performed on it, and the heat produced will cause the temperature of the brake rotors to increase. That temperature rise can be found by applying Equation (7.7).

 

\(Q=W+\Delta U\) (Equation 7.10)

 

\(U_{k}=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}\) (Equation 7.3)

 

\(Q=m c\left(T-T_{0}\right)\) (equation 7.7)

Admin
Solution

In dimensionally consistent units, the initial velocity is

 

\(\begin{aligned}v &=\left(100 \frac{ km }{ h }\right)\left(1000 \frac{ m }{ km }\right)\left(\frac{1}{3600} \frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=27.78\left(\frac{ km }{ h }\right)\left(\frac{ m }{ km }\right)\left(\frac{ h }{ s }\right) \\&=27.78 \frac{ m }{ s }\end{aligned}\)

 

The automobile’s kinetic energy decreases by the amount

 

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta U_{k} &=\frac{1}{2}(1200 kg )\left(27.78 \frac{ m }{ s }\right)^{2} \leftarrow\left[U_{k}=\frac{1}{2} mv ^{2}\right] \\&=4.630 \times 10^{5}\left(\frac{ kg \cdot m }{ s ^{2}}\right)( m ) \\&=4.630 \times 10^{5} N \cdot m \\&=4.630 \times 10^{5} J \\&=463.0 kJ\end{aligned}\)

 

Because the front brakes provide three-quarters of the braking capacity, the quantity

 

\(\begin{aligned}Q &=(0.75)(463.0 kJ ) \\&=347.3 kJ\end{aligned}\)

 

of heat flows into the front rotors. Their temperature rises according to

 

\(347.3 kJ =2(7 kg )\left(0.43 \frac{ kJ }{ kg \cdot{ }^{\circ} C }\right)\left(T-25{ }^{\circ} C \right) \quad \leftarrow\left[Q=m c\left(T-T_{0}\right)\right]\)

 

where the factor of 2 accounts for both front rotors. The equation is dimensionally consistent, and the final temperature becomes \(T=82.69^{\circ} C\).
