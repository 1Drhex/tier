Guest Question.

The composition of \(\mathrm{A}\) in the reactor is measured at various times with results shown in the following columns 1 and 2 . Find a rate equation to represent the data

\(\begin{array}{cccc}\hline \text { Column 1 } & \text { Column 2 } & \text { Column 3 } & \text { Column 4 } \\\hline \begin{array}{c}\text { Time } \\t, \mathrm{~s}\end{array} & \begin{array}{c}\text { Concentration } \\C_{\mathrm{A}}, \text { mol/liter }\end{array} & \ln \frac{C_{\mathrm{A} 0}}{C_{\mathrm{A}}} & \frac{1}{C_{\mathrm{A}}} \\\hline 0 & C_{\mathrm{A} 0}=10 & \ln 10 / 10=0 & 0.1 \\20 & 8 & \ln 10 / 8=0.2231 & 0.125 \\40 & 6 & 0.511 & 0.167 \\60 & 5 & 0.6931 & 0.200 \\120 & 3 & 1.204 & 0.333 \\180 & 2 & 1.609 & 0.500 \\300 & 1 & 2.303 & 1.000\end{array}\)

 

Admin
Solution

Guess First-Order Kinetics. Start by guessing the simplest rate form, or firstorder kinetics. This means that \(\ln C_{\mathrm{A} 0} / C_{\mathrm{A}}\) vs. \(t\) should give a straight line, see Eq. 11 or \(12,\) or Fig. \(3.1 .\) So column 3 is calculated and the plot of Fig. \(\mathrm{E} 3.1 a\) is made. Unfortunately, this does not give a straight line, so first-order kinetics cannot reasonably represent the data, and we must guess another rate form.

Guess nth-Order Kinetics. Let’s plan to use the fractional life method with \(\mathrm{F}=80 \%\). Then Eq. \(33 b\) becomes

\(t_{\mathrm{F}}=\frac{(0.8)^{1-n}-1}{k(n-1)} C_{\mathrm{A} 0}^{1-n}\)

Next take logarithms

\(\log t_{\mathrm{F}}=\overbrace{\log \left(\frac{0.8^{1-n_{-1}}}{k(n-1)}\right)}^{\text {a constant }}+(1-n) \log C_{\mathrm{A} 0}\)

The procedure is as follows. First accurately plot the \(C_{\mathrm{A}}\) vs. \(t\) data, draw a smooth curve to represent the data (most important) as shown in Fig. E3.1c, then pick \(C_{\mathrm{A} 0}=10,5,\) and 2 and fill in the following table from this figure.

\(\begin{array}{ccccc}\hline & C_{\mathrm{A} e n d} & \text { Time needed } & & \\C_{\mathrm{A} 0} & \left(=0.8 C_{\mathrm{A} 0}\right) & t_{\mathrm{F}} \mathrm{S} & \log t_{\mathrm{F}} & \log C_{\mathrm{A} 0} \\\hline 10 & 8 & 0 \rightarrow 18.5=18.5 & \log 18.5=1.27 & 1.00 \\5 & 4 & 59 \rightarrow 82=23 & 1.36 & 0.70 \\2 & 1.6 & 180 \rightarrow 215=35 & 1.54 & 0.30 \\\hline & & & & \\\hline\end{array}\)

 

Next, plot log \(t_{\mathrm{F}}\) vs. \(\log C_{\mathrm{A} 0},\) as shown in Fig. \(\mathrm{E} 3.1 d\) and find the slope.

We now have the reaction order. To evaluate the rate constant, take any point on the \(C_{\mathrm{A}}\) vs. \(t\) curve. Pick \(C_{\mathrm{A} 0}=10,\) for which \(t_{\mathrm{F}}=18.5 \mathrm{~s}\). Replacing all values into Eq.

(i) gives

\(18.5=\frac{(0.8)^{1-1.4}-1}{k(1.4-1)} 10^{1-1.4}\)

from which

\(k=0.005\)

Therefore, the rate equation that represents this reaction is

\(-r_{\mathrm{A}}=\left(0.005 \frac{\text { liter }^{0.4}}{\mathrm{~mol}^{0.4} \cdot \mathrm{s}}\right) C_{\mathrm{A}}^{1.4}, \quad \frac{\mathrm{mol}}{\text { liter } \cdot \mathrm{s}}\)
