Guest Question.

Composite Beam Deflections

Determine whether the composite beam in is adequate for immediate and long-term deflections, assuming the following conditions:

Slab dead load = 50 psf (ignore self-weight of the beam)

Superimposed dead load = 25 psf (partitions, mechanical and electrical)

Live load = 50 psf (assume that 20% of this is sustained)

Beam span = 28′ — 6″

Beam spacing = 8 ft.

Admin
Solution

Loads to Beam:

\(w_{CDL} =(50 psf)(8 ft.) = 400 plf (33.3 lb./in.)\)

 

\(w_{SDL}=(25 psf)(8 ft.) = 200 plf (16.7 lb./in.)\)

 

\(w_{LL}= (50 psf)(8 ft.) = 400 plf (33.3 lb./in.)\)

 

From ,

\(I = 199 in.^{4}\) (noncomposite moment of inertia)

\(I_{eff}=612 in.^{4}\) (the calculated effective moment of inertia)

From equation (7-24), the moment of inertia used for long-term deflections is

\(I_{actual}=0.75I_{eff}\) (7-24)

\(I_{actual}=0.75I_{eff}= (0.75)(612) = 459 in.^{4}\)

 

As an alternate check, recall from that \(Y_{1}=0.305\) in., \(Y_{2}=4.64\) in.,and \(\sum{Q_{n} }=172\) kips. From AISCM, Table 3-20

Table 3-20 Assigned seismic weights at each level of building

Level Height from Base,\(h_{i}\) Weight,\(W_{i}\)
Roof 30 ft. \(W_{roof}= (30 psf)(75 ft.)(50 ft.)+(0.20)(42 psf)(75 ft.)(50 ft.) = 144 kip\)
Second 15 ft. \(W_{2}=(80 psf)(75 ft.)(50 ft.) = 300 kip\)

we find that \( I_{LB}=496 in^{4}\) (by interpolation) Recall that research indicates that the actual moment of inertia is between 15% and 30% less than the calculated value of \(I_{eff}\). This explains the variation between \(I_{actual}=459 in^{4}\). and \( I_{LB}=496 in^{4}\).

Since there is no specific guidance in the AISC specification as to which value to use, we will proceed with the more conservative value of \(I_{actual}=459 in^{4}\)

Construction Phase Deflection:

\(\Delta _{CDL}=\frac{5w_{CDL}L^{4}}{384EI}\)

 

\(=\frac{(5)(33.3)[(28.5)(12)]^{4} }{(384)(29 \times 10^{6} )(199)}=1.03\) in.

If this beam were to be cambered, we would take 75% of the construction phase dead load deflection as the camber, as follows:

\(\Delta _{c}\)=(0.75)(1.03 in.) = 0.771 in. Use 3⁄4-in. camber.

The construction phase deflection would then be reduced by 3⁄4 in.:

\(\Delta _{CDL}\)=1.03 in.- 0.75 in. = 0.28 in.

A construction phase deflection of 1.03 in. would likely add more concrete in the floor slab due to ponding than would be desired, so a camber of 3⁄4 in. will be specified. The post-composite deflection is

\(\Delta _{SDL}=\frac{5w_{SDL}L^{4}}{384EI}\)

 

=\(\frac{(5)(16.7)[(28.5)(12)]^{4} }{(384)(29 \times 10^{6} )(459)}=0.223\) in.

\(\Delta _{LL}=\frac{5w_{LL}L^{4}}{384EI}\)

 

\(\frac{(5)(33.3)[(28.5)(12)]^{4} }{(384)(29 \times 10^{6} )(459)}=0.446\) in.

\(\Delta _{TL}=\Delta _{SDL}+\Delta _{LL}+\Delta _{CDL}\)

 

=0.223 in. + 0.446 in. + 0.28 in. = 0.949 in.

The deflection limits are found from equations (7-25) and (7-26):

\(\Delta _{LL}\leq \frac{L}{360}\) (7-25)

\(0.446\lt \frac{(28.5)(12)}{360}=0.95\) in., OK for live load deflection

\(\Delta _{TL}\leq \frac{L}{240}\) (7-26)

\(0.949\lt \frac{(28.5)(12)}{240}=1.43\) in., OK for total load deflection

It can be seen by inspection that the 3⁄4-in. camber is needed to meet the total load deflection limit.

We must also consider the long-term deflection to account for creep effects. To do this, the effective moment of inertia must be recalculated using a modular ratio of 2n. From, n = 8.87; therefore,

2n = 17.74

\(A_{ct}=\frac{A_{c} }{n}=\frac{(80)(2)}{17.74}=9.01in^{2}\)

 

\(I_{ct}=\frac{bt^{c} _{3} }{12n}=\frac{(80) (2)^{2} }{(12)(17.74)}=3.0 in^{4}\)

 

Using the top of the concrete slab as the datum, we develop Table 7-3.

Table 7-3 Composite beam section properties

Element A y Ay I \(d=y-\overline{y} \) \(I+Ad^{2}\)
Slab 9.01 1 9.01 3ˑ0 -4.54 188.7
W14 ᵡ 22 6.49 11.85 76.9 199 6.31 457.4
∑= 15.5 85.91 \(I_{tr}=646.1 \)

\(\overline{y}=\frac{\sum{Ay} }{\sum{A} }=\frac{85.91}{15.5} =5.54\) in.

\(I_{tr}=\sum{(I+Ad^{2} )}\)

\(I_{tr}=646.1 in^{4}\). (see Table 7-3)

Accounting for the partial-composite behavior (see),

\(I_{eff}=I_{s}+\sqrt{\frac{\sum{Q_{n} } }{C_{f} } }(I_{tr}-I_{s} )=199+\sqrt{\frac{172}{324.5} }(646.1 – 199)=524.5 in^{4}\) , and

\(I_{actual}=0.75I_{eff}=(0.75)(524.5)=393 in^{4}\) .

The ratio of the actual moment of inertia used for total loads versus that used for sustained loads is

\(\frac{459 in^{4}}{393 in^{4}}=1.168\).

This ratio can be used to calculate the actual sustained load deflection. The amount of sustained live load specified for this example is 20% of the total live load, and the remaining 80% is assumed to be transient. The total sustained load deflection is then

\(\Delta _{TL(sust)}=1.168(\Delta _{SDL}+0.2\Delta _{LL} ) +\Delta _{CDL}+0.8\Delta _{LL}\)

 

\( =1.168[0.223 + (0.2)(0.446)] + 0.28 + (0.8)(0.446)=1.02\) in.

This is less than the L/240 1.43 in. calculated previously, so the section is adequate for sustained loads.
