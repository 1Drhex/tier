Guest Question.

A \(2\text{-}\mathrm{kg}\) top is in a state of steady slow precession at a spin rate of \(500 \mathrm{rev} / \mathrm{min}\) with its axis at \(\theta=120^{\circ} .\) A vertical impulsive force acting through the axis of symmetry suddenly induces an upward nutation, such that the ensuing motion is observed to be cuspidial. The radii of gyration of the top about its pivot are \(360 \mathrm{~mm}\) and \(480 \mathrm{~mm}\) parallel and transversely to the axis of symmetry, respectively, and the distance from the center of mass to the pivot is \(200 \mathrm{~mm}\). Determine (a) the nutation rate induced by the impulsive force, (b) the largest and smallest values of the nutation angle in the cuspidial precession, (c) the number of cusps in the path of the axis of symmetry for one revolution of the top about the vertical axis, and (d) the maximum, minimum, and average precession rates in the cuspidial motion.

Admin
Solution

Many of the basic formulas for a spinning top are implemented in the solution of this example. We begin by evaluating the steady precession preceding the application of the impulse force. The first of Eqs. (10.2.34) gives an approximate value for the slow precession rate. We could use that relation provided that we verify that the associated value of \(\beta_{\phi}\) is sufficiently high to warrant using that expression. Instead, we use Eq. (10.2.31) to find the slow precession rate. The parameters for the present system are \(m=2 \mathrm{~kg}, \quad I=m \kappa^{2}=0.2592 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \quad I^{\prime}=\)\(m\left(\kappa^{\prime}\right)^{2}=0.4608 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, L=0.2 \mathrm{~m}\), and \(\dot{\phi}=52.36 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}\), which leads to the two roots

\(\dot{\psi}=\frac{I \dot{\phi} \pm\left[I^{2} \dot{\phi}^{2}-4 m g L\left(I^{\prime}-I\right) \cos \theta\right]^{1 / 2}}{2\left(I^{\prime}-I\right) \cos \theta}. (10.2.31)\)

 

\(\dot{\psi}_{1}=\frac{m g L}{I \dot{\phi}}, \quad \dot{\psi}_{2}=\frac{I}{\left(I^{\prime}-I\right) \cos \theta} \dot{\phi}. (10.2.34)\)

 

\(\dot{\psi}_{1}=0.28843 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}, \quad \dot{\psi}_{2}=-134.93 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

Both values are extremely close to the approximations obtained from Eqs. (10.2.34). Because it is stated that the initial precession is slow, we use \(\dot{\psi}_{1}\) as the initial precession rate.

The impulsive force induces an unknown nutation rate \(\dot{\theta}\), because it exerts a moment about the horizontal axis through the pivot. However, the spin and precession rates are not altered during the impulse interval because the force exerts no moment about either axis. We find \(\dot{\theta}\) from the fact that the subsequent precession is cuspidial. We need the values of the precession and spin momentum parameters to evaluate cuspidial motion, so we substitute the values of \(\dot{\psi}_{1}\) and \(\dot{\phi}\) into Eqs. (10.2.4), which gives

\(\begin{array}{c}p_{\psi}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\psi}}=I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}) \cos \theta+I^{\prime} \dot{\psi}(\sin \theta)^{2} \equiv I^{\prime} \beta_{\psi}, \\\\p_{\phi}=\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\phi}}=I(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}) \equiv I^{\prime} \beta_{\phi},\end{array} (10.2.4)\)

 

\(\beta_{\phi}=29.371 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}, \quad \beta_{\psi}=-14.469 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

From this we find that the highest elevation in the cuspidial motion is

\(u_{0}=\frac{\beta_{\psi}}{\beta_{\phi}}=-0.49264. (1)\)

The perturbation analysis of cuspidial motion may be employed if the parameter \(U\) is large. The imbalance parameter is found from Eqs. (10.2.10) to be

\(\varepsilon=\frac{2 E}{I^{\prime}}-\frac{I^{\prime}}{I} \beta_{\phi}^{2}, \quad \gamma=\frac{2 m g L}{I^{\prime}}. (10.2.10)\)

 

\(\gamma=\frac{2 m g L}{I^{\prime}}=17.026,\)

whose substitution into Eq. (10.2.19) gives

\(U=\frac{\beta_{\phi}^{2}}{2 \gamma}=\frac{I^{2} \acute{\phi}_{0}^{2}}{4 I^{\prime} m g L}. (10.2.19)\)

 

\(U=\frac{\beta_{\phi}^{2}}{2 \gamma}=25.334.\)

This value is sufficiently large to warrant using Eq. (10.2.27) to describe the nutation angle. Because \(u=\cos \theta\), we find that

\(u \approx u_{0}-\frac{v_{1}}{U}=u_{0}-\frac{\gamma}{2 \beta_{\phi}^{2}}\left(1-u_{0}^{2}\right)\left[1-\cos \left(\beta_{\phi} t\right)\right]. (10.2.27)\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\theta &=\cos ^{-1}\left[u_{0}-\frac{1-u_{0}^{2}}{4 U}\left[1-\cos \left(\beta_{\phi} t\right)\right]\right] \\\\&=\cos ^{-1}[-0.49264-0.007473[1-\cos (29.371 t)]] \mathrm~{rad} .\end{aligned} (2) \)

The extreme values of the nutation angle in Eq. (2) correspond to \(\cos (29.371 t)=\) \(\pm 1\), which leads to

\(\theta_{2}=\theta_{\min }=119.512^{\circ}, \quad \theta_{1}=\theta_{\max }=120.503 .^{\circ}\)

Direct substitutions into Eqs. (10.2.28) and (10.2.29) yield the corresponding nutation and precession rates:

\(\dot{\theta}=-\frac{\dot{u}}{\sin \theta_{2}} \approx \frac{\gamma}{2 \beta_{\phi}} \sin \theta_{2} \sin \left(\beta_{\phi} t\right). (10.2.28)\)

 

\(\dot{\psi}=\frac{\gamma}{2 \beta_{\phi}}\left[1-\cos \left(\beta_{\phi} t\right)\right]. (10.2.29) \)

 

\(\dot{\theta}=0.25223 \sin (29.371 t), \quad \dot{\psi}=0.28984[1-\cos (29.371 t)] \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}, (3)\)

To determine the nutation rate imparted by the impulsive force we need to establish when the force was applied. (Recall that \(t=0\) is an instant when \(\theta=\theta_{\min }\), at which the cusp occurs.) The instant \(t=t_{0}\) at which the force ceased may be determined from the fact that \(\theta=2 \pi / 3\) initially. Setting \(\theta\) in Eq. (2) to this value gives

\(-0.5=-0.49264-0.007473\left[1-\cos \left(29.371 t_{0}\right)\right] \Longrightarrow t_{0}=0.05299 \mathrm{~s}.\)

The first of Eqs. (3) indicates that the nutation rate at this instant is

\(\dot{\theta}=0.25223 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

 

To determine the average precession rate we observe that the average of a sinusoidal function is zero, so the second of Eqs. (3) leads to

\(\dot{\psi}_{\mathrm{av}}=0.2898, \quad \dot{\psi}_{\min }=0, \quad \dot{\psi}_{\max }=0.5796 \mathrm~{rad} / \mathrm{s}.\)

Finally, we note that cusps occur when \(\theta=\theta_{\min }\), or alternatively when \(\dot{\psi}=0 .\) According to Eqs. (2) and (3), these conditions occur whenever \(\cos (29.371 t)=1\). Therefore the time interval between two adjacent cusps is the period of the oscillation:

\(\Delta t=\frac{2 \pi}{29.371}=0.2139 \mathrm{~s} .\)

At the average precession rate, the time interval for one revolution about the vertical axis is

\(T=\frac{2 \pi}{\dot{\psi}_{\mathrm{av}}}=21.68 \mathrm{~s}.\)

The number of cusps is the ratio of this period to the period \(\Delta t\) for the \(\theta\) oscillation, so

\(N=101.34.\)
