Guest Question.

Consider a gray gas with an absorption coefficient of \(κ = 0.5 m^{−1}\). The temperature of the gas varies linearly from x = 0 to x = L = 1 m according to the relation \(T_{g}(x) = 400 − 100x\) where x is in m. A diffuse gray wall of emissivity ε = 0.8 is located at x = 0 and is maintained at a temperature of \(T_1 = 400 K\). A second diffuse gray wall of ε = 0.6 is located at x = 1 m and is maintained at a temperature of \(T_2 = 300 K\). What is the heat transfer across the gas slab? Use the discrete ordinate method with \(S_4\) to solve the problem.

Admin
Solution

The solution follows the procedure described above. The \(S_4\) method involves two discrete directions along the forward and backward paths. They are given by \(\begin{matrix}\mu_{1}=0.295876, & \mu_{2}=0.908248 \end{matrix} \) with corresponding weights \(\begin{matrix} w_1 = 4.188790, & w_2 = 2.094395 \end{matrix} \)

Step 1 We divide the gas slab in to 10 slabs of elemental thickness each of ΔL = 0.1 m such that each slab may be assumed to be isothermal. Temperatures of elemental slabs are as given below:

Using the given data, the quantities required before starting the iteration process for obtaining the wall radiosities are calculated and are shown in Table 11.7:

Black body emissive power at \(T_1\) \(E_{b1}=5.67 \times 10^{−8} \times 400^4 = 1451.52 W/m^2\)
Black body emissive power at \(T_2\) \(E_{b2}=5.67 \times 10^{−8} \times 300^4 = 459.27 W/m^2\)
Initial guess for radiosity of left wall \(J_{1}^{(0)}\) \(J_{1}^{(0)}=\varepsilon _{1}E_{b1}=0.8 \times 1161.22 W/m^2\)
Initial value of forward intensity \(I_{\mu}^{+}(x=0)\) \(I_{\mu}^{+}(x=0)=\frac{J_{1}^{(0)}}{\pi}=\frac{1451.52}{\pi}=459.27 W/m^2 sr\)
Optical thickness of the slab \(\tau _{L}\) \(\tau _{L}=0.5 \times 1=0.5\)
Optical thickness of the elemental slab \(\tau _{\Delta L}\) \(\tau _{\Delta L}=0.5 \times 0.1=0.05\)

Note that the forward going intensity is isotropic, and hence it is the same along both directions in the \(S_4\).

Step 3 From the values in the last two columns, we calculate the irradiation on the right wall as

\(G_{2}^{(0)}=w_{1}\mu_{1}I_{\mu_{1}}^{+}\left(x=L\right) + w_{2}\mu_{2}I_{\mu_{2}}^{+}\left(x=L\right)=4.188790 × 0.295876 × 264.76+2.094395 × 0.908248 × 326.50 = 949.21 W / m^{2}\)

The radiosity of surface 2 may then be evaluated as

\(J_{2}^{(0)}=\varepsilon _{2}E_{b2}+\left(1-\varepsilon _{2}\right)G_{2}^{(0)} =0.6 × 459.27 + (1 − 0.6) × 949.21 = 655.25 W / m^{2}\)

Since surface 2 is diffuse, the intensity of radiation leaving this wall is independent of μ and is given by \(I_{\mu_{1}}^{-}\left(x=L\right)=\frac{655.25}{\pi}=208.57W/m^2 sr\).

Step 4 From the values in the last two columns, we calculate the irradiation on the left wall as

\(G_{1}^{(0)}=w_{1}\mu_{1}I_{\mu_{1}}^{-}\left(x=L\right) + w_{2}\mu_{2}I_{\mu_{2}}^{-}\left(x=L\right)=4.188790 × 0.295876 × 276.84+2.094395 × 0.908248 × 364.24 = 1013.40 W / m^2\)

The radiosity of surface 1 may then be updated as

\(J_{1}^{(1)}=\varepsilon _{1}E_{b1}+\left(1-\varepsilon _{1}\right)G_{1}^{(0)}=0.8 × 1451.52 + (1 − 0.8) × 1013.40= 1363.90 W/m^2\)

Since surface 1 is diffuse, the intensity of radiation leaving this wall is independent of μ and is given by \(I_{\mu}^{+}\left(x=0\right) =\frac{1363.90}{\pi}=434.14W/m^2 sr\).

Step 5 After one forward and one backward pass the change in radiosity of surface 1 is

\(\Delta J_{1}=J_{1}^{(1)}-J_{1}^{(0)}=1363.90 − 1161.22 = 202.68 W/m^2\)

The change is substantial and hence we continue the iteration process. Iteration converges fast as seen in the following table.
