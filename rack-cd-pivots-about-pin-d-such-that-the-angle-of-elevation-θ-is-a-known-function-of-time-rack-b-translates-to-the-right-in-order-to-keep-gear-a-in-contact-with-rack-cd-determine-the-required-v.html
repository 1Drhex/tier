Guest Question.

Rack \(C D\) pivots about pin \(D\) such that the angle of elevation \(\theta\) is a known function of time. Rack \(B\) translates to the right in order to keep gear \(A\) in contact with rack \(C D\). Determine the required velocity \(v\), the corresponding angular velocity of gear \(A\), and the velocity of the gear’s center. Express all results in terms of the instantaneous values of \(\theta\) and \(\dot{\theta}\).

Admin
Solution

This example concerns a situation in which contacting surfaces involved in rolling have a normal velocity component, so its analysis will generalize our understanding of rolling in planar motion. Our approach treats the system as a linkage, in which some of the constrained points are subject to the no-slip condition. The sketch shows the global xyz coordinate system we shall use, as well as the geometrical parameters we will need to describe position vectors.

The constrained points are the center \(A\), which follows a straight horizontal path, point \(B\), at which there can be no slipping between gear \(A\) and the horizontal rack, point \(E\), at which there can be no slipping between gear \(A\) and rack \(C D\), and point \(C\), which is stationary. We set \(\bar{v}_{A}=-v_{A} \bar{i}, \bar{v}_{C}=\overline{0}\), and equate the velocities of point \(E\) obtained by considering it to be a point on rack \(C D\), or on gear \(A\). Thus,

\(\bar{v}_{E}=\dot{\theta} \bar{k} \times \bar{r}_{E / C}=-v_{A} \bar{i}+\omega_{A} \bar{k} \times \bar{r}_{E / A}\). (1)

Matching the velocity descriptions of contact point \(B\) based on considering it to be a point in the rack or in the gear gives

\(\bar{v}_{B}=-v \bar{i}=-v_{A} \bar{i}+\omega_{A} \bar{k} \times \bar{r}_{B / A}\). (2)

The length \(L\) from the pivot is related to the angle \(\theta\) by

\(L=R \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)\), (3)

so that

\(\bar{r}_{E / C}=R \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)(\cos \theta \bar{i}+\sin \theta \bar{j}), \quad \bar{r}_{E / A}=R(-\sin \theta \bar{i}+\cos \theta \bar{j}), \quad \bar{r}_{B / A}=-R \bar{j}\).

Substitution of these position vectors into Eqs. (1) and (2) gives

\( \begin{array}{c}\bar{v}_{E}=R \dot{\theta} \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)(-\sin \theta \bar{i}+\cos \theta \bar{j})=-v_{A} \bar{i}+\omega_{A} R(-\cos \theta \bar{i}-\sin \theta \bar{j}), \\\\\bar{v}_{B}=-v \bar{i}=\dot{v}_{A} \bar{i}+\omega_{A} R \bar{i}.\end{array} (4)\)

Matching like components in each of these equations leads to

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{v}_{B} \cdot \bar{i}=-v=-v_{A}+\omega_{A} R, \\\\\bar{v}_{E} \cdot \bar{i}=-R \dot{\theta} \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) \sin \theta=-v_{A}-\omega_{A} R \cos \theta,\\\\\bar{v}_{E} \cdot \bar{j}=R \dot{\theta} \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) \cos \theta=-\omega_{A} R \sin \theta.\end{array}\)

The solution of these equations is

\(\begin{array}{c}\omega_{A}=-\dot{\theta} \cot \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) \cot \theta \equiv-\dot{\theta} \frac{\cos \theta}{(1-\cos \theta)}, \\\\v_{A}=-\frac{\omega_{A} R}{\cos \theta}=R \dot{\theta} \frac{1}{(1-\cos \theta)}, \\\\v=-\omega_{A} R\left(\frac{1}{\cos \theta}+1\right)=R \dot{\theta} \frac{(1+\cos \theta)}{(1-\cos \theta)}.\end{array} (5)\)

An interesting aspect of the result for \(v_{A}\) is that it could have been obtained much more simply by a different approach. A geometrical analysis shows that \(L\) also is the distance from pivot \(C\) to the point of contact \(B\). Because this point is always directly below the center \(A\), we have \(\bar{r}_{A / C}=L \bar{i}+R \bar{j} .\) Differentiating this vector, with \(L\) given by Eq. (3), leads to

\(\bar{v}_{A}=\dot{L} \bar{i}=\frac{d L}{d \theta} \dot{\theta} \bar{i}=-R \dot{\theta} \frac{1}{2(\sin (\theta / 2))^{2}}. (6)\)

Because \(2(\sin (\theta / 2))^{2} \equiv 1-\cos \theta\), this result is the same as the second of Eqs. (5). Clearly the second approach represents a more direct route for analyzing \(\bar{v}_{A}\), but it gives neither the angular velocity of gear \(A\) nor the velocity of rack \(B\).

Suppose it had been requested to determine the accelerations of rack \(B\) and center \(A\) and the angular acceleration of the gear. As a consequence of the circular geometry, the derived expressions for \(\omega_{A}, v_{A}\), and \(v\) are valid at any \(\theta .\) Thus expressions for \(\dot{\omega}_{A}, \dot{v}_{A}\), and \(\dot{v}\) can be obtained by direct differentiation. For example,

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{\omega}_{A} &=-\ddot{\theta} \frac{\cos \theta}{(1-\cos \theta)}-\dot{\theta}^{2} \frac{d}{\partial \theta}\left[\frac{\cos \theta}{(1-\cos \theta)}\right] \\\\&=-\ddot{\theta} \frac{\cos \theta}{(1-\cos \theta)}+\dot{\theta}^{2} \frac{\sin \theta}{(1-\cos \theta)^{2}}.\end{aligned}\)
