Calculation of MIRR

Reconsider the project cash flows given in . At MARR of 6%, determine the MIRR and make an accept-reject decision. Assume that the financing cost is the same as MARR of 6%.

Inflow Outflow \(A_{n}\) n
$1,000 -$1,000 0
$3,900 3,900 1
5,030 -5,030 2
2,145 2,145 3
Admin
Solution

Given: Cash flow series as stated for mixed investment and i = k = 6%.
Find: MIRR.

Using Eq.

\(\frac{\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{max}\left(A_{n}, 0 \right)\left(1+i\right) ^{N-n} }{\sum\limits_{n=0}^{N}{min}\left(A_{n}, 0 \right)\left(1+k\right) ^{-n} } = \left(1+MIRR\right) ^{N}\)

\(\frac{\$3,900\left(F/P,6\%,2\right)+ \$2,145 }{\$1,000 + \$5,030 \left(F/P,6\%,2\right)}= \left(1+MIRR\right) ^{3}\)

\(=\frac{\$6,527.04}{\$5,476.38}\)

= 1.1919

\(1.1919 = \left(1+MIRR\right) ^{3}\)

\(MIRR = 6.026\% \gt 6\%\)

So, we accept the investment. Note that this MIRR figure is different from the RIC of 6.13% in . Figure 7.12 illustrates the process of obtaining the MIRR.

Comments: Since it is simpler than the RIC calculation, the method has been advocated in many engineering economy textbooks or by the practitioners. However, that does not mean that the MIRR more accurately reflect the cost and profitability of a project. Once again, if all we want is to modify the cash flow series to produce a unique rate of return, we just use the NPW criterion.
