Guest Question.

The circuit shown in Fig. 7.6-12 is the small-signal equivalent circuit of a transistor amplifier for analysis of operation at high frequency. Find the gain, i.e., ratio of output voltage phasor to input voltage phasor at 1 MHz.

Admin
Solution

As a first step, we convert the circuit to the left of \(100pF\) capacitor to current source in parallel with a resistor by applying Norton’s

Theorem in time-domain itself. The resulting Norton’s equivalent and the complete circuit with the Norton’s equivalent in place are shown in Fig. 7.6-13.

Let \(v_{S}(t) = V_{m}\) \(cos ω t V\) with \(ω = 2\pi × 10^{6}\) rad/s. The admittance of \(5 \pi F\) and \(100 \pi F\) capacitors are calculated as \(j3.1416×10^{-5}\mho\) and \(j6.283×10^{-4} \mho\) , respectively, at \(1 MHz\). The conductance of \(89.9Ω\) and \(2kΩ\) are \(0.0111 \mho\) and \(0.0005 \mho\) , respectively. The phasor equivalent circuit constructed using these values is shown in Fig. 7.6-14. Two nodes and corresponding node voltage phasors and the ground node are also marked.

\(\left(0.0111+j 6.6 \times 10^{-4}\right) V_{1}-j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5} V_{2}=0.01 V_{m} \angle 0^{\circ}\)

 

We note that the controlling voltage phasor \(V_{x}\) is same as the node voltage phasor \(V_{1}\). Therefore, the node equation at second node is:

\(\left(0.08-j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5}\right) V_{1}+\left(0.0005+j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5}\right) V_{2}=0\)

 

We need to solve for only \(V_{2}\). Substituting for \(V_{1}\) in terms of \(V_{2}\) as determined from Eqn. 7.6-2 in Eqn. 7.6-1 yields the solution for \(V_{2}\).

\(\left[-\left(0.0111+j 6.6 \times 10^{-4}\right) \frac{\left(0.0005+j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5}\right)}{\left(0.08-j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5}\right)}-j 3.1416 \times 10^{-5}\right] V_{2}=0.01 V_{m} \angle 0^{\circ}\)

 

Solving for \(V_{2}, we get, V_{2} = (-108.5 + j62.7) V_{m} V.\)

Therefore, gain at \(1 MHz = (-108.5 + j62.7) = -(108.5 – j62.7) = – 125.3∠-30°\). We express the gain this way since this is an inverting amplifier and the overall negative sign accounts for that. Thus, the output sine wave of amplifier lags by \(30°\) at \(1 MHz\) compared to the output when input is at low frequencies of few 100’s of \(Hz\).
