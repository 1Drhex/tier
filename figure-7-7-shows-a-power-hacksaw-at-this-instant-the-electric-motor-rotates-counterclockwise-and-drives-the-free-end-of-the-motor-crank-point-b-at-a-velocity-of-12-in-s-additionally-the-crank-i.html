Guest Question.

Figure 7.7 shows a power hacksaw. At this instant, the electric motor rotates counterclockwise and drives the free end of the motor crank (point B) at a velocity of 12 in./s. Additionally, the crank is accelerating at a rate of 37 \(rad/s^2\). The top portion of the hacksaw is moving toward the left with a velocity of 9.8 in./s and is accelerating at a rate of 82 \(in./s^2\). Determine the relative acceleration of point C with respect to point B. 1.Draw a Kinematic Diagram and Identify the Degrees of Freedom 2.Determine the Tangential Acceleration of Point B 3.Determine the Normal Acceleration of Point B 4.Specify the Acceleration of Point C 5.Construct the Velocity Polygon for the Acceleration of C Relative to B 6.Solve for the Unknown Vector Magnitudes.

Admin
Solution

1: Figure 7.8a shows the kinematic diagram of the power hacksaw. Notice that it is the familiar slider-crank mechanism with one degree of freedom.

2: From the kinematic diagram, it should be apparent that point B travels up and to the left as link 2 rotates counterclockwise. Because the motor crank (link 2) is in pure rotation, the components of the acceleration at the end of the link can be readily obtained. Equation \(a^t_A=\frac{dv_A}{dt}=\frac{d(\omega_2r_{OA} )}{dt}=r_{OA}\frac{d_{\omega 2}}{dt} =r_{OA}a _a\) can be used to determine the magnitude of the tangential acceleration.

\(a^{t}_{B} =r_{AB}a_2=(1.75\ in.)(37\ rad/s^2)=64.75\ in./s^2\)

Because the link accelerates, the direction of the vector is in the direction of the motion at the end of the link. Thus, the tangential acceleration is calculated as

\(A^{t}_{B} =64.75\ in./s^2\ 60^\circ \angle\)

3: Equation \(a^{n}_{A} =v_A\omega _2=v_A(\frac{v_A}{r_{OA}} )=\frac{v^2_A}{r_{OA}}\) can be used to determine the magnitude of the normal acceleration.

\(a^{n}_{B} =\frac{v^2_B}{r_{AB}}=\frac{(12\ in./s)^2}{1.75\ in.}=82.29\ in./s^2\)

Normal acceleration is always directed toward the center of rotation. Thus, normal acceleration is

\(a^n_B=82.29\ in./s^2\ 30^\circ \angle\)

Link 2 is isolated and the components of this acceleration are shown in Figure 7.8b.

4: Point C is constrained to linear motion. Therefore, point C does not experience a normal acceleration. The total acceleration is given in the problem statement as

\(A_C=82\ in./s^2\leftarrow\)

5: To determine the relative acceleration, equation \(A_A=A^n_A+\gt A^t_A\) can be written in terms of points B and C and rearranged as

\(A_{C/B}=A_C-\gt A_B\)

Because two acceleration components of point B exist, the equation is written as

\(A_{C/B}=A_C-\gt (A^n_B+\gt A^t_B)=A_C-\gt A^n_B-\gt A^t_B\)

A vector polygon is formed from this equation (Figure 7.8c). The unknown vector can be determined. Either a graphical or analytical solution can be used to determine vector \(A_{C/B}\).

6: Arbitrarily using an analytical method, the acceleration \(A_{C/B}\) can be found by separating the vectors into horizontal and vertical components. See Table 7.1.

TABLE 7.1 Horizontal and Vertical Vector Components for Acceleration \(A_{C/B}\)
Vector Reference Angle \((θ_x)\) Horizontal Component \(a_h = a\ cos\ θ_x\) Vertical Component \(a_v = a\ sin\ θ_x\)
\(A_c\) 180° – 82.00 0
\(A_B^n\) 210° -71.26 -41.15
\(A_B^t\) 120° -32.83 56.08

Separate algebraic equations can be written for the horizontal and vertical components as follows:

\(A_{C/B}=A_C-\gt A^n_B-\gt A^t_B\)

horizontal comp.: \( A^{h}_{C/B}=(-82.0)-(-71.27)-(-32.38)\)

\(=+21.35=21.35\ in./s^2\)

vertical comp.: \(A^{v}_{C/B}=(0)-(-41.15)-(+56.08)=-14.93\ in./s^2\)

The magnitude of the acceleration can be found by

\(A_{C/B}=\sqrt{(a^{h}_{C/B})^2+(a^{v}_{C/B})^2}\)

 

\(=\sqrt{(21.35)^2+(-14.93)^2}=26.05\ in./s^2\)

The direction of the vector can be determined by

\(\theta _x=tan^{-1}\left[\frac{a^{h}_{C/B}}{a^{v}_{C/B}} \right] =tan^{-1}\left[\frac{-14.93\ in./s^2}{21.35\ in./s^2} \right]=-35^\circ =35^\circ \angle\)

Finally, the relative acceleration of C with respect to B is

\(A_{C/B}=26.05\ in./s^2\ 35^\circ \angle\)
