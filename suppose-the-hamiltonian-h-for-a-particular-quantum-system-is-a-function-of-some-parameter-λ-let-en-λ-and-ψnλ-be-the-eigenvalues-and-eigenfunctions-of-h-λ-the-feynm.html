Guest Question.

Suppose the Hamiltonian H, for a particular quantum system, is a function of some parameter λ ; let \( E_{n}(\lambda) \text { and } \psi_{n}(\lambda) \) be the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of H (λ) . The Feynman–Hellmann theorem states that

\( \frac{\partial E_{n}}{\partial \lambda}=\left\langle\psi_{n}\left|\frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}\right| \psi_{n}\right\rangle \) (7.110).

(assuming either that \( E_n \) is nondegenerate, or—if degenerate—that the \( \psi_{n} s \) are the “good” linear combinations of the degenerate eigenfunctions).

(a) Prove the Feynman–Hellmann theorem. Hint: Use Equation 7.9.

\( E_{n}^{\prime}=\left\langle\psi_{n}^{0}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{n}^{0}\right\rangle \) (7.9).

(b) Apply it to the one-dimensional harmonic oscillator, (i) using \( \lambda=\omega \) (this yields a formula for the expectation value of , (ii) using \( \lambda=\hbar \) (this yields \( \langle T\rangle \), and (iii) using \( \lambda=m \) (this yields a relation between \( \langle T\rangle \) and \( \langle V\rangle \). Compare your answers to , and the virial theorem predictions ().

Solution

(a) Let the unperturbed Hamiltonian be \( H\left(\lambda_{0}\right) \), for some fixed value \( \lambda_{0} \). Now tweak \( \lambda \text { to } \lambda_{0}+d \lambda \). The perturbing Hamiltonian is \( H^{\prime}=H\left(\lambda_{0}+d \lambda\right)-H\left(\lambda_{0}\right)=(\partial H / \partial \lambda) d \lambda\left(\text { derivative evaluated at } \lambda_{0}\right) \).

The change in energy is given by Eq. 7.9:

\( E_{n}^{\prime}=\left\langle\psi_{n}^{0}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{n}^{0}\right\rangle \) (7.9).

\( d E_{n}=E_{n}^{1}=\left\langle\psi_{n}^{0}\left|H^{\prime}\right| \psi_{n}^{0}\right\rangle=\left\langle\psi_{n}\left|\frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}\right| \psi_{n}\right\rangle d \lambda\left(\text { all evaluated at } \lambda_{0}\right) ; \quad \text { so } \quad \frac{\partial E_{n}}{\partial \lambda}=\left\langle\psi_{n}\left|\frac{\partial H}{\partial \lambda}\right| \psi_{n}\right\rangle \).

[Note: Even though we used perturbation theory, the result is exact, since all we needed (to calculate the derivative) was the infinitesimal change in \( E_{n} \). ]

(b) \( E_{n}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega ; \quad H=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \frac{d^{2}}{d x^{2}}+\frac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x^{2} \).

(i)

\( \frac{\partial E_{n}}{\partial \omega}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar ; \quad \frac{\partial H}{\partial \omega}=m \omega x^{2} ; \quad \text { so } F – H \Rightarrow\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar=\left\langle n\left|m \omega x^{2}\right| n\right\rangle \). But

\( V=\frac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x^{2}, \quad \text { so } \quad\langle V\rangle=\left\langle n\left|\frac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x^{2}\right| n\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2} \omega\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar ; \quad\langle V\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega \).

(ii)

\( \frac{\partial E_{n}}{\partial \hbar}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \omega ; \quad \frac{\partial H}{\partial \hbar}=-\frac{\hbar}{m} \frac{d^{2}}{d x^{2}}=\frac{2}{\hbar}\left(-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \frac{d^{2}}{d x^{2}}\right)=\frac{2}{\hbar} T \);

\( \text { so } F – H \Rightarrow\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \omega=\frac{2}{\hbar}\langle n|T| n\rangle \), or \( \langle T\rangle=\frac{1}{2}\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar \omega \).

(iii)

\( \frac{\partial E_{n}}{\partial_{m}}=0 ; \quad \frac{\partial H}{\partial m}=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m^{2}} \frac{d^{2}}{d x^{2}}+\frac{1}{2} \omega^{2} x^{2}=-\frac{1}{m}\left(-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2 m} \frac{d^{2}}{d x^{2}}\right)+\frac{1}{m}\left(\frac{1}{2} m \omega^{2} x^{2}\right)=-\frac{1}{m} T+\frac{1}{m} V \).

\( \text { So } F – H \Rightarrow 0=-\frac{1}{m}\langle T\rangle+\frac{1}{m}\langle V\rangle \), or \( \langle T\rangle=\langle V\rangle \).
