Guest Question.

(a) Consider the p-n junction shown in Fig. 5.12. Derive the expression for the total depletion

width \(W=W_{p}+W_{n}\) in terms of \(V_{o}\), \(V\), \(N_{A}\) and \(N_{D}\) for the case when \(N_{A}\) is comparable with \(N_{D}\).

(b) Find an expression for the electric field at the junction

(c) Derive an expression for the transition capacitance of the junction .

(d) Using the result of Part (c), find an approximate expression for the transition capacitance of the

p‑n+ junction for which \(N_{D}\) >> \(N_{A}\).

(e) Calculate the transition capacitance for a silicon p‑n junction with

\(N_{A}=1.2\times 10^{15}cm^{-3}\) , \(N_{D}=1.5\times 10^{15} cm^{-3}\) , A = 0.001 cm2, reverse bias = – 2 V, \(\epsilon \)= 1.04 × 10–12 F/cm and \( n_{i}=1.5\times 10^{10} cm^{-3}\) .

Admin
Solution

(a) The Poission’s equation in this case can be given by

\(\frac{d^2V}{dx^2}= \begin{cases}\frac{eN_{A}}{\epsilon } ; & 0 \leq x \leq W_{p} \\ \frac{-eN_{D}}{\epsilon } ; & W_{p}\leq x\leq W =W_{p}+W_{n} \\ 0 ; & elsewhere \end{cases} \)

Let V1 (x) and V2 (x) be the potential functions in the depletion regions of p‑ and n‑sides respectively. The general expression of V1(x) in the region 0 ≤ x ≤ Wp of the p‑side can be given by

\(V_{1}(x)= \left\lgroup\frac{eN_{A}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup x^2 C_{1}x+C_{2}\)

where C1 and C2 are arbitrary constants. Using the boundary conditions V1(x) = 0 at x = 0 (i.e. reference potential) and the electric field \(\varepsilon _{1}(x)=\frac{dV_{1}(x)}{dx} =0\) at x = 0 in the above solution, we can get

\(V_{1}(x)= \left\lgroup\frac{eN_{A}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup x^2 \) for 0 ≤ x ≤ Wp

Similarly, the general expression of V2(x) for the region Wp ≤ x ≤ W in the n‑side can be given by

\(V_{2}(x)= \left\lgroup -\frac{eN_{D}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup x^2 +C^{′}_{1}{x}+C^{′}_{2} \)

where \(C^{′}_{1}\) and \(C^{′}_{2}\) are arbitrary constants to be determined from the boundary conditions as follows.

Since the electric field is continuous at x = Wp, the potential function must also be continuous at x = Wp. This implies that \(V_{2}(W_{p})=V_{1}(W_{p})\) . Further , \(\varepsilon _{2}(x)=-\frac{dV_{2}(x)}{dx}=0 \) at x=W . Now \(C^{′}_{1}\) and \(C^{′}_{2}\) can be determined by using the above boundary conditions in V2(x) and finally we can obtain

\(V_{2}(x)= \left\lgroup -\frac{eN_{D}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup x^2 +\left\lgroup\frac{eN_{D}W}{\epsilon } \right\rgroup x +\frac{e(N_{A}+N_{D})W^2_{p}}{2\epsilon }-\frac{eN_{D}W_{p}W}{\epsilon } \)

From Eq. (5.44), \(eN_{A}W_{p}=eN_{D}W_{n} \) , we can get

\(\frac{W_{p}}{W_{p}+W_{n}}=\frac{W_{P}}{W}=\frac{N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \)

Substituting \(W_{p}=\left\lgroup\frac{N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup W \) , V2(x) can be written as

\(V_{2}(x)= \left\lgroup -\frac{eN_{D}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup x^2 +\left\lgroup\frac{eN_{D}W}{\epsilon } \right\rgroup x -\frac{eN^2_{D}W}{2\epsilon (N_{A}+N_{D})} \)

Since V2(x=W) = VB = Vo – V we can write

\(V_{o}-V= \left\lgroup -\frac{eN_{D}}{2\epsilon } \right\rgroup W^2 +\left\lgroup\frac{eN_{D}W}{\epsilon } \right\rgroup W -\frac{eN^2_{D}W}{2\epsilon (N_{A}+N_{D})} \)

\(=\frac{e}{2\epsilon }\left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup W^2 \)

Hence the total width of the depletion region can be given by

\(W=\sqrt{\left\{\frac{2\epsilon }{e}\left\lgroup\frac{1}{N_{A}}+\frac{1}{N_{D}} \right\rgroup \right\} (V_{o}-V)} \)

which is the desired result.

(b) The electric field in the region 0 ≤ x ≤Wp is given by

\(\varepsilon _{1}(x)=-\frac{dV_{1}(x)}{dx}=-\left\lgroup\frac{eN_{A}}{\epsilon } \right\rgroup x \)

Similarly, the electric field in the Wp ≤ x ≤ W region is given as

\(\varepsilon _{2}(x)=-\frac{dV_{2}(x)}{dx}=-\frac{eN_{D}}{\epsilon } (W-x) \)

Since the electric field must be continuous at x = Wp , the electric field at the junction can be given by

\(\varepsilon _{junc}= \varepsilon _{1}(x=W_{p})=\varepsilon _{2}(x=W_{p})=-\frac{eN_{A}W_{p}}{\epsilon } =-\frac{e}{\epsilon }\left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup W \)

As the electric field is zero at x = 0 and x = W, hence \(\varepsilon _{junc}\) respresents the maximum electric field in the depletion region. The minus sign indicates that the electric field is in the – x direction. Substituting the expression for W in the above equation, the electric field at the junction can be given as

\(\varepsilon _{junc}= -\sqrt{\frac{2e}{\varepsilon }\left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup}(V_{o}-V) \)

Note that for a p+ – n junction where NA >> ND , the electric field at the junction can approximately be given by

\(\varepsilon _{junc}= -\sqrt{\frac{2e}{\varepsilon }\left\lgroup\frac{1}{1/N_{A}+1/N_{D}} \right\rgroup (V_{o}-V)}\approx -\sqrt{\frac{2eN_{D}}{\epsilon }(V_{o}-V) } \)

(c) Let the cross‑sectional area of the junction be A. Using (5.44) and the expression for W of part a, the magnitude of the total space‑charge on either side of the junction is given by

\(Q=eAN_{D}W_{n}=eAN_{A}W_{p}\)

\(=eA\left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup \sqrt{\left\{\frac{2\varepsilon }{e}\left\lgroup\frac{1}{N_{A}}+\frac{1}{N_{D}} \right\rgroup \right\} (V_{o}-V)} \)

\(=A \sqrt{\left\{2e\epsilon \left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup \right\} (V_{o}-V)} \)

Thus, the transition or space‑charge capacitance can be given by

\(C_{T}=\left|\frac{dQ}{dV} \right| =A\sqrt{\frac{e\epsilon }{2(V_{o}-V)} \left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup } =C_{To} \left\lgroup 1-\frac{V}{V_{o}} \right\rgroup ^{-\frac{1}{2} } \)

where

\(C_{T0}= A\sqrt{\frac{e\epsilon }{2V_{o}}\left\lgroup\frac{N_{A}N_{D}}{N_{A}+N_{D}} \right\rgroup } \)

is the transition capacitance under zero external bias condition.

(d) Since \(\frac{1}{N_{A}}\gt \gt \frac{1}{N_{D}} \) for \(N_{D}\gt \gt N_{A} \) , the transition capacitance \(C_{T} \) of Part (b) can be approximated for the p‑n+ junction as

\(C_{T}=\left|\frac{dQ}{dV} \right| =A\sqrt{\frac{e\epsilon }{2(V_{o}-V)} \left\lgroup\frac{1}{\frac{1}{N_{A}}+\frac{1}{N_{D}} } \right\rgroup } \)

\(= A\sqrt{\frac{e\epsilon N_{A}}{2(V_{o}-V)} }=C_{To}\left\lgroup1-\frac{V}{V_{o}} \right\rgroup^{-\frac{1}{2} } \)

where \(C_{T0}=A\sqrt{\frac{e\epsilon N_{A}}{2V_{o}} } \) is the zero‑bias capacitance of the junction.

(e) Using (5.8), \(E_{o}=kT 1n\frac{N_{D}N_{A}}{n^2_{i}} \) , the built‑in potential can be obtained as

\(V_{o}=(0.025)\times 1n\left\lgroup\frac{(1.2\times 10^{15})\times (1.5\times 10^{15})}{2.25\times 10^{20}} \right\rgroup =0.59 V \)

The capacitance under zero‑bias condition is given by

\(C_{TO}=(0.001)\sqrt{\frac{1.6\times 10^{-19}\times 1.04\times 10^{-12}}{2\times 0.59} \left\lgroup\frac{1.2\times 10^{15}\times 1.5\times 10^{15}}{1.2\times 10^{15}+ 1.5\times 10^{15}} \right\rgroup } \)

\(=9.71\times 10^{-12}F \)

Thus, the transition capacitance is given by

\(C_{T}=(9.71\times 10^{-12})\times \left\lgroup1-\frac{(-2)}{0.59} \right\rgroup ^{-\frac{1}{2} } \)

\(=4.63\times 10^{-12}F \)
