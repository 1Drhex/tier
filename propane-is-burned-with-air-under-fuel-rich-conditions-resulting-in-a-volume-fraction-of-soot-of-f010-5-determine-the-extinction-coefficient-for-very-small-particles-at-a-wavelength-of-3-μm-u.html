Guest Question.

Propane is burned with air under fuel-rich conditions, resulting in a volume fraction of soot of \(f_{0} =10^{-5}\) . Determine the extinction coefficient for very small particles at a wavelength of 3 μm using the refractive index data of (i) Lee and Tien, (ii) StuU and Plass, (iii) Dalzell and Sarofim, (iv) Chang and Charalampopoulos, (v) Felske and coworkers. If the soot consisted of long fractal aggregates with 100 soot particles each (a = 50 nm), how would the extinction coefficient change?

Admin
Solution

To determine the extinction coefficient for small spherical soot particles we use equation (11.116) \(\kappa _{\lambda }=\beta _{\lambda }=\Im \left\{\frac{m^{2}-1}{m^{2}+2} \right\} \frac{6\pi f_{0}}{\lambda }=\frac{36\pi nk}{(n^{2}-k^{2}+2)^{2}+4n^{2}k^{2}}\frac{f_{0}}{\lambda }\) together with optical property data from Fig. 11-25:

Lee and Tien: n = 2.21, k= 1.23 , \(\kappa _{\lambda }=0.1754 cm^{-1};\)

Stull and Plass: n = 2.63, k= 1.95 , \(\kappa _{\lambda }= 0.1472 cm^{-1};\)
Dalzell and Sarofim: n = 2.19, k= 1.30, \(\kappa _{\lambda }=1.835 cm^{-1};\)
Chang and Charalampopoulos n = 1.89, k= 0.92 , \(\kappa _{\lambda }=0.1904 cm^{-1};\)
Felske and coworkers: n = 2.31, k= 0.71 , \(\kappa _{\lambda }=0.1077 cm^{-1}\).

Thus, the values found from the data of Lee and Tien, Dalzell and Sarofim, and Chang and Charalampopoulos are fairly consistent, while the absorptive index based on Stull and Plass’ data is considerably higher, probably because amorphous carbon simply does not represent soot well. The absorptive index based on data of Felske and coworkers is by far the lowest. The extinction coefficient for soot aggregates can be estimated from equation (11.123) \(\left(\frac{C^{a}_{sca}}{NC^{p}_{sca}} \right) _{\infty }=\frac{k_{f}}{(4x)^{D_{f}}}\left[\frac{3}{2-D_{f}}-\frac{12}{(6-D_{f})(4-D_{f})} \right]\) . Reworking , we find \(x=2\pi \times 0.05/3=0.1048\) , \(Q_{abs}^{p}=1.17\times 10^{-1}, and Q_{sca}^{p}= 1.94\times 10^{-4}\). For an aggregate, the absorption coefficient remains unchanged, while the scattering coefficient becomes (using the fractal parameters of Fig. 11-26)

\(\sigma _{s\lambda }=Q^{a}_{sca}\pi a^{2}(N_{T}/N)=(C^{a}_{sca}/NC^{p}_{sca})Q_{sca}^{p}\pi a^{2}N_{T}= (C^{a}_{sca}/NC^{p}_{sca})Q_{sca}^{p}\frac{3f_{0}}{4a}\)

\(\left(\frac{C^{a}_{sca}}{NC^{p}_{sca}} \right) _{\infty }=\frac{6.5}{(4\times 0.1048)^{1.8}}\left[\frac{3}{0.2}-\frac{12}{ 4.2\times 2.2} \right] =425.9\)

\(\frac{C^{a}_{sca}}{NC^{p}_{sca}}=4.25^{1-100^{-1/4}}=62.8\)

and

\(\sigma _{s\lambda }\simeq 62.8\times 1.94\times 10^{-4}\times \frac{3\times 10^{-6}}{4\times 5\times 10^{-6}cm}\simeq 1.83\times 10^{-3}cm^{-1}\)

Adding together we find \(\beta _{\lambda }=\kappa _{\lambda }+\sigma _{s\lambda }=0.1754+.0018=0.1772 cm^{-1}\) , i.e., while scattering from aggregates is 63 times larger than that from individual particles, and may not be negligible (depending on the physical size of the soot cloud), its impact on the extinction coefficient is rather small.
