FINDING THE OPERATING POINT FOR A PUMPING SYSTEM

The pump of , operating at 1750 rpm, is used to pump water through the pipe system of Fig. 10.18a. Develop an algebraic expression for the general shape of the system resistance curve. Calculate and plot the system resistance curve. Solve graphically for the system operating point. Obtain an approximate analytical expression for the system resistance curve. Solve analytically for the system operating point.

Admin
Solution

Given: Pump of , operating at 1750 rpm, with \(H = H_0 – AQ^2\), where \(H_0 = 55.9\) ft and \(A = 3.44 × 10^{-5}\) ft/(gpm)\(^2\). System of Fig. 10.18a, where \(L_1 = 2\) ft of \(D_1 = 10\) in. pipe and \(L_2 = 3000\) ft of \(D_2 = 8\) in. pipe, conveying water between two large reservoirs whose surfaces are at the same level.
Find:

(a) A general algebraic expression for the system head curve.
(b) The system head curve by direct calculation.
(c) The system operating point using a graphical solution.
(d) An approximate analytical expression for the system head curve.
(e) The system operating point using the analytical expression of part (d).

Solution:
Apply the energy equation to the flow system of Fig. 10.18a.
Governing equation:

\(\frac{p_{0}}{\rho g}+\alpha_{0} \frac{\bar{V}_{0}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{0}+H_{a}=\frac{p_{3}}{\rho g}+\alpha_{3} \frac{\bar{V}_{3}^{2}}{2 g}+z_{3}+\frac{h_{l_{T}}}{g}\) (10.24b)

where \(z_{0}\) and \(z_{3}\) are the surface elevations of the supply and discharge reservoirs, respectively.

Assumptions:
(1) \(\underline{p_{0}}=\underline{p_{3}}=p_{\mathrm{atm}}\).
(2) \(\bar{V}_{0}=\bar{V}_{3}=0\).
(3) \(z_{0}=z_{3}\) (given).
Simplifying, we obtain

\(H_{a}=\frac{h_{l_{r}}}{g}=\frac{h_{l T_{0_{1}}}}{g}+\frac{h_{l T_{23}}}{g}=H_{l_{T}}\)

where sections (1) and (2) are located just upstream and downstream from the pump, respectively.
The total head losses are the sum of the major and minor losses, so

\(\begin{aligned}&h_{l T_{0_{1}}}=K_{\mathrm{ent}} \frac{\bar{V}_{1}^{2}}{2}+f_{1} \frac{L_{1}}{D_{1}} \frac{\bar{V}_{1}^{2}}{2}=\left(K_{\mathrm{ent}}+f_{1} \frac{L_{1}}{D_{1}}\right) \frac{\bar{V}_{1}^{2}}{2} \\\quad\\&h_{l T_{23}}=f_{2} \frac{L_{2}}{D_{2}} \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2}+K_{\mathrm{exit}} \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2}=\left(f_{2} \frac{L_{2}}{D_{2}}+K_{\mathrm{exit}}\right) \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2}\end{aligned}\)

From continuity, \(\bar{V}_{1} A_{1}=\bar{V}_{2} A_{2}\), so \(\bar{V}_{1}=\bar{V}_{2} \frac{A_{2}}{A_{1}}=\bar{V}_{2}\left(\frac{D_{2}}{D_{1}}\right)^{2} .\)

 

Hence

\(H_{l_{T}}=\frac{h_{l_{T}}}{g}=\left(K_{\mathrm{ent}}+f_{1} \frac{L_{1}}{D_{1}}\right) \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2 g}\left(\frac{D_{2}}{D_{1}}\right)^{4}+\left(f_{2} \frac{L_{2}}{D_{2}}+K_{\mathrm{exit}}\right) \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2 g}\)

or, upon simplifying,

\(H_{l_{T}}=\left[\left(K_{\text {ent }}+f_{1} \frac{L_{1}}{D_{1}}\right)\left(\frac{D_{2}}{D_{1}}\right)^{4}+f_{2} \frac{L_{2}}{D_{2}}+K_{\text {exit }}\right] \frac{\bar{V}_{2}^{2}}{2 g} \quad\quad \xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad H_{l_{T}}}\)

This is the head loss equation for the system. At the operating point, as indicated in Eq. 1 , the head loss is equal to the head produced by the pump, given by

\(H_{a}=H_{0}-A Q^{2}\) (2)

where \(H_{0}=55.9 \mathrm{ft}\) and \(A=3.44 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{ft} /(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}\).

The pump curve and the system resistance curve are plotted below:

The graphical solution is shown on the plot. At the operating point, \(H\) ≈ 36 ft and \(Q\) ≈ 750 gpm.
We can obtain more accuracy from the graphical solution using the following approach: Because the Reynolds number corresponds to the fully turbulent regime, \(f\) ≈ const., we can simplify the equation for the head loss and write it in the form

\(H_{l_T}\approx CQ^2\) (3)

 

where \(C=8/\pi^2D^4_2g\) g times the term in square brackets in the expression for \(H_{l_T}\) . We can obtain a value for \(C\) directly from Eq. 3 by using values for \(H_{l_T}\) and \(Q\) from the table at a point close to the anticipated operating point. For example, from the \(Q = 700\) gpm data point,

 

\(C=\frac{H_{l_{r}}}{Q^{2}}=\frac{30.6 \mathrm{ft}}{700^{2}(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}}=6.24 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{ft} /(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}\)

Hence, the approximate analytical expression for the system head curve is

\(H_{l_{T}}=6.24 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{ft} /(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}[Q(\mathrm{gpm})]^{2}\quad\quad\xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad H_{l_{T}}}\)

Using Eqs. 2 and 3 in Eq. 1, we obtain

\(H_{0}-A Q^{2}=C Q^{2}\)

Solving for \(Q\), the volume flow rate at the operating point, gives

\(Q=\left[\frac{H_{0}}{A+C}\right]^{1 / 2}\)
For this case,

\(Q=\left[55.9 \mathrm{ft} \times \frac{(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}}{\left(3.44 \times 10^{-5}+6.24 \times 10^{-5}\right) \mathrm{ft}}\right]^{1 / 2}=760 \mathrm{gpm}\quad\quad \xleftarrow[]{\quad\quad\quad\quad Q}\)

The volume flow rate may be substituted into either expression for head to calculate the head at the operating point as

 

\(H=C Q^{2}=6.24 \times 10^{-5} \frac{\mathrm{ft}}{(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}} \times(760)^{2}(\mathrm{gpm})^{2}=36.0 \mathrm{ft} \quad\quad\xleftarrow[] {\quad\quad\quad\quad H}\)

 

We can see that in this problem our reading of the operating point from the graph was pretty good: The reading of head was in agreement with the calculated head; the reading of flow rate was less than 2 percent different from the calculated result.
Note that both sets of results are approximate. We can get a more accurate, and easier, result by using Excel’s Solver or Goal Seek to find the operating point, allowing for the fact that the friction factors vary, however slightly, with Reynolds number. Doing so yields an operating point flow rate of 761 gpm and head of 36.0 ft.

 

This problem illustrates the procedures used to find the operating point of a pump and flow system.

  • The approximate methods—graphical, and assuming friction losses are proportional to \(Q^2\)—yielded results close to the detailed computation using Excel. We conclude that since most pipe flow friction coefficients are accurate to only about ±10 percent anyway, the approximate methods are accurate enough. On the other hand, use of Excel, when available, is easier as well as being more accurate.
  • Equation 3, for the head loss in the system, must be replaced with an equation of the form \(H = Z_0 + CQ^2\) when the head H required by the system has a component \(Z_0\) due to gravity as well as a component due to head losses.

The Excel workbook for this Example was used to generate the tabulated results as well as the most accurate solution. It can be adapted for use with other pump-pipe systems.
