Guest Question.

Suppose that the network in Fig. 3.4 has the following parameters: \(I_{A}\) = 1 mA, \(R_{1}\) = 12 kΩ, \(R_{2}\) = 6 kΩ, \(I_{B}\)= 4 mA, and \(R_{3}\) = 6 kΩ. Let us determine all node voltages and branch currents.

Admin
Solution

For purposes of illustration we will solve this problem using Gaussian elimination, matrix analysis, and MATLAB.
Using the parameter values Eq. (3.2 : \(-G_{2}v_{1} + (G_{2} + G_{3})v_{2} = -I_{B}\)) becomes

\(V_{1}\left[\frac{1}{12k}+ \frac{1}{6k} \right]- V_{2} \left[\frac{1}{6k} \right] = 1\times 10^{-6}\)

 

\(-V_{1}\left[ \frac{1}{6k} \right]+ V_{2} \left[\frac{1}{6k} + \frac{1}{6k} \right] = -4\times 10^{-3}\)

where we employ capital letters because the voltages are constant. The equations can be written as

\(\frac{V_{1}}{4k}- \frac{V_{2}}{6k}= 1\times 10^{-3}\)

 

\(-\frac{V_{1}}{6k}+ \frac{V_{2}}{3k}= -4\times 10^{-3}\)

Using Gaussian elimination, we solve the first equation for \(V_{1}\) in terms of \(V_{2}\) :

\(V_{1}= V_{2}\left(\frac{2}{3} \right)+ 4\)

This value is then substituted into the second equation to yield

\(\frac{-1}{6k} \left(\frac{2}{3}V_{2}+4 \right)+ \frac{V_{2}}{3k}= -4\times 10^{-3}\)

or

\(V_{2}\)= -15 V

This value for \(V_{2}\) is now substituted back into the equation for \(V_{1}\) in terms of \( V_{2}\) , which yields

\(V_{1}= \frac{2}{3}V_{2}+ 4\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ = -6 \ V\)

The circuit equations can also be solved using matrix analysis. The general form of the matrix equation is
GV = I

where in this case

\(G=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4k} & -\frac{1}{6k} \\ -\frac{1}{6k} & \frac{1}{3k} \end{bmatrix} , V= \left[\begin{matrix} V_{1} \\ V_{2} \end{matrix} \right] , and \ I= \left[\begin{matrix} 1\times 10^{-3} \\ -4 \times 10^{-3} \end{matrix} \right]\)

The solution to the matrix equation is

V=\(G^{-1}I\)

and therefore,

\(\left[\begin{matrix} V_{1} \\ V_{2} \end{matrix} \right] = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4k} & \frac{-1}{6k} \\ \frac{-1}{6k} & \frac{1}{3k} \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \left[\begin{matrix} 1\times 10^{-3} \\ -4 \times 10^{-3} \end{matrix} \right]\)

To calculate the inverse of G, we need the adjoint and the determinant. The adjoint is

Adj G = \(\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3k} & \frac{1}{6k} \\ \frac{1}{6k} & \frac{1}{4k} \end{bmatrix}\)

and the determinant is

\(\left|G\right| = \left(\frac{1}{3k} \right)\left(\frac{1}{4k} \right)- \left(\frac{-1}{6k} \right)\left(\frac{1}{6k} \right) \)

 

\(\ \ \ \ \ = \frac{1}{18k^{2}}\)

Therefore,

\(\left[\begin{matrix} V_{1} \\ V_{2} \end{matrix} \right]= 18k^{2}\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3k} & \frac{1}{6k} \\ \frac{1}{6k} & \frac{1}{4k} \end{bmatrix} \left[\begin{matrix} 1\times 10^{-3} \\ -4 \times 10^{-3} \end{matrix} \right]\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ = 18k^{2}\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{3k^{2}} & \frac{4}{6k^{2}} \\ \frac{1}{6k^{2}} & \frac{1}{k^{2}} \end{bmatrix}\)

 

\(\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ =\left[\begin{matrix} -6 \\ -15 \end{matrix} \right] \)

The MATLAB solution begins with the set of equations expressed in matrix form as
G*V=I
where the symbol * denotes the multiplication of the voltage vector V by the coefficient matrix G. Then once the MATLAB software is loaded into the PC, the coefficient matrix (G) and the vector V can be expressed in MATLAB notation by typing in the rows of the matrix or vector at the prompt >>. Use semicolons to separate rows and spaces to separate columns. Brackets are used to denote vectors or matrices. When the matrix G and the vector I have been defined, then the solution equation
V=inv(G)*I

which is also typed in at the prompt>>, will yield the unknown vector V. The matrix equation for our circuit expressed in decimal notation is

\(\begin{bmatrix} 0.00025 & -0.0001666 \\ -0.00016666 & 0.0003333 \end{bmatrix} \left[\begin{matrix} 0.001 \\ -0.004 \end{matrix} \right]\)

If we now input the coefficient matrix G, then the vector I and finally the equation V = inv(G)*I, the computer screen containing these data and the solution vector V appears as follows:

\(>>G= [0.00025 -0.000166666;-0.000166666 0.00033333]\)

 

\(G=1 .0e-003 *\)

 

\(\begin{matrix} 0.2500 & -0.1667 \\ -0.1667 & 0.3333 \end{matrix}\)

 

\(>>I=[0.001 ; -0.004] \)

I=

\(\begin{matrix} 0.0010 \\ -0.0040 \end{matrix}\)

 

\(>>v=inv(G)*I\)

V=

\(\begin{matrix} -6.001 \\ -15.0002 \end{matrix}\)

Knowing the node voltages, we can determine all the currents using Ohm’s law:

\(I_{1}= \frac{V_{1}}{R_{1}}= \frac{-6}{12k}=-\frac{1}{2}mA\)

 

\(I_{2}= \frac{V_{1}-V_{2}}{6k}= \frac{-6-(-15)}{6k}= \frac{3}{2}mA\)

and

\(I_{3}= \frac{V_{2}}{6k}= \frac{-15}{6k}=-\frac{5}{2}mA\)

Figure 3.5 illustrates the results of all the calculations. Note that KCL is satisfied at every node.
