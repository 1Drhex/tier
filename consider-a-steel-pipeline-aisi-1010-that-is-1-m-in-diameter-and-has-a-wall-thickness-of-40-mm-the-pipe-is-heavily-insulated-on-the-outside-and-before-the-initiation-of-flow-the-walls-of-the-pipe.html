Guest Question.

Consider a steel pipeline (AISI 1010) that is 1 m in diameter and has a wall thickness of 40 mm. The pipe is heavily insulated on the outside, and, before the initiation of flow, the walls of the pipe are at a uniform temperature of \(−20^{\circ}C\). With the initiation of flow, hot oil at \(60^{\circ}C\) is pumped through the pipe, creating a convective condition corresponding to \(h=500 W/m^{2}\cdot K\) at the inner surface of the pipe.

1. What are the values of the Biot number and the Fourier number 8 min after the initiation of flow?

2. At t = 8 min, what is the temperature of the exterior pipe surface covered by the insulation?

3. What is the heat flux \({q}''(W/m^{2})\) to the pipe from the oil at t = 8 min?

4. How much energy per meter of pipe length has been transferred from the oil to the pipe at t = 8 min?

Admin
Solution

Known: Wall subjected to sudden change in convective surface condition.

Find:
1. Biot number and Fourier number after 8 min.
2. Temperature of exterior pipe surface after 8 min.
3. Heat flux to the wall at 8 min.
4. Energy transferred to pipe per unit length after 8 min.

Assumptions:
1. Pipe wall can be approximated as plane wall, since thickness is much less than diameter.
2. Constant properties.
3. Outer surface of pipe is adiabatic.

Properties: , steel type AISI 1010

 

\([T=(-20+60)^{\circ}C/2\approx 300K]:\rho =7832 kg/m^{3}, c=434 J/kg\cdot K, k=63.9 W/m\cdot K, \alpha =18.8\times 10^{-6}m^{2}/s\).

 

Analysis:
1. At t = 8 min, the Biot and Fourier numbers are computed from Equations 5.10 and 5.12, respectively, with \(L_{c} = L\). Hence

 

\(Bi=\frac{hL_{c}}{k}< 0.1\) (5.10)

 

\(Fo\equiv \frac{\alpha t}{L_{c}^{2}}\) (5.12)

 

\(Bi=\frac{hL}{k}=\frac{500 W/m^{2}\cdot K\times 0.04 m}{63.9 W/m\cdot K}=0.313\)

 

\(Fo=\frac{\alpha t}{L^{2}}=\frac{18.8\times 10^{-6}m^{2}/s\times 8 min\times 60 s/min}{(0.04m)^{2}}=5.64\)

 

2. With Bi = 0.313, use of the lumped capacitance method is inappropriate. However, since Fo > 0.2 and transient conditions in the insulated pipe wall of thickness L correspond to those in a plane wall of thickness 2L experiencing the same surface condition, the desired results may be obtained from the one-term approximation for a plane wall. The midplane temperature can be determined from Equation 5.44

 

\(\theta _{o}^{\ast }=C_{1}\exp (-\zeta _{1}^{2}Fo)\) (5.44)

 

\(\theta _{o}^{\ast }=\frac{T_{O}-T_{\infty }}{T_{i}-T_{\infty }}=C_{1}\exp (-\zeta _{1}^{2}Fo)\)

 

where, with Bi = 0.313,

\(C_{1}=1.047\) and \(\zeta _{1}=0.531\)

 

rad from . With Fo = 5.64,

\(\theta _{o}^{\ast }=1.047\exp [-(0.531 rad)^{2}\times 5.64]=0.214\)

 

Hence after 8 min, the temperature of the exterior pipe surface, which corresponds to the midplane temperature of the plane wall solution, is

 

\(T(0, 8 min)=T_{\infty }+\theta _{o}^{\ast }(T_{i}-T_{\infty })=60^{\circ}C+0.214(-20-60)^{\circ}C=42.9^{\circ}C\)

 

3. Heat transfer to the inner surface at x = L is by convection, and at any time t the heat flux may be obtained from Newton’s law of cooling. Hence at t = 480 s,

 

\(q_{x}″(L, 480 s)\equiv q_{L}″=h[T(L, 480 s)-T_{\infty }]\)

 

Using the one-term approximation for the surface temperature, Equation 5.43b with x* = 1 has the form

 

\(\theta ^{\ast }=\theta _{o}^{\ast } \cos (\zeta _{1}x^{\ast })\) (5.43b)

 

\(\theta ^{\ast }=\theta _{o}^{\ast }\cos (\zeta _{1})\)

 

\(T(L, t)=T_{\infty }+(T_{i}-T_{\infty })\theta _{o}^{\ast }\cos (\zeta _{1})\)

 

\(T(L, 8min)=60^{\circ}C+(-20-60)^{\circ}C\times 0.214\times \cos (0.531 rad)\)

 

\(T(L, 8min)=45.2^{\circ}C\)

 

The heat flux at t = 8 min is then

 

\(q_{L}″=500W/m^{2}\cdot K(45.2-60)^{\circ}C=-7400W/m^{2}\)

 

4. The energy transfer to the pipe wall over the 8-min interval may be obtained from Equations 5.47 and 5.49. With

 

\(Q_{o}=\rho cV(T_{i}-T_{\infty })\) (5.47)

 

\(\frac{Q}{Q_{o}}=1-\frac{\sin \zeta _{1}}{\zeta _{1}}\theta _{o}^{\ast }\) (5.49)

 

\(\frac{Q}{Q_{o}}=1-\frac{\sin (0.531 rad )}{0.531 rad}\times 0.214=0.80\)

 

it follows that

 

\(Q=0.80\rho cV(T_{i}-T_{\infty })\)

 

or with a volume per unit pipe length of \({V}'=\pi DL,\)

 

\({Q}'=0.80\rho c\pi DL(T_{i}-T_{\infty })\)

 

\({Q}'=0.80\times 7832kg/m^{3}\times 434J/kg\cdot K\)

 

\(\times \pi \times 1 m\times 0.04 m(-20-60)^{\circ}C\)

 

\({Q}'=-2.73\times 10^{7}J/m\)

 

Comments:
1. The minus sign associated with \({q}''\) and \({Q}'\) simply implies that the direction of heat transfer is from the oil to the pipe (into the pipe wall).

2. The solution for this example is provided as a ready-to-solve model in the Advanced section of IHT, which uses the Models, Transient Conduction, Plane Wall option. Since the IHT model uses a multiple-term approximation to the series solution, the results are more accurate than those obtained from the foregoing one-term approximation. IHT Models for Transient Conduction are also provided for the radial systems treated in Section 5.6.
