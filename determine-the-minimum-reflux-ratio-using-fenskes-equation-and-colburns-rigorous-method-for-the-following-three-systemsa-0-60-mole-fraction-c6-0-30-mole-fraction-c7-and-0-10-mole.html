Guest Question.

Determine the minimum reflux ratio using Fenske’s equation and Colburn’s rigorous method for the following three systems:
(a) 0.60 mole fraction \(C_{6}\), 0.30 mole fraction \(C_{7}\), and 0.10 mole fraction \(C_{8}\) to give a product of 0.99 mole fraction \(C_{6}\).
(b) Components

Mole fraction Relative volatility, α \(x_{d}\)
A 0.3 2 1
B 0.3 1 __
C 0.4 0.5 __

(c) Components

A 0.25 2 1
B 0.25 1 __
C 0.25 0.5 __
D 0.25 0.25 __
Admin
Solution

(a) Under conditions where the relative volatility remains constant, Underwood developed the following equations from which \(R_{m}\) may be calculated:

\(\mathbf{\frac{\alpha _{A}x_{fA}}{\alpha _{A}-\theta }+\frac{\alpha _{B}x_{fB}}{\alpha _{B}-\theta }+\frac{\alpha _{C}x_{fC}}{\alpha _{C}-\theta }+}\cdots =1-q\) (equation 11.114)

and \(\mathbf{\frac{\alpha _{A}x_{dA}}{\alpha _{A}-\theta }+\frac{\alpha _{B}x_{dB}}{\alpha _{B}-\theta }+\frac{\alpha _{C}x_{dC}}{\alpha _{C}-\theta }+}\cdots =R_{m}+1\) (equation 11.115)

where \(x_{fA}\), \(x_{fB}\), \(x_{dA}\), \(x_{dB}\), and so on, are the mole fractions of components A and B, and so on, in the feed and distillate with A the light key and B the heavy key.
\(\alpha _{A}\), \(\alpha _{B}\), \(\alpha _{C}\), etc., are the volatilities with respect to the least volatile component.
\(\theta \) is the root of equation 11.114 and lies between the values of \(\alpha _{A}\) and \(\alpha _{B}\). Thus \(\theta \) may be calculated from equation 11.114 and substituted into 11.115 to give \(R_{m}\).

(b) Colburn’s method allows the value of \(R_{m}\) to be calculated from approximate values of the pinch compositions of the key components. This value may then be checked again empirical relationships .
The method is long and tedious and only the first approximation will be worked here.

\(R_{m}=\mathbf{\frac{1}{\alpha _{AB}-1}\left [ \left ( \frac{x_{dA}}{x_{nA}} \right )-\alpha _{AB} \left ( \frac{x_{dB}}{x_{nB}} \right )\right ]}\) (equation 11.108)

where \(x_{dA}\) and \(x_{nA}\) are the top and pinch compositions of the light key component, \(x_{dB}\) and \(x_{nB}\) are the top and pinch compositions of the heavy key component, and \(\alpha _{AB}\) is the volatility of the light key relative to the heavy key component.
The difficulty in using this equation is that the values of \(x _{nA}\) and \(x _{nB}\) are known only for special cases where the pinch coincides with the feed composition. Colburn has suggested that an approximate value for \(x _{nA}\) is given by:

\(x _{nA}(approx.)=\mathbf{\frac{r_{f}}{(1+r_{f})(1+\Sigma \alpha x_{fh})}}\) (equation 11.109)

and: \(x _{nB}(approx.)=\mathbf{\frac{x_{nA}}{r_{f}}}\) (equation 11.110)

where \(r_{f}\) is the estimated ratio of the key components on the feed plate.

For an all liquid feed at its boiling-point, \(r_{f}\) is equal to the ratio of the key components in the feed. Otherwise \(r_{f}\) is the ratio of the key components in the liquid part of the feed, \(x_{fh}\) is the mole fraction of each component in the liquid portion of feed heavier than the heavy key, and \(\alpha \) is the volatility of the component relative to the heavy key.

(a) Relative volatility data are required and it will be assumed that \(\alpha _{6,8}\) = 5, \(\alpha _{6,7}\) = 2.5, and also that \(x_{d7}\) = 0.01 and that q = 1.
Substituting in Underwood’s equation gives:

\(\boldsymbol{\left ( \frac{5\times 0.60}{5-\theta } \right )+\left ( \frac{2.5\times 0.30}{2.5-\theta } \right )+\left ( \frac{0.1\times 1}{1-\theta } \right )=1-q=0}\)

from which by trial and error, \(\theta =3.1\) = 3.1 .

Then: \(\mathbf{\left ( \frac{5\times 0.99}{5-3.1} \right )+\left (\frac{2.5\times 0.01}{2.5-3.1} \right )=R_{m}+1}\) and \(R_{m}=1.57\)

In Colburn’s equation, using \(C_{6}\) and \(C_{7}\) as the light and heavy keys respectively:

\(r_{f}\)= (0.6/0.3) = 2.0

\(\sum \alpha x_{fh}\)== (1/2.5) × 0.1 = 0.04, \(x_{nA}\) = 2/(3 × 1.04) = 0.641

\(\alpha _{AB}\)=(5.0/2.5) = 2.0, \(x_{nB}\)=(0.641/2) = 0.32

\(R_{m}=\mathbf{\frac{1}{1}\left [ \left ( \frac{0.99}{0.641} \right )-\left ( \frac{2\times 0.01}{0.32} \right )\right ]}\) and \(R_{m}=1.48\)

 

(b) The light key = A, the heavy key = B, and \(\alpha _{A}\) = 4, \(\alpha _{B}\) = 2, \(\alpha _{C}\) = 1, and if q is assumed to equal 1, then substitution into Underwood’s equations gives \(\theta \) = 2.8 and \(R_{m}\)=2.33 .

In Colburn’s method, \(x_{dB}\) = 0 and \(r_{f}\) = (0.3/0.3) = 1.0 .

\(\sum \alpha n_{fh}\)=(1/2) × 0.4 = 0.2

\(x_{nA}\)=1/(2 × 1.2) = 0.417

\(\alpha _{AB}\)=(4/2) = 2.0 and \(R_{m}\)=2.40

(c) In this case, \(\alpha _{A}\) = 8, \(\alpha _{B}\) = 4, \(\alpha _{C}\) = 2, \(\alpha _{D}\) = 1. If q = 1, then \(\theta \) is found from Underwood’s equation to be equal to 5.6 and \(R_{m}\) = 2.33.
In Colburn’s method, \(x_{dB}\) = 0 and \(r_{f}\) = 1.0, the light key = A, the heavy key = B.

\(\sum \alpha x_{fh}\)= (0.5 × 0.25) + (0.25 × 0.25) = 0.188

\(x_{nA}\)=1/(2 × 1.188) = 0.421

\(\alpha _{AB}\)=2 and \(R_{m}\)=2.38

In all cases, good agreement is shown between the two methods.
