Guest Question.

A helium atom (m = 6.6465 \(\times 10^{-27}\) kg) moving at a speed of \(\nu_{He}\) = 1.518 \(\times 10^{6}\) m/s collides with an atom of nitrogen (m = 2.3253 \(\times 10^{-26}\) kg) at rest. After the collision, the helium atom is found to be moving with a velocity of \(\nu'_{He}\) = 1.199 \(\times 10^{6}\) m/s at an angle of \(θ_{He} = 78.75°\) relative to the direction of the original motion of the helium atom. (a) Find the velocity (magnitude and direction) of the nitrogen atom after the collision. (b) Compare the kinetic energy before the collision with the total kinetic energy of the atoms after the collision.

Admin
Solution

(a) The law of conservation of momentum for this collision can be written in vector form as \(\overrightarrow{P} _{initial}=\overrightarrow{P} _{final}\),which is equivalent to

\(P _{x,initial}=P _{x,final}\) and \(P _{y,initial}=P _{y,final}\)

The collision is shown in Figure 1.1. The initial values of the total momentum are, choosing the x axis to be the direction of the initial motion of the helium atom,

\(P _{x,initial}=m_{He}\nu_{He}\) and \(P _{y,initial}=0\)

The final total momentum can be written

\( P_{x,final}=m_{He}\nu^{′}_{He}\cos \theta _{He}+m_{N}\nu^{′}_{N}\cos \theta _{N}\)

 

\(P_{y,final}=m_{He}\nu^{′}_{He}\sin \theta _{He}+m_{N}\nu^{′}_{N}\sin \theta _{N}\)

The expression for \(P_{y,final}\) is written in general form with a + sign even though we expect that \(\theta _{He}\) and \(\theta _{N}\) are on opposite sides of the x axis. If the equation is written in this way, \(\theta _{N}\) will come out to be negative. The law of conservation of momentum gives, for the x components, \( m_{He}\nu_{He}=m_{He}\nu^{′}_{He}\cos \theta _{He}+m_{N}\nu^{′}_{N}\cos \theta _{N} \) and for the y components, \( 0=m_{He}\nu^{′}_{He}\sin \theta _{He}+m_{N}\nu^{′}_{N}\sin \theta _{N}.\) Solving for the unknown terms, we find

\( \nu^{′}_{N} \cos \theta _{N}= \frac{m_{He}(\nu_{He}-\nu^{′}_{He}\cos \theta _{He})}{m_{N}} \)

 

\(={(6.6465 \times 10^{-27} kg)[1.518 \times 10^{6} m/s – (1.199 \times 10^{6} m/s )(\cos 78.75°)]}\times (2.3253 \times 10^{-26} kg)^{-1}\)

 

\(= 3.6704 \times 10^{5} m/s\)

 

\(\nu^{′}_{N}\sin \theta _{N} = -\frac{m_{He}\nu^{′}_{He}\sin \theta _{He}}{m_{N}}\)

 

\(= -(6.6465 \times 10^{-27} kg)(1.199 \times 10^{6} m/s )\times (\sin 78.75°)(2.3253 \times 10^{-26} kg)^{-1}\)

 

\(= -3.3613 \times 10^{5} m/s\)

We can now solve for \( \nu^{′}_{N}\) and \( \theta _{N}\)

\( \nu^{′}_{N}=\sqrt{(\nu^{′}_{N} \sin \theta _{N})^{2}+(\nu^{′}_{N}\cos \theta _{N})^{2}}\)

 

\( = \sqrt{(-3.3613 \times 10^{5} m/s)^{2}+(3.6704 \times 10^{5} m/s)^{2}}\)

 

\( =4.977 \times 10^{5} m/s \)

 

\(\Theta _{N}=\tan ^{-1}\frac{\nu^{′}_{N}\sin \theta _{N}}{\nu^{′}_{N}\cos \theta _{N}}\)

 

\(= \tan ^{-1}(\frac{−3.3613 \times 10^{5} m/s}{3.6704 \times 10^{5} m/s} ) = -42.48° \)

(b) The initial kinetic energy is

\( K_{initial}=\frac{1}{2} m_{He}\nu^{2}_{He} \)
\( =\frac{1}{2}(6.6465\times 10^{-27} kg)(1.518 \times 10^{6} m/s)^{2} \)
\( =7.658 \times 10^{-15} J \)

and the total final kinetic energy is

\(K_{final}=\frac{1}{2} m_{He}\nu\prime^{2}_{He} +\frac{1}{2} m_{N}\nu\prime^{2}_{N}\)
\(= \frac{1}{2}(6.6465\times 10^{-27} kg)(1.199 \times 10^{6} m/s)^{2} + \frac{1}{2}(2.3253\times 10^{-26} kg)(4.977 \times 10^{5} m/s)^{2}\)
\(=7.658 \times 10^{-15} J\)

Note that the initial and final kinetic energies are equal. This is the characteristic of an elastic collision, in which no energy is lost to, for example, internal excitation of the particles.
