Guest Question.

Solve using two, three-node line elements with equally spaced nodes. The problem and numerical data are repeated here as Figure 7.1a.

Admin
Solution

Per Equation 5.62, the element equations are

\(\ k_{x}AN_{i}(x)\frac{dT}{dx}\mid ^{x_{2} }_{x_{1} }-k_{x}A\int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{\frac{dN_{i} }{dx}\frac{dT}{dx}dx }+A\int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{QN_{i}(x)dx }=0 \) i = 1,3 (5.62)

where now there are three interpolation functions per element.

The interpolation functions for a three-node line element are, per Equations 6.23–
6.25

\(\ N_{1}(s) =2\left(s-\frac{1}{2} \right)(s-1) \) (6.23)

 

\(\ N_{2}(s) =-4s(s-1) \)

 

\(\ N_{3}(s) =2s\left(s-\frac{1}{2} \right) \) (6.25)

The components of the conductance matrix are then calculated as

\(\ k_{ij}=k_{x}A\int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{\frac{dN_{i} }{dx}\frac{dN_{j} }{dx} dx } \) i, j = 1,3

and the heat generation vector components are

\(\ f_{Qi}=A\int\limits_{x_{1} }^{x_{2} }{QN_{i}dx } \) i = 1,3

and all \(\ f_{Q} \) components are zero in this example, as there is no internal heat source.

In terms of the dimensionless coordinate s = x/L, we have dx = Lds and d/dx = (1/L)d/ds, so the terms of the conductance matrix are expressed as

\(\ k_{ij} =\frac{k_{x} A}{L}\int\limits_{0}^{1}{\frac{dN_{i} }{ds}\frac{dN_{j} }{ds}ds } \) i, j = 1,3

The derivatives of the interpolation functions are

\(\ \frac{dN_{1} }{ds}=4s-3 \)

 

\(\ \frac{dN_{2} }{ds}=4(1-2s) \)

 

\(\ \frac{dN_{3} }{ds}=4s-1 \)

Therefore, on substitution for the derivatives,

\(\ k_{11}=\frac{k_{x}A }{L}\int\limits_{0}^{1}{(4s-3)^{2}ds }= \frac{k_{x}A }{L}\int\limits_{0}^{1}{(16s^{2}-24s+9 )ds}=\frac{k_{x}A }{L}\left(\frac{16s^{3} }{3}-12s^{2} +9s \right) ^{1}_{0} =\frac{7k_{x}A }{3L} \)

Via mathematically identical procedures, the remaining terms of the conductance matrix are found to be

\(\ k_{12}=k_{21}=-\frac{8k_{x}A }{3L} \)

 

\(\ k_{13}=k_{31}=\frac{k_{x}A}{3L} \)

 

\(\ k_{22}=\frac{16k_{x}A}{3L} \)

 

\(\ k_{23}=k_{32}=-\frac{8k_{x}A}{3L} \)

 

\(\ k_{33}=\frac{7k_{x}A}{3L} \)

A two-element model with node numbers is shown in Figure 7.1b. Substituting numerical values, we obtain, for the aluminum half of the rod (element 1),

\(\ \left[k^{(1)} \right] =\frac{200({\pi }/{4})(0.006)^{2} }{3(0.5)}\left [ \begin{matrix} 7 & -8 & 1 \\ -8 & 16 & -8 \\ 1 & -8 & 7 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 2.6389 & −3.0159 & 0.3770 \\ −3.0159 & 6.0319 & −3.0159 \\ 0.3770 & −3.0159 & 2.6389 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

and for the copper portion (element 2),

\(\ \left[k^{(2)} \right] =\frac{389({\pi }/{4})(0.006)^{2} }{3(0.5)}\left [ \begin{matrix} 7 & -8 & 1 \\ -8 & 16 & -8 \\ 1 & -8 & 7 \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix} 5.1327 & −5.8660 & 0.7332 \\ −5.8660 & 11.7320 & −5.8660 \\ 0.7332 & −5.8660 & 5.1327 \end{matrix} \right ] \)

At the internal nodes of each element, the ﬂux terms are zero, owing to the nature of the interpolation functions \(\ \left[N_{2}(x_{1} )=N_{2}(x_{2}=0 ) \right] \) . Similarly, at the junction between the two elements, the ﬂux must be continuous and the equivalent “forcing” functions are zero. As no internal heat is generated, Q = 0, that portion of the force vector is zero for each element. Following the direct assembly procedure, the system conductance matrix is found to be

\(\ \left[k\right]=\left [ \begin{matrix} 2.6389 & -3.0159 & 0.3770 & 0 & 0 \\ -3.0159 & 6.0319 & -3.0159 & 0 & 0 \\ 0.3770 & -3.0159 & 7.7716 & -5.8660 & 0.7332 \\ 0 & 0 & -5.8660 & 11.7320 & -5.8660 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.7332 & -5.8660 & 5.1327 \end{matrix} \right ] W/ ^{\circ }C \)

and we note in particular that “overlap” exists only at the juncture between elements. The gradient term at node 1 is computed as

\(\ f_{g1}=-k_{x} A\frac{dT}{dx}\mid ^{}_{x_{1} } =q_{1} A=4000\left(\frac{\pi }{4} \right) (0.06)^{2} =11.3097 W \)

while the heat ﬂux at node 5 is an unknown to be calculated via the system equations. The system equations are given by

\(\ \left [ \begin{matrix} 2.6389 & -3.0159 & 0.3770 & 0 & 0 \\ -3.0159 & 6.0319 & -3.0159 & 0 & 0 \\ 0.3770 & -3.0159 & 7.7716 & -5.8660 & 0.7332 \\ 0 & 0 & -5.8660 & 11.7320 & -5.8660 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.7332 & -5.8660 & 5.1327 \end{matrix} \right ]\left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{1} \\ T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \\ 80 \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 11.3097 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ -Aq_{5} \end{matrix} \right \} \)

Prior to solving for the unknown nodal temperatures \(\ T_{1} -T_{4} \) , the nonhomogeneous boundary condition \(\ T_{5}=80ºC \) must be accounted for properly. In this case, we reduce the system of equations to 4×4 by transposing the last term of the third and fourth equations to the right-hand side to obtain

\(\ \left [ \begin{matrix} 2.3689 & -3.0159 & 0.3770 & 0 \\ -3.0159 & 6.0319 & -3.0159 & 0 \\ 0.3770 & -3.0159 & 7.7716 & -5.8660 \\ 0 & 0 & -5.8660 & 11.7320 \end{matrix} \right ] \left \{ \begin{matrix} T_{1} \\ T_{2} \\ T_{3} \\ T_{4} \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 11.3097 \\ 0 \\ -0.7332(80) \\ 5.8660(80) \end{matrix} \right \} =\left \{ \begin{matrix} 11.3097 \\ 0 \\ -58.6560 \\ 489.2800 \end{matrix} \right \} \)

Solving the equations by Gaussian elimination (Appendix C), the nodal temperatures are

\(\ T_{1} = 95.11^{\circ }C \)

 

\(\ T_{2} = 90.14^{\circ }C \)

 

\(\ T_{3} = 85.14^{\circ }C \)

 

\(\ T_{4} = 82.57^{\circ }C \)

and the heat ﬂux at node 5 is calculated using the ﬁfth equation

\(\ -Aq_{5} =0.7332T_{3} -5.8660T_{4} +5.1327(80) \)

to obtain

\(\ q_{5} =4001.9 W/m^{2} \)

which is observed to be in quite reasonable numerical agreement with the heat input at node 1.
