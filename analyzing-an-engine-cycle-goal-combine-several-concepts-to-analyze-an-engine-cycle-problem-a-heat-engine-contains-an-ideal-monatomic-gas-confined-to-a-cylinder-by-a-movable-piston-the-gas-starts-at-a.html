Analyzing an Engine Cycle

GOAL Combine several concepts to analyze an engine cycle.

PROBLEM A heat engine contains an ideal monatomic gas confined to a cylinder by a movable piston. The gas starts at A, where\(T=3.00 \times 10^{2} K\). (See Fig. 12.13a.) The process \(B \rightarrow C\) is an isothermal expansion. (a) Find the number n of moles of gas and the temperature at B. (b) Find \(\Delta U, Q\), and W for the isovolumetric process \(A \rightarrow B\). (c) Repeat for the isothermal process \(B \rightarrow C\). (d) Repeat for the isobaric process \(C \rightarrow A\). (e) Find the net change in the internal energy for the complete cycle. (f) Find the thermal energy \(Q_{h}\) transferred into the system, the thermal energy rejected, \(Q_{c}\), the thermal efficiency, and net work on the environment performed by the engine.

STRATEGY In part (a) n and T can be found from the ideal gas law, which connects the equilibrium values of P, V, and T. Once the temperature T is known at the points A, B, and C, the change in internal energy, \(\Delta U\), can be computed from the formula in Table 12.2 for each process. Q and W can be similarly computed, or deduced from the first law, using the techniques applied in the single-process examples.

Admin
Solution

(a) Find n and \(T_{B}\) with the ideal gas law:

\(\begin{aligned}n &=\frac{P_{A} V_{A}}{R T_{A}}=\frac{(1.00 atm )(5.00 L )}{(0.0821 L \cdot atm / mol \cdot K )\left(3.00 \times 10^{2} K \right)} \\&=0.203 mol \\T_{B} &=\frac{P_{B} V_{B}}{n R}=\frac{(3.00 atm )(5.00 L )}{(0.203 mol )(0.0821 L \cdot atm / mol \cdot K )} \\&=9.00 \times 10^{2} K\end{aligned}\)

 

(b) Find \(\Delta U_{A B}, Q_{A B}, \text { and } W_{A B}\) for the constant volume process \(A \rightarrow B\).

Compute \(\Delta U_{A B}, \text { noting that } C_{v}=\frac{3}{2} R=12.5 J / mol \cdot K\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta U_{A B}=& n C_{v} \Delta T=(0.203 mol )(12.5 J / mol \cdot K ) \\& \times\left(9.00 \times 10^{2} K -3.00 \times 10^{2} K \right) \\\Delta U_{A B}=& 1.52 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\Delta V=0\) for isovolumetric processes, so no work is done: \(W_{A B}=0\)

 

We can find \(Q _{A B}\) from the first law: \(Q_{A B}=\Delta U_{A B}=1.52 \times 10^{3} J\)

 

(c) Find \(\Delta U_{B C}, Q_{B C}\), and \(W_{B C}\) for the isothermal process \(B \rightarrow C\).

This process is isothermal, so the temperature doesn’t change, and the change in internal energy is zero: \(\Delta U_{B C}=n C_{v} \Delta T=0\)

 

Compute the work done on the system, using the negative of Equation 12.10: \(\begin{aligned}W_{B C}=&-n R T \ln \left(\frac{V_{C}}{V_{B}}\right) \\=&-(0.203 mol )(8.31 J / mol \cdot K )\left(9.00 \times 10^{2} K \right) \\& \times \ln \left(\frac{1.50 \times 10^{-2} m ^{3}}{5.00 \times 10^{-3} m ^{3}}\right) \\W_{B C}=&-1.67 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

\(W_{ env }=n R T \ln \left(\frac{V_{f}}{V_{i}}\right)\) [12.10]

 

Compute \(Q _{B C}\) from the first law: \(0=Q_{B C}+W_{B C} \rightarrow Q_{B C}=-W_{B C}=1.67 \times 10^{3} J\)

 

(d) Find \(\Delta U_{C A}, Q_{C A}\), \(\text { and } W_{C A}\) for the isobaric process \(C \rightarrow A\).

Compute the work on the system, with pressure constant:

\(\begin{aligned}W_{C A}=&-P \Delta V=-\left(1.01 \times 10^{5} Pa \right)\left(5.00 \times 10^{-3} m ^{3}\right.\\&\left.-1.50 \times 10^{-2} m ^{3}\right) \\W_{C A}=& 1.01 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

Find the change in internal energy, \(\Delta U_{C A}\) :

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta U_{C A}=& \frac{3}{2} n R \Delta T=\frac{3}{2}(0.203 mol )(8.31 J / K \cdot mol ) \\& \times\left(3.00 \times 10^{2} K -9.00 \times 10^{2} K \right) \\\Delta U_{C A}=&-1.52 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

Compute the thermal energy, \(Q_{C A}\), from the first law: \(\begin{aligned}Q_{C A} &=\Delta U_{C A}-W_{C A}=-1.52 \times 10^{3} J -1.01 \times 10^{3} J \\&=-2.53 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

(e) Find the net change in internal energy, \(\Delta U_{\text {net }}\), for the cycle:

\(\begin{aligned}\Delta U_{ net } &=\Delta U_{A B}+\Delta U_{B C}+\Delta U_{C A} \\&=1.52 \times 10^{3} J +0-1.52 \times 10^{3} J =0\end{aligned}\)

 

(f) Find the energy input, \(Q_{h}\); the energy rejected, \(Q_{c}\); the thermal efficiency; and the net work performed by the engine:

Sum all the positive contributions to find \(Q_{h}\):

\(\begin{aligned}Q_{h} &=Q_{A B}+Q_{B C}=1.52 \times 10^{3} J +1.67 \times 10^{3} J \\&=3.19 \times 10^{3} J\end{aligned}\)

 

Sum any negative contributions (in this case, there is only one): \(Q_{c}=-2.53 \times 10^{3} J\)

 

Find the engine efficiency and the net work done by the engine:

\(\begin{aligned}e &=1-\frac{\left|Q_{c}\right|}{\left|Q_{h}\right|}=1-\frac{2.53 \times 10^{3} J }{3.19 \times 10^{3} J }=0.207 \\W_{ eng } &=-\left(W_{A B}+W_{B C}+W_{C A}\right) \\&=-\left(0-1.67 \times 10^{3} J +1.01 \times 10^{3} J \right) \\&=6.60 \times 10^{2} J\end{aligned}\)

 

REMARKS Cyclic problems are rather lengthy, but the individual steps are often short substitutions. Notice that the change in internal energy for the cycle is zero and that the net work done on the environment is identical to the net thermal energy transferred, both as they should be.
