Guest Question.

A laboratory oven has outside dimensions of 2-ft wide × 2-ft tall × 2-ft long, as shown in Figure 8.6. Under certain operating conditions, the outside-surface temperature of the oven reaches 100°F.The air temperature around the oven is 60°F. Does the oven lose more heat through its upper surface or one of its vertical sides?

Admin
Solution

Heat is transferred by convection from the sides and the top to the ambient air. Flow patterns are affected at the edges of each surface.
Assumptions
1. The system is at steady state.
2. Air properties are constant and evaluated at the film temperature.

\(T_{f} =\left(100+60\right) /2=80^{\circ } F=540^{\circ } R\)

 

3. Edge effects are negligible.
4. Heat is transferred from the surface to the air by convection, which is the primary mode of heat transfer.
From Appendix Table D.1, for air at 540°R,

 

\(\rho =0.0735lbm/ft^{3}\) \( k_{f} =0.01516 BTU/\left(hr.ft.°R \right)\)

 

\(c_{p}=0.240 BTU/\left(lbm.°R \right)\) \(\alpha =0.859ft^{2} /hr\)

 

\(\nu =16.88\times 10^{-5}ft^{2}/s\) Pr=0.708

 

Also,

\(\beta =1/\left(60+460\right) =0.00192/°R\)

We now apply the appropriate convection correlation and calculate the convection coefficients. For one vertical side, we use Equation 8.20. The Rayleigh number is

\(\overline{Nu}_{L} =\frac{\overline{h}_{L}L }{k_{f}} =0.68+\frac{0.670Ra_{L}^{{1}/{4}}}{[1+(\frac{0.492}{Pr} )^{{9}/{16}}]^{{4}/{9}}} \) (8.20)

\({Ra}_{{L}} =\frac{g_{x} \beta\left(T_{{w}}-T_{\infty}\right) L^{3}}{v \alpha}=\frac{32.2(0.00192)(100-60)(2)^{3}}{\left(16.88 \times 10^{-5}\right)(0.859 / 3600)} \)

 

\({Ra}_{{L}} =4.92 \times 10^{8}\)

 

The flow is laminar, because \({Ra}_{{L}}<10^{9}.\)

 

The Nusselt number is calculated with

 

\(\overline{{Nu}}_{{L}}=\frac{\bar{h}_{{L}} L}{k_{{f}}}=0.68+\frac{0.670 {Ra}_{{L}}^{1 / 4}}{\left[1+\left(\frac{0.492}{\operatorname{Pr}}\right)^{9 / 16}\right]^{4 / 9}}\)

 

Rearranging and substituting gives

 

\(\overline{h_{L} } =\frac{0.01516}{2}\left\{0.68+\frac{0.67\left(4.92 \times 10^{8}\right)^{1 / 4}}{\left[1+\left(\frac{0.492}{0.708}\right)^{9 / 16}\right]^{4 / 9}}\right\} \)

 

 

\(\overline{h_{L} }=0.586 {BTU} /\left({hr} \cdot {ft}^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} {R}\right)\)

The heat transferred from one side then is

\(q_{1 \text { side }}=h_{{L}} A_{{s}}\left(T_{{w}}-T_{{\infty }}\right)=0.586(2)(2)(100-60) \)

 

\(q_{1 \text { side }}=93.7 {BTU} / {hr} .\)

For the top, we have a heated surface facing upward. The characteristic length is

\(L_{c}=\frac{A}{P}=\frac{(2)(2)}{2+2+2+2}=\frac{4}{8}=0.5\)

 

The Rayleigh number based on the characteristic length is

 

\(Ra_{L_{c} } =\frac{g_{x} \beta\left(T_{{w}}-T_{\infty}\right) L_{c}^{3}}{v \alpha}=\frac{32.2(0.00192)(100-60)(0.5)^{3}}{\left(16.88 \times 10^{-5}\right)(0.859 / 3600)}\)

 

\(Ra_{L_{c} } =7.67 \times 10^{6}\)

 

Thus, because of the value of the Rayleigh number, Equation 8.24 {b} applies

\(\overline{Nu}_{Lc} =0.54Ra_{Lc}^{{1}/{4}}\) \(2.6\times 10^{5}<Ra_{Lc}<10^{7}\) (8.24b)

\(\overline{{Nu}}_{{L}_{{c}}}=\frac{\bar{h}_{{L}} L_{{c}}}{k_{{f}}}=0.54 {Ra}_{{L}_{{c}}}^{1 / 4}\)

So

\(\overline{h_{L} } =\frac{0.01516}{0.5}(0.54)\left(7.67 \times 10^{6}\right)^{1 / 4}\)

 

or

\(\overline{h_{L} }=0.862 {BTU} /\left({hr} \cdot {ft}^{2} \cdot{ }^{\circ} {R}\right)\)

The corresponding heat transferred is

\(q_{\text {top }}=0.862(2)(2)(100-60) \)

 

\(q_{\text {top }}=138 {BTU} / {hr}\)
