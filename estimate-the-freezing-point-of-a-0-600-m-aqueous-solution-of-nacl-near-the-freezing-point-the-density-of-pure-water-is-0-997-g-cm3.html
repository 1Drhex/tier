CONTENT Seawater is full of dissolved ions, predominantly sodium and chloride ions at an efective NaCl molarity of roughly 0.60 M (about a tenth of the saturation limit). he high ion concentration lowers the freezing point of seawater relative to freshwater. his is one of the factors that prevents the Arctic Ocean from freezing completely in winter, although a much more important efect is the circulation of warm water from the Atlantic. Nonetheless, the change in freezing point with salinity leads to some complex and elegant dynamics in the Arctic. As one example, consider what happens below the ice when seawater begins to freeze on the ocean surface. As the water freezes, salt that separates from the ice crystals increases the salinity of the water beneath the ice, forming brine. he brine is more salty than the water that froze initially, so it continues to cool below the freezing point of the seawater without freezing itself. And, because it’s more dense than rest of the seawater that surrounds it, it tends to accumulate near the bottom of the ice loe. Once this accumulation reaches suicient mass, the brine drops down toward the ocean loor in a narrow stream. As it does so, it freezes the seawater around it, forming icy tubes called ice stalactites or brinicles that stretch down from the ice loe above. he brine is warming as it extracts heat from the surrounding seawater, and it eventually rises above the freezing point and the brinicle ends. Sometimes, however, it reaches the sealoor. In much the way that cool air and warm air dictate the dynamics of the atmosphere, we see here an example of how diferences in salinity contribute to the circulation of water in the ocean.

PROBLEM Estimate the freezing point of a 0.600 M aqueous solution of NaCl. Near the freezing point, the density of pure water is 0.997 g cm-³.

Admin
Solution

With water as the solvent, we use \(T^{\bullet }_{f} = 273.15 K\) and \(\Delta _{fus}H^{\bullet }_{A} = 6008 J mol^{-1}\).T he other number we need is the mole fraction of NaCl\( X_B\). his is not the same as the molarity, which is moles of solute per liter of solution. We need moles of solute per mole of solution, and we use the molarity of pure water,

\(\frac{0.997 g cm^{-3} }{18.015 g mol^{-1} } = 0.0553 mol cm^{-3}= 55.3 M\)

to find\(x_{NaCl}= 0.600/55.3= 0.0108 \) . However, because NaCl dissociates completely in water into\(Na^{+}\) and\(Cl^{-}\) ions, the mole fraction of particles in solution is twice the mole fraction of NaCl added, and the
value we want is \(X_B\) = 2(0.0108) = 0.0216. We therefore estimate the change in the freezing point to be

\(\Delta T_{f}\approx – \frac{(8.3145 J K^{-1}mol^{-1} )(273.15 K)^{2}(0.0216) }{6008 J mol^{-1} } = -2.23 K \)

the final freezing point we estimate is therefore

273.15 k – 2.23 k = 270.92 k.

So the freezing point of the seawater surrounding the brinicle is about 2°C cooler than for fresh water. The freezing point of the brine, which can have ion concentrations several times higher than this, will be several degrees lower.
