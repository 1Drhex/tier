Guest Question.

Determine the forces in the members of the pin-jointed framework shown in Fig. 5.8. Each member has the same value of the product AE.

Admin
Solution

The first step is to choose the redundant member. In this example, no advantage is gained by the choice of any particular member, although in some cases careful selection can result in a decrease in the amount of arithmetical labor. Taking BD as the redundant member, we assume that it sustains a tensile force R due to the external loading. The total complementary energy of the framework is, with the notation of Eq. (5.9),

\(C=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} \int_{0}^{F_{i}} \lambda_{i} d F_{i}-\sum\limits_{r=1}^{n} \Delta_{r} P_{r}\) (5.9)

\(C=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} \int_{0}^{F_{i}} \lambda_{i} d F_{i}-P \Delta\)

\(\frac{\partial C}{\partial R}=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{k} \lambda_{i} \frac{\partial F_{i}}{\partial R}=0\) (i)

Substitution for R in column ➂ of Table 5.2 gives the force in each member. Having determined the forces in the members, the deflection of any point on the framework may be found by the method described in Section 5.3. Unlike the statically determinate type, statically indeterminate frameworks may be subjected to self-straining. Therefore, internal forces are present before external loads are applied. Such a situation may be caused by a local temperature change or by an initial lack of fit of a member. Suppose that the member BD of the framework of Fig. 5.8 is short by a known amount \(\Delta_{R}\) when the framework is assembled but is forced to fit. The load R in BD has suffered a displacement \(\Delta_{R}\) in addition to that caused by the change in length of BD produced by the load P. The total complementary energy is then

\(C=\sum\limits _{i=1}^{k} \int_{0}^{F_{i}} \lambda_{i} d F_{i}-P \Delta-R \Delta_{R}\)

 

and

\(\frac{\partial C}{\partial R}=\sum\limits _{i=1}^{k} \lambda_{i} \frac{\partial F_{i}}{\partial R}-\Delta_{R}=0\)

 

or

\(\Delta_{R}=\frac{1}{A E} \sum\limits _{i=1}^{k} F_{i} L_{i} \frac{\partial F_{i}}{\partial R}\) (iii)

Obviously, the summation term in Eq. (iii) has the same value as in the previous case, so that

\(R=-0.56 P+\frac{A E}{4.83 L} \Delta_{R}\)

 

Hence, the forces in the members due to both applied loads and an initial lack of fit. Some care should be given to the sign of the lack of fit \(\Delta_{R}\). We note here that the member BD is short by an amount \(\Delta_{R}\), so that the assumption of a positive sign for \(\Delta_{R}\) is compatible with the tensile force R. If BD were initially too long, then the total complementary energy of the system would be written

\(C=\sum\limits _{i=1}^{k} \int_{0}^{F_{i}} \lambda_{i} d F_{i}-P \Delta-R\left(-\Delta_{R}\right)\)

 

giving

\(-\Delta_{R}=\frac{1}{A E} \sum\limits _{i=1}^{k} F_{i} L_{i} \frac{\partial F_{i}}{\partial R}\)
