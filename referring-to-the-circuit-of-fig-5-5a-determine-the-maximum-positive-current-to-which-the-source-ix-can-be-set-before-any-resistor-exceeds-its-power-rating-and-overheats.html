Guest Question.

Referring to the circuit of Fig. 5.5a, determine the maximum positive current to which the source \(I_{x}\) can be set before any resistor exceeds its power rating and overheats.

Admin
Solution

♦ Identify the goal of the problem.
Each resistor is rated to a maximum of 250 mW. If the circuit allows this value to be exceeded (by forcing too much current through either resistor), excessive heating will occur—possibly leading to an accident. The 6 V source cannot be changed, so we are looking for an equation involving \(I_{x}\) and the maximum current through each resistor.

♦ Collect the known information.
Based on its 250 mW power rating, the maximum current the 100 Ω resistor can tolerate is

\( \sqrt{\frac{P_{max}}{R} } =\sqrt{\frac{0.250}{100} } =50mA \)

and, similarly, the current through the 64 Ω resistor must be less than 62.5 mA.

♦ Devise a plan.
Either nodal or mesh analysis may be applied to the solution of this problem, but superposition may give us a slight edge, since we are primarily interested in the effect of the current source.

♦ Construct an appropriate set of equations.
Using superposition, we redraw the circuit as in Fig. 5.5b and find that the 6 V source contributes a current

\( i^{\prime }_{100 \Omega }=\frac{6}{100+64} =36.59 mA \)

to the 100 Ω resistor and, since the 64 Ω resistor is in series, \(i^{\prime }_{64 \Omega} = 36.59 mA\) as well.

Recognizing the current divider in Fig. 5.5c, we note that \(i^{\prime\prime}_{64 \Omega}\)will add to \(i^{\prime}_{ 64 \Omega}\) , but \( i^{\prime\prime}_{100 \Omega}\) is opposite in direction to \(i^{\prime}_{100 \Omega}\) . Therefore, \(I_{X}\) can safely contribute 62.5 − 36.59 = 25.91 mA to the 64 Ω resistor current, and \( 50 − \left(−36.59\right) = 86.59 mA\) to the 100 Ω resistor current. The 100 Ω resistor therefore places the following constraint on \(I_{x}\):

\(I_{x}\lt \left(86.59\times 10^{-3}\right) \left(\frac{100+64}{64} \right) \)

and the 64 Ω resistor requires that

\( I_{x}\lt \left(25.91\times 10^{-3}\right) \left(\frac{100+64}{100} \right) \)

♦ Attempt a solution.
Considering the 100 Ω resistor first, we see that \( I_{x}\) is limited to \( I_{x} \lt 221.9 mA\). The 64 Ω resistor limits \(I_{x}\) such that \(I_{x} \lt 42.49 mA\). In order to satisfy both constraints, \( I_{x}\) must be less than 42.49 mA. If the value is increased, the 64 Ω resistor will overheat long before the 100 Ω resistor does.

♦ Verify the solution. Is it reasonable or expected?
One particularly useful way to evaluate our solution is to perform a dc sweep analysis in PSpice as described after the next example. An interesting question, however, is whether we would have expected the 64 Ω resistor to overheat first.

Originally we found that the 100 Ω resistor has a smaller maximum current, so it might be reasonable to expect it to limit \(I_{x}\).

However, because \( I_{x}\) opposes the current sent by the 6 V source through the 100 Ω resistor but adds to the 6 V source’s contribution to the current through the 64 Ω resistor, it turns out to work the other way—it’s the 64 Ω resistor that sets the limit on \(I_{x}\).
