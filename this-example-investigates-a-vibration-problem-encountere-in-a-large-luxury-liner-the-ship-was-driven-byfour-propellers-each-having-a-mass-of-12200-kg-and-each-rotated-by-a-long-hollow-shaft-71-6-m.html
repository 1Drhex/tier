Guest Question.

This example investigates a vibration problem encountere in a large luxury liner. The ship was driven byfour propellers, each having a mass of 12,200 kg and each rotated by a long hollow shaft 71.6 m long with a 0.56 m OD (outer diameter) and 0.28 m ID (inner diameter).The shaft was made of plated steel with a density of \(7.50 kg/m^{3}\) and modulus of elasticity of \(2.05×10^{11} N/m^{2}\). At the ship’s cruising speed, the propellers were rotating at 258 rpm. Determine the natural frequency of longitudinal vibration of the propeller and shaft. What will happen if each shaft drives a four-bladed propeller? What

Solution

We focus on one propeller shaft and model it as having both longitudinal (that is, axial) stiffness and mass. The effective spring constant of the shaft is

\(K_{s} =\frac{P}{\Delta ^{,} }\)

where P is the axial load on the shaft and Δ is the longitudinal
deflection of the shaft given by

\(\Delta =\frac{Pl}{AE ^{,} } \)

where l is the length of the shaft, A is the cross-sectional area of the shaft, and E is the modulus of elasticity of the shaft material. Combining these, the effective stiffness of the propeller shaft is

\(K_{s} =\frac{AE}{l ^{,} } \)

or with numbers, where \(A=\pi \left[\left(x0.56\right) ^{2}-\left(0.28\right) ^{2} \right] /4=0.185 m^{2} \)

\(K_{s}=\frac{\left(m^{2} \right) \left(2.05\times 10^{11} N/m^{2}\right) }{71.6} m =5.30\times10^{8}N/m \).

The mass of the propeller shaft is its density times its volume,

\(m_{s}=\left(7.50 Kg/m^{3}\right) \left(0.185 m^{2}\right) \left(71.6 m\right) =99,200Kg \),

which is an order of magnitude larger than the mass of the propeller. Thus, calculating the natural frequency without considering the mass of the effective spring the propeller shaft — would result in a significant error.
Accounting for the mass of the shaft, and using the results of the previous example, the natural frequency for longitudinal vibration is
\(\omega _{n}=\sqrt{\frac{K_{s}}{m+m_{s}/3} } =\sqrt{\frac{5.30\times10^{8}N/m }{12,200+99,200/3 Kg} } =108 rad/s\),

or, equivalently,

\(f_{n}=\frac{\omega _{n}}{2\pi} =17.2 Hz= 1032 cycles/min = 1302 rpm.\).

At the cruising speed corresponding to 258 rpm of the propeller shaft, one blade of the four-bladed propeller passes by a critical area between the propeller and the hull 4 × 258 = 1032 times/min, or every 0.058 s. This matches the natural frequency (1032 cycles/min). Thus, we predict a serious vibration problem at the normal cruising speed, which did occur. The problem was corrected by using three-bladed propellers.
