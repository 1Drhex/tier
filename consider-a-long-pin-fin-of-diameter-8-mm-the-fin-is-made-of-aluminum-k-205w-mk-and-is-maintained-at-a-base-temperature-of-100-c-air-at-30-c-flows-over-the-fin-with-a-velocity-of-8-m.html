Guest Question.

Consider a long pin fin of diameter 8 mm. The fin is made of aluminum (k = 205W /mK) and is maintained at a base temperature of 100 \(^\circ C\). Air at 30 \(^\circ C\) flows over the fin with a velocity of 8 m/s.
a. Determine the heat transfer coefficient from the fin.
b. Determine the net heat transfer from the fin.
c. If the diameter of the fin is changed to 14 mm, with all other conditions being the same, determine the heat transfer coefficient and the heat transfer from the fin.

Admin
Solution

The Reynolds number, \(Re_{_{D}}=\frac{u_{_{\infty }}D}{v} \)

Air properties at

\(T_{_{m}}=\frac{T_{_{w}}+T_{_{\infty }}}{2} =\frac{100+30}{2}=65^\circ C=338K \).

Now, we obtain the air properties at 338 K from Table 5.2

\(\rho =1.038kg/m^3\)

 

\(c_{_{p}}=1009 J/kg.K\)

 

\(v=1.951\times 10^{-5}\)

 

\(k=0.0289W/mK\)

 

\(Pr=0.707\)

(For convenience, all properties were taken at 340 K.)

\(\therefore Re_{_{D}}=\frac{U_{_{\infty }}D}{v}=\frac{8\times 8\times 10^{-3}}{19.51\times 10^{-6}} =3280\)

∴The constants C and m from Table 5.1

Re C m
0.4–4 0.989 0.33
4–40 0.911 0.385
40–4000 0.683 0.466
4000–40000 0.193 0.618
40000–400000 0.027 0.805

corresponding to this Reynolds number are C = 0.683 and m = 0.466.

\(\overline{Nu_{_D}}=0.683Re^{0.466}Pr^{0.37} \left\lgroup\frac{Pr}{Pr_{s}} \right\rgroup ^{0.25}\)

 

\(Pr_s=0.706\)

(It is to be noted that for air, unless the temperature difference between the surface and the surrounding is very high (100 to 150 \(^\circ C\)), the Prandtl number correction parameter will have a negligible role in the determination of the Nusselt number.)

a. For the problem under consideration,

\(\overline{Nu_{_{D}}}=0.683\times (3280)^{0.466}\times (0.707)^{0.37}\times \left\lgroup\frac{0.707}{0.706} \right\rgroup^{0.25} \)

 

\(\overline{Nu_{_{D}}}=26.13\)

 

\(h=\frac{\overline{Nu}.k }{D}=\frac{25.87\times 0.0289}{8\times 10^{-3}} \)

 

\(h=94.43W/m^2K\)

b.

\(Q=\sqrt{hpAk} \theta _b\)

 

\(Q=\sqrt{94.43\times \pi \times 8\times 10^{-3}\times \pi \times \frac{(8\times 10^{-3})^{^{^2}}}{4}\times 205 } \times 70\)

 

\(Q=10.94W\)

c. When the fin diameter is increased to 14 mm

\(Re_{_{D}}=3280\times \frac{14}{8}=5740 \)

Now, the constants C and m change to 0.193 and 0.618, respectively.

\(\therefore \overline{Nu_{_D}}=0.193\times (5740)^{0.618}\times (0.707)^{0.37}\times \left\lgroup\frac{0.707}{0.706} \right\rgroup^{0.25} \)

 

\(\therefore \overline{Nu_{_D}}=35.72\)

 

\( h=\frac{\overline{Nu_{_D}}.k}{D} =\frac{35.72\times 0.0289}{14\times 10^{-3}}=73.75 W/m^2K\)

 

\(Q=\sqrt{h.p.A.k} \theta _b\)

 

\(Q=\sqrt{73.75\times \pi \times 14\times 10^{-3}\times \pi \times \frac{(14\times 10^{-3})^2}{4}\times 205 } \times 70\)

 

\(Q=22.37W\)

The key takeaway from this problem is that if we had tried to solve this problem with knowledge of only fin heat transfer but without any idea of how the heat transfer coefficient will change for a change in diameter, our solution would have been

\(\frac{Q_{_{1}}}{Q_{_{2}}} =\frac{\sqrt{h.p_{_2}.A_{_2}.k}\times \theta _b }{\sqrt{h.p_{_{_1}}.A_1.k}\times \theta _b} =\frac{\sqrt{p_{_2}A_{_2}} }{\sqrt{p_{_1}A_{_1}}} =\frac{\sqrt{D^{^3}_{_2}} }{\sqrt{D^{^3}_{_1}}} =\left\lgroup\frac{D_{_2}}{D_{_1}} \right\rgroup^{1.5} \)

 

\(\therefore \frac{Q_{_2}}{Q_{_1}} =2.311\)

The actual ratio of \(Q_{_2}/Q_{_1}\) We obtained is 2.04 (i.e.,22.37 W/10.94 W), which leads to a significantly different result by about 15%.
