Guest Question.

Kirchhoff’s Laws with Time-Varying Currents and Voltages. The circuit shown in Figure 3.2-10 contains a circuit element called a capacitor. We will learn more about capacitors in Chapter 7. The only thing we will need to know about the capacitor in this example is its voltage, \(v_c(t)\), and that will be given.

In this example we will determine the voltage, \(v_o(t)\), across the 25-Ω resistor and the voltage source current, \(i_s(t)\), for each of the following cases:
(a) The voltage source voltage is \(v_s(t)\) = 50 V and the capacitor voltage is
\(v_c(t) = 40 - 40e^{-25t} V.\)
(b) The voltage source voltage is \(v_s(t) = 10 \cos(8t)\) V and the capacitor voltage is
\(v_c(t) = 7.62 \cos (8t - 17.7°) V.\)

Notice that \(v_s(t) and v_c(t)\) are not constant functions of time.

Admin
Solution

Let’s label the circuit as shown in Figure 3.2-11. We’ve labeled the nodes of the circuit in Figure 3.2-11. Also, we’ve labeled the voltage and current of each circuit element. In anticipation of using Ohm’s Law, we’ve labeled the current and voltage of each resistor to adhere to the passive convention.

Let’s see what information we can obtain using Ohm’s law and Kirchhoff’s laws. Applying Ohm’s law to each of the resistors gives
\(v_1(t) = 10i_1(t), v_o(t) = 25i_2(t) and v_3(t) = 15i_3(t)\) (3.2-3)

Apply KCL at node a and also at node c to get
\(i_s(t) = i_1(t) and i_2(t) = i_3(t)\) (3.2-4)

Apply KVL to the loop consisting of the voltage source, 10-Ω resistor, and the capacitor to get
\(v_s(t) = v_1(t) + v_c(t)\) (3.2-5)

Apply KVL to the loop consisting of the capacitor, 25-Ω resistor, and the 15-Ω resistor to get
\(v_c(t) = v_o(t) + v_3(t)\) (3.2-6)

Doing a little algebra, we get
\(i_s(t) = i_1(t) = \frac{v_1(t)}{10} = \frac{v_s(t) – v_c(t)}{10}\) (3.2-7)

Recalling that \(i_2(t) = i_3(t)\), we do the following algebra
\(v_c(t) = v_o(t) + v_3(t) = 25i_2(t) + 15i_3(t) = 40i_2(t)\) (3.2-8)

Combining Eqs. 3.2-8 and 3.2-3 gives
\(v_o(t) = 25i_2(t) = 25 \frac{v_c(t)}{40} = \frac{5}{8} v_c(t)\) (3.2-9)

In summary \(v_o(t) = \frac{5}{8} v_c(t) and i_s(t) = \frac{v_s(t) – v_c(t)}{10}\) (3.2-10)

These equations prepare us to consider case (a) and case (b) of this example.
In case (a) \(v_o(t) = \frac{5}{8} (40 – 40e^{-25t}) = 25 (1 – e^{-25t})\) V

and \(i_s(t) = \frac{50 – (40 – 40e^{-25t})}{10} = 1 + 4e^{-25t}\) A

In case (b) \(v_o(t)=\left(\frac{5}{8}\right) 7.62 \cos (8t – 17.7°) = 4.76 \cos (8t – 17.7°)\) V

and \(i_s(t) = \frac{10 \cos (8t) – 7.62 \cos (8t – 17.7°)}{10}\) A (3.2-11)

We can simplify this expression for \(i_s(t)\) using trigonometric identities, but that process is somewhat tedious. In Chapter 10 we’ll use complex arithmetic to simplify Eq. 3.2-11. The result is
\(i_s(t) = 0.349 \cos (8t + 40°)\) A
