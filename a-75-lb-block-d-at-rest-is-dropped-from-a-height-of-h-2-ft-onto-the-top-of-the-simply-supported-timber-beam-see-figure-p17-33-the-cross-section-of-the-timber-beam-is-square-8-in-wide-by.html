Guest Question.

A 75-lb block D at rest is dropped from a height of h = 2 ft onto the top of the simply-supported timber beam (see Figure P17.33). The cross section of the timber beam is square—8 in. wide by 8 in. deep—and the modulus of elasticity of the wood is E = 1,600 ksi. The beam spans L = 14 ft, and it is supported at A and C by springs that each have a stiffness of k = 1,000 lb/in. Assume that the springs at A and C do not restrain beam rotation. Compute:
(a) the maximum beam deflection at B due to the falling block.
(b) the equivalent static load required to produce the same deflection.
(c) the maximum bending stress in the timber beam.

Admin
Solution

(a) Beam deflection at B due to falling block: The moment of inertia of the timber beam is

\(I=\frac{(8 \text { in. })(8 \text { in. })^{3}}{12}=341.3333 in .^{4}\)

From Appendix C, for a simply-support beam with a concentrated load acting at midspan:

\(v_{B}=\frac{P L^{3}}{48 E I}\)

The spring constant of the beam can be considered as:

\(k_{\text {beam }}=\frac{P}{v_{B}}=\frac{48 E I}{L^{3}}=\frac{48(1,600 ksi )\left(341.3333 in. { }^{4}\right)(1,000 lb / kip )}{(14 ft )^{3}(12 in . / ft )^{3}}=5,528.5604 lb / in.\)

From equilibrium, the forces at the spring supports at A and C is related to the load on the beam by:

\(2 F_{\text {spring }}=P_{\text {beam }}\)

and thus

\(\begin{aligned}2 k_{\text {spring }} \Delta_{\text {spring }} &=k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }} \\2(1,000 lb / in .) \Delta_{\text {spring }} &=(5,528.5604 lb / in .) v_{\text {beam }} \\\Delta_{\text {spring }} &=2.764280 v_{\text {beam }} (1)\end{aligned}\)

Equate the work done by the falling block D to the energy stored in the spring supports and in the flexing beam:

\(W_{D}\left(h+\Delta_{\text {spring }}+v_{\text {beam }}\right)=2\left(\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {spring }} \Delta_{\text {spring }}^{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}^{2} (2)\)

Substitute Equation (1) into Equation (2) and note that h = 2 ft = 24 in.:

\((75 lb )\left(24 \text { in. }+2.764280 v_{\text {beam }}+v_{\text {beam }}\right)=2\left[\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {spring }}\left(2.764280 v_{\text {beam }}\right)^{2}\right]+\frac{1}{2} k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}^{2} \)

This quadratic equation can be solved for the beam deflection:

\(\begin{gathered}1,800 lb – in .+(282.321 lb ) v_{\text {beam }}=(7,641.243918 lb / in .) v_{\text {beam }}^{2}+(2,764.280200 lb / in .) v_{\text {beam }}^{2} \\(10,405.52412 lb / in .) v_{\text {beam }}^{2}-(282.321 lb ) v_{\text {beam }}-1,800 lb \text {-in. }=0 \\v_{\text {beam }}=\frac{282.321 \pm \sqrt{(-282.321)^{2}-4(10,405.52412)(-1,800)}}{2(10,405.52412)}=0.429702 in .,-0.402570 \text { in. }\end{gathered}\)

Use the positive value for the beam deflection; thus, the maximum dynamic beam deflection is

\(v_{\max }=0.429702 in . = 0 .430 in.\)

 

(b) Equivalent static load:
The equivalent static load required to produce this deflection is:

\(P_{\max }=k_{\text {beam }} v_{\text {beam }}=(5,528.5604 lb / in .)(0.429702 in .)=2,375.6322 lb =2,380 lb\)

 

(c) Maximum bending stress:
Bending moment due to dynamic load:

\(M=\frac{P L}{4}=\frac{(2,375.6322 lb )(14 ft )}{4}=8,314.7129 lb – ft\)

The bending stress from this moment is:

\(\begin{aligned}\sigma &=\frac{M c}{I}=\frac{(8,314.7129 lb – ft )(8 in . / 2)(12 in . / ft )}{341.3333 in .^{4}} \\&=1,169.2565 psi =1,169 psi\end{aligned}\)
