Guest Question.

Designing a Hartley oscillator

(a) Design the Hartley oscillator shown in Fig. 13.27 so that \( f_{ o }=5 MHz \). Use a depletion NMOS whose parameters are \( K_{ p }=250 \mu A / V ^{2} \text { and } V_{ p }=-2.83 V \). The load resistance is R = 150 Ω, and the power supply voltage is \( V_{ DD }=15 V \). This oscillator uses the capacitor C to feed back the oscillating signal to the gate input, to sustain the oscillation.

(b) Use PSpice to plot the transient response of the output voltage \( v_{ o }(t) \) in part (a) from 10 µs to 10.5 µs.Assume \( V_{ DD }=15 V \) .

Admin
Solution

The small-signal AC equivalent circuit is shown in Fig. 13.28, which is similar to Fig. 13.26(b):

\( g_{ m }=2 K_{ p }\left|V_{ p }\right|=2 \times 250 \mu A / V ^{2} \times|-2.83|=1.415 mA / V \)

Step 1. Choose suitable values of \( L_{1} \text { and } L_{2}: \text { Let } L_{1}=L_{2}=10 \mu H \).
Step 2. Calculate the value of C from Eq. (13.65):

\(f_{ o }=\frac{1}{2 \pi}\left[\frac{1}{C\left(L_{1}+L_{2}\right)}\right]^{1 / 2} \quad(\text { in } Hz )\) (13.65)

 

\( C=\frac{1}{4 \pi^{2} f_{ o }^{2}\left(L_{1}+L_{2}\right)}=\frac{1}{4 \pi^{2} \times(5 MHz )^{2}(10 \mu H +10 \mu H )}=50.66 pF \)

 

Step 3. Find the value of effective load resistance \( R_{L} \). The Q-point of the circuit is \( V_{ DSQ }=V_{ DD }=15 V \), and \( V_{ GSQ }=0 \). The value of \( g_{m} \) varies from 3 mA ⁄ V to 6.5 mA ⁄ V. To ensure oscillation, we choose \( g_{ m }=3 mA / V \). Since \( R_{1} \) is infinity in Fig. 13.28, Eq. (13.66) can be reduced to

\(g_{ m } R_{1}=\frac{L_{1}}{L_{2}}+\frac{R_{1} L_{2}}{R_{ L } L_{1}}\) (13.66)

 

\( g_{ m }=\frac{L_{1}}{R_{1} L_{2}}+\frac{L_{2}}{R_{L} L_{1}}=\frac{L_{2}}{R_{L} L_{1}} \)

which gives the value of effective load resistance \( R_{ L } \) as

\( R_{ L }=\frac{L_{2}}{g_{ m } L_{1}}=\frac{10 \mu H }{1.415 mA / V \times 10 \mu H }=707 \Omega \)

This is the lowest value of \( R_{ L } \) at which sustained oscillation can occur for \( g_{ m }=1.415 mA / V \). Therefore, we select a higher value—say, \( R_{ L }=1.5 k \Omega \).

Step 4. Calculate the value of turns ratio n, which is related to the load resistance R and the effective load resistance \( R_{ L } \), by

\( n=\sqrt{\frac{R}{R_{ L }}}=\sqrt{\frac{100 \Omega}{1.5 k \Omega}}=0.316 \)

Step 5. Calculate the inductance of the transformer secondary \( L_{3} \), which is related to \( L_{2}\), by

\( L_{3}=n^{2} L_{2}=0.316^{2} \times 10 \mu H =1 \mu H \)

(b) The PSpice schematic with the desired values is shown in Fig. 13.29. The PSpice plot of the output voltage \( v_{ o } \equiv V ( R : 2) \) is shown in Fig. 13.30, which gives the peak-to-peak voltage of \( V_{ pp }=+3.75 V \text { to }-4.0 V \) at \( f_{ o }=1(10.326 \mu s -10.124 \mu s )=4.95 MHz \) (expected 5 MHz). Note that an initial voltage of 2 V has been assigned to the capacitor C in order to start the oscillator, and UIC (use initial condition) is used in transient analysis. In practice, random noise or transients can cause the oscillations to begin, and they are sustained by the feedback of the appropriate signal.

NOTE: Increasing the value of R will increase the output voltage and the readers are encouraged to simulate the circuit with different values of R. * symbols in Figs. 13.27 and 13.29 are the dot symbols of the transformer and refer to the direction of the positive induced voltages in the windings.
