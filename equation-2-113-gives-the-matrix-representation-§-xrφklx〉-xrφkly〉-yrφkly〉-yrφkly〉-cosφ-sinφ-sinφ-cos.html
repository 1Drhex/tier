Guest Question.

Equation (2.113) gives the matrix representation

\(S=\left(\begin{matrix} \left\langle X|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|X \right\rangle & \left\langle X|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|Y \right\rangle \\ \left\langle Y|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|X \right\rangle & \left\langle Y|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|Y \right\rangle \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} cos\phi & -sin\phi \\ sin\phi & cos\phi \end{matrix} \right)\)

of the rotation operator \(\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)\) using the linear polarization vectors|x〉 and |Y〉 for photons as a basis. shows that

\(\hat{J} _{z}\rightarrow \hslash \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\)

in the same basis. Show that these operators commute and therefore have eigenstates in common. What are these eigenstates and what are the matrix representations for \(\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)\) and \(\hat{J} _{z}\) using these eigenstates as a basis?

Admin
Solution

It is straightforward to verify that these operators commute:

\(\left(\begin{matrix} cos\phi & -sin\phi \\ sin\phi & cos\phi \end{matrix} \right)\hslash \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)-\hslash \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix} \right)\left(\begin{matrix} cos\phi & -sin\phi \\ sin\phi & cos\phi \end{matrix} \right)=0\)

We know from Section 2. 7 that the eigenstates of \(\hat{J} _{z}\) are the circular polarization states |R〉 and |L〉 with eigenvalues\(\hslash\) and \(-\hslash\) , respectively. Consequently, as given in ,

\(\left(\begin{matrix} \left\langle R|\hat{J} _{z}|R \right\rangle & \left\langle R|\hat{J} _{z}|L \right\rangle \\ \left\langle L|\hat{J} _{z}|R \right\rangle & \left\langle L|\hat{J} _{z}|L \right\rangle \end{matrix} \right)=\left(\begin{matrix} \hslash & 0 \\0 & -\hslash \end{matrix} \right)\)

Since \(\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)=e^{-i\hat{J} _{z}\phi /\hbar }\), we also see that

\(\left(\begin{matrix} \left\langle R|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|R \right\rangle &\left\langle R|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|L \right\rangle\\ \left\langle L|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|R \right\rangle & \left\langle L|\hat{R}\left(\phi K\right)|L \right\rangle \end{matrix} \right) =\left(\begin{matrix} e^{-i\phi } & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\phi } \end{matrix} \right)\)

consistent with the fact that these two operators have the eigenstates |R〉 and |L〉 in common. Using these eigenstates as a basis, the matrix representations of both operators are diagonal with the corresponding eigenvalues as the diagonal matrix elements.
