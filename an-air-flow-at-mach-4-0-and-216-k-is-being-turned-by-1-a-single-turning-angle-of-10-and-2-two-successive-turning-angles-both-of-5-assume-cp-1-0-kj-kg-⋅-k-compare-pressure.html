Guest Question.

An air flow at Mach 4.0 and 216 K is being turned by (1) a single turning angle of 10° and (2) two successive turning angles, both of 5°. Assume \(c_{p} = 1.0 kJ/kg ⋅ K\). Compare pressure increases, the stagnation pressure change, entropy change, and the final flow speeds of the two scenarios.

Admin
Solution

From Fig. 4.12, for \(M_{1} = 4.0\) and \(δ = 10°\), \(θ ≈ 23°\). Therefore, \(M_{1n} = M_{1} \sin 23° ≈ 1.56\). Therefore, using shock table or Eqns. (4.16) \(\frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }=\frac{1+\gamma M^{2}_{1} }{1+\gamma M^{2}_{2}} \) and (4.17d) \(\frac{p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }=e^{-\left(s_{2}-s_{1}\right) /R} =\left[\frac{\frac{\gamma +1}{2} M^{2}_{1} }{1+\frac{\gamma -1}{2} M^{2}_{1}} \right] ^{\gamma /\left(\gamma -1\right) } \left[\frac{1}{\frac{2\gamma }{\gamma +1} M^{2}_{1} -\frac{\gamma -1}{\gamma +1} } \right] ^{1/\left(\gamma -1\right) }\lt 1\) yields

\(\frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }=2.673\) and \(\beta =\frac{p_{2}- p_{1}}{p_{1} }=1.673\)

 

\(\frac{p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }=0.910 \frac{p_{t2}- p_{t1}}{p_{t1} }=9\)%

 

\(\frac{T_{2} }{T_{1} }=1.361\)

 

\(M_{2n}=0.681\)

 

\(M_{2t} =M_{1t} \sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }} =M_{1} \cos \theta _{1} \sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }}=4 \cos 23° \sqrt{\frac{1}{1.361} }=3.156\)

Therefore \(M_{2} = 3.229\) and \(V_{2}=M_{2} \sqrt{\gamma RT_{2}} =3.229\times \sqrt{1.4\times 288\times 1.361\times 216} =1,111.7 m/s\)

\(\Delta s =s_{2} -s_{1} =c_{p} \ln \frac{T_{2} }{T_{1} } -R\ln \frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }=1.0\times \ln \left(1.361\right) -0.287\times \ln \left(2.673\right) =0.026 kJ/kg\cdot K\)

With successive turning, the first turning results in (with \(M_{1} = 4.0\) and \(δ_{1} = Δδ = 5°\)) \(θ_{1} ≈ 18°\) and \(M_{1n} = M_{1} \sin18° ≈ 1.24\). Therefore, using shock table or Eqns. (4.16) \(\frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }=\frac{1+\gamma M^{2}_{1} }{1+\gamma M^{2}_{2}} \) and (4.17d) \(\frac{p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }=e^{-\left(s_{2}-s_{1}\right) /R} =\left[\frac{\frac{\gamma +1}{2} M^{2}_{1} }{1+\frac{\gamma -1}{2} M^{2}_{1}} \right] ^{\gamma /\left(\gamma -1\right) } \left[\frac{1}{\frac{2\gamma }{\gamma +1} M^{2}_{1} -\frac{\gamma -1}{\gamma +1} } \right] ^{1/\left(\gamma -1\right) }\lt 1\) again yields

\(\frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }=1.627\)

\(\frac{p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }=0.988\) or \(\frac{p_{t2} -p_{t1}}{p_{t1} } =1.2\)%

 

\(\frac{T_{2} }{T_{1} }=1.153\)

 

\(M_{2n}=0.818\)

 

\(M_{2t}= M_{1t}\sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }} =M_{1} \cos \theta _{1} \sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }}=4\cos 18° \sqrt{\frac{1}{1.153} } =3.543\)

Therefore \(M_{2} ≈ 3.64\)

Now with \(\delta _{2} =\Delta \delta =5°\) ,\(\theta _{2} \approx 19° \). Therefore \(M_{2n} =M_{2} \sin 19° \approx 1.19\). Thus

\(\frac{p_{3} }{p_{2} } =1.486\)

 

\(\frac{p_{t3} }{p_{t2} }=0.993\)

 

\(\frac{T_{3} }{T_{2} }=1.120\)

 

\(M_{n3}=0.848\)

 

\(M_{t3} =M_{t2} \sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }} =M_{2} \cos \theta _{2} \sqrt{\frac{T_{1} }{T_{2} }}=3.64 \cos 19° \sqrt{\frac{1}{1.120} }=3.253\)

\(M_{3}=3.360\) and \(V_{3}=M_{3} \sqrt{\gamma RT_{3}} =3.36\times \sqrt{1.4\times 288\times 1.293\times 216} =1,127.5 m/s\)

\(\frac{p_{t3} }{p_{t1} }=\frac{p_{t3} }{p_{t2} } \frac{p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }= 0.993\times 0.988=0.981\) and \(\frac{p_{t3}- p_{t1}}{p_{t1} }\approx 2\)%

\(\frac{p_{3} }{p_{1} }=\frac{p_{3} }{p_{2} } \frac{p_{2} }{p_{1} }= 1.486\times 1.627=2.418\) and \(\beta =\frac{p_{3}- p_{1}}{p_{1} }=1.418\)

\(\frac{T_{3} }{T_{1} }=\frac{T_{3} }{T_{2} } \frac{T_{2} }{T_{1} }=1.121\times 1.153=1.293\)

 

\(\Delta s =s_{3} -s_{1} =c_{p} \ln \frac{T_{3} }{T_{1} } -R\ln \frac{p_{3} }{p_{1} }=1.0\times \ln \left(1.293\right) -0.287\times \ln \left(2.418\right) =0.004 kJ/kg\cdot K\)

It can be seen that indeed \(\Delta s\sim 1/n^{2}\). In this example, \(n = 2\) and the values of Δs differ by a factor of 4.5, which is close to \(n_{2} = 4\). Furthermore, the pressure loss given by Eqn. (4.37), \(\frac{p_{t1} – p_{t2} }{p_{t1} }=1- e^{-\left(s_{2} -s_{1}\right) /R} = O \left(\delta ^{3}\right)\) suggests that by halving the angle, the pressure loss should decrease by a factor of 8. The first turning by results in \(\frac{p_{t2} – p_{t1} }{p_{t1} }=1.2%\) close to being one-eighth (\(= 1/n_{3}\), with \(n = 2\)) of loss due to the 10° turning. If p1 is known, both the static and stagnation pressures before and after the shock waves can be calculated. The static pressure contributes to the force acting on a wing surface and its value is therefore necessary when aerodynamic lift and drag are of interest. For the same total turning angle, a series of smaller turning angle results in smaller β.
