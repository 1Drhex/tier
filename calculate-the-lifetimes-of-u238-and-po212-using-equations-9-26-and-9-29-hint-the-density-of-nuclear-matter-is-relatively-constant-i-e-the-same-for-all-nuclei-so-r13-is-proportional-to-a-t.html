Guest Question.

Calculate the lifetimes of \( \text { of } U ^{238} \text { and } Po ^{212} \), using Equations 9.26 and 9.29. Hint: The density of nuclear matter is relatively constant (i.e. the same for all nuclei), so \( \left(r_{1}\right)^{3} \) is proportional to A (the number of neutrons plus protons). Empirically,

\( \gamma \cong \frac{\sqrt{2 m E}}{\hbar}\left[\frac{\pi}{2} r_{2}-2 \sqrt{r_{1} r 2}\right]=K_{1} \frac{Z}{\sqrt{E}}-K_{2} \sqrt{Z r_{r}} \) (9.26).

\( \tau=\frac{2 r_{1}}{v} e^{2 \gamma} \) (9.29).

\( r_{1} \cong(1.07 fm ) A^{1 / 3} \) . (9.30).

The energy of the emitted alpha particle can be deduced by using Einstein’s formula \( \left(E=m c^{2}\right) \):

\( E=m_{p} c^{2}-m_{d} c^{2}-m_{\alpha} c^{2} \) . (9.31).

where \( m_{p} \) is the mass of the parent nucleus, \( m_{d} \) is the mass of the daughter nucleus, and \( m_{α} \) is the mass of the alpha particle (which is to say, the \( He ^{4} \) nucleus). To figure out what the daughter nucleus is, note that the alpha particle carries off two protons and two neutrons, so Z decreases by 2 and A by 4. Look up the relevant nuclear masses. To estimate v, use \( E=(1 / 2) m_{\alpha} v^{2} \); this ignores the (negative) potential energy inside the nucleus, and surely underestimates v, but it’s about the best we can do at this stage. Incidentally, the experimental lifetimes are \( 6 \times 10^{9} \text { yrs and } 0.5 \mu s \text {, respectively. } \)

Admin
Solution

I take the masses from Thornton and Rex, Modern Physics, Appendix 8. They are all atomic masses, but the electron masses subtract out in the calculation of E. All masses are in atomic units (u): \( 1 u =931 MeV / c^{2} \).

The mass of \( He ^{4} \) is 4.002602 u, and that of the α-particle is \( 3727 MeV / c^{2} \).

\( U ^{238}: Z=92, A=238, m=238.050784 u \rightarrow Th ^{234}: m=234.043593 u \).

\( r_{1}=\left(1.07 \times 10^{-15} m \right)(238)^{1 / 3}=6.63 \times 10^{-15} m \).

\( E=(238.050784-234.043593-4.002602)(931) MeV =4.27 MeV \).

\( V=\sqrt{\frac{2 E}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{(2)(4.27)}{3727}} \times 3 \times 10^{8} m / s =1.44 \times 10^{7} m / s \).

\( \gamma=1.980 \frac{90}{\sqrt{4.27}}-1.485 \sqrt{90(6.63)}=86.19-36.28=49.9 \).

\( \tau=\frac{(2)\left(6.63 \times 10^{-15}\right)}{1.44 \times 10^{7}} e^{98.8} s =7.46 \times 10^{21} s =\frac{7.46 \times 10^{21}}{3.15 \times 10^{7}} yr =2.4 \times 10^{14} yrs \).

\( Po ^{212}: Z=84, A=212, m=211.988842 u \rightarrow Pb ^{208}: m=207.976627 u \).

\( r_{1}=\left(1.07 \times 10^{-15} m \right)(212)^{1 / 3}=6.38 \times 10^{-15} m \).

\( E=(211.988842-207.976627-4.002602)(931) MeV =8.95 MeV \).

\( V=\sqrt{\frac{2 E}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{(2)(8.95)}{3727}} \times 3 \times 10^{8} m / s =2.08 \times 10^{7} m / s \).

\( \gamma=1.980 \frac{82}{\sqrt{8.95}}-1.485 \sqrt{82(6.38)}=54.37-33.97=20.4 \).

\( \tau=\frac{(2)\left(6.38 \times 10^{-15}\right)}{2.08 \times 10^{7}} e^{40.8} s =3.2 \times 10^{-4} s \).

These results are way off-but note the extraordinary sensitivity to nuclear masses: a tiny change in E produces enormous changes in τ .
