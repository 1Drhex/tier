Guest Question.

Quantitative Discussion of Heat Exchanger Characteristics. Refer to the operation/performance aspect of the heat exchanger in the previous . Paul Farber, an authority in the heat exchanger field, has suggested that doubling the coolant flow rate will increase Q and perhaps satisfy the original design criteria. Comment on Paul’s recommendation.

Admin
Solution

There are three equations that arise in the development that follows (employing earlier notation).

1. The continuity or conservation law for mass equation:

\(\dot{m}=\rho A_c\overline{v};\ \ \ A_c\) = area available for flow

2. The conservation law for energy:

\(Q=\dot{m}c_p(t_2-t_1)=\dot{m}c_p\Delta t\)

3. The heat transfer equation:

Q = UAΔ\(T_{LM}\)

where,

\(\Delta T_{LM}=\frac{t_2-t_1}{\ln \left\lgroup\frac{T_c-t_1}{T_c-t_2} \right\rgroup };\ \ \ T_c\) = condensing temperature

The term U in Equation Q = USΔ\(T_{LM}\) compounds this problem/analysis since there are three terms that affect the numerical value of U — the condensing medium, the wall, and the coolant fluid. In addition, the term U can be constrained by two values:

1. The resistance is present only in coolant fluid (i.e., \(h_o → \infty\) and \(k → \infty\)). For this condition, the Dittus-Boelter equation is employed to estimate \(h_i\) :

\(U = h_i = a · \overline{v}^{0.8} = f(\overline{v})\)

This will be referred to as Condition (1) in the developments that follow in this and the next chapter.

2. The resistance of the coolant fluid is negligible so that \(h_i → \infty\) and

\(U = h_i ≠ f (\overline{v})\)

In effect U is no longer a function of the coolant’s velocity (i.e., it does not increase as \(\overline{v}\) increases); it is constant. This will be referred to as Condition (2) in the developments that follow.

Combining Equations \(\dot{m}=\rho A_c\overline{v};\ \ \ A_c\) = area available for flow thru \(U = h_i = a · \overline{v}^{0.8} = f(\overline{v})\) above for Condition (1) results in the following equation:

\(Q=\rho \overline{v}A_cc_p(t_2-t_1)=a(\overline{v}^{0.8})A\left\{\frac{t_2-t_1}{\ln \left[(T_c-t_1)/(T_c-t_2)\right] } \right\}\)

Equation \(Q=\rho \overline{v}A_cc_p(t_2-t_1)=a(\overline{v}^{0.8})A\left\{\frac{t_2-t_1}{\ln \left[(T_c-t_1)/(T_c-t_2)\right] } \right\}\) may be rearranged to

\(\ln \left\lgroup\frac{T_c-t_1}{T_c-t_2} \right\rgroup=\left\lgroup\frac{a(\overline{v}^{-0.2})}{\rho c_p} \right\rgroup\left\lgroup\frac{A}{A_c} \right\rgroup=\frac{B}{\overline{v}^{0.2} }\)

where,

\(B=\frac{a}{\rho c_p}\left\lgroup\frac{A}{A_c} \right\rgroup\)

Equation \(\ln \left\lgroup\frac{T_c-t_1}{T_c-t_2} \right\rgroup=\left\lgroup\frac{a(\overline{v}^{-0.2})}{\rho c_p} \right\rgroup\left\lgroup\frac{A}{A_c} \right\rgroup=\frac{B}{\overline{v}^{0.2} }\) may also be rearranged to yield,

\(\frac{T_c-t_1}{T_c-t_2}=e^{B/\overline{v}^{0.2}}\)

The above analysis may now be applied to this example. For this case, as \(\overline{v}\) increases, Equation \(\frac{T_c-t_1}{T_c-t_2}=e^{B/v^{0.2}}\) predicts that the resulting cold fluid outlet temperature \(t_2\), will decrease below its initial value. Although Δt has decreased, \(\dot{m}\) has doubled — producing an increase in Q.

With reference to the problem question, first note that since the coolant film coefficient is proportional to the (velocity)\(^{0.8}\) or \(\overline{v}^{0.8}\), U would increase. In addition, the increased flow rate would produce a lower cold fluid outlet temperature resulting in a larger Δ\(T_{LM}\). Now, examine the heat transfer equation.

Q = UAΔ\(T_{LM}\)

Since A is constant, and both U and Δ\(T_{LM}\) have increased, Q will correspondingly increase.

Farber [10] computerized the above equation for a specified set of conditions and the results are presented in Figure 6.10. One notes that doubling the flow has produced only a small decrease in Δt, not nearly compensating for the doubling (increasing) of the mass flow rate (see also Equation \(Q=\dot{m}c_p(t_2-t_1)=\dot{m}c_p\Delta t\)). The same conclusion is drawn from Equation Q = USΔ\(T_{LM}\) where U and Δ\(T_{LM}\) have increased.

The same conclusion can also be drawn for Condition (2). Here, the RHS (right-hand side) of Equation \(U = h_i ≠ f (\overline{v})\) is given by a constant since \(\overline{v}\) is constant. Once again, doubling the velocity produces a decrease in \(t_2\) but an increase in Δ\(T_{LM}\). Thus, Q also increases for Condition (2) since this decrease in \(t_2\) (or t) results in a greater Δ\(T_{LM}\).

To help the reader visualize Δ\(T_{LM}\) of Condition (2), one could represent Δ\(T_{LM}\) via the area between two curves, similar to the temperature profile graphs shown earlier in this chapter. In Figure 6.11, the shaded region represents the extra (or additional) Δ\(T_{LM}\) gained from the increased flow rate of the coolant.
