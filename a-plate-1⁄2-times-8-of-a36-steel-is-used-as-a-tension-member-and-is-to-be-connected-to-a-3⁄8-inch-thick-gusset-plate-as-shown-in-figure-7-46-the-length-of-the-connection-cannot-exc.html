Guest Question.

A plate 1⁄2 \(\times\) 8 of A36 steel is used as a tension member and is to be connected to a 3⁄8-inch-thick gusset plate, as shown in Figure 7.46. The length of the connection cannot exceed 8 inches, and all welding must be done on the near side. Design a weld to develop the full tensile capacity of the member.

Admin
Solution

The design strength of the member based on its gross area is
\(\phi P_n = 0.90F_{y}A_{g} = 0.90(36)\left ( \frac{1}{2} \right )(8) = 129.6 kips\)
Compute the design strength of the member based on its effective area. For a plate connection, if the welds are along the sides only,\( A_e = A_{g}U\), where U is determined by Case 4 in Table D3.1. If there is also a transverse weld at the end, then \(A_e = A_g.\) Assuming the latter, we have
\(\phi P_n = 0.90F_{u}A_{e} = 0.75(58) \left ( \frac{1}{2} \right )(8)=174.0 kips\)
Design for a factored load of 129.6 kips and use E70 electrodes.
From AISC Table J2.4, the minimum weld size is 3⁄16 inch. Because of the length constraint, however, try a slightly larger weld.

Try a 1 ⁄4-inch E70 fillet weld:
Design strength per inch of weld = 1.392D = 1.392(4) = 5.568 kips/in.
The base metal shear yield strength is
\(0.6F_{y}t = 0.6(36)\left ( \frac{3}{8} \right )= 8.1 kips/in.\)
and the base metal shear rupture strength is
\(0.45F_{u}t = 0.45(58)\left ( \frac{3}{8} \right )= 9.788 kips/in.\)
The weld strength of 5.568 kips/in. governs. Both longitudinal and transverse welds will be used. To determine the required length of the longitudinal welds, we investigate the two options specified in AISC J2.4c. First, assuming the same strength for both the longitudinal and transverse welds,
Total required length of weld =\( \frac{129.6}{5.568} = 23.28 in.\)
Length of longitudinal welds = \(\frac{23.28 – 8} {2} = 7.64 in\).
For the second option, the strength of the longitudinal welds is
0.85(5.568) = 4.733 kips/in.
and the strength of the transverse weld is
1.5(5.568) = 8.352 kips/in.

The load to be carried by the longitudinal welds is
129.6 – 8(8.352) =62.78 kips
so the required length of the longitudinal welds is
\(\frac{62.78}{2(4.733)}= 6.63 in.\)
The second option requires shorter longitudinal welds. Try an 8-inch transverse weld and two 7-inch longitudinal welds. Check the block shear strength of the gusset plate.
\(A_{gv} = A_{nv} = 2 \times \frac{3}{8}(7) = 5.25 in.^{2}\)

\(A_nt =\frac{3}{8}(8) = 3.0 in.^{2}\)

From AISC Equation J4-5,

\(R_n = 0.60F_{u}A_{nv} + U_{bs}F_{u}A_{nt}\)
\(= 0.60(58)(5.25) + 1.0(58)(3.0) = 356.7 kips\)
with an upper limit of

\(0.60F_{y}A_{gv} + U_{bs}F_{u}A_{nt} = 0.60(36)(5.25) + 1.0(58)(3.0) = 287.4 kips\) (controls)
The design strength is

\(\phi R_n = 0.75(287.4) = 216 kips > 129.6 kips (OK)\)

The allowable strength of the member based on its gross area is
\(\frac{P_n}{\Omega _t}= \frac{F_{y}A_{g}}{1.67} =\frac{ 36(1/2)(8)}{1.67 }= 86.23 kips\)
Compute the allowable strength based on the effective area. For a plate connection,if the welds are along the sides only, \(A_e = A_{g}U\), where U is determined by Case 4 in Table D3.1. If there is also a transverse weld at the end, then \(A_e = A_g.\) Assuming the latter, we have
\(\frac{P_n}{\Omega _t}= \frac{F_{u}A_{g}}{2.00} = \frac{58(1/2)(8)}{2.00} = 116.0 kips\)
Design for a load of 86.23 kips and use E70 electrodes. From AISC Table J2.4, the minimum weld size is 3⁄16 inch. Because of the length constraint, a slightly larger size will be tried.
Try a 1⁄4-inch E70 fillet weld:
Allowable strength per inch of weld = 0.9279D = 0.9279(4) = 3.712 kips/in.
The allowable base metal shear yield strength is
\(0.4F_{y}t = 0.4(36)\left ( \frac{3}{8} \right )= 5.4 kips/in\).
and the allowable base metal shear rupture strength is
\(0.3F_{u}t = 0.3(58)\left ( \frac{3}{8} \right )= 6.525 kips/in.\)
The weld strength of 3.712 kips/in. governs. Both longitudinal and transverse welds will be used. To determine the required length of the longitudinal welds, we investigate the two options specified in AISC J2.4c. First, assuming the same strength for both the longitudinal and transverse welds,
Total required length of weld = \(\frac{86.23}{3.712} = 23.23 in.\)
Length of longitudinal welds = \(\frac{23.23 – 8}{2} = 7.62 in.\)
For the second option, the strength of the longitudinal welds is
0.85(3.712) = 3.155 kips/in.
and the strength of the transverse weld is
1.5(3.712) = 5.568 kips/in.
The load to be carried by the longitudinal welds is
86.23 – 8(5.568) = 41.69 kips
so the required length of the longitudinal welds is
\(\frac{41.69}{2(3.155)} = 6.61 in.\)
The second option requires shorter longitudinal welds. Try an 8-inch transverse weld and two 7-inch longitudinal welds. Check the block shear strength of the gusset plate.
\(A_{gv} = A_{nv} = 2 \times \frac{3}{8}(7) = 5.25 in.^{2}\)

\(A_{nt} = \frac{3}{8}(8) = 3.0 in.^{2}\)

From AISC Equation J4-5,
\(R_{n} = 0.60F_{u}A_{nv} + U_{bs}F_{u}A_{nt}\)
\(= 0.60(58)(5.25) + 1.0 (58)(3.0) = 356.7 kips\)
with an upper limit of
\(0.60F_{y}A_{gv} + U_{bs}F_{u}A_{nt} = 0.60(36)(5.25) + 1.0(58)(3.0) = 287.4 kips (controls)\)
and the allowable strength is
\(\frac{R_n}{\Omega} = \frac{287.4}{2.00} = 144 kips > 86.23 kips (OK)\)
