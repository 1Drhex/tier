Guest Question.

The current source in the circuit in Fig. 9.9-3 represents a power electronic load (called a \(DC–DC\) chopper, used to step down \(DC\) voltages) that is drawing a pulsed current at \(20 kHz\). The current drawn by the load under a particular operating condition is shown as \(i_{S}(t)\) in Fig. 9.9-3. The \(LC\) filter is expected to hold the voltage presented to the load at a constant level and to smooth the current in the battery. Pulsed current has adverse impact on battery life. Solve for \(i_{L}(t)\), \(i_{C}(t)\) and \(v_{o}(t)\) under steady-state.

Admin
Solution

The Fourier series coefficients of a symmetric square wave has been worked out in Fig. 9.6-5.

They are \(\frac{4}{\pi n}\) in the trigonometric Fourier series. The waveform of \(i_{S}(t)\) here contains a \(DC\) component of \(10 A\). Hence \(i_{S}(t) –\) \(10\) will be a symmetric square wave that has even symmetry. The square wave in Fig. 9.6-5. had odd symmetry and had sine terms in its Fourier series. Here it will be cosine terms.

\(\therefore i_{s}(t)=10+\frac{40}{\pi} \sum_{n=1 \atop n \text { odd }}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \cos 40 \times 10^{3} n \pi t \mathrm{~A}\)

 

Inductor behaves as an open-circuit and capacitor behaves as a short-circuit under \(DC\) steady-state conditions. \(\therefore DC\) component in \(v_{o}(t)(t) = 200 V\), in \(i_{L}(t) = 10 A\) and in \(i_{C}(t) = 0 A.\)

The \(200 V\) battery is replaced by a short-circuit when the circuit solution for the \(AC\) components of \(i_{S}(t)\) is attempted. This results in a parallel \(LC\) circuit with current excitation. Current division principle in parallel impedances gives the frequency response of inductor current as

\(\frac{I_{L}(j \omega)}{I_{S}(j \omega)}=\frac{1 / j \omega C}{j \omega L+1 / j \omega C}=\frac{1}{1-\omega^{2} L C}=\frac{1}{1-789.6 n^{2}} \approx-\frac{1.267 \times 10^{-3}}{n^{2}}\)

 

Thus the amplitude of inductor current at \(20 kHz\) will be \(1.267 \times 10^{-3} \times 40 / \pi=0.016 \mathrm{~A}\) This is \(0.16\)% of the \(DC\) current in it. We do not have to calculate the contribution due to other current harmonics.

They will be very negligible due to the \(n^{2}\) factor in the denominator of current division ratio and the \(1/n\) factor in the Fourier series of \(i_{S}(t)\). Therefore, the inductor current is practically \(DC\). Similarly, it can be shown that \(v_{o}(t)\) is practically \(DC\).

However, in practice, the \(100\mu F\) aluminium electrolytic capacitor used will have a series resistance along with its capacitance. It is called effective series resistance \((ESR)\). It comes up due to the lead resistance and foil resistance. In this case, a \(100\mu F\) capacitor is likely to have an \(ESR\) of \(0.3 ΩV\) to \(0.8 Ω\) depending on the grade and quality of the capacitor chosen. Note that the impedance of \(100 \mu F\) capacitor at \(20 kHz\) is \(-j0.08 Ω\) and that of \(0.5 mH\) inductor is \(j62.8 Ω\). Obviously, the \(ESR\) of capacitor rules the situation. Current division will take place between the impedance of inductor and \(ESR\) of capacitor and the filter performance is not going to be as good as we calculated.

Increasing the capacitance value decreases the \(ESR\). Thus the size of capacitor chosen in this kind of applications is based on \(ESR\) considerations rather than the kind of calculations we carried out without taking the ESR into account.
