Guest Question.

At a given moment, suppose that flux \(\phi_{ X }\) is + 15 mWb. The plus () sign indicates that \(\phi_{ X }\) is directed towards the right. Suppose also that flux \(\phi_{ Y }=-8 mWb\). The minus (-) sign indicates that \(\phi_{ Y }\) is directed downwards. Suddenly, the X and Y windings are connected as shown in Fig. 65. Determine

a. The initial value of the resultant flux \(\phi_{ S }\) and its orientation in space

b. The value of flux \(\phi_{ S }\) and its orientation 2.2 ms later

Admin
Solution

a. Because coils X and Y are displaced 90° in space, the initial orientation of the fluxes is as shown in Fig. 66a. It follows that \(\phi_{ S }\) is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}\phi_{ S } &=\sqrt{\phi_{ X }^{2}+\phi_{ Y }^{2}} \\&=\sqrt{15^{2}+(-8)^{2}}=17 mWb\end{aligned}\)

 

It is directed at an angle:

 

\(\alpha_{ S }=\arctan \frac{\phi_{ Y }}{\phi_{ X }}=\arctan \frac{-8}{15}=-28^{\circ}\)

 

b. Since \(x_{ 1 }\) is negative with respect to \(x_{ 2 }\) (Fig. 65), it follows that \(E_{ X }=-200 V . \text { Flux } \phi_{ X }\) is therefore changing at a rate of – 10 mWb/ms. It tends to point towards the left. However, because \(E_{ Y }=0, \text { flux } \phi_{ Y }\) does
not change, and so it remains at – 8 mWb.

 

Change of flux \(\phi_{ X }\) during 2.2 ms

 

\(2.2 ms \times-10 mWb / s =-22 mWb\)

 

New flux \(\phi_{ X }\) (Fig. 66b)

 

+ 15 mWb – 22 mWb = – 7 mWb

 

During this interval of 2.2 ms, flux \(\phi_{ X }\) has gradually dropped to zero and then reversed to reach – 7 mWb. The minus sign shows that \(\phi_{ X }\) is directed to the left.

 

Resulting flux \(\phi_{ S }\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\phi_{ S } &=\sqrt{\phi_{ X }^{2}+\phi_{ Y }^{2}} \\&=\sqrt{(-7)^{2}+(-8)^{2}}=10.6 mWb\end{aligned}\)

 

Its orientation in space is

 

\(\alpha_{ S }=\arctan \frac{\phi_{ Y }}{\phi_{ X }}=\arctan \frac{-8}{-7}=-131^{\circ}\)

 

We can see that during the interval of 2.2 ms, the spatial flux has moved from – 28° to – 131°. It has therefore turned clockwise through an angle of 103°. This proves that we can create a revolving field by opening and closing the switches in an appropriate way.

 

Strange as it may seem, the flux changes are not created by the current that happens to flow in a particular winding. Because the resistances are assumed to be negligible, the change of fluxes \(\phi_{ X }\) and \(\phi_{ Y }\) depends exclusively upon the volt-seconds that are applied to the terminals of the respective windings.
