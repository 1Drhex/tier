Guest Question.

A standard description of a planar curve defines y as a function of x, that is, y = y(x), and z = 0. Derive expressions for the tangent and normal unit vectors and the radius of curvature of the path.

Admin
Solution

This example is used to demonstrate the application of the general path variable expressions, rather than merely substituting into the parametric equations. The formulas that result can be quite useful. To match the given functional form to the standard parametric representation, we consider Î± = x, so that \(x^{\prime} = 1\), \(y^{\prime} = dy/dx\), and \(z^{\prime} = 0\). It follows that \(\bar{r}^{\prime}=\bar{i}+(d y / d x) \bar{j} \), which combined with the requirement that \(\left|\bar{e}_{t}\right|=1, \) leads to

\(\Large \begin{array}{c}\bar{e}_{t}=\frac{d \bar{r}}{d s}=\frac{\left(\frac{d \bar{r}}{d x}\right)}{\left(\frac{d s}{d x}\right)}=\frac{\bar{i}+\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{j}}{s^{\prime}} \\\\ s^{\prime} \equiv \frac{d s}{d x}=\left|\bar{i}+\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{j}\right|=\left[1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}. \end{array} \) (2.1.30)

Differentiation of \(\bar{e}_{t}\) with respect to s by use of the chain rule gives

\(\large \begin{aligned}\bar{e}_{n} &=\rho \frac{d \bar{e}_{t}}{d s}=\rho \frac{\left(\frac{d \bar{e}_{t}}{d x}\right)}{s^{\prime}} \\\\ &=\frac{\rho}{s^{\prime}}\left[\frac{\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}} \bar{j}}{s^{\prime}}+\left(\bar{i}+\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{j}\right) \frac{\left(-s^{\prime \prime}\right)}{\left(s^{\prime}\right)^{2}}\right].\end{aligned} \)

From the second of Eqs. (2.1.30) we find that

\(\Large s^{\prime \prime}=\frac{\frac{d y}{d x} \frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}}{\left[1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}} ,\)

which reduces \(\bar{e}_{n}\) to

\(\Large \bar{e}_{n}=\frac{\rho}{\left(s^{\prime}\right)^{4}} \frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}\left(-\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{i}+\bar{j}\right). \)

Because \(\bar{e}_{n}\) is a unit vector, it must be that

\(\Large \bar{e}_{n} \cdot \bar{e}_{n}=\frac{\rho^{2}}{\left(s^{\prime}\right)^{8}}\left(\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}\right)^{2}\left[1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}\right]=1 .\)

In view of the fact that \(s^{\prime}=\left[1+(d y / d x)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}\) , solving the preceding expression for Ï gives

\(\Large \rho=\frac{\left[1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}\right]^{3 / 2}}{\left|\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}\right|},\) (2.1.31)

which is the same as the formula derived in a course on calculus. Back substitution of Ï into the preceding expression for \(\bar{e}_{n}\) leads to

\(\Large \bar{e}_{n}=\frac{\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}}{\left|\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}\right|} \frac{1}{s^{\prime}}\left(-\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{i}+\bar{j}\right)=\operatorname{sgn}\left(\frac{d^{2} y}{d x^{2}}\right) \frac{-\frac{d y}{d x} \bar{i}+\bar{j}}{\left[1+\left(\frac{d y}{d x}\right)^{2}\right]^{1 / 2}},\) (2.1.32)

where sgn( ) denotes the signum function, that is, the sign of the argument. It tells us the sense in which the normal vector is perpendicular to the tangent vector. If \(d^{2} y/dx^{2}\) is positive, which means that the slope increases with increasing x, then \(\bar{e}_{n}\) will have a positive \(\bar{e}_{y}\) component.
