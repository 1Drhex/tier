Evaluating Exergy Destruction in a Heat Exchanger

Compressed air enters a counterflow heat exchanger operating at steady state at 610 K, 10 bar and exits at 860 K, 9.7 bar. Hot combustion gas enters as a separate stream at 1020 K, 1.1 bar and exits at 1 bar. Each stream has a mass 1 flow rate of 90 kg/s. Heat transfer between the outer surface of the heat exchanger and the surroundings can be ignored. The effects of motion and gravity are negligible. Assuming the combustion gas stream has the properties of air, and using the ideal gas model for both streams, determine for the heat exchanger

a. the exit temperature of the combustion gas, in K.

b. the net change in the flow exergy rate from inlet to exit of each stream, in MW.

c. the rate exergy is destroyed, in MW.

Let \(T_{0}=300 K , p_{0}=1\) bar.

Admin
Solution

Known Steady-state operating data are provided for a counterflow heat exchanger.

Find For the heat exchanger, determine the exit temperature of the combustion gas, the change in the flow exergy rate from inlet to exit of each stream, and the rate exergy is destroyed.

Schematic and Given Data:

Engineering Model

1. The control volume shown in the accompanying figure is at steady state.

2. For the control volume, \(\dot{Q}_{ cv }=0, \dot{W}_{ cv }=0\), and the effects of motion and gravity are negligible.

3. Each stream has the properties of air modeled as an ideal gas.

4. \(T_{0}=300 K , p_{0}=1\) bar.

Analysis

a. The temperature \(T_{4}\) of the exiting combustion gases can be found by reducing the mass and energy rate balances for the control volume at steady state to obtain

 

\(0=\underline{\dot{Q}_{ cv }}-\underline{\dot{W}_{ cv }}+\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right)+\left(\underline{\frac{ V _{1}^{2}- V _{2}^{2}}{2}}\right)+\underline{g\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)}\right]\)

 

\(+\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{3}-h_{4}\right)+\left(\underline{\frac{ V _{3}^{2}- V _{4}^{2}}{2}}\right)+\underline{g\left(z_{3}-z_{4}\right)}\right]\)

 

where \(\dot{m}\) is the common mass flow rate of the two streams. The underlined terms drop out by listed assumptions, leaving

 

\(0=\dot{m}\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right)+\dot{m}\left(h_{3}-h_{4}\right)\)

 

Dividing by \(\dot{m}\) and solving for \(h_{4}\)

 

\(h_{4}=h_{3}+h_{1}-h_{2}\)

 

From , \(h_{1}=617.53 kJ / kg , h_{2}=888.27 kJ / kg , h_{3}=1068.89 kJ / kg\). Inserting values

 

\(h_{4}=1068.89+617.53-888.27=798.15 kJ / kg\)

 

Interpolating in gives \(T_{4}=778 K \left(505^{\circ} C \right)\).

 

b. The net change in the flow exergy rate from inlet to exit for the air stream flowing from 1 to 2 can be evaluated using Eq. 7.18, neglecting the effects of motion and gravity. With Eq. 6.20a and data from

 

\(s\left(T_{2}, p_{2}\right)-s\left(T_{1}, p_{1}\right)=s^{\circ}\left(T_{2}\right)-s^{\circ}\left(T_{1}\right)-R \ln \frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}\) (6.20a)

 

\(e _{ f 1}- e _{ f 2}=\left(h_{1}-h_{2}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{1}-s_{2}\right)+\frac{ V _{1}^{2}- V _{2}^{2}}{2}+g\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)\) (7.18)

 

\(\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 2}- e _{ f 1}\right)=\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{2}-s_{1}\right)\right]\)

 

\(=\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{2}-h_{1}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{2}^{ o }-s_{1}^{ o }-R \ln \frac{p_{2}}{p_{1}}\right)\right]\)

 

\(=90 \frac{ kg }{ s }\left[(888.27-617.53) \frac{ kJ }{ kg }-300 K \right.\)

 

\(\left.\times\left(2.79783-2.42644-\frac{8.314}{28.97} \ln \frac{9.7}{10}\right) \frac{ kJ }{ kg \cdot K }\right]\)

 

\(=14,103 \frac{ kJ }{ s }\left|\frac{1 MW }{10^{3} kJ / s }\right|=14.1 MW\)

 

2 As the air flows from 1 to 2, its temperature increases relative to \(T_{0}\) and the flow exergy increases.

 

Similarly, the change in the flow exergy rate from inlet to exit for the combustion gas is

 

\(\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 4}- e _{ f 3}\right)=\dot{m}\left[\left(h_{4}-h_{3}\right)-T_{0}\left(s_{4}^{ o }-s_{3}^{ o }-R \ln \frac{p_{4}}{p_{3}}\right)\right]\)

 

\(=90[(798.15-1068.89)\)

 

\(\left.-300\left(2.68769-2.99034-\frac{8.314}{28.97} \ln \frac{1}{1.1}\right)\right]\)

 

\(=-16,934 \frac{ kJ }{ s }\left|\frac{1 MW }{10^{3} kJ / s }\right|=-16.93 MW\)

 

As the combustion gas flows from 3 to 4, its temperature decreases relative to \(T_{0}\) and the flow exergy decreases.

3 c. The rate of exergy destruction within the control volume can be determined from an exergy rate balance, Eq. 7.13a,

 

\(0=\sum_{j}\left(1-\frac{T_{0}}{T_{j}}\right) \dot{Q}_{j}-\dot{W}_{ cv }+\sum_{i} \dot{m}_{i} e _{ f i}-\sum_{ e } \dot{m}_{e} e _{ f e}-\dot{ E }_{ d }\) (7.13a)

 

\(0=\sum_{j}\left(1-\frac{T_{0}}{T_{j}}\right) \dot{Q}_{j}^{\nearrow0}-\dot{W}_{ cv }^{\nearrow0}+\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 1}- e _{ f 2}\right)+\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 3}- e _{ f 4}\right)-\dot{ E }_{ d }\)

 

Solving for \(\dot{ E }_{ d }\) and inserting known values

 

\(\dot{ E }_{ d }=\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 1}- e _{ f 2}\right)+\dot{m}\left( e _{ f 3}- e _{ f 4}\right)\)

 

4 \(=(-14.1 MW )+(16.93 MW )=2.83 MW\)

 

Comparing results, the exergy increase of the compressed air: 14.1 MW is less than the magnitude of the exergy decrease of the combustion gas: 16.93 MW, even though the energy changes of the two streams are equal in magnitude. The difference in these exergy values is the exergy destroyed: 2.83 MW. Thus, energy is conserved but exergy is not.

1 Heat exchangers of this type are known as regenerators (see Sec. 9.7).

2 The variation in temperature of each stream passing through the heat exchanger is sketched in the schematic. The dead state temperature \(T_{0}\) also is shown on the schematic for reference.

3 Alternatively, the rate of exergy destruction can be determined using \(\dot{ E }_{ d }=T_{0} \dot{\sigma}_{ cv }\), where \(\dot{\sigma}_{ cv }\) is the rate of entropy production
evaluated from an entropy rate balance. This is left as an exercise.

4 Exergy is destroyed by irreversibilities associated with fluid friction and stream-to-stream heat transfer. The pressure drops for the streams are indicators of frictional irreversibility. The average temperature difference between the streams, \(\Delta T_{\text {ave }}\), is an indicator of heat transfer irreversibility.
