Guest Question.

The house wall of Example 3.1 is now modified to have convection boundary conditions rather than temperature boundary conditions, as shown in Fig. 3.6 below. The outside air is at 31 F with\( h_{o}\) = 2 Btu / h \( ft^{2}\) F. The inside air is at 74 F with \( h_{i}\)= 0.7 Btu/h \( ft^{2}\) F.
(a) What is the rate of heat flow through the wall?
(b) What is the temperature at the interface between the T-111 and the fiberglass?

Admin
Solution

Rate of Heat Flow

We can again use Eq. (3.18), but now we have five resistances in the denominator, three for the wall layers and two for the convections at the wall surfaces.

\(q=\frac{\Delta T_{overall} }{\sum{R} } \)……..(3.18)

where Δ\(T_{overall}\) is the temperature difference across the whole system, which now includes the convection. Thus, the overall temperature difference is the difference between the temperature of the room air and that of the outside air. Eq.(3.20) is revised to

\(q= \frac{74-31}{(\frac{1}{2A}+ \frac{0.781}{A}+\frac{12.963}{A}+\frac{0.417}{A}+\frac{1}{0.7A})}= 2.673A \)

From ,\(A= 120 ft_{2} \) so q = 2.673 ×120 = 321 Btu/h.

Interface Temperature

The series electrical circuit for the problem is shown in Fig. 3.7. The heat flow through each layer is the same. Once we have determined q, we can determine the temperature at any location in the system by using Eq. (3.18). We wish to find temperature \(T_{2}\). We can apply Eq. (3.18) between \(T_{2}\) and the outside air temperature \(T_{\infty _{0} }\) . Between these two temperatures there is one conductive resistance (the T-111 layer) and the outside convective resistance. Hence, Eq. (3.18) becomes

\(q= \frac{T_{2}-T_{\infty _{0} }}{(\frac{L}{KA})_{T-111} +\frac{1}{h_{0A} } )} \)

Putting in values in Eq. (3.23), we have

\(321= \frac{T_{2}-31}{(\frac{0.781}{120})_{T-111} +\frac{1}{2\cdot 120} } \)

Solving for \(T_{2}\) we have \(T_{2}\) = 34.4 F.
