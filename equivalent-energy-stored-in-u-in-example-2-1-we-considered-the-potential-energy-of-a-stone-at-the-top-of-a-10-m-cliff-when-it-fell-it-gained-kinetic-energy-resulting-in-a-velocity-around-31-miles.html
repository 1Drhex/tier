Guest Question.

Equivalent Energy Stored in u

In , we considered the potential energy of a stone at the top of a 10-m cliff. When it fell, it gained kinetic energy, resulting in a velocity around 31 miles/hr. Consider now an equivalent mass of water initially at 25ºC. How hot would the water end up if its internal energy increased by the same amount?

Admin
Solution

If we write Equation (2.2) on a per-mass basis, we have:

 

\(E_{K}=\frac{1}{2} m \vec{V}^{2}\) (2.2)

 

\(\Delta \hat{e}_{K}=\frac{1}{2} \vec{V}_{2}^{2}=98[J / kg ]=0.098[ kJ / kg ]\)

 

We have used specifi c energy and converted the units to be consistent with the steam tables. Again we will use state 1 to denote the initial state and state 2 to denote the fi nal state. Liquid water is subcooled at 25ºC and 1 atm; however, we do not expect the properties of a liquid to be signifi cantly affected by pressure. Therefore, we can use the temperature tables for saturated liquid water at 25ºC (which is technically at a pressure of 0.03 atm).4 From Appendix B.1:

 

\(\hat{u}_{l, 1}=104.86[ kJ / kg ]\)

 

The problem statement says the internal energy of the water increases by the same amount as the energy of the stone, that is,

 

\(\Delta \hat{u}=\hat{u}_{l, 2}-\hat{u}_{l, 1}=\Delta \hat{e}_{\kappa}=0.098[ kJ / kg ]\)

 

So for the fi nal state of water, we have:

 

\(\hat{u}_{l, 2}=\Delta \hat{u}+\hat{u}_{l, 1}=104.96[ J / kg ]\)

 

We can now go to the steam tables and determine at which temperature saturated water has this energy. Again we neglect the effect of the pressure difference between the subcooled state and the saturated state. Interpolating, we get:

 

\(\frac{\hat{u}_{l, 2}\left(\text { at } T_{2}\right)-\hat{u}_{1}\left(\text { at } 25\left[{ }^{\circ} C \right]\right)}{\hat{u}_{l}\left(\text { at } 30\left[{ }^{\circ} C \right]\right)-\hat{u}_{l}\left(\text { at } 25\left[{ }^{\circ} C \right]\right)}=\frac{104.96-104.86}{125.77-104.86}=0.005\)

 

Finally, solving for the fi nal temperature yields:

 

\(T_{2}=25+(0.005) 5=25.02^{\circ} C\)

 

The temperature of the water barely changes! Thus the energy stored in a stone 10 m up a cliff corresponds to a negligible amount of internal energy. This example illustrates that a large amount of energy is stored in u relative to the other forms of energy, and, consequently, why we are so interested in internal energy. As engineers, it provides us a large resource to be harvested.

 

\(^{4}\)We often use this trick to fi nd the properties of subcooled water (or other substances) when the pressure is not appreciably different from the saturation pressure. It will serve you well to catalog your experiences of such tricks for reference for solving problems in the future.
