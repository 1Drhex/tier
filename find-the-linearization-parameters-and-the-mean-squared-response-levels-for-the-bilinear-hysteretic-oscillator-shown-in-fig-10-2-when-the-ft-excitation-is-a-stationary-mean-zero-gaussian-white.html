Guest Question.

Find the linearization parameters and the mean-squared response levels for the bilinear hysteretic oscillator shown in Fig. 10.2 when the \( \left\{F(t)\right\} \) excitation is a stationary, mean-zero, Gaussian white noise with autospectral density \( S_{0} \)

Admin
Solution

The system is described by Eq. 10.56 \( m\ddot{X} (t)+c\dot{X} (t)+k_{2}X(t)+g[\left\{X(t)\right\} ]=F(t) \) with \( g[\left\{X(t)\right\} ] \) as given in Fig. 10.1. Recall that the symmetry of the situation gives \( b_{0}=0 \) . To evaluate \( b_{1} \) and \( b_{2} \) we need to evaluate the \( g_{c}(u) \) and \( g_{s}(u) \) functions defined by Eqs. 10.61 \( g_{c}(u)=\frac{1}{\pi } \int_{0}^{2\pi }{\cos (\psi )g[u\cos (\psi )]d\psi } \) and 10.65 \( g_{c}(u)=\frac{1}{\pi } \int_{0}^{2\pi }{\cos (\psi )g[u\cos (\psi )]d\psi } \)

First we note that for \( u\lt x_{y} \) , the elastoplastic term is \( g[u\cos (\psi )]=k_{1}u\cos (\psi ) \) , so \( g_{c}(u)=k_{1}u,g_{s}(u)=0 \) for \( u\lt x_{y} \) . For \( u\gt x_{y} \) , we split the integration with respect to \( \psi \) in Eq. 10.61 \( g_{c}(u)=\frac{1}{\pi } \int_{0}^{2\pi }{\cos (\psi )g[u\cos (\psi )]d\psi } \) into four parts, corresponding to the four straight segments of the hysteresis loop. The result is

\( g_{c}(u)=\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\int_{0}^{\psi ^{\ast }}{\cos (\psi )}k_{1}[x_{y}-u(1-\cos \psi )]d\psi +\int_{\psi ^{\ast }}^{\pi}{\cos (\psi )(-k_{1}x_{y})}d\psi +\int_{\pi}^{\pi +\psi ^{\ast }}{\cos (\psi )k_{1}[-x_{y}+u(1-\cos \psi )]d\psi }+\int_{\pi +\psi ^{\ast }}^{2 \pi}{\cos (\psi )(k_{1}x_{y})d\psi } \right) \)

in which \( \psi ^{\ast }=\cos ^{-1}[(u-2x_{y})/u] \) , as shown in the following sketch. After simplification, this becomes

\( g_{c}(u)=\frac{k_{1}u\psi ^{\ast }}{\pi}-\frac{2k_{1}}{\pi}\left(\frac{u-2x_{y}}{u}\right) [x_{y}(u-x_{y})]^{1/2} \) for \( u\gt x_{y} \)

One can obtain \( g_{s}(u) \) either by performing a similar integration around the loop or by finding the area of the hysteresis loop in Fig. 10.1. The result is

\( g_{s}(u)=-\frac{4k_{1}}{\pi}\frac{x_{y}(u-x_{y})}{u} \) for \( u\gt x_{y} \)

Presuming that the response is narrowband and Gaussian, we now use the Rayleigh probability density function for \( A(t) \)

\( p_{A(t)}(u)=\frac{u}{\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\exp \left(\frac{-u^{2}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\right) \)

and evaluate the expectations of Eqs. 10.63 \( b_{1}=\frac{E\left(A(t)g_{c}[A(t)]\right) }{E[A^{2}(t)]} \) and 10.67 \( b_{2}=\frac{1}{\omega _{a}} \frac{E\left(A(t) g_{s} [A(t)]\right) } {E[A^{2}(t)]} \)

The integral in Eq. 10.63 has the form

\( b_{1}=\frac{2}{\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\left(\int_{0}^{x_{y}}{k_{1}u^{2}}\frac{u}{\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\exp \left[\frac{-u^{2}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\right]du +\int_{x_{y}}^{\infty }{\left[\frac{k_{1}u}{\pi}\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{u-2x_{y}}{u} \right)-\frac{2k_{1}}{\pi}\left(\frac{u-2x_{y}}{u} \right)[x_{y}(u-x_{y})]^{1/2} \right]\frac{u}{\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\exp \left[\frac{-u^{2}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}} \right] }du \right) \)

and Caughey (1960b) showed that it can be converted into

\( b_{1}=k_{1}\left(1-\frac{8}{\pi}\int_{1}^{\infty }{\left[\frac{1}{z^{3}}+\frac{x^{2}_{y}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}z} \right](z-1)^{1/2}\exp \left[\frac{-x^{2}_{y}z^{2}}{2\sigma ^{2}_{X}} \right]dz } \right) \)

No closed form has been found for this integral, but it can be easily evaluated numerically if one first chooses a value for the dimensionless ratio \( \sigma _{X}/x_{y} \) . Note that \( \sigma _{X}/x_{y} \) can viewed as a root-mean-square (rms) ductility ratio, because it represents the rms response displacement divided by the yield displacement. The evaluation of \( b_{2} \) is somewhat easier. Writing out the integral for the expected value in Eq. 10.67 \( b_{2}=\frac{1}{\omega _{a}} \frac{E\left(A(t) g_{s} [A(t)]\right) } {E[A^{2}(t)]} \) gives

\( b_{2}=\frac{8k_{1}}{\pi \omega _{\alpha }\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\int_{x_{y}}^{\infty }{x_{y}(u-x_{y})}\frac{u}{\sigma ^{2}_{X}}\exp [-u^{2}/(2\sigma ^{2}_{X})]du \)

and this reduces to

\( b_{2}=\left(\frac{2}{\pi} \right) ^{1/2}\frac{k_{1}x_{y}}{\omega _{\alpha }\sigma _{X}}\left(1-\Phi (x_{y}/\sigma _{X})\right) \)

in which \( \Phi (\cdot ) \) denotes the cumulative distribution function for a standardized Gaussian random variable and is related to the error function (see ). The following sketches show normalized values of \( b_{1} \) and \( b_{2} \) for a fairly wide range of \( \sigma _{X}/x_{y} \) values.

The decay of \( b_{1} \) from its initial value of \( k_{1} \) for \( \sigma _{X}\lt \lt x_{y} \) to its final value of zero for \( \sigma _{X}\gt \gt x_{y} \) corresponds to \( \omega ^{2}_{a} \) varying from \( (k_{1}+k_{2})/m \) to \( k_{2}/m \) . If the excitation is very small, then \( \sigma _{X}\lt \lt x_{y} \) and the elastoplastic element experiences little or no yielding, so the \( k_{1} \) spring does contribute effectively to the system stiffness. If the excitation is very large so that \( \sigma _{X}\gt \gt x_{y} \) , then the force added by the elastoplastic element is relatively small, so the total stiffness approaches \( k_{2} \) . The damping effect of the elastoplastic element has a rather different characteristic. The dashpot effect is the greatest when \( \sigma _{X}\approx 1.5x_{y} \) and is near zero for both very large and very small values of \( \sigma _{X}/x_{y} \) . Of course, the energy dissipated per cycle does continue to grow as \( \sigma _{X} \) increases beyond \( 1.5X_{y} \) , even though the value of the “equivalent” dashpot coefficient \( b_{2}\) decreases.

One can use an iterative procedure to find the solution of Eqs. 10.63 \( b_{1}=\frac{E\left(A(t)g_{c}[A(t)]\right) }{E[A^{2}(t)]} \), 10.67 \( b_{2}=\frac{1}{\omega _{a}} \frac{E\left(A(t) g_{s} [A(t)]\right) } {E[A^{2}(t)]} \), 10.68 \( \omega _{a}=\left(\frac{k_{2}+b_{1}}{m} \right) ^{1/2} \), and 10.69 \( \sigma _{X}^{2} = \frac{\pi S_{0}}{(c+b_{2})(k_{2}+b_{1})} \) for given values of the system parameters \( m, c, k_{1}, k_{2} \) , and \( x_{y} \) , and the excitation level \( S_{0} \) . Alternatively, one can consider fixed values of some parameters and plot the results versus other parameters. We include three plots of this form, in which \( \sigma _{X}/\sigma ^{\ast }_{F} \) is plotted versus \( \sigma ^{\ast }_{F}/x_{y} \) , with \( \sigma ^{\ast }_{F} \) defined as

\( (\sigma ^{\ast }_{F})^{2}=\frac{2S_{0}\omega _{0}}{(k_{1}+k_{2})^{2}} \)

with \( \omega _{0}=[(k_{1}+k_{2})/m]^{1/2}= \) resonant frequency for the linear system that results when \( x_{y} \) is infinite. For each curve the values of \( k_{2}/k_{1} \) and \( \zeta _{0}\equiv c/(2[(k_{1}+k_{2})m]^{1/2}) \) are held constant. Note that for given system parameters, \( \sigma ^{\ast }_{F} \) is a measure of the level of the excitation. Thus, any curve of the type given can be considered to represent response normalized by the excitation plotted versus the excitation normalized by the yield level. The curves were obtained by choosing a value of \( \sigma _{X}/x_{y} \) , evaluating \( b_{1}/k_{1} \) and \( \omega _{a}b_{2}/k_{1} \) as for the preceding plots, using Eq. 10.68 \( \omega _{a}=\left(\frac{k_{2}+b_{1}}{m} \right) ^{1/2} \) for the \( \omega _{a} \) term in the latter of these expressions, and finally using Eq. 10.69 \( \sigma _{X}^{2} = \frac{\pi S_{0}}{(c+b_{2})(k_{2}+b_{1})} \) to give the response variance.

The plots include three different values of \( k_{2}/k_{1} \) , to represent three different situations. For \( k_{2}/k_{1}=9 \) , it is reasonable to consider the nonlinearity to be small so that the assumptions used in the linearization procedure should be appropriate. For \( k_{2}/k_{1}=1 \) the system can lose up to half of its stiffness due to yielding and there is considerable hysteretic energy dissipation, so it is questionable whether the narrowband and Gaussian assumptions are appropriate for \( \left\{X(t)\right\} \) . The situation with \( k_{2}/k_{1}=0.05 \) goes far beyond the range in which it is reasonable to expect the assumptions to be valid. Numerical data for the response levels of the bilinear hysteretic oscillator with \( k_{2}/k_{1}=1 \) and \( k_{2}/k_{1}=0.05 \) were presented by lwan and Lutes (1968), and some of these data are reproduced in the plots. In particular, the plots include data for \( \zeta _{0}=0 \) and \( \zeta _{0}=0.01 \) for \( k_{2}/k_{1}=1 \) and for \( \zeta _{0}=0 \) and \( \zeta _{0}=0.05 \) for \( k_{2 }/k_{1}=0.05\) .

Somewhat surprisingly, the data show that the Iinearization results are generally quite good for \( k_{2}/k_{1}=1 \) , even though this is hardly a situation with a small amount of nonlinearity. For \( k_{2} /k_{1}=0.05\) , on the other hand, there are sometimes significant discrepancies between the results from the bilinear hysteretic system and those from linearization.
