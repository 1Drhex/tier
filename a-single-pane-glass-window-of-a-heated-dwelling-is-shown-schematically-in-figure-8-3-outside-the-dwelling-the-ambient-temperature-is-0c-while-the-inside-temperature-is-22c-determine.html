Guest Question.

A single-pane glass window of a heated dwelling is shown schematically in Figure 8.3. Outside the dwelling, the ambient temperature is \(0^{\circ } C\), while the inside temperature is \(22^{\circ }C\). Determine the heat lost through the glass per unit area. The glass thickness is 5 mm, and the window height is 60 cm.

Admin
Solution

Outside the dwelling we have cold air in contact with a warmed vertical surface. Inside we have warm air in contact with a cooler vertical surface. Natural convection is important on both sides of the glass. Figure 8.3 shows also the expected temperature profile and the thermal circuit.

Assumptions

1. One-dimensional heat flow exists.
2. Air properties are evaluated at the ambient temperatures, because surface temperatures are unknown.
3. Steady-state conditions exist.
4. End and edge effects are negligible.

For the one-dimensional flow of Figure 8.3, we write

\(q=\frac{Overall temperature difference}{Sumof thermal resistances}\)

 

\(=\frac{T_{\infty 2}-T_{\infty 1} }{\frac{1}{\overline{h} _{c1}A }+\frac{t}{KA} +\frac{1}{\overline{h} _{c2}A} }\)

or

\(\frac{q}{A}=q^{''} =\frac{T_{\infty 1}- T_{\infty 2} }{\frac{1}{\overline{h} _{c1} }+\frac{t}{k} +\frac{1}{\overline{h} _{c2} } } \)

 

The convection coefficients can be calculated with the Churchill–Chu equation if \(Ra_{L} \lt 10^{9}\) .From Appendix Table D.1, for air at \(22+273=295K\cong 300K,\)

 

\(\rho =1.177kg/m^{3}\) \( k_{f} =0.02624W/\left(m.K\right)\)

 

\(c_{p} =1005J/\left(kg.K\right) \) \( \alpha =0.22160\times 10^{-4} m^{2} /s\)

 

\(\nu =15.68\times 10^{-6} m^{2} /s\) Pr=0.708

 

Also,

\(\beta =1/295=0.00338/K\)

 

For air at \(0 + 273 \cong 275 K\), we interpolate to get

 

\(\rho =1.295 kg/m^{3}\) \( k_{f} =0.02426W/\left(m.K\right)\)

 

\(c_{p} =1005.5J/\left(kg.K\right) \) \( \alpha =0.17661\times 10^{-4} m^{2} /s\)

 

\(\nu =12.59\times 10^{-6} m^{2} /s\) Pr=0.713

 

Also,

\(\beta =1/273=0.00366/K\)

 

From Appendix Table B.3, for glass, \(k=0.81 W/\left(m.K\right)\) .The Rayleigh number \(\left(=Gr Pr\right)\) for use in
the Churchill–Chu equation is

 

\(Ra_{L} =\frac{g_{x}\beta \left(T_{w}-T_{\infty } \right)L^{3} }{\nu \alpha }\)

 

The wall temperatures are unknown, so we will have to formulate an iterative procedure to solve the problem. Substituting for the outside wall, we find

\(Ra_{L_{1} } =\frac{9.81\left(0.00366\right) \left(T_{w1}-0 \right)\left(0.60\right) ^{3} }{12.59\times 10^{-6}\left(0.17661\times 10^{-4} \right) } =3.49\times 10^{7} \left(T_{w1} -0\right)\)

 

For the inside surface,

\(Ra_{L_{2} } =\frac{9.81\left(0.00338\right) \left(T_{w2}-22 \right)\left(0.60\right) ^{3} }{15.68\times 10^{-6}\left(0.22160\times 10^{-4} \right) }\)

 

\(=-2.06\times 10^{7} \left(T_{w2} -22\right)\)

 

where the negative sign denotes that the surface is cooler than the ambient temperature (i.e., gravity is in the positive x direction). We now assume wall temperatures \(T_{w1}\) and \(T_{w2}\), then calculate the Rayleigh and Nusselt numbers. The convection coefficients and the heat-transfer rate (Equation i) are then determined. From these numbers, we calculate refined estimates of the

wall temperatures. The process is repeated until convergence is achieved. Assume \(T_{w2}=18^{\circ } C\) and \(T_{w1}=4^{\circ }C\). Then,

 

\(Ra_{L_{1} }=1.396\times 10^{8}\)

 

\(Ra_{L_{2} }=8.24\times 10^{7}\)

 

From Equation 8.20,

\(\overline{Nu} _{L} =\frac{\overline{h} _{L}L }{k_{f} } =0.68\mid +\frac{0.67Ra^{1/4}_{L} }{\left[1+\left\lgroup\frac{0.492}{Pr} \right\rgroup ^{9/16} \right] ^{4/9} }\)

 

we find

\(\overline{h} _{c1}=\frac{0.02426}{0.6}\left\{0.68+\frac{0.67\left(1.396\times 10^{8} \right) ^{1/4} }{\left[1+\left\lgroup\frac{0.492}{0.713} \right\rgroup ^{9/16} \right] ^{4/9} } \right\}\)

 

\(\overline{h} _{c1} =2.29 W/\left(m^{2}.K \right)\)

 

Also,

\(\overline{h} _{c2} =\frac{0.02624}{0.6}\left\{0.68+\frac{0.67\left(8.24\times 10^{7} \right) ^{1/4} }{\left[1+\left\lgroup\frac{0.492}{0.708} \right\rgroup ^{9/16} \right] ^{4/9} } \right\}\)

 

\(\overline{h} _{c2} =2.17 W/\left(m^{2}.K \right)\)

 

The heat flux from Equation i is

\(q^{"} =\frac{22-0}{1/2.29+0.005/0.81+1/2.17,} =24.3W/m^{2}\)

 

For each surface,

\(q^{"} =\frac{T_{\infty 2}-T_{w2} }{1/\overline{h} _{c2} }\)

 

\(q^{"} =\frac{T_{w1}-T_{\infty 1} }{1/\overline{h} _{c1} }\)

 

Solving for wall temperature,

\(T_{w2} =22-\left(24.3\right) \left(1/2.17\right) =10.8^{\circ } C\)

 

and

\(T_{w1}=\frac{24.3}{2.29} +0=10.6^{\circ } C\)

 

Using these temperatures as second estimates and repeating the calculations, we get

\(Ra_{L1} =3.70\times 10^{8} \) , \(\overline{h} _{c} =2.92W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

\(Ra_{L2}=2.31\times 10^{8}\), \( \overline{h} _{c} =2.80W/\left(m^{2}.K \right) \)

 

The heat flux is

\(q^{"} =\frac{22-0}{1/2.92+0.005/0.81+1/2.80} =31.2W/m^{2} \)

 

The wall temperatures are

\(T_{w2} =22-31.2/2.80=10.8^{\circ } C\)

 

\(T_{w1} =10.7^{\circ }C\)

 

These values are close enough to the trial values. The method converges rapidly. The heat loss then is

\(q^{"} =31.2 W/m^{2}\)
