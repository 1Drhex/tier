Guest Question.

As a design example, consider the heat exchanger system in Figure below. The block labeled “equipment” represents all items (except the control valve) that reduce pressure in the pipeline (e.g., piping fittings and other obstructions). Denote the pressure drop for the “equipment” as the system pressure drop \(ΔP_{s}\) and its value at the design conditions as \(ΔP_{sd}\). Similarly, the pressure drop across the control valve is denoted by \(ΔP_{v}\) and its design value as \(ΔP_{vd}\).
Suppose that the nonflashing liquid has a specific gravity of \( g_{s} = 1 \) and the design flow rate is \(q_{d} = 200 gpm\). At this design flow rate, the equipment pressure drop is\(ΔP_{sd}=40 psi\) . The pump discharge pressure \(P_{2}\) is assumed to be constant and the discharge pressure at the pipe exit is atmospheric, \(P_{1} = 0 psig\). Since \(P_{1} \ and \space P_{2}\) are constant, the total pressure drop ΔP is also constant. It can be expressed as
\(ΔP = P_{2} − P_{1} = ΔP_{v} + ΔP_{s}\) (1)
Assume that \(ΔP_{s}\) varies with square of the flow rate:
\(ΔP_{s} = Kq^{2}\) (2)
The value of constant K can be determined from Equation (2) and the design conditions
\(K=\frac{\Delta P_{sd}}{q^{2}_{d}}=\frac{40psi}{(200gpm)^{2}}=0.001psi/gpm^{2} \) (3)

(a) For five values of \(ΔP_{vd}\) (10, 20, 40, 100, and 200 psi) determine the corresponding values of \(C_{v}\).
(b) Determine the maximum and minimum values of q (\(q_{\max} \ and \ q_{\min}\)) for each value of \(C_{v}\).

Admin
Solution

(a) The values of \(C_{v}\) can be determined from Equation
\(C_{v}=\frac{q_{d}}{0.5N\sqrt{\frac{\Delta P_{vd}}{g_{s}} } } \)
with \(N = 1 gpm/(psi)^{1/2} \space and \space g_{s} = 1\). The resulting \(C_{v}\) values are 126.5, 89.4, 63.2, 36.5 and 28.3, respectively.

(b) Find \(q_{\max}\):
Consider Equation
\(q=C_{v}Nf(\ell )\sqrt{\frac{\Delta P_{v}}{g_{s}} } \)
with \(N = 1, q = q_{\max} \ and \space f = 1\) (because the maximum flow rate occurs when the control valve is full open):
\(q_{\max}=C_{v}\sqrt{\Delta P_{v}}\) (4)
As noted earlier, \(ΔP_{v}\) changes with q. Thus \(ΔP_{v}≠ ΔP_{vd}\) when \(q ≠ q_{d}\). Rearranging Equation (1)
\(ΔP_{v} = ΔP − ΔP_{s}\) (5)

and substituting Eqsuations (2) and (3) into Equation (4) with \(q = q_{\max}\) gives
\(q_{\max }=C_{v}\sqrt{\frac{\Delta P-Kq^{2}_{\max}}{g_{s}} } \) (6)
Solving Equation (6) for \(q_{\max}\) after substituting Equation (3) gives the desired result:
\(q_{\max }=K_{1}\sqrt{\frac{\Delta Pq^{2}_{d}}{q^{2}_{d}+K^{2}_{1}\Delta P_{sd}} } \) (7)
where \(K_{1} = C_{v}/ \sqrt{g_{s}}\).

Find \(q_{\min}\):
A similar derivation with f = 0.1 (the minimum value of f for effective flow control) gives an expression for \(q_{\min}\):
\(q_{\min }=K_{2}\sqrt{\frac{\Delta Pq^{2}_{d}}{q^{2}_{d}+K^{2}_{1}\Delta P_{sd}} } \) (8)
where \(K_{2} = 0.1 C_{v}/ \sqrt{g_{s}}\).
The results for this example are shown in Table 9.3.

Some important trends in Table 9.3 are
1. For small values of \(ΔP_{vd}, q_{\max}\) is not much greater than \(q_{d} = 200 gpm \) and R is small.
2. As \(ΔP_{vd}\) increases, \(q_{\max}\) increases, \(q_{\min}\) decreases, and R increases. Thus from a control perspective, a large value of \(ΔP_{vd}\), is desirable to provide effective flow control over a larger range.
3. But as\(ΔP_{vd}\) increases, ΔP also increases (see Equation (1)). Thus, a larger pump is required and the pump operating cost increases due to the cost of power for the pump motor. Thus from an economic perspective, a small value of \(ΔP_{vd}\) is desirable. Note that it is also possible to simultaneously calculate the required pump discharge pressure \(P_{2}\) and the control valve sizing coefficient \(C_{v}\) that produce the desired values of \(q_{\max} \space and \space q_{\min}\). Thus, Equations (7) and (8) can be solved simultaneously for the two unknowns, ΔP and \(C_{v}\) (Luyben and Luyben, 1996, pp. 76–81). Then \(P_{2}\) can be determined from Equation(1).
