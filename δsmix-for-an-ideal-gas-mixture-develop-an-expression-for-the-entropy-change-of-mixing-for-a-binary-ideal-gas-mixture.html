Guest Question.

\(\Delta s_{m i x}\), for an Ideal Gas Mixture

Develop an expression for the entropy change of mixing for a binary ideal gas mixture.

Admin
Solution

This example is a generalization of . A schematic of the process represented by \(\Delta S _{m i x}\) is shown in Figure E6.11A. By defi nition the temperature and pressure of the pure species must be the same as the mixture. Therefore, the (extensive) volume of the mixture will be larger than each of the pure species.

We are free to choose any path to calculate the entropies in Equation (E6.11A). One possible solution path is shown in Figure 6.11B. In the fi rst step (Step I), each of the pure species, a and b, are isothermally expanded to the size of the container of the mixture. During this process, the pressure drops to \(p_{a}\) and \(p_{b}\), the partial pressure of species a and b in the mixture. The next step (Step II) is superimposing both these expanded systems. For this process,

 

\(\Delta S_{m i x}=\Delta S_{a}^{I}+\Delta S_{b}^{I}+\Delta S^{I I}\) (E6.11A)

 

We begin by applying Equation (3.22) at constant temperature to species a:

 

\(\Delta s=\Delta s_{\text {step } 2}+\Delta s_{\text {stepl }}=\int_{T_{1}}^{T_{2}} \frac{c_{P}}{T} d T-R \ln \left(\frac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}\right)\) (3.22)

 

\(\Delta s_{a}^{I}=-R \ln \frac{p_{a}}{P}=-R \ln \frac{y_{a} P}{P}=-R \ln y_{a}\)

 

For the extensive entropy we multiply by the number of moles of a to get:

 

\(\Delta S_{a}^{I}=n_{a} \Delta s_{a}^{I}=-R n_{a} \ln y_{a}\) (E6.11B)

 

Similarly for b:

 

\(\Delta s_{b}=-R \ln y_{b}\)

 

and,

 

\(\Delta S_{b}^{I}=n_{b} \Delta s_{b}^{I}=-R n_{b} \ln y_{b}\) (E6.11C)

 

For step II, we recognize that ideal gas a does not know b is there and vice versa. Thus, the properties of each individual species do not change and:

 

\(\Delta S^{I I}=n_{a} \Delta s_{a}^{I I}+n_{b} \Delta s_{b}^{I I}=0\) (E6.11D)

 

The path shown in Figure E6.11B has interesting implications in terms of the molecular viewpoint of the entropy increase for species a. Increased entropy is not directly from b mixing in a, but rather that species a has more room in which to move so that there is more uncertainty where it is; therefore, information is lost, and the entropy is higher.

Substituting Equations (E6.11B), (E6.11C), and (E6.11D) in (E6.11A) gives:

 

\(\Delta S_{m i x}=-R\left(n_{a} \ln y_{a}+n_{b} \ln y_{b}\right)\)

 

To find the intensive entropy change of mixing, we divide by the total number of moles:

 

\(\Delta s_{\operatorname{mix}}=\frac{\Delta S_{\operatorname{mix}}}{n_{a}+n_{b}}=-R\left(y_{a} \ln y_{a}+y_{b} \ln y_{b}\right)\)

 

In general, when there are m species, we can sum together similar contributions from each species to get:

 

\(\Delta s_{m i x}=-R \sum y_{i} \ln y_{i}\)

 

Equation (E6.11E) is presented in the text as Equation (6.25).

 

\(\Delta s_{\text {mix }}=-R \sum_{i=1}^{m} y_{i}, \ln y_{i}\) (6.25)
