Guest Question.

Find the state-space representation of the transfer function shown in Figure 3.12(a).

Admin
Solution

Step 1 Separate the system into two cascaded blocks, as shown in Figure 3.12(b). The first block contains the denominator and the second block contains the numerator.

Step 2 Find the state equations for the block containing the denominator. Hence, the state equation is

\(\begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}_{1}\\ \dot{x}_{2} \\ \dot{x}_{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ -24 & -26 & – 9 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\1 \end{bmatrix} r\) (3:63)

Step 3 Introduce the effect of the block with the numerator. The second block of Figure 3.12(b), where \(b_{2} = 1\); \(b_{1} = 7\), and \( b_{0} = 2\), states that

\(C(s) = ( b_{2}s^{2} + b_{1}s + b_{0}) X_{1} (s) = (s^{2} + 7s + 2) X_{1} (s)\) (3:64)

Taking the inverse Laplace transform with zero initial conditions, we get

\(c = \ddot {x_{1}} + 7\dot {x_{1}} + 2x_{1}\) (3:65)

But
\(x_{1} = x_{1}\)
\(\dot {x_{1}}= x_{2}\)
\(\ddot {x_{1}} = x_{3}\)

Hence,

\(y = c(t) = b_{2}x_{3} + b_{1}x_{2} + b_{0}x_{1} = x_{3} + x_{2} + 2x_{1}\) (3:66)

Thus, the last box of Figure 3.11(b)(the third one below) ‘‘collects’’ the states and generates the output equation. From Eq. (3.66),

\(y = \begin{bmatrix} b_{0} & b_{1} & b_{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 7 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \\ x_{3} \end{bmatrix}\) (3:67)

Although the second block of Figure 3.12(b) shows differentiation, this block was implemented without differentiation because of the partitioning that was applied to the transfer function. The last block simply collected derivatives that were already formed by the first block.

Once again we can produce an equivalent block diagram that vividly represents our state-space model. The first block of Figure 3.12(b) is the same as Figure 3.10(a) below except for the different constant in the numerator. Thus, in Figure 3.12(c) we reproduce Figure 3.10(b) below except for the change in the numerator constant, which appears as a change in the input multiplying factor. The second block of Figure 3.12(b) is represented using Eq. (3.66), which forms the output from a linear combination of the state variables, as shown in Figure 3.12(c).
