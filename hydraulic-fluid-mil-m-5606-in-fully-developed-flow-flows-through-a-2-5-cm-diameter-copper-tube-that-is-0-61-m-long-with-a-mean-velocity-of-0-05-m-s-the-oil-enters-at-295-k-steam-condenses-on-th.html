Guest Question.

Hydraulic fluid (MIL-M-5606), in fully developed flow, flows through a 2.5-cm-diameter copper tube that is 0.61 m long, with a mean velocity of 0.05 m/s. The oil enters at 295 K. Steam condenses on the outside tube surface with an effective heat-transfer coefficient of \( 11,400 W/m^{2} \cdot K \). find the rate of heat transfer to the oil.

Admin
Solution

To evaluate oil properties at either the film temperature or the mean bulk temperature, we need to know the exiting oil temperature. Equation (\( \frac{dT}{dx} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} \left ( T – T_{s} \right ) = 0 \) ) applies in this case:

 

\( ln \frac{T_{L} – T_{s}}{T_{o} – T_{s}} + 4 \frac{L}{D} \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon c_{p}} = 0 \)

 

If the thermal resistance of the copper tube wall is negligible, the heat-transfer rate can be expressed as

\( q = \frac{A_{surf} \left ( T_{stm} – T_{oil} \right )}{1/h_{i} – 1/h_{o}} = \rho A \upsilon c_{p} \left ( T_{L} – T_{o} \right ) \)

 

To get an indication of whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, we will assume a bulk oil temperature of 300 K. The Reynolds number, at this temperature, is

 

\( Re_{D} = \frac{\left ( 0.025 m \right )\left ( 0.05 m/s \right )}{9.94 \times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s} = 126 \)

 

and the flow is clearly laminar. The heat-transfer coefficient on the oil side can then be determined using equation ( \( Nu_{D} = 1.86 \left ( Pe \frac{D}{L} \right )^{1/3} \left ( \frac{\mu _{b}}{\mu _{w}} \right )^{0.14} \) )

 

\( h_{i} = \frac{k}{D} Nu_{D} = \frac{k}{D} 1.86 \left ( Pe \frac{D}{L} \right )^{0.33} \left ( \frac{\mu_{b}}{\mu_{w}} \right )^{0.14} \)

 

Initially, the bulk temperature of the oil and the wall temperature will be assumed to be 300 and 372 K, respectively. Using fluid properties at these temperatures, we have

 

\( h_{i} = \frac{\left ( 0.123 W/m \cdot K \right )\left ( 1.86 \right )}{0.025 m} Nu_{D} = \left [ \left ( 126 \right )\left ( 155 \right ) \frac{0.025}{0.61}\right ]^{0.33} \left ( \frac{1.036 \times 10^{-4}}{3.72 \times 10^{-3}} \right )^{0.14} = 98.1 W/m^{2} \cdot K \)

 

Substituting into equation (\( \frac{dT}{dx} + \frac{h}{\rho \upsilon_{x} c_{p}} \frac{4}{D} \left ( T – T_{s} \right ) = 0 \)), we get

 

\( ln \frac{T_{s} – T_{L}}{T_{s} – T_{o}} = – 4 \left ( \frac{0.61 m}{0.025 m} \right) \frac{98.1 W/m^{2} \cdot K}{\left ( 843 kg/m^{3} \right ) \left ( 0.05 m/s \right )\left ( 1897 J/kg \cdot K \right )} = – 0.120 \)

 

\( \frac{T_{s} – T_{L}}{T_{s} – T_{o}} = e^{- 0.120} = 0.887 \)

 

\( T_{L} = 372 – 0.887 \left ( 372 – 295 \right ) = 304 K \)

 

With this value of \( T_{L} \), the mean bulk temperature of the oil is

\( T_{b} = \frac{295 + 304}{2} = 299.5 K \)

 

which is sufficiently close to the initial assumption that there is no need to iterate further.
With an exiting temperature of 304 K, the heat-transfer rate to the oil is

\( q = \rho A \upsilon c_{p} \left ( T_{L} – T_{o} \right ) \)

 

\( = \left ( 843 kg/m^{3} \right )\left ( \frac{\pi }{4} \right )\left ( 0.025 m \right )^{2}\left ( 0.05 m/s \right )\left ( 1897 J/kg \cdot K \right )\left ( 9 K \right ) = 353 W \)
