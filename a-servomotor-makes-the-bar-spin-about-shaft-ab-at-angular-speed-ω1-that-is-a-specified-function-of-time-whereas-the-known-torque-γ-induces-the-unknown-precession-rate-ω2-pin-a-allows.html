Guest Question.

A servomotor makes the bar spin about shaft \(A B\) at angular speed \(\Omega_{1}\) that is a specified function of time, whereas the known torque \(\Gamma\) induces the unknown precession rate \(\Omega_{2} .\) Pin \(A\) allows the angle \(\theta\) to change. The bar, which may be considered to be thin, has mass are \(m_{1}\), whereas the mass of shaft \(A B\) is \(m_{2}\), and the moment of inertia of the vertical shaft about its axis of rotation is \(I_{3}\). Derive the equations of motion and an expression for the required torque.

Admin
Solution

This example generalizes the techniques for treating spatial motion by introducing rotation transformations to describe the motion of a nonaxisymmetric body. In terms of Eulerian angles \(\Omega_{1}=\dot{\phi}\) is the spin rate, \(\Omega_{2}=\dot{\psi}\) is the precession rate, and \(\theta\) is the nutation angle. Integrating the spin rate subject to the initial condition that \(\phi=0\) at \(t=0\) gives

\(\phi=\int_{0}^{t} \Omega_{1}(\tau) d \tau. (1)\)

Hence, the position of all parts is specified by knowing \(t\), which gives \(\phi\), and the corresponding values of the other Eulerian angles. Thus we select as the generalized coordinates, \(q_{1}=\psi\) and \(q_{2}=\theta\). To ensure that we recognize the functional dependence of all terms, we denote the respective rotation rates as \(\dot{\psi}\) and \(\dot{\phi}\), rather than \(\Omega_{1}\) and \(\Omega_{2}\).

The vertical shaft’s bearing permits rotation only about its axis. Thus a freebody diagram of the system would show, at that bearing, a force-couple reaction that is arbitrary, except that the couple would have no vertical component. The free-body diagram also would show the conservative gravity forces acting on the flywheel and shaft \(A B\), and the nonconservative torque \(\Gamma\).

Unlike the previous example, the present system is not axisymmetric, so describing the kinetic energy will require somewhat more effort. The sketch defines two moving coordinate systems: \(x y z\) is attached to the bar, whereas \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) only processes and nutates. Both the \(z^{\prime}\) and \(z\) axes coincide with the spin axis, and \(x^{\prime}\) is defined to align with the line of nodes, whereas the \(x\) axis is parallel to the bar’s longitudinal axis. The angle between \(x^{\prime}\) and \(x\) is \(\phi\).

The \(x y z\) coordinate system executes a simple rotation about the \(z^{\prime}\) axis relative to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\), so the associated rotation transformation is

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}x \\\\y \\\\z\end{array}\right\}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \phi & \sin \phi & 0 \\\\-\sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}x^{\prime} \\\\y^{\prime} \\\\z^{\prime}\end{array}\right\}. (4) \)

The angular velocity of the bar is readily found in terms of \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) components to be \(\bar{\omega}_{\mathrm{bar}}=\dot{\theta} \bar{i}^{\prime}+\dot{\psi}\left(\sin \theta \bar{j}^{\prime}+\cos \theta \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)+\dot{\phi} \bar{k}^{\prime} .\) The introduction of rotation transformations makes it convenient to switch to matrix notation. Then the \(x y z\) components are given by

\(\left\{\omega_{\mathrm{bar}}\right\}=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}\cos \phi & \sin \phi & 0 \\\\-\sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}\dot{\theta} \\\\\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \\\\\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}\dot{\theta} \cos \phi+\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \sin \phi \\\\-\dot{\theta} \sin \phi+\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \cos \phi \\\\\psi \cos \theta+\dot{\phi}\end{array}\right\}. (5) \)

The inertia matrix for the bar relative to \(x y z\) is obtained from the tabulated properties and the parallel axis theorems, which give

\(\left[I_{\mathrm{bar}}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}m_{1} L^{2} & 0 & 0 \\\\0 & \frac{1}{12} m_{1}(2 R)^{2}+m_{1} L^{2} & 0 \\\\0 & 0 & \frac{1}{12} m_{1}(2 R)^{2}\end{array}\right]. \)

The kinetic energy of the bar is \(T_{\text {bar }}=\frac{1}{2}\left\{\omega_{\text {bar }}\right\}^{\mathrm{T}}\left[I_{\mathrm{bar}}\right]\left\{\omega_{\mathrm{bar}}\right\} .\) The \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) axes are principal axes for shaft \(A B\), with \(I_{x^{\prime} x^{\prime}}=I_{y^{\prime} y^{\prime}}=(1 / 3) m_{2} L^{2}, I_{z^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=0\), and the angular velocity of this shaft is \(\bar{\omega}_{A B}=\dot{\theta} \bar{i}^{\prime}+\dot{\psi}\left(\sin \theta \bar{j}^{\prime}+\cos \theta \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)\). The vertical shaft is in pure rotation at rate \(\dot{\psi}\), and its moment of inertia about its rotation axis is \(I_{3}\). The sum of the kinetic energy of each body is

\(\begin{aligned}T_{\text {bar }}=& \frac{1}{2}\left[\left(m_{1} L^{2}\right)(\dot{\theta} \cos \phi+\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \sin \phi)^{2}\right.\\\\&+\left(\frac{1}{3} m_{1} R^{2}+m_{1} L^{2}\right)(-\dot{\theta} \sin \phi+\dot{\psi} \sin \theta \cos \phi)^{2} \\\\&\left.+\left(\frac{1}{3} m_{1} R^{2}\right)(\dot{\psi} \cos \theta+\dot{\phi})^{2}\right]+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{3} m_{2} L^{2}\right)\left[(\dot{\psi} \sin \theta)^{2}+\dot{\theta}^{2}\right]+\frac{1}{2} I_{3} \dot{\psi}^{2} .\end{aligned} (6) \)

Collecting like coefficients of the generalized velocities in \(T\) expedites evaluation of the derivatives in Lagrange’s equations, so we write the expression as

\(\begin{aligned}T=& \frac{1}{2}\left\{\left(I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}\right)(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{1}^{\prime}\left[1-(\sin \theta)^{2}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right]+I_{3}\right\} \dot{\psi}^{2} \\\\&+\frac{1}{2}\left[I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}+I_{2}^{\prime}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right] \dot{\theta}^{2}+\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\phi}^{2}-\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\psi} \dot{\theta}(\sin \theta)(\sin 2 \phi) \\\\&+I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\psi} \dot{\phi}(\cos \theta),\end{aligned} (7) \)

where

\(I_{1}^{\prime}=\frac{1}{3} m_{1} R^{2}, \quad I_{2}^{\prime}=m_{1} L^{2}, \quad I_{3}^{\prime}=\frac{1}{3} m_{2} L^{2}.\)

The elevations of the centers of mass depend only on \(\theta .\) The corresponding potential energy is

\(V=-\left(m_{1}+\frac{1}{2} m_{2}\right) g L \cos \theta (8)\)

We may now form Lagrange’s equations for each generalized coordinate. The derivatives of \(T\) are more intricate than they were in the previous problem because the precession angle now is a generalized coordinate and the spin rate is variable. The derivatives are

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\psi}}\right)=& \frac{d}{d t}\left\{\left[\left(I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}\right)(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{1}^{\prime}\left[1-(\sin \theta)^{2}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right]+I_{3}\right] \dot{\psi}\right.\\\\ & \left.-\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\theta}(\sin \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)+I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\phi}(\cos \theta)\right\} \\\\=&\left\{\left(I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}\right)(\sin \theta)^{2}+I_{1}^{\prime}\left[1-(\sin \theta)^{2}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right]+I_{3}\right\} \ddot{\psi} \\\\ &-\left[\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \ddot{\theta}+I_{1}^{\prime} \ddot{\phi}(\cos \theta) \\\\ &+\left[\left(I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}-I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right)(\sin 2 \theta)\right] \dot{\psi} \dot{\theta}-\left[I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \theta)^{2}(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \dot{\psi} \dot{\phi} \\\\ &-\left[\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime}(\cos \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \dot{\theta}^{2}-\left[2 I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \theta)(\cos \phi)^{2}\right] \dot{\theta} \dot{\phi}, \end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{d}{d t}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{\theta}}\right)=& \frac{d}{d t}\left\{\left[I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}+I_{2}^{\prime}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right] \dot{\theta}-\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\psi}(\sin \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)\right\} \\\\=&\left[I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}+I_{2}^{\prime}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right] \ddot{\theta}-\left[\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \ddot{\psi} \\\\&+\left[I_{2}^{\prime}(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \dot{\theta} \dot{\phi}-\frac{1}{2}\left[I_{1}^{\prime}(\cos \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)\right] \dot{\psi} \dot{\theta} \\\\&-\left[I_{1}^{\prime}(\sin \theta)(\cos 2 \phi)\right] \dot{\psi} \dot{\phi}, \end{aligned} \)

\(\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial T}{\partial \psi}=0, \quad \frac{\partial T}{\partial \theta}=& \frac{1}{2}\left[I_{2}^{\prime}+I_{3}^{\prime}-I_{1}^{\prime 2}(\sin \phi)^{2}\right](\sin 2 \theta) \dot{\psi}^{2} \\\\ &-\frac{1}{2} I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\psi} \dot{\theta}(\cos \theta)(\sin 2 \phi)-I_{1}^{\prime} \dot{\psi} \dot{\phi}(\sin \theta), \end{aligned}\)

We could proceed to substitute the preceding terms into Lagrange’s equations, but there is little to be gained from doing so because we will not solve the resulting equations of motion. It is obvious that the loss of axisymmetry complicated the analysis. At the same time, it also is obvious that these complications entail mathematical operations, rather than the basic formulation. As was mentioned earlier, symbolic mathematical software can significantly alleviate these complications.
