Guest Question.

Heat and material balances in combustion
The waste gas from a process of 1000 mol/h of CO at 473 K is burned at 1 atm pressure in a furnace using air at 373 K. The combustion is complete and 90% excess air is used. The flue gas leaves the furnace at 1273 K. Calculate the heat removed in the furnace.

Admin
Solution

A schematic diagram is shown in Fig. E3.7.

Combustion stoichiometry

\(CO +1 / 2 O _{2} \rightarrow CO _{2}\)

Heats of formation can be found as

\(\Delta H_{f, 298}^{\circ}-110.523 \times 10^{3} k J / kmol \text { for } CO ; \text { and }-393.513 \times 10^{3} k J / kmol \text { for } CO _{2}\)

 

\(\Delta H_{R, 298}^{\circ}=H_{ CO _{2}, 298}^{\circ}-H_{C O, 298}^{\circ}-\frac{1}{2} H_{O_{2}, 298}^{\circ}=\Delta H_{f C O_{2}, 298}^{\circ}-\Delta H_{f C O, 298}^{\circ}-\frac{1}{2} \Delta H_{f O_{2}, 298}^{\circ}\)

 

\(=-282.990 \times 10^{3} kJ / mol\)

 

From stoichiometry, 1-mole of CO requires half a mole of \(O _{2}\) to produce one mole of C\(O _{2}\) at complete combustion. Therefore, the minimum \(O _{2}\) in feed is half of that of CO.

\(F_{O_{2}, \min }=\frac{1}{2} F_{ CO , 0} \text { and } F_{O_{2}, 0}=1.9 F_{O_{2}, \min }=\frac{1.9}{2} F_{ CO , 0}=950 mol / h\)

The \(O _{2}\) is supplied by air. In air, there are about 21% mole percent of \(O _{2} \) and 79% mole percent of \(N_{2} \). Thus, the requirement of \(O _{2} \) brings \(N_{2}\) along from the air. That is,

\(F_{N_{2} 0}=\frac{0.79}{0.21} F_{O_{2}, 0}=\frac{0.79}{0.21} \times \frac{1.9}{2} F_{C O, 0}=3573.8 mol / h\)

In the flue gas,

\(\begin{aligned}& F _{ N _{2}, e }= F _{ N _{2,0}}=3573.8 mol / h ; \\& F _{ CO _{2}, e }= F _{ CO , 0}=1000 mol / h ; \\& F _{ O _{2}, e }= F _{ O _{2}, 0}- F _{ O _{2}, \min }=0.45 F _{ CO , 0}=450 mol / h\end{aligned}\)

Energy balance over the system:

\( \underset{ \underset{\underset{Steady \space state}{0} }{\swarrow}}{ \frac{d(U \cdot m)}{d t} } = Q – \underset{\underset{\underset{no \space work \space done }{0} }{\swarrow }}{ {W}_{s} } + \sum_{j=1}^{N_{S}} F_{j, i n} H_{j, i n}-\sum_{j=1}^{N_{S}} F_{j, o u t} H_{j, o u t}\)

Therefore:

\(Q=\sum_{j=1}^{N_{s}}\left(F_{j, \text { out }} H_{j, \text { out }}-F_{j, i n} H_{j, \text { in }}\right)\)

Since the pressure is constant (at standard pressure), we have:

\(H_{j, T}=\Delta H_{f j, 298}^{0}+\int_{298}^{T} C_{P j} d T=\Delta H_{fj, 298}^{\circ}+\bar{C}_{P j}(T-298)\)

Remark: if a table of \(C _{ P }\) versus T is tabulated, numerical integration will be needed. If a correlation equation is known, then direct integration can be applied.

 

\(Q=\sum_{J=1}^{N_{s}}\left(F_{j, \text { out }} H_{j, \text { out }}-F_{j, \text { in }} H_{j, \text { in }}\right)\)

 

\(=\sum_{J=1}^{N_{s}}\left\{F_{j, e}\left[\Delta H_{fj, 298}^{\circ}+\bar{C}_{P_{j}, e}\left(T_{j, e}-298\right)\right]-F_{j, 0}\left[\Delta H_{fj, 298}^{\circ}+\bar{C}_{P_{j}, 0}\left(T_{j, 0}-298\right)\right]\right\}\)

 

\(\left.=\sum_{J=1}^{N_{s}}\left(F_{j, e}-F_{j, 0}\right) \Delta H_{f j, 298}^{\circ}+\sum_{j=1}^{N_{s}}\left[F_{j, e} \bar{C}_{P_{j, e}}\left(T_{j, e}-298\right)-F_{j, 0} \bar{C}_{P_{j, 0}}\left(T_{j, 0}-298\right)\right]\right\}\)

 

\(\left.=F_{C O, 0} \Delta H_{R, 298}^{\circ}+\sum_{j=1}^{N_{s}}\left[F_{j, e} \bar{C}_{p_{j}, e}\left(T_{j, e}-298\right)-F_{j, 0} \bar{C}_{p_{j}, 0}\left(T_{j, 0}-298\right)\right]\right\}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}&Q=1000 \times\left(-282.990 \times 10^{3}\right)+(3573.8 \times 31.47+1000 \times 49.91+450 \times 33.25) \times(1273-298)- \\&{[3573.8 \times 31.47 \times(373-298)+1000 \times 39.38 \times(473-298)+950 \times 29.66 \times(373-298)J / h } \\&=-127523350 J / h =-1.2752 \times 10^{8} J / h =-35.42 kW\end{aligned}\)
