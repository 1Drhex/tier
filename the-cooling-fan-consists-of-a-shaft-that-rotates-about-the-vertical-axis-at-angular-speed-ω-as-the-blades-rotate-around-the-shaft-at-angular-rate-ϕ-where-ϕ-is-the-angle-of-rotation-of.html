Guest Question.

The cooling fan consists of a shaft that rotates about the vertical axis at angular speed Ω as the blades rotate around the shaft at angular rate \(\dot{ϕ}\), where ϕ is the angle of rotation of one of the blades from the top-center position. Both rotation rates are constant. Derive expressions for the velocity and acceleration of the blade tip P in terms of components relative to the body-fixed xyz reference system.

Admin
Solution

This example synthesizes many of the concepts developed in this chapter, including rotation transformations. The problem statement requires that xyz be used as the global coordinate system. We also use it as the reference frame for the relative motion, as a precursor for the kinetics principles in Chapter 5, which require the usage of body-ﬁxed axes. The rotation of xyz is the sum of the rotation \( \Omega \) about the vertical axis and the rotation \( \dot{\phi} \) about shaft AB. To define the first rotation, we attach reference frame \(x_{1}y_{1}z_{1} \) to the vertical shaft, with the z1 axis vertically upward. The only meaningful horizontal direction is the one that lies in the vertical plane containing shaft AB, so we deﬁne the \(x_{1} \) axis to lie in that plane, to the right for the given diagram. Then \(y_{1} \) is inward relative to the plane of the diagram. The x axis always coincides with shaft AB, so we set \( \overline{e}_{2}=\overline{i} .\) Thus general descriptions of the angular velocities of the reference frames are

\( \overline{w} = \Omega \overline{k}_{1}+\dot{\phi}\overline{i}, \quad \overline{\Omega }_{1} = \Omega \overline{k}_{1}, \quad \overline{\Omega }_{2} = \overline{w}. \quad \quad (1) \)

The rotation rates are constant, so the angular acceleration of xyz is

\( \overline{\alpha } = \Omega (\overline{\Omega }_{1}×\overline{k}_{1})+\dot{\phi}(\overline{\Omega }_{2}×\overline{i} ) = \dot{\phi }(\overline{w}×\overline{i} ). \quad \quad (2) \)

Several approaches for expressing \( \overline{k}_{1} \) in terms of global xyz components are available; we shall evaluate the rotation transformation from \(x_{1}y_{1}z_{1} \) to xyz. The transformation may be visualized as the result of a pair of body-ﬁxed rotations. The ﬁrst is a rotation of π/2−β about the \(y_{\prime} \) axis. This transforms \(x_{1}y_{1}z_{1} \) to \( x^{\prime}y^{\prime}z^{\prime} \) , where the \( x^{\prime} \) axis is aligned with the x axis and the \( z^{\prime} \) axis is the upward reference line in view C-C. A rotation by angle \( \phi \) about the \( x^{\prime} \) axis moves \( z^{\prime} \) into alignment with the z axis without disturbing the \( x^{\prime} \) axis. Thus the transformation is

\( \left\{\overline{i}\space \overline{j}\space \overline{k} \right\}^{T} = [R]\left\{\overline{i}_{1}\space \overline{j}_{1}\space \overline{k}_{1} \right\}^{T}, \) \( [R]=[R_{x}][R_{y}]= [\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos \phi & \sin \phi\\ 0 & – \sin \phi & \cos \phi \end{matrix} ]\left[\begin{matrix} \cos (\frac{\pi }{2}-\beta ) &0& -\sin (\frac{\pi }{2}-\beta ) \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ \sin (\frac{\pi }{2}-\beta ) & 0 & \cos (\frac{\pi }{2}-\beta ) \end{matrix} \right] \) \( = \left[ \begin{matrix} \sin \beta & 0 & -\cos \beta \\ \sin \phi \cos \beta & \cos \phi & \sin \phi \sin \beta \\ \cos \phi \cos \beta & -\sin \phi & \cos \phi \sin \beta \end{matrix} \right]. \)

The inverse transformation gives the unit vectors of \(x_{1}y_{1}z_{1} \) in terms of the xyz unit vectors, so the last row of \( [R]^{T} \) , which is the last column of [R], gives the global components of the rotation direction \( \overline{e}_{1}=\overline{k}_{1}, \)

\( \overline{k}_{1}=-\cos \beta \overline{i}+\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}.\quad \quad (3) \)

This enables us to evaluate the global components of \( \overline{w}\space and \space \overline{\alpha } \) :

\( \overline{w} = (\dot{\phi }-\Omega \cos \beta )\overline{i}+\Omega \sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\Omega \cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}, \quad \quad (4a) \)

 

\( \overline{\alpha } = [(\dot{\phi }-\Omega \cos \beta )\overline{i}+\Omega \sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\Omega \cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}]×\overline{i} \) \( = \Omega \dot{\phi }(\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}). \quad \quad (4b) \)

We may construct the velocity and acceleration of origin B by recognizing that this point follows a circular path in the horizontal plane. However, using an alternative approach based on its being a point on shaft AB is more suitable to expressing the results in terms of components relative to xyz. The angular velocity of shaft AB is \( \overline{\Omega}_{1}, \) which is constant, and point A is stationary, so we have

\( \overline{v}_{B} = \Omega \overline{k}_{1}×\overline{r}_{B/A},\quad \overline{a}_{B}=\Omega \overline{k}_{1}×(\Omega \overline{k}_{1}×\overline{r}_{B/A} ). \)

The global components of \( \overline{k}_{1} \) are given by Eq. (3), whose substitution into the preceding yields

\( \overline{v}_{B}=\Omega (-\cos \beta \overline{i}+\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k})×L\overline{i} \) \( = \Omega L(\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k} ), \quad \quad (5a)\)

 

\( \overline{a}_{B}=\Omega (-\cos \beta \overline{i}+\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k})×\Omega L(\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k} ) \) \( =\Omega ^{2}L[-(\sin \beta )^{2}\overline{i}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \cos \beta \overline{j}-\cos \phi \sin \beta \cos \beta \overline{k} ]\quad \quad (5b) \)

Because xyz is attached to the propeller, there is no relative velocity or acceleration. Hence \( \overline{v}_{p} \space and \space \overline{a}_{p} \) also are described by Eqs(3.5.6),

\( \overline{v}_{p} = \overline{v}_{O^{\prime}}+\overline{w}×\overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}}, \) \( \overline{a}_{p} = \overline{a}_{O^{\prime}}+\overline{\alpha }×\overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}}+\overline{w}×(\overline{w}× \overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}}). \quad \quad (3.5.6) \)

 

which indicate that

\( \overline{v}_{p} = \overline{v}_{B}+\overline{w}×\overline{r}_{P/B},\quad \overline{a}_{P}= \overline{a}_{B}+\overline{\alpha }×\overline{r}_{P/B}+\overline{w}×(\overline{w}×\overline{r}_{P/B} ). \)

 

Substitution of the global descriptions in Eqs. (4) and (5) yields

\( \overline{v}_{P}=\Omega L (\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k})+[(\dot{\phi }-\Omega \cos \beta )\overline{i}+\Omega \sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\Omega \cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k} ]×R\overline{k} \) \( = \Omega [R\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{i}+(L\cos \phi \sin \beta +R\cos \beta )\overline{j}-L\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}]. \)

The corresponding evaluation of acceleration gives

\( \overline{a}_{P}=\Omega ^{2}L [- (\sin \beta)^{2} \overline{i}+\sin \phi \sin \beta \cos \beta \overline{j}-\cos \phi \sin \beta\cos \beta \overline{k}] \) \( +\Omega \dot{\phi } (\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}-\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{k})×R\overline{k} \)

 

\( +[(\dot{\phi }-\Omega \cos \beta )\overline{i}+\Omega \sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{j}+\Omega \cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{k}] \)

 

\( ×\Omega R(\sin \phi \sin \beta \overline{i}+\cos \beta \overline{j}) \)

 

\( = \Omega ^{2} \) { \( -\sin \beta (L\sin \beta +R\cos \phi \cos \beta )\overline{i}+\sin \phi \sin \beta (-L\cos \beta +R\cos \phi \sin \beta )\overline{j} \)

 

\( + [-L\cos \phi \sin \beta \cos \beta -R(\cos \beta )^{2}-R(\sin \phi )^{2}(\sin \beta )^{2}]\overline{k} \) } \( -\dot{\phi }^{2}R\overline{k} \)

 

\( +2\Omega \dot{\phi}R(\cos \phi \sin \beta \overline{i}+\cos \beta \overline{k}). \)

These results are displayed in groups of like coefﬁcients of the rate variables to highlight that the underlying physical phenomena are a superposition of effects. The terms in \( \overline{v}_{P} \) that contain \( \Omega \) and those in \( \overline{a}_{P} \) that contain \( \Omega^{2} \) represent the motion that would be present if \( \phi \) were constant ,giving \( \dot{\phi }\equiv 0. \) In this case, point P would follow a circular path in the horizontal plane. Similarly, the terms in \( \overline{v}_{P} \) that contain \( \dot{\phi } \) and those in \( \overline{a}_{P} \) that contain \( \dot{\phi } ^{2} \) correspond to a pure rotation about the x axis, in which casepoint P would follow a circular path of radius R. The effect that has no obvious description as a superposition is represented by the acceleration term that has the factor \( 2\Omega\dot{\phi} \). The two factor identiﬁes it as a Coriolis acceleration that exists only if both rotation rates are nonzero. This physical Coriolis acceleration arises, even though the Coriolis acceleration term \( 2\overline{w}×(\overline{v}_{P} )_{xyz} \) in relative acceleration equations (3.5.4) was identically zero in our analysis.

\( \overline{v}_{p} = \overline{v}_{O^{\prime}}+(\overline{v}_{p})_{xyz}+\overline{w}× \overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}}, \) \( \overline{a}_{p} = \overline{a}_{O^{\prime}}+(\overline{a}_{p})_{xyz}+\overline{\alpha }× \overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}} + \overline{w} × (\overline{w}×\overline{r}_{P/O^{\prime}})+2\overline{w}×(\overline{v}_{P})_{xyz}. \quad \quad (3.5.4) \)
