Guest Question.

A solid steel shaft 14 ft long has a diameter of 6 in. for 9 ft of its length and a diameter of 4 in. for the remaining 5 ft. The shaft is in equilibrium when subjected to the three torques shown in Fig. 7–8a. The modulus of rigidity (shear modulus) of the steel is 12,000 ksi. Determine
(a) The maximum shearing stress in the shaft.
(b) The rotation of end B of the 6-in. segment with respect to end A.
(c) The rotation of end C of the 4-in. segment with respect to end B.
(d) The rotation of end C with respect to end A.

Admin
Solution

In general, free-body diagrams should be drawn to evaluate the resisting torque in each section of the shaft. Such diagrams are shown in Figs. 7–8b and c. From the free-body diagram of Fig. 7–8b,

\(+ \searrow \sum{ M_x} =0: \quad\quad T_6-20+5=0 \quad\quad T_6=15kip\cdot ft-\searrow –\)

From the free-body diagram of Fig. 7–8c,

\(+ \searrow \sum{ M_x} =0: \quad\quad -T_4+5=0 \quad\quad T_4=5kip\cdot ft-\nearrow –\)

A torque diagram, such as the one shown in Fig. 7-8d, provides a pictorial representation of the levels of resisting torque being transmitted by each of the sections and serves as an aid for stress and deformation calculations. Equations for the shearing stress and angle of twist in a circular shaft subjected to a torque contain the polar second moment J of the cross section, which is given by \(J=\int \rho^{2} d A=\int_{0}^{c} \rho^{2}(2 \pi \rho d \rho)=\frac{\pi c^{4}}{2}\) as

\(J_4=\frac{\pi}{2} r^4=\frac{\pi}{2} (2^4)=25.13in^4\)

 

\(J_6=\frac{\pi}{2} r^4=\frac{\pi}{2} (3^4)=127.23in^4\)

(a) The location of the maximum shearing stress is not apparent; hence, the stress must be checked at both sections. The maximum shearing stress on a transverse cross section occurs at the outer surface of the shaft and is given by

\(\tau _\rho =\frac{T_\rho }{J} \quad or \quad \tau _c =\frac{T_c }{J}\) as

\(\tau _{AB} =\frac{T_{AB}c_{AB} }{J_{AB}} =\frac{15(12)(3)}{127.23}=4.244ksi \)

 

\(\tau _{BC} =\frac{T_{BC}c_{BC} }{J_{BC}} =\frac{5(12)(2)}{25.13}=4.775ksi \)

 

Therefore, \(\tau _{max} = \tau _{BC}=4.775ksi\cong 4.78ksi\)

(b) As the resisting torque of 15 kip · ft is transmitted from section to section in segment AB of the shaft, the section at B twists relative to the section at A by an amount \(θ_{B/A}\) as shown on the angle of twist diagram of Fig. 7-8e. The slope of the angle of twist diagram is constant since the term T/GJ (in \(\theta =\frac{TL}{GJ} \)) is constant. The rotation of the section at B (angle of twist in the 6-in. section) is given by \(\theta =\frac{TL}{GJ} \) as

\(\theta _{B/A}=\frac{T_{AB}L_{AB}}{G_{AB}J_{AB}}=\frac{15(12)(9)(12)}{12,000(127.23)}=0.012733rad\cong 0.01273 rad- \nearrow –\)

(c) Similarly for segment BC:

\(\theta _{C/B}=\frac{T_{BC}L_{BC}}{G_{BC}J_{BC}}=\frac{5(12)(5)(12)}{12,000(25.13)}=0.011938rad\cong 0.01194 rad- \searrow –\)

(d) If there was no resisting torque being transmitted by segment BC it would rotate as a rigid body through angle \(θ_{B/A}\). However, the resisting torque of 5 kip · ft causes the section at C to rotate relative to the section at B by an amount \(θ_{C/B}\) as segment BC deforms, as shown in Fig. 7-8e. The resultant of the deformations in the two segments of the shaft is

\(\theta _{C/A}=\theta _{B/A}-\theta _{C/B}=0.012733-0.011938=0.000795 rad- \nearrow –\)

The distortion for the entire shaft is pictorially shown in Fig. 7-8f.
