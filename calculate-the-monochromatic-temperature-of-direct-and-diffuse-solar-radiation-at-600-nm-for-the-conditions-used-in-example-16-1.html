Guest Question.

Calculate the monochromatic temperature of direct and diffuse solar radiation at 600 nm for the conditions used in

Admin
Solution

Monochromatic temperatures are calculated according to Equ.(16.3):

\(T_{\lambda}=\frac{h c}{k \lambda} \frac{1}{\ln \left(\frac{2 h c^{2}}{\lambda^{5} i_{E \lambda}}+1\right)}\)

 

Now, the direct and the diffuse irradiances have been computed in The normal direct spectral irradiance is \(464\) \(W/(m^{2}μm)/cos(60°) = 9.28 · 10^{8}\) \(W/(m^{2}m)\), while the value for the diffuse light is \(162 W/(m^{2}μm) = 1.62 · 10^{8}\) \(W/(m^{2}m)\). The spectral energy intensities are therefore equal to

\(i_{E \lambda, d i r}=\frac{1}{\Omega_{s}} G_{\lambda, n, d i r}=\frac{9.28 \cdot 10^{8}}{6.80 \cdot 10^{-5}} \mathrm{~W} /\left(\mathrm{m}^{2} \mathrm{~m} \cdot \mathrm{sr}\right)=1.37 \cdot 10^{13} \mathrm{~W} /\left(\mathrm{m}^{2} \mathrm{~m} \cdot \mathrm{sr}\right)\)

 

and

\(i_{E \lambda, d i f f}=\frac{1}{\pi} G_{\lambda, d i f f}=\frac{1.62 \cdot 10^{8}}{\pi} \mathrm{W} /\left(\mathrm{m}^{2} \mathrm{~m} \cdot \mathrm{sr}\right)=5.16 \cdot 10^{7} \mathrm{~W} /\left(\mathrm{m}^{2} \mathrm{~m} \cdot \mathrm{sr}\right)\)

 

The appropriate temperatures then turn out to be

\(\begin{array}{l} T_{\lambda, d i r}=\frac{h c}{k \cdot 6 \cdot 10^{-7}} \frac{1}{\ln \left(\frac{2 h c^{2}}{\left(6 \cdot 10^{-7}\right)^{5} 1.37 \cdot 10^{13}}+1\right)}=5072 \mathrm{~K} \\ T_{\lambda, d i f f}=\frac{h c}{k \cdot 6 \cdot 10^{-7}} \frac{1}{\ln \left(\frac{2 h c^{2}}{\left(6 \cdot 10^{-7}\right)^{5} 5 \cdot 16 \cdot 10^{7}}+1\right)}=1394 \mathrm{~K} \end{array}\)

 

which can also be read off the curves displayed in Fig. 16.8. For a less turbid atmosphere and for higher elevation of the Sun, the temperature of the direct beam approaches that of the surface of the sun. Diffuse radiation has a surprisingly high temperature which might be useful in solar energy engineering.
