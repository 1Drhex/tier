Guest Question.

Inside a Fusion Reactor

In 1998, the JT-60U tokamak in Japan operated with a D-T plasma density of \(4.8 \times 10^{13} cm ^{-3}\) at a temperature (in energy units) of \(24.1 keV\). It confined this plasma inside a magnetic field for \(1.1 s\).

(A) Do these data meet Lawson’s criterion?

(B) How does the plasma density compare with the density of atoms in an ideal gas when the gas is under standard conditions \(\left(T=0^{\circ} C\right.\) and \(\left.P=1 atm \right) ?\)

Admin
Solution

Conceptualize With the help of the third of Equations \(45.4\), imagine many such reactions occurring in a plasma of high temperature and high density.

\(\begin{array}{ll}{ }_{1}^{2} H +{ }_{1}^{2} H \rightarrow{ }_{2}^{3} He +{ }_{0}^{1} n & Q=3.27 MeV \\{ }_{1}^{2} H +{ }_{1}^{2} H \rightarrow{ }_{1}^{3} H +{ }_{1}^{1} H & Q=4.03 MeV \\{ }_{1}^{2} H +{ }_{1}^{3} H \rightarrow{ }_{2}^{4} He +{ }_{0}^{1} n & Q=17.59 MeV\end{array}\) (45.4)

Categorize We use the concept of the Lawson number discussed in this section, so we categorize this example as a substitution problem.

Evaluate the Lawson number for the JT-60U:

\(n \tau=\left(4.8 \times 10^{13} cm ^{-3}\right)(1.1 s )=5.3 \times 10^{13} s / cm ^{3}\)

This value is close to meeting Lawson’s criterion of \(10^{14} s / cm ^{3}\) for a D-T plasma given in Equation 45.5. In fact, scientists recorded a power gain of \(1.25\), indicating that the reactor operated slightly past the break-even point and produced more energy than it required to maintain the plasma.

\(\begin{aligned}&n \tau \geq 10^{14} s / cm ^{3} & (D–T) \\& n \tau \geq 10^{16} s / cm ^{3} & (D–D) \end{aligned}\) (45.5)

Find the density of atoms in a sample of ideal gas by evaluating \(N_{ A } / V_{ mol }\), where \(N_{ A }\) is Avogadro’s number and \(V_{ mol }\) is the molar volume of an ideal gas under standard conditions, \(2.24 \times 10^{-2} m ^{3} / mol :\)

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{N_{ A }}{V_{ mol }} &=\frac{6.02 \times 10^{23} atoms / mol }{2.24 \times 10^{-2} m ^{3} / mol }=2.7 \times 10^{25} \text { atoms } / m ^{3} \\&=2.7 \times 10^{19} atoms / cm ^{3}\end{aligned}\)

This value is more than 500 000 times greater than the plasma density in the reactor.
