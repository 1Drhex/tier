Guest Question.

Given the potential \(V=\frac{10}{r^{2}}\sin \theta \cos \phi ,\)

(a) Find the electric flux density D at (2, π/2, 0 ).

(b) Calculate the work done in moving a 10 μ,C charge from point A(1, 30°, 120°) to B( 4, 90°, 60°).

Admin
Solution

(a) \(D=\varepsilon _{0}E\)

But
\(E=-\triangledown V=-[\frac{\partial V}{\partial r}a_{r}+\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta }a_{\theta }+\frac{1}{r\sin \theta }\frac{\partial V}{\partial \phi } a_{\phi } ]\)
\(=\frac{20}{r^{3}}\sin \theta \cos \phi a_{r}- \frac{10}{r^{3}}\cos \theta \cos \phi a_{\theta }+\frac{10}{r^{3}}\sin \phi a_{\phi }\)
At \((2,\pi /2,0)\)
\(D=\varepsilon _{0}E(r=2,\theta =\pi /2,\phi =0)=\varepsilon _{0}(\frac{20}{8}a_{r}-0a_{\theta }+0a_{\phi } )\)
\(=2.5\varepsilon _{0}a_{r} C/m^{2}=22.1 a_{r} pC/m^{2}\)

(b) The work done can be found in two ways, using either E or V.
Method 1:
\(W=-Q\int\limits_{L}^{}{E.dl}\) or \(-\frac{W}{Q}=\int\limits_{L}^{}{E.dl}\)

and because the electrostatic field is conservative, the path of integration is immaterial.
Hence the work done in moving Q from A( 1, 30°, 120°) to B( 4, 90°, 60°) is the same as that in moving Q from A to A’, from A’ to B’, and from B’ to B, where

\(\begin{matrix} A(1,30^{\circ },120^{\circ }) & & B(4,90^{\circ },60^{\circ }) \\ \downarrow dl=dr a_{r} & dl=r d\theta a_{\theta } & \uparrow dl=r \sin \theta d\phi a_{\phi } \\ A'(4,30^{\circ },120^{\circ }) & \rightarrow &B'(4,90^{\circ },120^{\circ }) \end{matrix} \)

That is, instead of being moved directly from A to B, Q is moved from A→ A’, A’ → B ‘,
B’ → B, so that only one variable is changed at a time. This makes the line integral much
easier to evaluate. Thus

\(\frac{-W}{Q}=-\frac{1}{Q}(W_{AA'}+W_{A'B'}+W_{B'B})\)
\(=(\int\limits_{AA'}^{}{}+\int\limits_{A'B'}^{}{}+\int\limits_{B'B}^{}{} )E.dl\)
\(=\int\limits_{r=1}^{4}{\frac{20\sin \theta \cos \phi }{r^{3}}dr }|_{\theta =30^{\circ },\phi =120^{\circ }}+\int\limits_{\theta =30^{\circ }}^{90^{\circ }}{\frac{-10\cos \theta \cos \phi }{r^{3}}r d\theta }|_{r=4,\phi =120^{\circ }} +\int\limits_{\phi =120^{\circ }}^{60^{\circ }}{\frac{10\sin \phi }{r^{3}}r\sin \theta d\phi }|_{r=4,\theta =90^{\circ }}\)
\(=20(\frac{1}{2} )(\frac{-1}{2} )[-\frac{1}{2r^{2}}|_{r=1}^{4} ]-\frac{10}{16}\frac{(-1)}{2}\sin \theta |_{30^{\circ }}^{90^{\circ }}+\frac{10}{16}(1)[-\cos \phi |_{120^{\circ }}^{60^{\circ }}]\)
\(-\frac{W}{Q}=\frac{-75}{32}+\frac{5}{32}-\frac{10}{16}\)

or
\(W=\frac{45}{16}Q=28.125\) \mu J

Method 2:
Since V is known, this method is much easier.
\(W=-Q\int\limits_{A}^{B}{E.dl}=QV_{AB}\)
\(=Q(V_{B}-V_{A})\)
\(=10(\frac{10}{16}\sin 90^{\circ }\cos 60^{\circ }-\frac{10}{1}\sin 30^{\circ }\cos 120^{\circ } ).10^{-6}\)
\(=10(\frac{10}{32}-\frac{-5}{2} ).10^{-6}\)
=28.125 \mu J as obtained before
