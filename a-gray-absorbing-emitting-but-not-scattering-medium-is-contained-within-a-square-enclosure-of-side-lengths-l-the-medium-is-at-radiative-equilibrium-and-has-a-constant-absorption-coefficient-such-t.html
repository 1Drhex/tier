Guest Question.

A gray, absorbing/emitting (but not scattering) medium is contained within a square enclosure of side lengths L. The medium is at radiative equilibrium and has a constant absorption coefficient such that κL = 1. The top and both side walls are at zero temperature, while the bottom wall is isothermal at temperature \(T_{w}\) (with constant blackbody intensity \(I_{bw}\)); all four surfaces are black.
Calculate the local heat loss from the bottom surface using the discrete ordinates method.

Admin
Solution

For the illustrative purposes of this example we shall limit ourselves to the simple nonsyrmnetric \(S_{2}\)– approximation, with the crude nodal system indicated in Fig. 16-4. For the nonsymmetric \(S_{2}\)-approximation (without dependence in the z-direction) we have to consider four discrete ordinates whose direction vectors (projected into the x-y-plane) are \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{i}}=\xi _{i} \overset{\wedge }{i} + \eta _{i} \overset{\wedge }{J} = \pm 0.5 (\overset{\wedge }{i}\pm \overset{\wedge }{J})\) , as given by .
The quadrature weight for each direction is, after doubling because of the two-dimensionality, \(w_{i}=\pi\) .
For radiative equilibrium in a gray, nonscattering medium ∇ · q = 0, and the source function is, from equations (9.55) \(\triangledown \cdot q=\kappa \left\lgroup4\sigma T^{4}-\int_{4\pi }^{}{} Id\Omega \right\rgroup =\kappa (4\sigma T^{4} – G)\) and (16.39) \(S_{i}= (1-w)I_{b}+\frac{w}{4\pi }\sum\limits_{j=1}^{n}{w_{j}\Phi _{ij}I_{j}}, i= 1,2,…,n\) , S = \(I_{b} = G/4\pi\) , which is not a function of direction.
We will first solve the problem with the popular diamond spatial differencing scheme, i.e., \(\gamma _{x}=\gamma _{y}=\frac{1}{2}\) . Since all nodal surface areas are \(A=L/2 , all \left|\xi _{i}\right|=\left|\eta _{i} \right| = 0.5 , and \beta V =\kappa (L/2)^{2}=0.25\kappa L^{2}=0.25L \), equation (16.48) becomes

 

\(I_{pi}= \frac{\frac{1}{8}S_{p} + \frac{1}{4}I_{x_{i}i} +\frac{1}{4}I_{y_{i}i} }{\frac{1}{8}+\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{4} } =\frac{1}{5} \left(S_{P}+2I_{x_{i}i}+2I_{y_{i}i}\right) .\)

 

We start in the lower left comer with all directions for which \(\xi _{i}\gt 0 and \eta _{i}\gt 0\) (i.e., a single direction for the \(S_{2}\)-approximation). For this direction \(x_{i}\) = West and \(y_{i}\) = South. To distinguish among the different nodes we attach the node number after the W, etc. For example, \(I_{W2.1}\) is the intensity at the West face of volume element 2, pointing into the direction of \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{1}}\).

 

i=1 [ \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{1}}=0.5(\overset{\wedge }{i}+\overset{\wedge }{j})\): For all nodes

 

\(I_{pj,1}=\frac{1}{5} \left(S_{pj}+ 2I_{Wj,1}+2I_{Sj,1}\right),\)
\(I_{Ej,1}= 2I_{pj,1}- I_{Wj,1},\)
\(I_{Nj,1}= 2 I_{pj,1}-I_{Sj,1}, j=1,2,3,4.\).

 

Starting at Element 1 we have \(I_{W1,1} = 0, I_{S1,1} = I_{bw}\), and

 

\(I_{p1,1}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p1}+ 2I_{bw}\right),\)

\(I_{E1,1}= 2 I_{p1,1}=I_{W2,1} , I_{N1,1}=2I_{p1,1} -I_{bw}= I_{S3,3};\)

\(I_{p2,1}= \frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p2}+2I_{W2,1} +2I_{S2,1}\right) =\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p2}+4I_{p1,1}+2I_{bw}\right),\)

\(I_{N2,1}= 2I_{p2,1}-I_{bw}=I_{S4,1};\)

\(I_{p3,1}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p3}+ 2I_{S3,1}\right) =\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p3}+4I_{p1,1}-2I_{bw}\right) ,\)

\(I_{E3,1}= 2I_{p3,1}=I_{W4,1};\)

\(I_{p4,1}= \frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p4}+2I_{W4,1} +2I_{S4,1}\right)\)

\(=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p4}+4I_{p3,1}+4I_{p2,1}-2I_{bw}\right),\)

 

i =2 [ \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{2}}=0.5(-\overset{\wedge }{i}+\overset{\wedge }{j})\): In a problem without symmetry we would start in the lower right comer, scanning again over all elements. However, in this problem we can determine the intensities right away through synmietry, as

 

\(I_{p1,2}=I_{p2,1},I_{p2,2}=I_{p1,1},I_{p3,2}=I_{p4,1}, I_{p4,2}=I_{p3,1}\)

 

i =3 [ \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{3}}=-0.5(\overset{\wedge }{i}+\overset{\wedge }{j})\):Starting in the upper right comer, we have, for all nodes,

 

\(I_{pj,3}= \frac{1}{5} \left(S_{pj}+ 2I_{Ej,3}+2I_{Nj,3}\right),\)

 

\(I_{Wj,3}=2I_{pj,3} – I_{WE,3},\)

\(I_{Sj,3}=2 I_{pj,3}-I_{Nj,3}\)

 

Starting at Element 4 with \(I_{E4,3}=I_{N4,3}=0\) we find

 

\(I_{p4,3}= \frac{1}{5}S_{p4},\)

\(I_{S4,3}= 2I_{p4,3} = I_{N2,3}, I_{W4,3}=2I_{p4,3} = I_{E3,3};\)

 

\(I_{p3,3} =\frac{1}{5} \left(S_{P3}+2I_{E3,3}\right)=\frac{1}{5 } \left(S_{P3}+4I_{p4,3}\right),\)

\(I_{S3,3}= 2I_{p3,3}= I_{N1,3};\)

\(I_{p2,3} =\frac{1}{5} \left(S_{P2}+2I_{N2,3}\right)=\frac{1}{5 } \left(S_{P2}+4I_{p4,3}\right),\)

\(I_{W2,3}= 2I_{p2,3}= I_{E1,3};\)

\(I_{p1,3}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p1}+2I_{E1,3}+2I_{N1,3}\right) =\frac{1}{5} \left(S_{p1}+4I_{p2,3}+4I_{p3,3}\right).\)

 

Also

 

\(I_{S1,3}= 2I_{p1,3} -I_{N1,3} = 2(I_{p1,3}-I_{p3,3}),\)

\(I_{S2,3}= 2I_{p2,3} -I_{N2,3} = 2(I_{p2,3}-I_{p4,3}),\)

 

which will be needed later for the calculation of wall heat fluxes from equation (16.8) \(q\cdot \overset{\wedge }{n}(r_{w})= \epsilon (r_{w})[\pi I_{b}(r_{w})- H(r_{w})]\) \(\simeq \epsilon (r_{w})\left(\pi I_{b}(r_{w}) – \sum\limits_{\overset{\wedge }{n}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{s_{i}}\lt 0}{w_{i}I_{i}(r_{w})\left|\overset{\wedge }{n}\cdot \overset{\wedge }{s_{i}}\lt 0\right| } \right)\) .i =4 [ \(\overset{\wedge }{s_{4}}=0.5(\overset{\wedge }{i}-\overset{\wedge }{j})\): Again, by symmetry it follows immediately that

 

\(I_{p1.4}=I_{p2.3},I_{p2.4}=I_{p1.3},I_{p3.4}=I_{p4.3},I_{p4.4}=I_{p3.3},\)

 

and also

 

\(I_{S1.4}=I_{S2.3},I_{S2.4}=I_{S1.3}.\)

 

Summarizing, we have

 

\(I_{p1.1}=I_{p2.2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p1}+2I_{bw}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p2.1}=I_{p1.2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p2}+4I_{p1.1}+2I_{bw}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p3.1}=I_{p4.2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p3}+4I_{p1.1}-2I_{bw}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p4.1}=I_{p3.2}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p4}+4I_{p3.1}+4I_{p2.1}-2I_{bw}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p1.3}=I_{p2.4}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p1}+4I_{p2.3}+4I_{p3.3}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p2.3}=I_{p1.4}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p2}+4I_{p4.3}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p3.3}=I_{p4.4}=\frac{1}{5}\left(S_{p3}+4I_{p4.3}\right),\)

 

\(I_{p4.3}=I_{p3.4}=\frac{1}{5}S_{p4},\)

 

\(I_{S1.3}=I_{S2.4}=2(I_{p1.3}-I_{p3.3}),\)

 

\(I_{S2.3}=I_{S1.4}=2(I_{p2.3}-I_{p4.3})\)

 

The source functions are readily evaluated from equation (16.7) \(G(r)=\int_{4\pi }^{}{} I(r,\widehat{s} )d\Omega \simeq \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{}w_{i}I_{i}(r)\) and symmetry as

 

\(S_{p1}=S_{p2}=\frac{1}{4}(I_{p1.1}+I_{p1.2}+I_{p1.3}+I_{p1.4}),\)

 

\(S_{p3}=S_{p4}=\frac{1}{4}(I_{p3.1}+I_{p3.2}+I_{p3.3}+I_{p3.4}).\)

 

Since the equations are linear, one could substitute the relations for the \(S_{pj}\) into the above equations and solve for the unknown \(I_{pj,i}\) by matrix inversion. However, in general one would have many more, and much more complicated, equations, which are best solved by iteration. We start by setting all \(S_{pj}\) = 0, finding values for the \(I_{pj,i}\) , updating the \(S_{pj}\), re-evaluating the \(I_{pj,i}\) , and so on, until convergence has been reached. The changing values of the intensity (normalized with \(I_{bw}\)) as a function of iteration are given in

. Values accurate to \(\simeq\) 5% are reached after three iterations, and fully converged values (to four significant digits) are obtained after nine iterations. The converged intensities are used to determine the net radiative heat flux from the bottom wall at x = L/4 and x = 3L/4. From equation (16.8) \(q\cdot \widehat{n}(r_{w})\simeq \epsilon (r_{w})\left(\pi I_{b}(r_{w})-\sum\limits_{\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}_{i}<0 }{w_{i}I_{i}(r_{w})\left|\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}_{i}\right| } \right)\) we have

 

\(q(x=0.25L)=q(x=0.75L)=\pi I_{bw}-\sum\limits_{4}^{i=3}{w_{i}I_{s.i}\left|\eta _{i}\right| }=\pi I_{bw}-\frac{\pi }{2}(I_{s1.3}+I_{S1.4})\)

 

or

 

\(\Psi =\frac{q_{0.25L}}{E_{bw} }=\frac{q_{0.75} }{E_{bw}}=1-\frac{I_{p1.3}-I_{p3.3}+I_{p2.3}-I_{p4.3} }{I_{bw}}=0.7704\)

 

For comparison we will work this example also with the simpler, but more stable step differencing scheme, i.e., \(\gamma_{x}=\gamma_{y}=1\) . Then we obtain from equations (16.47) \(I_{pi}=\gamma _{x}I_{x_{e}i}+(1-\gamma _{x})I_{x_{i}i}=\gamma _{y}I_{y_{e}i}-(1-\gamma _{y})I_{y_{i}i}\) and (16.48) \(I_{pi}=\frac{\beta VS_{pi}+\left|\xi _{i}\right|A_{x}I_{x_{i}i}/\gamma _{x}+\left|\eta _{i}\right| A_{y}I_{y_{i}i} /\gamma _{y}}{BV+\left|\xi _{i}\right|A_{x_{e}}/\gamma _{x}+\left|\eta _{i}\right|A_{y_{e}} /\gamma _{y}}\)

 

\(I_{pi}=\frac{1}{3} (S_{p}+I_{x_{i}i }+L_{y_{i}i } )\),

\(I_{x_{e}i}=I_{y_{e}i }=I_{pi}\)

 

Then, following the same procedure we obtain (a little more easily)

 

\(I_{p1,1}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p1}+0+I_{bw} )=I_{p2,2}\)

 

\(I_{p2.1}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p2}+I_{p1.1}+I_{bw})=I_{p1.2}\)

 

\(I_{p3.1}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p3}+0+I_{p1.1})=I_{p4.2}\)

 

\(I_{p4.1}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p4}+I_{p3.1}+I_{p2.1})=I_{p3.2}\)

 

\(I_{p4.3}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p4}+0+0)=I_{p3.4}\)

\(I_{p3.3}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p3}+I_{p4.3}+0)=I_{p4.4}\)

\(I_{p2.3}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p2}+0+I_{p4.3})=I_{p1.4}\)

 

\(I_{p1.3}=\frac{1}{3}(S_{p1}+I_{p2.3}+I_{p3.3})=I_{p2.4}\)

 

The formulas for the source functions and heat fluxes remain the same, but the nondimensional flux becomes, after substituting for \(I_{s}=I_{p}\),

 

\(\Psi =1-\frac{I_{p1.3}+I_{p1.4}}{2I_{bw}}=0.844\)

 

The iteration results for the step scheme are also included in . Results for the nondimensional heat flux from both schemes are shown in Fig. 16-5 along with exact results reported by Razzaque and coworkers [48], and with \(S_{2}\)– and \(S_{4}\)-calculations of Truelove [15] (for a much finer mesh). Truelove’s results demonstrate the importance of good ordinate sets, at least for low-order approximations: The \(S_{2}^{\prime }\) and \(S_{4}^{\prime }\) results were obtained with sets that do not obey the half-moment condition of equation (16.13) \(\int_{\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}<0 }^{}{\left|\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}\right| } d\Omega =\int_{\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}>0 }\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}d\Omega =\pi=\sum\limits_{\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}_{i}>0 }{w_{i}\widehat{n}\cdot \widehat{s}_{i} }\) (as used by Fiveland [11] in a first investigation of rectangular enclosures), while the \(S_{2}\) and \(S_{4}\) results were obtained with the sets given in Table 16.1

(using the nonsymmetric ordinates for \(S_{2}\)). Not surprisingly, the diamond scheme (similar to a Crank-Nicolson finite differencing scheme) is more accurate than the step scheme (similar to fully implicit finite differencing). The step scheme shows a smoother distribution for the \(I_{p}\) and \(S_{p}\) and is always stable. The diamond scheme, on the other hand, gives nonphysical negative intensities for \(I_{p3.1}\),1 during the first few iterations, which were set to zero.
