Guest Question.

Calculate the probability of adsorption of an ideal gas on \(N_{tot}\) independent sites that are either unoccupied, with energy zero, or singly occupied with energy \(ε_{1}.\)

Admin
Solution

The grand partition function for a single site is \(Z^{(1)} = 1 + \lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1} } \) so the total grand partition function is \(Z = \left(Z^{(1)}\right)^{N_{tot}}.\)

The average number of adsorbed atoms, which in this case happens to equal the number of occupied sites, is

\(\left\langle N\right\rangle = \lambda \frac{\partial q}{\partial \lambda } = N_{tot} \frac{\lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}} }{1 + \lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}}} \) (21.72)

and the average energy is

\(U = – \frac{\partial q}{\partial \beta } = N_{tot} \frac{\varepsilon _{1} \lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}} }{1 + \lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}}}.\) (21.73)

Except for the very important factors of λ, Eq. (21.73) resembles the energy for independent two-state systems.

In order for the gas to adsorb at low temperatures, we want \(ε_{1} < 0.\) The fraction of occupied sites is \(\theta = \left\langle N\right\rangle / N_{tot},\) so

\(\theta = \frac{\lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}}}{1 + \lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}}} = \frac{\lambda e^{-\beta \varepsilon _{1}}}{Z^{(1)}} .\) (21.74)

Of course the fraction of unoccupied sites is \(1 – \theta = 1/Z^{(1)},\) so these results could have been deduced entirely from the ratios of the corresponding terms in \(Z^{(1)} to Z^{(1)}\) itself.

From Eq. (19.66), the chemical potential of an ideal gas is \(\mu = k_{B}T\ln \left(n/n_{Q}\right) = k_{B}T \ln \left(p/\left(n_{Q}k_{B}T\right)\right) ,\) where n is the number density and \(n_{Q}\left(T\right) = \left(mk_{B}T/2\pi \hbar ^{2}\right)^{3/2} \) is the quantum concentration.

\(\mu = \frac{\partial F}{\partial N} = k_{B}T \ln \left[N / \left(Vn_{Q}\right) \right] = k_{B}T \ln \left[p / \left(n_{Q} k_{B} T\right) \right],\) ideal gas, (19.66)

The absolute activity is therefore

\(\lambda = \frac{n}{n_{Q}(T)} = \frac{p}{n_{Q}(T) k_{B}T } , \) (21.75)

which is the ratio of the actual pressure to a quantum pressure. We can therefore define a temperature-dependent pressure

\(p_{0}(T) := n_{Q}(T)k_{B}T e^{\beta \varepsilon _{1}} = n_{Q}(T) k_{B}T e^{- \beta \left|\varepsilon _{1}\right|},\) (21.76)

for \(ε_{1} < 0,\) which increases with temperature. Then Eq. (21.74) takes the simple form

\(\theta = \frac{p}{p_{0} + p} .\) (21.77)

Equation (21.77) has the form of a Langmuir adsorption isotherm and is plotted in Figure 21–1. See Kittel and Kroemer [6, p. 142] for a plot of data for adsorption of an oxygen molecule by a heme group of myoglobin, which closely follows such an isotherm.
