A Boat Stuck at Hopewell Rocks during Low Tide

At 09:00, you find that your sailboat is stuck in 0.3 m deep water during low tide at Hopewell Rocks in the Bay of Fundy (Figure 13-30). You look at the tide table and verify that the next high tide is at 15:15, when the water height will be 13.1 m. Your boat needs a water depth of at least 3.0 m to float. Assuming that the tides cause the water height at Hopewell Rocks to change in harmonic motion, estimate the earliest time when your boat will be able to float. Use the tide data given in Table 13-2.

Admin
Solution

Let us assume that the water height, h, at Hopewell Rocks varies in simple harmonic motion. We can then use the given data to determine the amplitude, the period, and the phase constant for this motion. From the equation for h(t) we can determine the time when the height will be 3.0 m. The period of the tides is the time between two successive high tides or two successive low tides. Taking the time between two successive high tides, we get

 

\(T=15: 15-02: 48=12 h \text { and } 27 min =44820 s\)

 

Let us determine the amplitude of the tides. Note that there is a variation in the height of successive high and low tidesâ€”the generation of tides is a complicated phenomenon that depends on the relative positioning of the Sun and the Moon. We will take the maximum tide height to be 13.1 m, corresponding to 15:15 ADT (Atlantic Daylight Time). The amplitude of the tides is, therefore,

 

\(\begin{aligned}A &=\frac{\text { water height at high tide }-\text { water height at low tide }}{2} \\&=\frac{13.1 m -0.30 m }{2}=6.4 m\end{aligned}\)

 

Since the difference between the highest and the lowest positions in simple harmonic motion is equal to twice the amplitude, we divided the difference between the high and low tides by 2.

In this case, the equilibrium height, denoted by h(0), is the average of the heights at high and low tides:

 

\(\begin{aligned}h_{0} &=\frac{\text { height at high tide }+\text { height at low tide }}{2} \\&=\frac{13.1 m +0.30 m }{2}=6.7 m\end{aligned}\)

 

Given these quantities, we can now write an equation that describes the height of the water at Hopewell Rocks:

 

\(h(t)=6.7 m +(6.4 m ) \cos \left(\frac{2 \pi}{T} t+\phi\right)\) (1)

 

The first term in Equation (1) is the equilibrium height, \(h_{0}\). The variation in the water height is measured with respect to \(h_{0}\). This term is absent when x = 0 is taken as the equilibrium point.

From the previous examples, we know that we can determine \(\phi\) if we know h(t) at a given time. For ease of calculating, we take the morning low tide time, 09:23, as the start time, t = 0. At this time, the sea is 0.30 m high. Inserting this information in the above equation yields

 

\(h(0)=6.7 m +(6.4 m ) \cos \phi=0.30 m\)

 

Therefore,

 

\(\begin{gathered}\cos \phi=\frac{0.30 m -6.7 m }{6.4 m }=-1.0 \\\phi=\cos ^{-1}(-1.0)=\pi rad\end{gathered}\)

 

Now we can write Equation (1) for the water height:

 

\(h(t)=6.7 m +(6.4 m ) \cos \left(\frac{2 \pi}{T} t+\pi\right)\) (2)

 

The last task is to determine the time when the water height is 3.0 m. Setting h(t) = 3.0 m in Equation (2) and using the trigonometric identity \(\cos (\theta+\pi)=-\cos \theta\), we get

 

\(\begin{aligned}3.0 m &=6.7 m +(6.4 m ) \cos \left(\frac{2 \pi}{T} t+\pi\right) \\&=6.7 m -(6.4 m ) \cos \left(\frac{2 \pi}{T} t\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

Solving for t gives

 

\(\frac{2 \pi}{T} t=\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{6.7 m -3.0 m }{6.4 m }\right)=0.95\)

 

\(t=\frac{T}{2 \pi} \times 0.95=\frac{44820 s \times 0.95}{2 \pi} \approx 6.8 \times 10^{3} s =1 h 53 min\)

 

Since t = 0 was at 09:23, your boat should be ready to float again at about 11:16. Happy sailing, and donâ€™t leave your tide table at home!
