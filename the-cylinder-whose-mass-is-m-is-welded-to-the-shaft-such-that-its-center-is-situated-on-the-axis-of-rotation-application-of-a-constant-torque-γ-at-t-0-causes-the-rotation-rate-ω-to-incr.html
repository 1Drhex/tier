Guest Question.

The cylinder, whose mass is m, is welded to the shaft such that its center is situated on the axis of rotation. Application of a constant torque Γ at t = 0 causes the rotation rate Ω to increase from zero. Derive expressions for Ω and the reactions at bearings A and B as functions of the elapsed time.

Admin
Solution

This example reinforces our recognition that a planar analysis might not suffice, even though the motion is a simple rotation. Examination of the solution will lead us to a general discussion of the topic of dynamic imbalance. The forces of interest are the reactions the bearings apply to the shaft. These are represented in the free-body diagram as components transverse to the shaft at both bearings and a thrust component parallel to the shaft at bearing A. We may consider the shaft and the cylinder to constitute a single body because they are welded together. Doing so makes the forces exerted between them internal to the system. We ignore the weight because it is a static force. It is balanced by a static force acting in the (stationary) vertical direction at each bearing that superposes on the dynamic reactions we shall evaluate.

The cylinder executes a simple rotation, so any point along shaft \(A B\) would be an allowable point for summing moments. We use the center of mass \(G\). We align the \(x y z\) axes with the centerline of the cylinder in accord with the Appendix, and exploit the axisymmetry to define the \(y\) axis to be perpendicular to the plane formed by the shaft and the centerline. We also define an \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) coordinate system aligned with the shaft and with its \(y^{\prime}\) axis coincident with the \(y\) axis to facilitate describing the bearing forces and the rotation.

The angular velocity is parallel to the shaft, and the direction of this rotation is constant, so

\(\bar{\omega}=\Omega \bar{k}^{\prime}, \quad \bar{\alpha}=\dot{\Omega} \bar{k}^{\prime}\), (6.1.7)

which when resolved into \(x y z\) components become

\(\bar{\omega}=\Omega(\sin \theta \bar{i}+\cos \theta \bar{k}), \quad \bar{\alpha}=\dot{\Omega}(\sin \theta \bar{i}+\cos \theta \bar{k})\). (6.1.8)

We know from symmetry that xyz are principal axes, and the tabulated inertia properties are

\(I_{x x}=I_{y y}=m\left(\frac{1}{4} R^{2}+\frac{1}{12} h^{2}\right), \quad I_{z z}=\frac{1}{2} m R^{2}.\) (6.1.9)

We may employ Euler’s equations because \(x y z\) are principal axes. To form the moment resultants the applied couple \(\Gamma\) must be resolved into components. The moment of the bearing forces may be computed vectorially. For example, for bearing \(A\) the moment is \(\bar{r}_{A / G} \times\left(A_{x^{\prime}} \bar{i}^{\prime}+A_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}\right)\). Alternatively the moment sums can be computed as moments about the coordinate axes. Thus the bearing forces \(A_{x^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{x^{\prime}}\) exert moments about only the \(y\) axis, for which the lever arms are \(L / 2\), whereas \(A_{y^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{y^{\prime}}\) have lever arms of \((L / 2) \sin \theta\) about the \(z\) axis and \((L / 2) \cos \theta\) about the \(x\) axis. Applying the right-hand rule to ascertain the sign of each moment yields

\(\begin{aligned}\Sigma \bar{M}_{A} \cdot \bar{i} &=\Gamma \sin \theta+\left(-A_{y^{\prime}}+B_{y^{\prime}}\right) \frac{L}{2} \cos \theta=I_{x x} \alpha_{x} \\\\&=m\left(\frac{1}{4} R^{2}+\frac{1}{12} h^{2}\right) \dot{\Omega} \sin \theta, \\\\\Sigma \bar{M}_{A} \cdot \bar{j} &=\left(A_{x^{\prime}}-B_{x^{\prime}}\right) \frac{L}{2}=-\left(I_{z z}-I_{x x}\right) \omega_{x} \omega_{z} \\\\&=m\left(-\frac{1}{4} R^{2}+\frac{1}{12} h^{2}\right) \Omega^{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta, \\\\\Sigma \bar{M}_{A} \cdot \bar{k} &=\Gamma \cos \theta+\left(A_{y^{\prime}}-B_{y^{\prime}}\right) \frac{L}{2} \sin \theta=I_{z z} \alpha_{x} \\\\&=m\left(\frac{1}{2} R^{2}\right) \dot{\Omega} \cos \theta.\end{aligned}\)

The unknowns in these three equations are the four transverse bearing forces and \(\Omega\). The force equation of motion provides the additional equations required for determining these parameters and the thrust bearing force. The center of mass is on the axis of rotation, so \(\bar{a}_{G}=\overline{0} .\) In terms of components relative to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\), the force resultants are

\(\begin{array}{l}\Sigma \bar{F} \cdot \bar{i}^{\prime}=A_{x^{\prime}}+B_{x^{\prime}}=0, \\\\\Sigma \bar{F} \cdot \bar{\jmath}^{\prime}=A_{y^{\prime}}+B_{y^{\prime}}=0, \\\\\Sigma \bar{F} \cdot \bar{k}^{\prime}=A_{z^{\prime}}=0.\end{array} \)

The solution of these equations is

\(\large\begin{array}{c}\dot{\Omega}=\frac{12 \Gamma}{m\left[6 R^{2}+\left(h^{2}-3 R^{2}\right)(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]}, \\\\A_{x^{\prime}}=-B_{x^{\prime}}=\frac{m \Omega^{2}}{24 L}\left(h^{2}-3 R^{2}\right) \sin 2 \theta, \\\\A_{y^{\prime}}=-B_{y^{\prime}}=-\frac{m \dot{\Omega}}{24 L}\left(h^{2}-3 R^{2}\right) \sin 2 \theta, \\\\A_{z^{\prime}}=0.\end{array} \)

The torque is constant, so it follows that \(\dot{\Omega}\) is constant. Because \(\Omega=0\) initially, integrating \(\dot{\Omega}\) leads to a rotation rate that increases proportionally to elapsed time:

\(\large\Omega=\frac{12 \Gamma t}{m\left[6 R^{2}+\left(h^{2}-3 R^{2}\right)(\sin \theta)^{2}\right]}.\)

Substitution of these results into the expressions for the bearing forces shows that \(A_{x^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{x^{\prime}}\) increase as \(t^{2}\), whereas \(A_{y^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{y^{\prime}}\) are independent of the elapsed time.

Each of these results may be readily explained by following the manner in which the angular momentum \(\bar{H}_{G}\) changes. Such an examination would show that the rotation moves the head of \(\bar{H}_{G}\) in the \(-y^{\prime}\) direction, which requires a couple about the negative \(y^{\prime}\) axis. Such a moment is generated by the bearing forces \(A_{x^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{x^{\prime}}\), which depend solely on the current rotation rate. The bearing forces rotate at \(\Omega\), which means that the forces the shaft exerts in the bearing are experienced by the foundation as rotating forces. Such forces cause vibration in the foundation because their components relative to a fixed reference frame oscillate in magnitude. This is a condition of dynamic imbalance. The other transverse bearing forces, \(A_{y^{\prime}}\) and \(B_{y^{\prime}}\), are proportional to \(\dot{\Omega} .\) They are associated with the changing magnitude of \(\bar{H}_{G}\) and remain constant as long as \(\dot{\Omega}\) does not change. They too rotate at \(\Omega\) relative to a fixed reference frame, and therefore also produce oscillations in the foundation, although their value usually is small compared with that of the dynamic imbalance forces.

The nature of the forces would be more apparent if we had formulated the equations of motion by using \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) as the body-fixed reference frame. This would have required the rotation transformation to evaluate \(\left[I^{\prime}\right] .\) We may generalize such an analysis. Consider an arbitrary body that executes a pure rotation at angular speed \(\Omega\) about a shaft as the result of a torque \(\Gamma .\) The center of mass is situated at an arbitrary distance from this rotation axis. Let \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) be a body-fixed coordinate system whose \(z^{\prime}\) axis coincides with the shaft, and let \(\left[I^{\prime}\right]\) be the corresponding inertia matrix. In this case the angular motion is \(\bar{\omega}=\Omega \bar{k}^{\prime}, \bar{\alpha}=\dot{\Omega} \bar{k}^{\prime}\). Because \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime}\) is not necessarily principal, we use Eqs. (6.1.3) to form \(\bar{H}_{G}\) and its relative derivative, which gives

\(\begin{aligned}\bar{H}_{A}=&\left(I_{x x} \omega_{x}-I_{x y} \omega_{y}-I_{x z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \omega_{y}-I_{y x} \omega_{x}-I_{y z} \omega_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(I_{z z} \omega_{z}-I_{z x} \omega_{x}-I_{z y} \omega_{y}\right) \bar{k}, \\\\\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}=&\left(I_{x x} \alpha_{x}-I_{x y} \alpha_{y}-I_{x z} \alpha_{z}\right) \bar{i}+\left(I_{y y} \alpha_{y}-I_{y x} \alpha_{x}-I_{y z} \alpha_{z}\right) \bar{j} \\\\&+\left(I_{z z} \alpha_{z}-I_{z x} \alpha_{x}-I_{z y} \alpha_{y}\right) \bar{k} .\end{aligned}\) (6.1.3)

 

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{H}_{G}=-I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \Omega \bar{i}^{\prime}-I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \Omega \bar{j}^{\prime}+I_{z^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \Omega \bar{k}^{\prime}, \\\\\partial \bar{H}_{G} / \partial t=-I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega} \bar{i}^{\prime}-I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega} \bar{j}^{\prime}+I_{z^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega} \bar{k}^{\prime}.\end{array} \)

We refer to the free-body diagram to sum forces and moments relative to \(x^{\prime} y^{\prime} z^{\prime} .\) The equations of motion, Eqs. (6.1.1), then require that

\(\begin{array}{c}\Sigma \bar{F}=m \bar{a}_{G} \\\Sigma \bar{M}_{A}=\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{A}}{\partial t}+\bar{\omega} \times \bar{H}_{A}.\end{array}\) (6.1.1)

 

\(\begin{array}{c}\left(A_{x^{\prime}}+B_{x^{\prime}}\right) \bar{i}^{\prime}+\left(A_{y^{\prime}}+B_{y^{\prime}}\right) \bar{j}^{\prime}+A_{z^{\prime}} \bar{k}^{\prime}=m \bar{a}_{G}, \\\\\left(-A_{y^{\prime}}+B_{y^{\prime}}\right) \frac{L}{2} \bar{i}+\left(A_{x^{\prime}}-B_{x^{\prime}}\right) \frac{L}{2} \bar{j}^{\prime}+\Gamma \bar{k}^{\prime} \\\\=\left(-I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega}+I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \Omega^{2}\right) \bar{i}^{\prime}+\left(-I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega}-I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \Omega^{2}\right) \bar{j}^{\prime}+I_{z^{\prime} z^{\prime}} \dot{\Omega} \bar{k}^{\prime}.\end{array}\)

A dynamically balanced system is one in which any rotation can occur without generating dynamic reactions at either bearing, \(A_{x^{\prime}}=A_{y^{\prime}}=A_{z^{\prime}}=B_{x^{\prime}}=B_{y^{\prime}}=0 .\) The preceding equations of motion indicate that such a condition requires that \(\bar{a}_{G}=0\) and \(I_{x^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=I_{y^{\prime} z^{\prime}}=0 .\) In other words, dynamic balancing requires that the center of mass be situated on the rotation axis and that the rotation axis be a principal axis of inertia. In contrast, the condition of static balance merely requires the former.

This general observation is manifested by the specific results for the skewly mounted cylinder of the present system. When \(\theta=0\) the \(z\) axis coincides with the rotation axis, whereas \(\theta=90^{\circ}\) corresponds to alignment of \(x\) and the rotation axis. Both \(x\) and \(z\) are principal axes, and the results confirm that the bearing forces are zero in both cases. We also see that the forces are zero for any \(\theta\) if \(h=\sqrt{3} R\). In that case \(I_{x x}=I_{y y}=I_{z z}\), so that the cylinder is inertially equivalent to a sphere, for which all centroidal axes are principal. In any other case, bearing forces are required to sustain the motion, even though the center of mass coincides with the shaft.
