Guest Question.

Consider a rod consisting of two different metals A and B in thermal contact with one another. Each one has a thickness d and a cross-section surface area A. The metals are characterised by their electric conductivities, \(σ_A\) and \(σ_B\), their thermal conductivities \(κ_A\) and \(κ_B\), their Seebeck coefficients \(ε_A\) and \(ε_B\). These transport properties can all be considered as temperature independent. The end of metal rod A is in contact with a thermal bath at high temperature and the end of metal rod B is in contact with a thermal bath at low temperature. In other words, a temperature difference ΔT is imposed on the whole rod.
A constant electric current density \(j_q\) is driven through the rod. The electrostatic potential difference between the ends of the rod is \(\Delta \varphi \) (Fig. 11.11).

By conservation of the electric charge and because the state of the system is stationary, the electric current density \(j_q\) and the heat current density \(j_Q\) are conserved at the interface between metals A and B, i.e. \(j_q = j_{q_A} = j_{q_B}\) and \(j_Q = j_{Q_A} = j_{Q_B}\) . The electric current I flowing through materials A and B is the integral of the electric current densities \(j_{q_A}\) and \(j_{q_B}\) over the cross-section surface area A,

\(I = \int_{S}{j _{q_A } .dS} = \int_{S}{j _{q_B } .dS}\).

where the infinitesimal surface vector dS is oriented along the electric current density \(j_q\).
The thermal power \(P_Q\) exerted on materials A and B is the integral of the heat current densities \(j_{Q_A}\) and \(j_{Q_B}\) over the cross-section surface area A,

\(P_Q = \int_{S}{j _{Q_A } .dS} = \int_{S}{j _{Q_B } .dS}\).

The temperature differences \(ΔT_A\) and \(ΔT_B\) and the electric potential differences \(\Delta \varphi _A\) and \(\Delta \varphi _B\) across metals A and B are given by,

\(\Delta T_A = \int_{-d}^{0}{dr .(-\nabla T_A )}\) and \(\Delta T_B = \int_{0}^{d}{dr .(-\nabla T_B )}\).

\(\Delta \varphi _A = \int_{-d}^{0}{dr .(-\nabla \varphi _A )}\) and \(\Delta \varphi _B = \int_{0}^{d}{dr .(-\nabla \varphi _B )}\).

where the infinitesimal length vector dr is oriented along the electric current density \( j_q\) and the heat current density \(j_Q\). The temperature difference ΔT and the electrostatic potential difference \(\Delta \varphi \) across the whole rod satisfy,

\(ΔT = ΔT_A +ΔT_B \) and \(\Delta \varphi = \Delta \varphi_A + \Delta \varphi _B \).

The thermoelectric materials A and B have a length d and a cross-section surface area A, which can be written as,

\(d = \int_{-d}^{0}{dr.\hat{x} } =\int_{0}^{d}{dr .\hat{x} }\) and \(A = \int_{S}{dS.\hat{x} }\).

where \(\hat{x}\) is a unit vector oriented clockwise along the electric current density \(j_q\) and the heat current density \(j_Q\).

1. Express the electric charge and heat transport equations (11.95) for metals A and B at the interface between the metals in terms of the generalised forces \(∇T_A, ∇T_B, \nabla \varphi _A , \nabla \varphi _B\) and the temperature \(T_{AB}\) evaluated at the interface between the metals.

\(\begin{cases}j _Q=- \kappa \nabla T +T\varepsilon j _q\\j _q = - \sigma \varepsilon \nabla T -\sigma \nabla \varphi \end{cases}\) (11.95)
2. If the thickness d of the layers is small enough, the gradients can be considered as independent of the position. In this case, integrate the electric charge transport equations between the ends of metals A and B.
3. In the same case, integrate the heat transport equations between the ends of metals A and B.
4. Find expressions for \(ΔT_A\) and \(ΔT_B\) in terms of I, ΔT and the empirical coefficients.
5. Find expressions for \(Δ\varphi _A\) and \(Δ\varphi _B\) in terms of I, ΔT and the empirical coefficients.
6. Determine an expression for \(Δ\varphi \) in terms of \(T_{AB}\), I, ΔT and the empirical coefficients.

Solution

1. The electric charge transport equations for metals A and B are expressed at the interface between the metals as,

\(j_{q_A} = −σ_A ε_A ∇T_A − σ_A ∇\varphi _A\).

\(j_{q_B} = −σ_B ε_B ∇T_B − σ_B ∇\varphi _B\).

Likewise, the heat transport equations for metals A and B are expressed at the interface between the metals as,

\(j_{Q_A} = −κ _A ∇ T_A + T_{AB} ε_A j_q\).
\(j_{Q_B} = −κ _B ∇ T_B + T_{AB} ε_B j_q\).

2. The integrals of the electric charge transport equations over the volume are the product of integrals over the cross-section area A times integrals over the length d of the metals,

\(\int_{S}{j _{q_A} .dS} \int_{-d}^{0}{dr}.\hat{x} =\sigma _A\varepsilon _A \int_{-d}^{0}{dr.(-\nabla T_A)} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} } +\sigma _A\int_{-d}^{0}{dr.(-\nabla \varphi _A )} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} }\).

\(\int_{S}{j _{q_B} .dS} \int_{0}^{d}{dr}.\hat{x} =\sigma _B\varepsilon _B \int_{0}^{d}{dr.(-\nabla T_B)} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} } +\sigma _B\int_{0}^{d}{dr.(-\nabla \varphi _B )} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} }\).

Using the integral relations for \(I, ΔT_A, ΔT_B, Δ\varphi _A\) and \(Δ\varphi _B\), the integral of the electric charge transport equation across metals A and B can be written as,

\(I=\frac{A}{d}(σ_Aε_AΔT_A+σ_AΔφ_A)=\frac{A}{d}(σ_Bε_BΔT_B+σ_BΔφ_B]\).

3. The integrals of the heat transport equations over the volume of metals A and B are the product of integrals over the cross-section area A times integrals over the length d of the metals,

\(\int_{S}{j _{Q_A} .dS} \int_{-d}^{0}{dr}.\hat{x} =k _A\int_{-d}^{0}{dr.(-\nabla T_A)} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} } +T_{AB}\varepsilon _A\int_{S}{j_{q_A}.dS}\int_{-d}^{0}{dr.\hat{x}} \) .

\(\int_{S}{j _{Q_B} .dS} \int_{0}^{d}{dr}.\hat{x} =k _B\int_{0}^{d}{dr.(-\nabla T_B)} \int_{S}{dS .\hat{x} } +T_{AB}\varepsilon _B\int_{S}{j_{q_B}.dS}\int_{0}^{d}{dr.\hat{x}} \)

Using the integral relations for \(P_Q, I, ΔT_A\) and \(ΔT_B\), the integral of the heat transport equation across metals A and B can be written as,

\(P_Q = κ_A\frac{A}{d} Δ T_A + T_{AB} ε_A I = κ_B\frac{A}{d}Δ T_B + T_{AB} ε_B I\).

4. Using the relation \(ΔT_B = ΔT − ΔT_A\) in the heat transport equation, we find,

\(κ_A Δ T_A + T_{AB} ε_A\frac{d}{A} I = κ_B (ΔT − Δ T_A) + T_{AB} ε_B \frac{d}{A} I\).

This can be recast as,

\(Δ T_A = \frac{ε_B − ε_A}{κ_A + κ_B} T_{AB} \frac{d}{A} I +\frac{κ_B}{κ_A + κ_B}Δ_T\).

Using the relation \(ΔT_A = ΔT− ΔT_B\) in the heat transport equation, the latter becomes,

\(Δ T_B = \frac{ε_A − ε_B}{κ_A + κ_B} T_{AB} \frac{d}{A} I +\frac{κ_A}{κ_A + κ_B}Δ_T\).

5. Applying this expression for \(ΔT_A\) in the electric charge transport equation, the latter becomes,

\(I = σ_A \frac{ε_A (ε_B − ε_A)}{κ_A + κ_B} T_{AB} I + σ_A \frac{ε_A κ_B}{κ_A + κ_B} \frac{A}{d} Δ T + σ_A\frac{A}{d}Δ\varphi _A\).

This can be recast as,

\(\Delta \varphi _A = \Bigl(\frac{1}{\sigma _A}-\frac{\varepsilon _A(\varepsilon _B-\varepsilon _A)}{\kappa _A+\kappa _B}T_{AB} \Bigr) \frac{d}{A}I -\frac{\varepsilon _A\kappa _B}{\kappa _A+\kappa _B} \Delta T\).

Similarly, applying the expression for \(ΔT_B\) in the electric charge transport equation, the latter becomes,

\(I = σ_B\frac{ε_B (ε_A − ε_B)}{κ_A + κ_B} T_{AB} I + σ_B \frac{ε_B κ_A}{κ_A + κ_B} \frac{A}{d} Δ T + σ_B\frac{A}{d}Δ\varphi _B\).

This can be recast as,

\(\Delta \varphi _B = \Bigl(\frac{1}{\sigma _B}-\frac{\varepsilon _B(\varepsilon _A-\varepsilon _B)}{\kappa _A+\kappa _B}T_{AB} \Bigr) \frac{d}{A}I -\frac{\varepsilon _B\kappa _A}{\kappa _A+\kappa _B} \Delta T\).

6. An expression for the electrostatic potential difference \(\Delta \varphi\) at the ends of the rod is obtained by substituting the expressions for \(\Delta \varphi_A \) and \(\Delta \varphi_B \) in the equation
\(\Delta \varphi = \Delta \varphi_A +\Delta \varphi_B \),

\(\Delta \varphi =\biggl[\Bigl(\frac{1}{\sigma _A}+\frac{1}{\sigma _B} \Bigr)+\frac{(\varepsilon _A-\varepsilon _B)^2}{\kappa _A+\kappa _B} T_{AB} \biggr] \frac{d}{A} I -\frac{\varepsilon _A \kappa _B +\varepsilon _B \kappa _A}{\kappa _A +\kappa _B} \Delta T\).

The first term in brackets represents Ohm’s law. The last term represents the Seebeck effect and the second term in brackets implies the existence of thermal gradients in each metal even in the case where ΔT = 0 [184].
