Applying the Ideal Rocket Equation

A space probe has a total mass of \(1.30 \times 10^{3} kg , 5.20 \times 10^{2} kg\) of which is fuel. The probe is at rest near the edge of the solar system. The probe fires its rocket thrusters to make a final course correction, burning the fuel at a constant rate until it is gone. The burn lasts \(1.00 \times 10^{2} s\) and produces a constant thrust of 11.0 kN. Estimate

(a) the initial acceleration of the probe

(b) the speed of the probe 80.0 s into the burn

(c) the maximum speed of the probe

(d) the maximum acceleration of the probe

Admin
Solution

If we assume that all external forces acting on the probe are negligible, we can apply the ideal rocket equation. To do so, we need to find the rate of fuel consumption, R, and the effective exhaust speed, \(y _{ er ^{}}\). Since the rate of fuel consumption is constant and the rocket burns \(5.20 \times 10^{2} kg \text { of fuel in } 1.00 \times 10^{2} s \text {, }\)

 

\(R=\frac{d m}{d t}=\frac{5.20 \times 10^{2} kg }{1.00 \times 10^{2} s }=5.20 kg \cdot s ^{-1}\)

 

We can determine the effective exhaust speed from the thrust, T = Ma (Equation 7-35b):

 

\(M a=R v_{ er }\) {7-35b}

 

\(T=R v_{e r}\)

 

\(\therefore v_{e r}=\frac{T}{R}=\frac{1.1 \times 10^{4} N }{5.20 kg \cdot s ^{-1}}=2.12 \times 10^{3} m \cdot s ^{-1}\)

 

(a) Assume the probe is so far away from any celestial bodies that the only significant force acting on the probe is the thrust from the rocket. Therefore,

 

\(T=M a\)

 

\(\therefore a=\frac{T}{M}=\frac{1.10 \times 10^{4} N }{1.30 \times 10^{3} kg }=8.46 m \cdot s ^{-2}\)

 

Note that to find the initial acceleration of the probe, we have used the total initial mass of the probe.

(b) The mass of the probe and the remaining fuel after 80.0 s is

 

\(M(80 s )=M(0 s )-R t\)

 

\(=1.30 \times 10^{3} kg -\left(5.20 kg \cdot s ^{-1}\right)(80.0 s )\)

 

\(=8.84 \times 10^{2} kg\)

 

We can use this mass in Equation 7-34, recalling that the initial speed is zero, to find

 

\(v_{ f }-v_{ i }=v_{ er } \ln \left(\frac{M_{ i }}{M_{ f }}\right)\) {7-34}

 

\(v_{f}(80.0 s )=v_{e r} \ln \left(\frac{M_{i}}{M_{f}}\right)\)

 

\(=\left(2.12 \times 10^{3} m \cdot s ^{-1}\right) \ln \left(\frac{1.30 \times 10^{3} kg }{8.84 \times 10^{2} kg }\right)\)

 

\(=8.18 \times 10^{2} m \cdot s ^{-1}\)

 

(c) The maximum speed of the rocket occurs when the rocket has burned all its fuel, at which point the mass of the probe is \(1.30 \times 10^{3} kg -5.20 \times 10^{2} kg =7.80 \times 10^{2} kg\) .

We can use Equation 7-34 again to find

 

\(v_{f}\left(1.00 \times 10^{2} s \right)=v_{e r} \ln \left(\frac{M_{i}}{M_{f}}\right)\)

 

\(=\left(2.12 \times 10^{3} m \cdot s ^{-1}\right) \ln \left(\frac{1.30 \times 10^{3} kg }{7.80 \times 10^{2} kg }\right)\)

 

\(=1.08 \times 10^{3} m \cdot s ^{-1}\)

 

(d) Since the thrust is constant, the maximum acceleration will occur when the probe is least massive—just as the rocket runs out of fuel. At this instant, the mass of the probe is \(7.80 \times 10^{2} kg\) and we can use our acceleration equation from part (a) to find

 

\(a_{\max }=\frac{T}{M_{\min }}=\frac{1.10 \times 10^{4} N }{7.80 \times 10 kg }=14.1 m \cdot s ^{-2}\)

 

Making sense of the result

 

We can calculate the average acceleration by dividing the change in speed by the time for the burn to find \(a_{ avg }=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}=\frac{1.08 \times 10^{3} m \cdot s ^{-1}}{1.00 \times 10^{2} s }=10.8 m \cdot s ^{-2}\) . This value falls somewhere between our calculated minimum and maximum values and thus they seem reasonable.
