Guest Question.

Consider a first-order-plus-time-delay model:

\(\frac{Y(s) }{U(s)}=\frac{Ke^{-θs} }{\tau s+1}\) (20-7)

(a) Derive the equivalent step-response model by considering the analytical solution to a unit step change in the input.
(b) Calculate the step-response coefficients, {\( S_{i}\) }, for the following parameter values: \(K = 5, \tau= 15 min, θ = 3 min\), and a sampling period of \( Δt = 1 min\) . Also, calculate and plot the response \( y(k)\) for \( 0 ≤ k ≤ 80\) after a step change in u from 0 to 3 occurs at \( t = 2 min\) .

Admin
Solution

(a) The step response for a first-order model without a time delay (θ = 0) was derived in Chapter 5

\(y(t) = KM(1 − e^{-t/\tau } )\) (5-16)

where M is the magnitude of the step change. The corresponding response for the model with a time delay is

\(y(t) =0 for t ≤ θ\)
\(y(t) = KM (1 −e^{-(t-\theta )/\tau } ) for t > θ\) (20-8)

The sampling instants are denoted by t = iΔt whereΔt is the sampling period and i = 1, 2,…Substituting t = iΔt into Eq. 20-8 gives the response for 0 ≤ i ≤ 80:

\(y(iΔt) =0 for iΔt ≤ θ\)

\(y(iΔt) = KM(1 −e^{-(iΔt-\theta )/\tau }) for Δt > θ\) (20-9)

where i = 1, 2,…, 80

The number of step-response coefficients, N in Eq. 20-6, is specified to be N = 80 so that NΔt is slightly larger than the process settling time of approximately \( 5\tau + θ\) . As indicated earlier in this section, the ith step-response coefficient is the value of the unit step response at the ith sampling instant. Thus, the step-response coefficients can be determined from Eq. 20-9 after setting M = 1:

\(y(k+1)=y(0)+\sum\limits_{i=1}^{N-1}{S_i\Delta u(k-i+1)+S_Nu(k-N+1)} \) (20.6)

 

\(S_{i} =0 for iΔt ≤ θ\)
\(S_{i} = K(1 − e^{-(i\Delta t−\theta )∕\tau } ) for iΔt > θ\) (20-10)
where i = 1, 2, . . . , 80

(b) Substituting numerical values into Eq. 20-10 gives the step-response coefficients in Table 20.1. The step response y(k) in Fig. 20.4 can be calculated either from Eqs. 20-6 and 20-10, or from Eq. 20-9. For \(θ = 2 min\), \(S_{1} = S_{2} = 0\), and the step response is zero until t > 2 min. The new steady-state value is y = 15 because the steady-state gain in Eq. 20-7 is K = 5 and the magnitude of the step change is M = 3. Because \(Δt = 1\) min and the step change occurs at t = 3 min, \(Δu(k) = M = 3 for k = 3\) and \(Δu(k) = 0\) for all other values of k. Recall that \(Δu(k)\) is defined as \(Δu(k) ≜ u(k) – u(k − 1)\).

For this example, the response y(k) could be calculated analytically from Eq. 20-9 because a transfer function model was assumed. However, in many MPC applications, the transfer function model is not known, and thus the response must be calculated from the step-response model in Eq. 20-6.
