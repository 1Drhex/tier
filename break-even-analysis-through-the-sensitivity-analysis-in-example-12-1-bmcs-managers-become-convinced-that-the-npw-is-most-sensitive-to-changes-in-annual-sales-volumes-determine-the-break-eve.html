Break-Even Analysis

Through the sensitivity analysis in , BMC’s managers become convinced that the \(NPW\) is most sensitive to changes in annual sales volumes. Determine the break-even \(NPW\) value as a function of that variable.

Admin
Solution

The analysis is shown in \(Table 12.3\), in which the revenues and costs of the \(BMC\) transmission-housings project are set out in terms of an unknown amount of annual sales \(X\).

We calculate the \(PW\)s of cash inflows and outflows as follows:

• \(PW\) of cash inflows:

PW\((15\%)_{Inflow}\) = (PW of after-tax net revenue)

+ (PW of net salvage value)

+ (PW of tax savings from depreciation)

\(= 30X(P/A, 15\%, 5) + \$37,389(P/F, 15\%, 5)\)

The \(NPW\) of cash flows for the BMC is thus

\(PW(15\%) = 100.5650X + \$44,490\)

 

\(= -(30.1694X + \$145,113)\)

 

\(= 70.3956X – \$100,623\).

In \(Table 12.4\), we compute the \(PW\) of the inflows and the \(PW\) of the outflows as a function of demand \((X)\).

The \(NPW\) will be just slightly positive if the company sells \(1,430\) units. Precisely calculated, the zero-\(NPW\) point (break-even volume) is \(1,429.43\) units:
