Behavior Near Equilibrium of
Classify the type of equilibrium point for the cases of by linearizing the equations of motion near the equilibrium points.

Admin
Solution

In , the stabilities of the equilibrium points were identified from the phase plot. In this example, the equations of motion are linearized about the equilibrium points, and the characteristic roots are obtained to identify the behavior near the equilibria. The system model is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{x}=y \\&\dot{y}=-\omega_{n}^{2}\left(x-2 \alpha x^{3}\right) .\end{aligned}\)

 

The equilibrium equation is given by

 

\(-\omega_{n}^{2}\left(x_{e q}-2 \alpha x_{e q}^{3}\right)=0\)

 

There are three equilibrium points and they are \((0,0),(1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}, 0), \text { and }(-1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}, 0)\). From Figure 11.7, we already have found that the equilibrium point at (0, 0) is a center and the other two are saddle points. We show this again by examining the characteristic roots of the linearized equations of motion about these three equilibrium points. The first equation is already linear, and the second equation must be linearized.
(a) Equilibrium point at (0, 0)

The right-hand side of the second equation is expanded about \(x\) = 0 and is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}\dot{y} &=g_{2}(x)=-\omega_{n}^{2}\left(x-2 \alpha x^{3}\right) \\& \simeq g_{2}(0)+\left.\frac{d g_{2}(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=0} \cdot x \\&=-\omega_{n}^{2} x\end{aligned}\)

 

The equations of motion are then

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{x} \\\dot{y}\end{array}\right\} \simeq\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\-\omega_{n}^{2} & 0\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}x \\y\end{array}\right\}\)

 

The characteristic roots are obtained from

 

\(\operatorname{det}\left[\begin{array}{cc}0-\lambda & 1 \\-\omega_{n}^{2} & 0-\lambda\end{array}\right]=0\)

 

giving the eigenvalues, \(\lambda=\pm i \omega_{n}\). Therefore, the equilibrium point at (0, 0) is a center

(b) Equilibrium point at \((1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}, 0)\)

The linearized \(g_{2}(x)\) about \(x=1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}\) is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}g_{2}(x) & \sim g_{2}(1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha})+\left.\frac{d g_{2}(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}}\left(x-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \alpha}}\right) \\&=2 \omega_{n}^{2}\left(x-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \alpha}}\right) .\end{aligned}\)

 

The equations of motion are rewritten as

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{x}=y \\&\dot{y}=2 \omega_{n}^{2}\left(x-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \alpha}}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

or equivalently,

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{x} \\\dot{y}\end{array}\right\} \simeq\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\2 \omega_{n}^{2} & 0\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}x \\y\end{array}\right\}+\left\{\begin{array}{c}0 \\-2 \omega_{n}^{2} / \sqrt{2 \alpha}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

The characteristic roots are obtained from

 

\(\operatorname{det}\left[\begin{array}{cc}0-\lambda & 1 \\2 \omega_{n}^{2} & 0-\lambda\end{array}\right]=0\)

 

and the eigenvalues are \(\pm \sqrt{2} \omega_{n}\) They are real and have opposite signs. Therefore, the equilibrium point is a saddle point.
(c) Equilibrium point at \((-1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha} , 0)\)

The linearized \(g_{2}(x)\) about \(x= -1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}\) is given by

 

\(\begin{aligned}g_{2}(x) & \sim g_{2}(1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha})+\left.\frac{d g_{2}(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}}(x+1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}) \\&=2 \omega_{n}^{2}(x-1 / \sqrt{2 \alpha}) .\end{aligned}\)

 

The equations of motion are rewritten as

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\dot{x}=y \\&\dot{y}=2 \omega_{n}^{2}\left(x-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \alpha}}\right)\end{aligned}\)

 

or in matrix form,

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{x} \\\dot{y}\end{array}\right\} \simeq\left[\begin{array}{cc}0 & 1 \\2 \omega_{n}^{2} & 0\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}x \\y\end{array}\right\}+\left\{\begin{array}{c}0 \\2 \omega_{n}^{2} / \sqrt{2 \alpha}\end{array}\right\}\)

 

The eigenvalues are \(\pm \sqrt{2} \omega_{n}\). They are real and have the opposite signs. Therefore, the equilibrium point is a saddle point.
