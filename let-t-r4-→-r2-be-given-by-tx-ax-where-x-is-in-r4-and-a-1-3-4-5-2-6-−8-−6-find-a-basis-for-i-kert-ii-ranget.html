Guest Question.

Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^{4} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{2}\) be given by \(T(\mathbf{x})=\mathrm{Ax}\) where \(\mathrm{x}\) is in \(\mathbb{R}^{4}\) and

\(\mathrm{A}=\left(\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\2 & 6 & -8 & -6\end{array}\right)\)

Find a basis for (i) \(\operatorname{ker}(T)\) (ii) \(\operatorname{range}(T)\)

Admin
Solution

(i) The kernel is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^{4}\) because \(\mathbb{R}^{4}\) is the start vector space of \(T\).

What is \(\operatorname{ker}(T)\) equal to?

It is the null space \(\mathrm{x}\) of matrix \(\mathrm{A}\).

How do we find a basis for \(\operatorname{ker}(T)\) ?

We need to place the above matrix A into (reduced) row echelon form \(\mathrm{R}\) and then solve \(\mathrm{Rx}=\mathrm{O}\). The general solution \(\mathrm{x}\) gives a basis (axes) for the kernel. We can use MATLAB or hand calculations to obtain the reduced row echelon form:

\(\mathrm{A}=\left(\begin{array}{rrrr}1 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\2 & 6 & -8 & -6\end{array}\right) ⇒ \left(\begin{array}{llll}1 & 3 & 0 & 1 \\0 & 0 & 1 & 1\end{array}\right)=\mathbf{R}\)

Let \(\mathbf{x}=\left(\begin{array}{lll}x & y & z & w\end{array}\right)^{T}\). Expanding the rows of \(\mathrm{Rx}=\mathrm{O}\) gives the simultaneous equations:

\(\begin{aligned}x+3 y+w &=0 (1)\\z+w &=0 (2)\end{aligned}\)

From the bottom equation (2) we have \(z=-w\). Let \(w=s\) where \(s\) is any real number. We have \(z=-w=-s\). Substituting \(w=s\) into the top equation ( 1 ) gives

\(x+3 y+s=0 \quad \text { implies } \quad x=-3 y-s\)

Let \(y=t\) where \(t\) is any real number. We have \(x=-3 y-s=-3 t-s\). Hence \(x=-3 t-s, y=t\), \(z=-s\) and \(w=s:\)

\(\mathbf{x}=\left(\begin{array}{c}x \\y \\z \\w\end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{r}-3 t-s \\t \\-s \\s\end{array}\right)=s\left(\begin{array}{r}-1 \\0 \\-1 \\1\end{array}\right)+t\left(\begin{array}{r}-3 \\1 \\0 \\0\end{array}\right)\)

Therefore a set \(B\) of basis (axes) vectors for the kernel of \(T\) is given by

\(B=\left\{\begin{array}{llll}(-1 & 0 & -1 & 1\end{array})^{T},\left(\begin{array}{llll}-3 & 1 & 0 & 0\end{array}\right)^{T}\right\} \)

Hence \(n u l l i t y(T)=2\) because we have two basis (axes) vectors for \(\operatorname{ker}(T)\)

(ii) What is range \((T)\) equal to?

By the above Proposition (5.13):

range \((T)\) is the column space of \(\mathrm{A}\).

The column space of A gives range \((T)\). We convert the columns of matrix A into rows by taking the transpose of \(\mathrm{A}\).

A set \(B^{\prime}\) of basis (axes) vectors for the range of \(T\) can be found by determining the reduced row echelon form of \(\mathrm{A}^{T}\) (matrix A transposed). The non-zero rows of this reduced row echelon form give a basis for the range of \(T\). We have

\(\mathbf{A}^{T}=\left(\begin{array}{cc}1 & 2 \\3 & 6 \\4 & -8 \\5 & -6\end{array}\right) ⇒ \left(\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\0 & 1 \\0 & 0 \\0 & 0\end{array}\right)\begin{aligned}=\mathbf{R}^{\prime}\end{aligned} \begin{matrix} \begin{matrix} \leftarrow \text { non-zero row }\\\leftarrow \text { non-zero row } &\end{matrix}\\ \\ \\ \end{matrix}\)

The first two rows of the reduced row echelon form matrix \(\mathbf{R}^{\prime}\) is a basis for the range of \(T\) :

\(B^{\prime}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll}(1 & 0)\end{array}^{T},\left(\begin{array}{ll}0 & 1\end{array}\right)^{T}\right\}\)

Since the range of \(T\) has two basis (axes) vectors so \(\operatorname{rank}(T)=2\).

Proposition (5.13). Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^{n} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m}\) be a linear transformation given by \(T(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{A x}\). Then range \((T)\) is the column space of \(\mathbf{A}\).
