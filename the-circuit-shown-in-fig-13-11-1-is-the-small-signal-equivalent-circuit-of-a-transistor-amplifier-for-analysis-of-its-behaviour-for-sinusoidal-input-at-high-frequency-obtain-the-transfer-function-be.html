Guest Question.

The circuit shown in Fig. 13.11-1 is the small signal equivalent circuit of a transistor amplifier for analysis of its behaviour for sinusoidal input at high frequency. Obtain the transfer function between the output voltage and input source voltage.

Admin
Solution

We find the Norton’s equivalent of the circuit to the left of \(100p F\) capacitor first.

The sub-circuits needed for determining this equivalent are shown in Fig. 13.11-2. The short-circuit current in the first circuit is \(\frac{v_{s}(t)}{50+2000 / / 50} \times \frac{2000}{2050}=9.876 \times 10^{-3} v_{s}(t) \)

The Norton’s equivalent resistance is \([(50Ω//2kΩ) + 50Ω] //1kΩ = 89.9 Ω\). Thus the required Norton’s equivalent is \(9.876×10^{6}\) \(v_{S}(t) A\) in parallel with \(89.9 Ω\). The original circuit with this Norton’s equivalent in place is shown in circuit of Fig. 13.11-3 (a) with \(R_{1} = 89.9 Ω,\) \(R_{2} = 2 kΩ,\) \(C_{1} = 100 pF,\) \(C_{2} = 5 pF\) and \(g_{m} = 0.08.\)

The corresponding s-domain equivalent circuit is shown in circuit (b) of Fig. 13.11-3.

The node equations written for the two node voltage transforms \(V(s)\) and \(V_{o}(s)\) are as follows:

\(V(s)\left[s\left(C_{1}+C_{2}\right)+\frac{1}{R_{1}}\right]+V_{o}(s)\left[-s C_{2}\right]=k V_{s}(s)\)

 

\(V(s)\left[-s C_{2}+g_{m}\right]+V_{o}(s)\left[s C_{2}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}\right]=0\)

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc} s\left(C_{1}+C_{2}\right)+\frac{1}{R_{1}} & -s C_{2} \\ -s C_{2}+g_{m} & s C_{2}+\frac{1}{R_{2}} \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{c} V(s) \\ V_{o}(s) \end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{c} k V_{s}(s) \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\)

 

Solving for \(V_{o}(s)\) and simplifying the expression, we get

\(H(s)=\frac{V_{o}(s)}{V_{s}(s)}=\frac{k}{C_{1}} \frac{\left(s-\frac{g_{m} /}{C_{2}}\right)}{s^{2}+s\left(\frac{1+g_{m} R_{1}}{R_{1} C_{1}}+\frac{1_{1}}{R_{2} C_{1}}+\frac{1_{1}}{R_{2} C_{2}}\right)+\left(\frac{1_{1}}{R_{1} R_{2} C_{1} C_{2}}\right)}\)

 

Substituting the numerical values for various parameters, we get

\(H(s)=\frac{98.76 \times 10^{6}\left(s-1.6 \times 10^{10}\right)}{s^{2}+1.016 \times 10^{9} s+\left(105.47 \times 10^{6}\right)^{2}}\)

 

The poles are at \(s = -10^{9} nepers/s\) and \(s = -11.07×10^{6} nepers/s\). The zero is at \(s = 1.6×10^{10} nepers/s\).

 

Note that compared to the pole at \(–11.07×10^{6}\) the other pole and the zero are located two orders away from it. The natural response term contributed by the pole at \(s = -11.07×10^{6} nepers/s\) will have a time constant of \(90.3 ns\) whereas the natural response term contributed by the pole at \(s = -10^{9} nepers/s\) will have a time constant of \(1 ns\). Thus the natural response term contributed by the pole at \(s = -10^{9} nepers/s\) will disappear in about \(5\)% of the time constant of the other term. Therefore, the time constant of \(90.3 ns\) is the dominant time constant in this amplifier and the corresponding pole at \(–11.07×10^{6}\) is the dominant pole. The amplifier transfer function can be approximated by neglecting the zero and the non-dominant pole to

\(H(s)=\frac{98.76 \times 10^{6}\left(s-1.6 \times 10^{10}\right)}{\left(s+10^{9}\right)\left(s+11.07 \times 10^{6}\right)} \approx \frac{-1.58 \times 10^{9}}{\left(s+11.07 \times 10^{6}\right)}=-142.7\left(\frac{11.07 \times 10^{6}}{s+11.07 \times 10^{6}}\right)\)
