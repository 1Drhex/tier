Guest Question.

Neutron diffraction. Consider a beam of neutrons scattering from a crystal (Figure 10.14). The interaction between neutrons and the nuclei in the crystal is short ranged, and can be approximated as

\( V( r )=\frac{2 \pi \hbar^{2} b}{m} \sum_{i} \delta^{3}\left( r - r _{i}\right) \),

where the \( r_i \) are the locations of the nuclei and the strength of the potential is expressed in terms of the nuclear scattering length b.

(a) In the first Born approximation, show that

\( \frac{d \sigma}{d \Omega}=b^{2}\left|\sum_{i} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}}\right|^{2} \).

\( \text { where } q \equiv k-k^{\prime} \text {. } \)

(b) Now consider the case where the nuclei are arranged on a cubic lattice with spacing a. Take the positions to be

\( r _{i}=l a \hat{\imath}+m a \hat{\jmath}+n a \hat{k} \).

where l, m, and n all range from 0 to N-1, so there are a total of N³ nuclei. Show that

\( \frac{d \sigma}{d \Omega}=b^{2} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(N q_{x} a / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{x} a / 2\right)} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(N q_{y} a / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{y} a / 2\right)} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(N q_{z} a / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{z} a / 2\right)} \).

(c) Plot

\( \frac{1}{N} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(N q_{x} a / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{x} a / 2\right)} \).

as a function of \( q_{x} a \text { for several values of } N(N=1,5,10) \) to show that the function describes a series of peaks that become progressively sharper as N increases.

(d) In light of (c), in the limit of large N the differential scattering cross section is negligibly small except at one of these peaks:

\( q = G _{\ell m n}=\frac{2 \pi}{a}(l \hat{\imath}+m \hat{\jmath}+n \hat{k}) \).

for integer l, m, and n. The vectors \( G _{l m n} \) are called reciprocal lattice vectors. Find the scattering angles (θ) at which peaks occur. If the neutron’s wavelength is equal to the crystal spacing a, what are the three smallest (nonzero) angles?.

Comment: Neutron diffraction is one method used, to determine crystal structures (electrons and x-rays can also be used and the same expression for the locations of the peaks holds). In this problem we looked at a cubic arrangement of atoms, but a different arrangement (hexagonal for example) would produce peaks at a different set of angles. Thus from the scattering data one can infer the underlying crystal structure.

Solution

(a) In the first Born approximation (Equation 10.79):

\( f(\theta, \phi) \approx-\frac{m}{2 \pi \hbar^{2}} \int e^{i\left( k ^{\prime}- k \right) \cdot r _{0}} V\left( r _{0}\right) d^{3} r _{0} \) (10.79).

\( f(\theta, \phi) \approx-\frac{m}{2 \pi \hbar^{2}} \int e^{i\left( k ^{\prime}- k \right) \cdot r _{0}} V\left( r _{0}\right) d^{3} r _{0}=-\frac{m}{2 \pi \hbar^{2}} \frac{2 \pi \hbar^{2} b}{m} \sum_{i} \int e^{i\left( k ^{\prime}- k \right) \cdot r _{0}} \delta^{3}\left( r _{0}- r _{i}\right) d^{3} r _{0} \).

\( =-b \sum_{i} e^{i\left( k ^{\prime}- k \right) \cdot r _{i}}=-b \sum_{i} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}} \).

\( \frac{d \sigma}{d \Omega}=|f|^{2}=b^{2}\left|\sum_{i} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}}\right|^{2} \).

(b)

\( \sum_{i} e^{-i q \cdot r _{i}}=\sum_{l, m, n} e^{-i\left(q_{x} l a+q_{y} m a+q_{z} n a\right)}=\sum_{l=0}^{N-1} e^{-i q_{x} l a} \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} e^{-i q_{y} m a} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} e^{-i q_{z} n a} \).

\( \text { Now, letting } u \equiv \exp \left(-i q_{x} a\right) \).

\( \sum_{l=0}^{N-1} e^{-i q_{x} l a}=\sum_{l=0}^{N-1} u^{l}=\frac{1-u^{N}}{1-u}=\frac{1-e^{-i q_{x} a N}}{1-e^{-i q_{x} a}}=\frac{e^{-i q_{x} a N / 2}\left(e^{i q_{x} a N / 2}-e^{-i q_{x} a N / 2}\right)}{e^{-i q_{x} a / 2}\left(e^{i q_{x} a / 2}-e^{-i q_{x} a / 2}\right)} \).

\( =e^{-i q_{x} a(N-1) / 2} \frac{\sin \left(q_{x} a N / 2\right)}{\sin \left(q_{x} a / 2\right)} \).

so

\( \frac{d \sigma}{d \Omega}=b^{2} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{x} a N / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{x} a / 2\right)} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{y} a N / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{y} a / 2\right)} \frac{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{z} a N / 2\right)}{\sin ^{2}\left(q_{z} a / 2\right)} \).

(c) Here’s the graph:

\( f\left[N_{-}, x_{-}\right]:=\left(\frac{1}{N}\right)(\operatorname{Sin}[N x / 2] / \operatorname{Sin}[x / 2])^{2} \).

\( \operatorname{Plot}[\{f[1, x], f[5, x], f[10, x]\},\{x, 0,4 \pi\}, \text { PlotRange } \rightarrow\{0,10\}] \).

(d) The vectors k, k′ and G form an isosceles triangle (Figure 10.11), and

\( \sin (\theta / 2)=\frac{G / 2}{k}=\frac{\pi}{k a} \sqrt{l^{2}+m^{2}+n^{2}} \).

so, in terms of the wavelength \( \lambda=2 \pi / k \).

\( \theta=2 \arcsin \left(\frac{\lambda}{2 a} \sqrt{l^{2}+m^{2}+n^{2}}\right) \).

The smallest (nonzero) angles occur when \( \left(l^{2}+m^{2}+n^{2}\right)=1,2 \text {, or } 3 \) corresponding to angles

\( \pi / 3, \pi / 2 \text {, and } 2 \pi / 3 \).
