Guest Question.

The solid shaft shown in Figure E3.1(a) is subjected to two concentrated torques, respectively, at A and B. The diameter of the shaft changes from 1m to 0.5m at B. What is the total angle of twist at locations A and B and what is the maximum shear stress within the shaft? \(E=200 GPa \text { and } \nu=0.25\).

Admin
Solution

This is a statically determinate system subjected to only twist moments. The reaction torque at the built-in end can be determined from the equilibrium of moment. The method of section can be used to calculate the torques on the sections between A and B and B and C. The stresses on these sections can then be calculated by Equation (3.2). Because the shaft is under a discrete variation of torques and geometrical dimension, Equation (3.6) is used to calculate the angle of twist.

 

\(\tau=\frac{T r}{J}\) (3.2)

 

\(\theta=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{T_{i} L_{i}}{G_{i} J_{i}}\) (3.6)

 

From the equilibrium of Figure E3.1(b):

 

\(T_{c}-T_{A}-T_{B}=0\)

 

\(T_{ c }=T_{ A }+T_{ B }=5 MNm\)

 

By taking sections between A and B, and B and C (see Figure E3.1(b)), the twist moment diagram is drawn in Figure E3.1(c). Following the right-hand rule, the twist moments on the sections along the axis are defined as positive.

Total angle of twist:

 

\(\text { Since } G_{ AB }=G_{ BC }=G=\frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}=\frac{200}{2(1+0.25)}=80 GPa\)

 

\(J_{ AB }=\frac{\pi}{32}(0.5)^{4}=\frac{\pi}{512} m ^{4}\)

 

\(J_{ BC }=\frac{\pi}{32}(1)^{4}=\frac{\pi}{32} m ^{4}\)

 

From Equation (3.6), the angle of twist at A is:

 

\(\theta_{ A }=\sum_{i=1,2} \frac{T_{i} L_{i}}{G_{i} J_{i}}=\frac{T_{A B} L_{A B}}{G_{A B} J_{A B}}+\frac{T_{B C} L_{B C}}{G_{B C} J_{B C}}\)

 

\(=\frac{2 \times 4}{80 \times 10^{3} \pi / 512}+\frac{5 \times 5}{80 \times 10^{3} \pi / 32}=19.48 \times 10^{-3}( rad )\)

 

The angle of twist at B is:

 

\(\theta_{ B }=\frac{T_{ BC } L_{ BC }}{G_{ BC } J_{ BC }}=\frac{5 \times 5}{80 \times 10^{3} \pi / 32}=3.18 \times 10^{-3}( rad )\)

 

Maximum shear stress The maximum shear stresses within AB and BC are needed for the overall maximum. From Equation (3.2):

 

\(\tau_{A B}^{\max }=\frac{T_{A B} r_{\max }}{J_{A B}}=\frac{2 \times(0.5 / 2)}{\pi / 512}=81.49 MPa\)

 

\(\tau_{ BC }^{\max }=\frac{T_{ BC } r_{\max }}{J_{ BC }}=\frac{5 \times(1 / 2)}{\pi / 32}=25.46 MPa\)

 

Thus, the maximum shear stress occurs between A and B.
