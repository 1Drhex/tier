Guest Question.

Carnot Cycle Efficiency

Consider 1 mole of an ideal gas in a piston窶田ylinder assembly. This gas undergoes a Carnot cycle, which is described below. The heat capacity is constant, \(c_{v}=(3 / 2) R\).

(i) A reversible, isothermal expansion from 10 bar to 0.1 bar.

(ii) A reversible, adiabatic expansion from 0.1 bar and 1000 K to 300 K.

(iii) A reversible, isothermal compression at 300 K.

(iv) A reversible, adiabatic compression from 300 K to 1000 K and 10 bar.

Perform the following analysis:

(a) Calculate Q, W, and \(\Delta U\) for each of the steps in the Carnot cycle.

(b) Draw the cycle on a Pv diagram.

(e) Calculate the effi ciency of the cycle.

(d) Compare \(\eta \text { to } 1-\left(T_{ c } / T_{ H }\right)\).

(e) If what is found in part (d) is true, in general, suggest two ways to make the above process more efficient.

Admin
Solution

(a) We will analyze each of the steps separately, with a little help from the results of Section 2.7. We label each state in a manner consistent with Figure 2.17.

(i) The fi rst process is a reversible, isothermal expansion at 1000 K from state 1 at 10 bar to state 2 at 0.1 bar. By defi nition, the internal energy change for an ideal gas at constant temperature is:

 

\(\Delta U=0\)

 

We can calculate the work using a result from Section 2.7:

 

\(W=\int \frac{n R T}{P} d P=n R T \ln \frac{P_{2}}{P_{1}}=-38,287[ J ]\)

 

The negative sign indicates that the system is performing work on the surroundings (we are getting useful work out). To fi nd the heat, we apply the first law:

 

\(Q_{ H }=\Delta U-W=38,287[ J ]\)

 

(ii) The second process is a reversible, adiabatic expansion from 0.1 bar and 1000 K to state 3 at 300 K. The pressure decreases during this process. By the defi nition of an adiabatic process:

 

\(Q=0\)

 

At constant heat capacity, the change in internal energy becomes:

 

\(\Delta U=n c_{v}\left(T_{3}-T_{2}\right)=-8730[ J ]\)

 

Applying the first law gives:

 

\(W=\Delta U=-8730[ J ]\)

 

(iii) The third process is a reversible, isothermal compression at 300 K. Again:

 

\(\Delta U=0\)

 

and,

 

\(W=-\int P d V=\int \frac{n R T}{P} d P=n R T \ln \frac{P_{4}}{P_{3}}\) (E2.23A)

 

However, we now need to fi nd \(P_{3} \text { and } P_{4}\). From Section 2,7, we know \(P V^{k}=\) const for the polytropic, adiabatic processes (ii) and (iv). We first find k:

 

\(k=\frac{c_{p}}{c_{v}}=\frac{c_{v}+R}{c_{v}}=1.67\)

 

Setting \(P V^{k}\) equal for states 2 and 3 gives:

 

\(P V^{k}=P_{2} V_{2}^{1.67}=\frac{\left(n R T_{2}\right)^{1.67}}{P_{2}^{0.67}}=7347=\frac{\left(n R T_{3}\right)^{1.67}}{P_{3}^{0.67}}\)

 

Solving for \(P_{3}\), we get:

 

\(P_{3}=\left[\frac{\left(n R T_{3}\right)^{1.67}}{7347}\right]^{1.5}=0.0049\) bar

 

Similarly for \(P_{4}:\)

 

\(P V^{k}=P_{1} V_{1}^{1.67}=\frac{\left(n R T_{1}\right)^{1.67}}{P_{1}^{0.67}}=341=\frac{\left(n R T_{4}\right)^{1.67}}{P_{4}^{0.67}}\)

 

and,

 

\(P_{4}=\left[\frac{\left(n R T_{4}\right)^{1.67}}{341}\right]^{1.5}=0.49\) bar

 

Thus, the work given by Equation (E2.23A) is:

 

\(W=n R T \ln \frac{0.49 \text { bar }}{0.0049 \text { bar }}=11,486[ J ]\)

 

The work is positive for this compression process. From the first law,

 

\(Q_{ C }=\Delta U-W=-11,486[ J ]\)

 

(iv) The fourth process is a reversible, adiabatic compression from state 4 at 300 K and 0.52 bar back to state 1 at 1000 K and 10 bar \((\text { process } 4 \rightarrow 1)\). After this process, the gas can repeat steps (i), (ii) . . . Again, for this adiabatic compression:

 

\(Q=0\)

 

At constant heat capacity, the change in internal energy becomes:

 

\(\Delta U=n c_{v}\left(T_{1}-T_{4}\right)=8730[ J ]\)

 

Applying the fi rst law gives:

 

\(W=\Delta U=8730[ J ]\)

A summary of \(\Delta U\), W , and Q for the four processes and the totals for the cycle are presented in Table E2.23A. We get a net work of 26.8 kJ after one cycle.

(b) To sketch this process on a Pv diagram, we fi rst calculate the molar volume at each state using the ideal gas law. The results are presented in Table E2.23B. A sketch (not to scale) of the Pv diagram is presented in Figure E2.23. The work for a reversible process is given by the area under the Pv curve; hence, the net work is given by the shaded area in the box in Figure E2.23. Isotherms, \(T_{ H } \text { and } T_{ C }\), for processes (i) and (iii) are also labeled.

(c) The effi ciency is given by Equation (2.53)

 

\(\eta \equiv \frac{\text { net work }}{\text { heat absorbed from the hot reservoir }}=\frac{W_{\text {net }}}{Q_{ H }}\) (2.53)

 

\(\eta \equiv \frac{\text { net work }}{\text { heat absorbed from the hot reservoir }}=\frac{26,800}{38,287}=0.70\) (E2.23B)

 

In practice, electrical power plants have effi ciencies around 40%.

(d) Applying the relation in the problem statement, we get:

 

\(1-\frac{T_{ C }}{T_{ H }}=1-\frac{300}{1000}=0.7\) (E2.23C)

 

Comparing the values of Equations (E2.23B) and (E2.23C), we get:

 

\(\eta=1-\frac{T_{ C }}{T_{ H }}\) (E2.23D)

 

(e) If Equation (E2.23D) holds, the process can be made more effi cient by raising \(T_{ H }\) or lowering \(T_{ C }\). Note that these options will push the isotherms depicted in Figure E2.23 up and down, respectively. Thus either raising \(T_{ H } \text { or lowering } T_{ C }\) will serve to make the shaded box, which represents net work, larger. We will learn that, indeed, Equation (E2.23D) is true in general. However, we can also reach this conclusion by realizing that the isotherms in Figure E2.23 are fixed on the Pv plane.
