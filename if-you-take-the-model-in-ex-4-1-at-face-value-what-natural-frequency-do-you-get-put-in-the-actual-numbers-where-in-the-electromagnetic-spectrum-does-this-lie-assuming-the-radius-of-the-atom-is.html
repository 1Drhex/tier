Guest Question.

If you take the model in at face value, what natural frequency do you get? Put in the actual numbers. Where, in the electromagnetic spectrum, does this lie, assuming the radius of the atom is 0.5 Å? Find the coefficients of refraction and dispersion, and compare them with the measured values for hydrogen at 0°C and atmospheric pressure: \(A=1.36 \times 10^{-4}, B=7.7 \times 10^{-15} m ^{2}\).

Admin
Solution

\(E=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q d}{a^{3}} \Rightarrow F=-q E=-\left(\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2}}{a^{3}}\right) x=-k_{\text {spring }} x=-m \omega_{0}^{2} x \text { (Eq. 9.151). So } \omega_{0}=\sqrt{\frac{q^{2}}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0} m a^{3}}}\) .

\(F_{\text {binding }}=-k_{\text {spring }} x=-m \omega_{0}^{2} x \) (9.151)

\(\nu_{0}=\frac{\omega_{0}}{2 \pi}=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \sqrt{\frac{\left(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\right)^{2}}{4 \pi\left(8.85 \times 10^{-12}\right)\left(9.11 \times 10^{-31}\right)\left(0.5 \times 10^{-10}\right)^{3}}}=7.16 \times 10^{15} Hz\) . This is ultraviolet.

From Eqs. 9.173 and 9.174,

\(n=1+\left(\frac{N q^{2}}{2 m \epsilon_{0}} \sum_{j} \frac{f_{j}}{\omega_{j}^{2}}\right)+\omega^{2}\left(\frac{N q^{2}}{2 m \epsilon_{0}} \sum_{j} \frac{f_{j}}{\omega_{j}^{4}}\right)\) (9.173)

\(n=1+A\left(1+\frac{B}{\lambda^{2}}\right)\) (9.174)

\(A=\frac{n q^{2}}{2 m \epsilon_{0}} \frac{f}{\omega_{0}^{2}},\left\{\begin{array}{l}N=\# \text { of molecules per unit volume }=\frac{\text { Avogadro's } \#}{22.4 \text { liters }}=\frac{6.02 \times 10^{23}}{22.4 \times 10^{-3}}=2.69 \times 10^{25}, \\f=\# \text { of electrons per molecule }=2\left(\text { for } H _{2}\right).\end{array}\right.\)

\(=\frac{\left(2.69 \times 10^{25}\right)\left(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\right)^{2}}{\left(9.11 \times 10^{-31}\right)\left(8.85 \times 10^{-12}\right)\left(4.5 \times 10^{16}\right)^{2}}=4.2 \times 10^{-5}\) (which is about 1/3 the actual value);

\(B=\left(\frac{2 \pi c}{\omega_{0}}\right)^{2}=\left(\frac{2 \pi \times 3 \times 10^{8}}{4.5 \times 10^{16}}\right)^{2}=1.8 \times 10^{-15} m ^{2}\) (which is about 1/4 the actual value).

So even this extremely crude model is in the right ball park.
