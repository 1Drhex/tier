Guest Question.

Determine the view factor \(F_{d 1-2}\) between a differential area \(\left(d A_{1}\right)\)and a finite disk of mdius \(r_{o}\) at a height H.

Admin
Solution

\(F_{d A_{1}-A_{2}} \text { or } F_{d 1-2}=\int_{A_{2}} \frac{\cos \theta_{i} \cos \theta_{j} d A_{2}}{\pi R^{2}}\) (4.72)

We have to convert the problem into one in which the variable is r, which can take a value between 0 and \(r_{o}\). Therefore, first, we have to take an infinitesimally small area on the disc, take a thickness of dr so that the area will be \(2 \pi r d r\). The area \(d A_{2}\) , can be replaced by \(2 \pi r d r\). Then if it is at a radius r, from fundamental trigonometric principles, we can find out what R is, {Please note that R â‰ H). Then the two terms \(\cos \theta_{i}\) and \(\cos \theta_{j}\) also have to be represented in terms of known quantities. After all this, the integration can be done easily.

\(\theta_{i}=\theta_{j}=\theta\) (4.73)

\(R^{2}=r^{2}+H^{2}\) (4.74)

\(\cos \theta=H / R\) (4.75)

\(d A_{2}=2 \pi r d r\) (4.76)

Substituting for \(\cos \theta, R \text { and } d A_{2}\) in the integral,

\(F_{d1-2}=\int_{0}^{r0}\frac{H^{2}\pi rdr}{R^{2}\pi R^{2}}\) (4.77)

\(F_{d 1-2}=2 H^{2} \int_{0}^{r_{0}} \frac{r d r}{R^{4}}\) (4.78)

\(F_{d 1-2}=2 H^{2} \int_{0}^{r_{0}}\frac{r d r}{\left(r^{2}+H^{2}\right)^{2}}\) (4.79)

\(L e t, r^{2}+H^{2}=y, 2 r d r=d y\)(4.80)

\(\text { Limits, } r=0, y=H^{2} ; r=r_{0}, y=H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}, d r=\frac{d y}{2 r}\) (4.81)

\(F_{a 1-2}=2 H^{2} \int_{H^{2}}^{H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}}\frac{r\frac{dy}{2r}}{y^2}\) (4.82)

\(F_{d 1-2}=H^{2} \int_{H^{2}}^{H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}} \frac{d y}{y^{2}}\) (4.83)

\(F_{d 1-2}=H^{2}\left(\frac{-1}{y}\right)_{H^{2}}^{H^{2}+\tau_{0}^{2}}\) (4.84)

\(F_{d 1-2}=H^{2}\left(\frac{-1}{H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}}+\frac{1}{H^{2}}\right)\) (4.85)

\(F_{d 1-2}=H^{2}\left(\frac{H^{2}-H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}}{\left(H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}\right) H^{2}}\right)\) (4.86)

\(F_{d 1-2}=\left(\frac{r_{0}^{2}}{H^{2}+r_{0}^{2}}\right)\) (4.87)

What is the physical interpretation of the solution to this problem? When H is infinite, F = O. If \(r_{o} / H<<1\), we can reduc.e the expression 2 to \(F_{d 1-2}=\frac{r_{o}^{2}}{H^{2}}\), and it confirms that the view factor varies as the inverse of the square of the distance between the two.

Please remember that since one area was infinitesimal, the integration was not only possible but was also straightforward. If both are finite areas and one is not a disc, the resulting mathematics would be very tedious. Many groups of people in the world in the 1960s – 1980s worked on developing view factor relations and solving these integrais, which was considered a very important activity. The view factors are repeatedly used in radiosity calculations and multi-mode heat transfer problems. View factors are also onc of the reasons why double precision was required.We need view factors up to the seventh or eighth decimal accuracy, as, if we have a hundred thousand view factors, we cannot round off each to the second or third decimal. When we are doing a convective solver or convection and radiation together, such an approximation will lead to a lot of errors.

But now, with computational resources becoming more powerful and more programs being available, this activity of research is not so prominent these days. These are mostly considered well settled problems.

So far, we have restricted our working to two dimensional surfaces. But most of the surfaces in reality are three dimensional surfaces and the view factors cannot be got by just algebra. People have developed elaborate techniques for this, of which one of the most important is contour integration. If we look at \(d A _{1} \text { and } d A _{2}\) we see that they can be written as \(d A_{1}=d x_{1} d y_{1} \text { and } d A_{2}=d x_{2} d y_{2}\).

Therefore, 4 integrations are involved. Elemental strips on \(A_{1} \text { and } A_{2}\) are considered, \(\cos \theta_{i} \text { and } \cos \theta_{j}\) are determined and four integrations are performed. These are available in the form of charts and Tables. These are given in Figs.4.17-4.20 for three commonly encountered geometries. We will now solve a few problems using these charts.
