Guest Question.

The edge mill shown schematically in Fig. 16.17 is a gyroscopic grinder used for crushing ore, seeds, grain, and such. It consists of a large steel pan in which one or more heavy conical rollers, called mullers, roll without slipping on the bottom of the pan, and, at the same time, revolve about a vertical shaft passing through the central axis of the pan. The mullers rotate about either horizontal or inclined axles that are attached to the vertical shaft, which is rotated under power. The radius of a muller is r = 18 in, and the length of its axle is l = 30 in; its weight is W = 2 200 lb, and its mass moment of inertia can be approximated by that of a cylindrical disk. Assume that the vertical shaft is driven at a constant angular velocity of ωp = 40 rev/min and that the muller rolls without slipping. Determine the optimum inclination angle θ for the muller axle, which maximizes the crushing force between the muller and the pan.

Admin
Solution

First, we use the assumption of rolling without slip to find the angular velocity of the muller. From Fig. 16.17, the angular velocity of the muller axis, link 3, is

\(\omega_{p}=\omega_{3}=40 \hat{ j } rev / min =4.19 \hat{ j } rad / s\) (1)

whereas the angular velocity of spin of the muller about its axle is

\(\omega _{s}= \omega _{4 / 3}=\omega_{s}(-\sin \theta \hat{ i }+\cos \theta \hat{ j })\) (2)

Since the muller undergoes precession in addition to spin about its axle, the absolute angular velocity of the muller is

\(\omega _{4}=\omega_{3}+ \omega _{4 / 3}=\left(-\omega_{s} \sin \theta\right) \hat{ i }+\left(4.19 rad / s +\omega_{s} \cos \theta\right) \hat{ j }\) (3)

Since point A is fixed, the velocity of point B of link 3 is

\(\begin{aligned}V _{B} &= \omega _{3} \times R _{B A} \\&=(4.19 \hat{ j } rad / s ) \times(30 \sin \theta \hat{ i }-30 \cos \theta \hat{ j } \text { in }) \\&=-125.7 \sin \theta \hat{ k } in / s\end{aligned}\) (4)

Also, since there is no slip at point C, the velocity of point B of link 4 is

\(\begin{aligned}V _{B} &= \omega _{4} \times R _{B C} \\&=\left[\left(-\omega_{s} \sin \theta\right) \hat{ i }+\left(4.19 rad / s +\omega_{s} \cos \theta\right) \hat{ j }\right] \times(18 \cos \theta \hat{ i }+18 \sin \theta \hat{ j } \text { in }) \\&=-18\left(\omega_{s}+4.19 \cos \theta rad / s \right) \hat{ k }\end{aligned}\) (5)

Equating Eqs. (4) and (5), the angular velocity of spin of the muller about its axle is

\(\omega_{s}=6.98 \sin \theta-4.19 \cos \theta rad / s\) (6)

From Eqs. (1) and (2), the cross-product is

\(\begin{aligned}\left( \omega _{p} \times \omega _{s}\right) &=(4.19 \hat{ j } rad / s ) \times \omega_{s}(-\sin \theta \hat{ i }+\cos \theta \hat{ j }) \\&=4.19 \omega_{s} \sin \theta \hat{ k } rad / s\end{aligned}\)

Then, substituting Eq. (6) into this result gives

\(\left( \omega _{p} \times \omega _{s}\right)=4.19(6.98 \sin \theta-4.19 \cos \theta) \sin \theta \hat{ k } rad / s ^{2}\) (7)

Using the formula for a round disk from Table 5, Appendix A, the mass moment of inertia of the muller is

 

\(\begin{gathered}I^{s}=\frac{m r^{2}}{2}=\frac{(2200 lb )(18 in )^{2}}{\left(386 in / s ^{2}\right) 2}=923.3 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}, \\I=\frac{m r^{2}}{4}+m l^{2}=5590 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\end{gathered}\) (8)

Then substituting Eqs. (7) and (8) into Eq. (16.35), the net externally applied moment on the muller is

\(\sum M =\left[I^{s}+\left(I^{s}-I\right) \frac{\omega_{p}}{\omega_{s}} \cos \theta\right]\left( \omega _{p} \times \omega _{s}\right)\) (16.35)

 

\(\sum M =\left[923.3+\frac{-4667(4.19) \cos \theta}{6.98 \sin \theta-4.19 \cos \theta}\right](6.98 \sin \theta-4.19 \cos \theta) 4.19 \sin \theta \hat{ k } \text { in } \cdot 1 b\)

Rearranging this equation, the moment applied to the muller can be written as

\(\sum M =\left(27001 \sin ^{2} \theta-98082 \sin \theta \cos \theta\right) \hat{ k } \text { in } \cdot 1 b\) (9)

This moment must be externally applied to the muller in order to sustain the forced precession. It must come from the net effect of the weight of the muller and the crushing force between the muller and the pan. Formulating these effects for the other side of the equation we obtain

 

\(\begin{aligned}\sum M =& R _{B A} \times W _{4}+ R _{C A} \times F _{c} \\=&(30 \sin \theta \hat{ i }-30 \cos \theta \hat{ j } \text { in }) \times(-2200 \hat{ j }{1 b}) \\&+[(30 \sin \theta-18 \cos \theta) \hat{ i }+(18 \sin \theta-30 \cos \theta) \hat{ j } \text { in }] \times\left(F_{c} \hat{ j }\right) \\=&\left(-66000 \sin \theta+30 F_{c} \sin \theta-18 F_{c} \cos \theta\right) \hat{ k } \text { in } \cdot 1 b .\end{aligned}\) (10)

 

Equating Eqs. (9) and (10), the crushing force becomes

\(F_{c}=\frac{27001 \sin ^{2} \theta-98082 \sin \theta \cos \theta+66000 \sin \theta \text { in } \cdot 1 b}{30 \sin \theta-18 \cos \theta \text { in }}\) (11)

We note that the crushing force is a function of the angle θ of inclination of the muller axis; we now hope to choose this angle θ to maximize the crushing force. To do this we differentiate Eq. (11) with respect to θ and set the result equal to zero; that is

\(\begin{aligned}&\left(54002 \sin \theta \cos \theta-98082 \cos ^{2} \theta+98082 \sin ^{2} \theta+66000 \cos \theta\right)(30 \sin \theta-18 \cos \theta) \\&-(30 \cos \theta+18 \sin \theta)\left(27001 \sin ^{2} \theta-98082 \sin \theta \cos \theta+66000 \sin \theta\right)=0\end{aligned}\)

This equation simplifies to

\(\begin{aligned}&2456442 \sin ^{3} \theta+810030 \sin ^{2} \theta \cos \theta \\&\quad-972036 \sin \theta \cos ^{2} \theta+1765476 \cos ^{3} \theta-1188000=0\end{aligned}\)

and can be solved numerically (the equation has multiple roots). When this is done, the root that maximizes the crushing force Fc of Eq. (11) is found to be

θ = 115.9°.

This is the optimum angle of inclination of the muller axle. Substituting this value into Eq. (11), the crushing force of the mill is

Fc = 3 437 lb.

We should note that this crushing force is more than 50% larger than the weight of the muller; the additional force is attributable to choosing the inclination angle θ for which both the gyroscopic and the centrifugal force effects contribute as much as possible to the crushing effect of the mill. There are other designs for crushing machines in which the pan rotates under a muller that has a fixed axis. In such a design, there is no gyroscopic action, and the crushing action is due solely to the weight of the muller.
