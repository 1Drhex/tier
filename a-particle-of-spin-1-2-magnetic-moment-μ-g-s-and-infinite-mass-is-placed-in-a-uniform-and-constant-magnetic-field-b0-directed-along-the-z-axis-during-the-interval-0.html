Guest Question.

A particle of spin \(\frac{1}{2}\), magnetic moment \(\mu=g \mathbf{S}\) and infinite mass is placed in a uniform and constant magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}_{\mathbf{0}}\) directed along the \(\mathbf{z}\) axis. During the interval 0<t<T, a uniform and constant magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}_{1} \)along the x axis is also applied, so that the system will still be in a uniform and constant magnetic field \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{B}_{0}+\mathbf{B}_{1}\). For t<0, the system is in the \(S_{z}\)eigenstate corresponding to the eigenvalue \(+\frac{\hbar}{2}.\)

(a) \(At t=0^{+}\), what will be the probability amplitudes of finding the spin component along the \(\mathbf{B}\) direction equal to \(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\) ?

(b) How do the energy eigenstates evolve over time in the interval 0<t<T ?

(c) What is the probability of observing the system at the time t=T in the \(S_{z}\) eigenstate corresponding to the eigenvalue –\(\frac{\hbar}{2}\) ?

Admin
Solution

For t<0, the system Hamiltonian is

\(\mathcal{H}=-g \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B}_{0}=-\frac{g B_{0} \hbar}{2} \sigma_{z}=-\hbar \omega_{0} \sigma_{z}, \quad \text { where } \quad \omega_{0}=\frac{g B_{0}}{2}\)

In the interval between 0 and T, the Hamiltonian becomes

\(\mathcal{H}=-g \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B}=-\frac{g \hbar}{2}\left(B_{0} \sigma_{z}+B_{1} \sigma_{x}\right)=-\frac{g \hbar}{2}\left(\begin{array}{cc}B_{0} & B_{1} \\B_{1}-B_{0}\end{array}\right)=-\hbar \omega\left(\begin{array}{cc}\cos \vartheta & \sin \vartheta \\\sin \vartheta & -\cos \vartheta\end{array}\right)\) \(\text { where } \omega=\frac{g B}{2}, \quad B=\sqrt{B_{0}^{2}+B_{1}^{2}} \text { and } \vartheta=\arctan \frac{B_{1}}{B_{0}} \text { . }\)

This Hamiltonian is proportional to the spin component in the \(\hat{n} \)direction, set by the \(\vartheta\) angle, which is given by

\(\mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n}=-\frac{\hbar}{2}\left(\begin{array}{cc}\cos \vartheta & \sin \vartheta \\\sin \vartheta & -\cos \vartheta\end{array}\right)\)

\(\mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n}\) has eigenvalues \(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\) and eigenstates (see Problem \(\left.4.2\right)\)

 

\(\left|\chi_{+}\right\rangle=\left|\mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n}=+\frac{\hbar}{2}\right\rangle=\left(\begin{array}{c}\cos \frac{\vartheta}{2} \\\sin \frac{\pi}{2}\end{array}\right), \quad\left|\chi_{-}\right\rangle=\left|\mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n}=-\frac{\hbar}{2}\right\rangle=\left(\begin{array}{c}-\sin \frac{\vartheta}{2} \\\cos \frac{\vartheta}{2}\end{array}\right)\)

(a) The state of the system at the time t=0 can be expressed in terms of the eigenstates of \(\mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n} :\)

\(|\psi(0)\rangle=\left(\begin{array}{l}1 \\0\end{array}\right)=c_{+}\left|\chi_{+}\right\rangle+c_{-}\left|\chi_{-}\right\rangle\)

 

\(c_{+} e c_{-}\) are the amplitudes that we are looking for:

 

\(c_{+}=\left\langle\chi_{+} \mid \psi(0)\right\rangle=\left(\begin{array}{cc}\cos \frac{\vartheta}{2} & \sin \frac{\vartheta}{2}\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}1 \\0\end{array}\right)=\cos \frac{\vartheta}{2}\)

 

\(c_{-}=\left\langle\chi_{-} \mid \psi(0)\right\rangle=\left(\begin{array}{ll}-\sin \frac{\vartheta}{2} & \cos \frac{\vartheta}{2}\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{l}1 \\0\end{array}\right)=-\sin \frac{\vartheta}{2}\)

(b) As we said,

\(\mathcal{H}=-g \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B}=-g B \mathbf{S} \cdot \hat{n}\)

therefore, its eigenkets are \(\left|\chi_{+}\right\rangle\) and \(\left|\chi_{-}\right\rangle\) and its eigenvalues are

\(E_{\pm}=-g B\left(\pm \frac{\hbar}{2}\right)=\mp \hbar \omega .\)

In the time interval between t=0 and t=T, the eigenstates’ evolution is given by

\(\left|\chi_{\pm}(t)\right\rangle=e^{-i \frac{E \pm t}{\hbar}}\left|\chi_{\pm}(0)\right\rangle=e^{\pm i \omega t}\left|\chi_{\pm}(0)\right\rangle\)
