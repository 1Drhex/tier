Guest Question.

Before concluding this chapter it is important to consider a numerical problem for illustrating the theory presented. For this purpose we consider again a fin problem as shown in Figure 3.29. The linear variation for the temperature within each finite element is assumed. We shall derive the element equations from the most general formulation given in Section (3.4) and determine the temperature distribution, heat dissipation capacity and the efficiency of the fin, assuming that the tip is insulated.

Admin
Solution

Since we are using linear elements, the element will only have two nodes. First we divide the given length of the fin into number of divisions – say n elements. Therefore we will have (n+1) nodes to represent the fin see Table (3.6) The variation of temperature in the elements is linear. Hence,

\(T=N_{i} T_{i}+N_{j} T_{j}\) (3.276)

\(\text { and the first derivative is given by }\)

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{d T}{d x} &=\frac{d N_{i}}{d x} T_{i}+\frac{d N_{j}}{d x} T_{j} \\&=-\frac{1}{l} T_{i}+\frac{1}{l} T_{j}\end{aligned}\) (3.277)

\(\text { that is, the gradient matrix is }\)

\(g=\frac{d T}{d x}=\left[\begin{array}{ll}-\frac{1}{l} & \frac{1}{l}\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}T_{i} \\T_{j}\end{array}\right\}=[ B ]\{ T \}\) (3.278)

\(\text { where, l is the element length and }\)

\([ B ]=\frac{1}{l}\left[\begin{array}{ll}-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\) (3.279)

\(\begin{aligned}&\text { With the above relationships we can write the relevant element matrices as follows(Equa- }\\&\operatorname{tion}(3.264)) \end{aligned}\)

:

\(\begin{array}{l}{[ K ]=\int_{\Omega}[ B ]^{T}[ D ][ B ] d \Omega+\int_{S_{3}} h[ N ]^{T}[ N ] d s} \\\{ f \}=\int_{\Omega} G[ N ]^{T} d \Omega-\int_{S_{2}} q[ N ]^{T} d s+\int_{S_{3}} h T_{a}[ N]^{T} d s\end{array}\)

 

\([ K ]_{e}=\int_{l}[ B ]^{T}[ D ][ B ] d \Omega+\int_{S} h[ N ]^{T}[ N ] d S\)

\(=\int_{l} \frac{1}{l}\left[\begin{array}{r}-1 \\1\end{array}\right]\left[k_{x}\right] \frac{1}{l}\left[\begin{array}{ll}-1 & 1\end{array}\right] A d x+\int_{S} h\left[\begin{array}{l}N_{i} \\N_{j}\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{ll}N_{i} & N_{j}\end{array}\right] P d x .\) (3.280)

where A is the cross-sectional area of the fin and P is the perimeter of the fin from which convection takes place. Note that \( [D]=k_{x}\) for one-dimensional problems, \( d \Omega=A d x\) and d S=P d x

Rearranging Equation (3.280), we have

\([ K ]_{e}=\int_{l} \frac{A k_{x}}{l^{2}}\left[\begin{array}{rr}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right] d x+\int_{l} h P\left[\begin{array}{cc}N_{i}^{2} & N_{i} N_{j} \\N_{i} N_{j} & N_{j}^{2}\end{array}\right] d x .\) (3.281)

Here \( N_{i}=L_{i}\) and \( N_{j}=L_{j}\), which is generally true for all linear elements. Hence, we can make use of the formula (Appendix C):

\(\int_{l} L_{i}^{a} L_{j}^{b} d l=\frac{a ! b ! l}{(a+b+1) !}\) (3.282)

For example,

\(\int_{l} N_{i}^{2} d l=\int_{l} L_{i}^{2} d l=\frac{2 ! 0 ! l}{(2+0+1) !}=\frac{l}{3}\) (3.283)

and other terms can be similarly integrated. If \( A, k_{x}, P\) and h are all assumed to be constant throughout the element, we obtain the following [K] matrix:

\([ K ]_{e}=\frac{A k_{x}}{l}\left[\begin{array}{rr}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]+\frac{h P l}{6}\left[\begin{array}{ll}2 & 1 \\1 & 2\end{array}\right]\) (3.284)

\(\text { Let us next consider the thermal loading. From Equation }(3.264) \text { we can write: }\)

\(\{ f \}_{e}=\frac{G A l}{2}\left\{\begin{array}{l}1 \\1\end{array}\right\}-\frac{q P l}{2}\left\{\begin{array}{l}1 \\1\end{array}\right\}+\frac{h T_{a} P l}{2}\left\{\begin{array}{l}1 \\1\end{array}\right\}\) (3.285)

Note that the heat flux (q) boundary is assumed along the surface. This is not necessarily always the case. In the problem considered, no heat flux boundary condition exists. Thus, the heat fux term (the second term) in Equation (3.285) is zero. Similarly no heat generation is assumed and thus the first term in Equation (3.285) is also zero.

The solution of the given problem may be found by substitution of the numerical values.

(a) First let us consider a one-element solution for the case where l=2 cm, as shown in Figure 3.30. The element stiffness matrix is

\([ K ]_{e}=\frac{A k_{x}}{l}\left[\begin{array}{rr}1 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]+\frac{h P l}{6}\left[\begin{array}{ll}2 & 1 \\1 & 2\end{array}\right]\)

\(\begin{array}{l}=\left[\begin{array}{rr}0.06 & -0.06 \\-0.06 & 0.06\end{array}\right]+\left[\begin{array}{ll}0.008 & 0.004 \\0.004 & 0.008\end{array}\right] \\=\left[\begin{array}{rr}0.068 & -0.056 \\-0.056 & 0.068\end{array}\right]\end{array}\) (3.286)

\(\text { and the loading term is given by }\)

\(\begin{aligned}\{ f \} &=\frac{h P l T_{a}}{2}\left\{\begin{array}{l}1 \\1\end{array}\right\} \\&=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0.30 \\0.30\end{array}\right\}\end{aligned}\) (3.287)

Since only one element is employed, no assemblage of element contribution is necessary. Thus, the simultaneous equation system may be written as

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}0.068 & -0.056 \\-0.056 & 0.068\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}T_{1} \\T_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}0.30+Q \\0.30\end{array}\right\}\) (3.288)

where Q is the total heat flow at node 1 . We now incorporate the known base temperature of \( 100^{\circ} C\) at node 1 . It is done in such a way that the symmetry of the [K] matrix is retained. This essential if the symmetric matrix solution procedure is employed in the solution of simultaneous equations. The following steps give a typical implementation procedure for the temperature boundary condition.

(i) The diagonal element of the first row is assigned a value of I and the remaining elements on that row are zero.

\(\begin{aligned}&\text { (ii) Replace the first row value of the loading vector } f \text { by the known value of } T_{1} \text { , that is, }\\&100\end{aligned}\)

(iii) In order to retain the symmetry, the first term of the second row in the [K] matrix is transferred to the right-hand side and replace with a zero value as given below:

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}1.0 & 0.0 \\0.0 & 0.068\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}T_{1} \\T_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}100.0 \\0.30+0.056(100.0)\end{array}\right\}\) (3.289)

\(\text { The simultaneous equation to be solved is }\)

\(0.068 T_{2}=0.3+0.056(100)\) (3.290)

Therefore the solution is \( T_{1}=100^{\circ} C\) and\( T_{2}=86.765^{\circ} C\). Heat dissipated is calculated using the nodal equation for the the node number (I) as given in Equation (3.288), that is,

\(Q=0.068 T_{1}-0.056 T_{2}-0.3=1.64 W\) (3.291)

The heat dissipated may also be determined by using the following convection condition, that is,

\(Q=\sum_{e=1}^{M} h P l\left(\frac{T_{i}+T_{j}}{2}-T_{a}\right)=1.64 W\) (3.292)

where M is the total number of elements (subscripts i and j indicate the two nodes of an element). The maximum theoretically possible heat transfer is (when \( \left.T_{i}=T_{j}\right) \)

\(Q_{\max }=\sum_{e=1}^{M} h P l\left(T_{i}-T_{a}\right)=1.8 W\) (3.293)

\(\text { The efficiency is defined as }\)

\(\eta_{f}=\frac{Q}{Q_{\max }}=\frac{1.64}{1.80}=91.11 \%\) (3.294)

\(\text { The exact solution for this problem is }\)

\(Q_{\text {exact }}=\operatorname{kAm} \tan (k m l)=1.593 W\) (3.295)

\(\text { where } m=\sqrt{h P / k A}=31.62 . \text { Therefore, the exact fin efficiency is }\)

\(\left(\eta_{f}\right)_{\text {exact }}=\frac{Q_{\text {exact }}}{Q_{\max }}=88.48 \%\) (3.296)

(b) Let us consider a two-element solution of the same problem (3 nodes). The length of the fin is divided equally into two elements, that is, l=1.0 cm . The stiffness matrix calculation is similar to the one for the single element case, that is,

\(\left[ K _{1}\right]=\left[ K _{2}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{cc}0.124 & -0.118 \\-0.118 & 0.124\end{array}\right]\) (3.297)

\(\text { and the loading vectors are }\)

\(\left\{ f _{1}\right\}=\left\{ f _{2}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0.15 \\0.15\end{array}\right\}\) (3.298)

\(\text { On assembly we obtain }\)

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0.124 & -0.118 & 0.0 \\-0.118 & 0.124+0.124 & -0.118 \\0.0 & -0.118 & 0.124\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}T_{1} \\T_{2} \\T_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}0.15 \\0.15+0.15 \\0.15\end{array}\right\}\) (3.299)

\(\text { Now we have to incorporate the known values of base temperature, that is, } T_{1}=100^{\circ} C \text { . }\)

\(\left[\begin{array}{ccc}1.0 & 0.0 & 0.0 \\0.0 & 0.248 & -0.118 \\0.0 & -0.118 & 0.124\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}T_{1} \\T_{2} \\T_{3}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{c}100.0 \\0.30+0.118(100) \\0.15\end{array}\right\}\) (3.300)

Therefore, the two equations to be solved are \( 0.248 T_{2}-0.118 T_{3}=12.1\)

and \( -0.118 T_{2}+0.124 T_{3}=0.15\)

Solving these equations we get \( T_{2}=90.209^{\circ} C , T_{3}=87.057^{\circ} C\)

Results, which have been generated using different number of elements, are tabulated in Tables 3.7 and 3.8.

As can be seen, the two-element solution is very good and is further improved with the use of four elements. As a first idealization even the one element solution is reasonably good considering the small effort involved.
