Guest Question.

Use Kane’s equations to derive the differential equations of motion of the wheelbarrow in .

Admin
Solution

The objective here is to compare the operations required to implement Kane’s equations and the Gibbs-Appell equations. We proceed as though we had not previously solved this problem using the Gibbs-Appell equations, except that we call on the previous results of the kinematical analysis. We begin with a free-body diagram of the system. In this diagram the \(m \bar{a}\) vector for each center of mass and the centroidal \(d \bar{H} / d t\) for each body are shown as dashed lines in order to identify them as the inertial effects. (In situations in which there are many bodies and/or many forces, it might be advantageous to draw separate diagrams for the actual forces and their inertial equivalents.) Note that, because the wheelbarrow is in planar motion, \(d \bar{H}_{G} / d t\) for it reduces to \(I \ddot{\theta}\) about the vertical axis.

We use the same generalized coordinates and quasi-velocities as in the previous analysis, so \(q_{1}=X_{C}, q_{2}=Y_{C}, q_{3}=\theta, \dot{\gamma}_{1}=v, \dot{\gamma}_{2}=\dot{\theta} .\) It is helpful to use Eqs. (9.3.47) to describe the partial velocities explicitly when we write Kane’s equation. Thus Kane’s equations for this system are

\(\left(\bar{v}_{G k}\right)_{j} \equiv \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}} \bar{v}_{G k}, \quad\left(\bar{\omega}_{k}\right)_{j} \equiv \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}} \bar{\omega}_{k}.\) (9.3.47)

 

\(\begin{array}{l} \left(m_{1} \bar{a}_{G}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{G}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}}+\left(I \ddot{\gamma}_{2}\right) \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}}+\left(m_{2} \bar{a}_{C}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{C}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}}+\left(\frac{d \bar{H}_{C}}{d t}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_{2}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}} \\\\ =\left(\bar{F}_{A}+\bar{F}_{C}+N \bar{j}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \bar{v}_{C}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}}+\left(\bar{r}_{A / C} \times \bar{F}_{A}+\bar{r}_{A / C} \times \bar{F}_{C}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \dot{\theta}}{\partial \dot{\gamma}_{j}} \bar{k}, \quad j=1,2,\end{array} (1)\)

where we have used the previous approach to replace the forces acting on the handles with an equivalent force-couple system acting at point \(C\). Each term must be described solely in terms of the \(q_{i}\) and \(\dot{\gamma}_{i}\) variables, which would require a kinematical analysis if we had not already done so in . From that development we have

\(\begin{array}{c} \bar{a}_{C}=\ddot{\gamma}_{1} \bar{i}+\dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{j}, \quad \bar{a}_{G}=\left(\ddot{\gamma}_{1}+\dot{\gamma}_{2}^{2} L\right) \bar{i}+\left(\dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2}-\ddot{\gamma}_{2} L\right) \bar{j} \\\\ \bar{\omega}_{2}=\frac{\dot{\gamma}_{1}}{R} \bar{j}+\dot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{k}, \quad \bar{\alpha}_{2}=-\frac{\dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2}}{R} \bar{i}+\frac{\ddot{\gamma}_{1}}{R} \bar{j}+\ddot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{k}.\end{array} (2) \)

We had not explicitly described the velocity of point \(G\), but it is readily obtained. Thus the quantities required for evaluating the partial velocities are

\(\bar{v}_{C}=v \bar{i}=\dot{\gamma}_{1} \bar{i}, \quad \bar{v}_{G}=\bar{v}_{C}+\dot{\theta} \bar{k} \times(-L \bar{i})=\dot{\gamma}_{1} \bar{i}-L \dot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{j}. (3)\)

The angular momentum terms corresponding to Eqs. (2) for \(\bar{\omega}_{2}\) and \(\bar{\alpha}_{2}\) are

\(\begin{array}{c}\bar{H}_{C}=J\left(\frac{\dot{\gamma}_{1}}{R}\right) \bar{j}+\frac{J}{2}\left(\dot{\gamma}_{2}\right) \bar{k}, \quad \frac{\partial \bar{H}_{C}}{\partial t}=\frac{J}{2}\left(-\frac{\dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2}}{R}\right) \bar{i}+J\left(\frac{\ddot{\gamma}_{1}}{R}\right) \bar{j}+\frac{J}{2} \ddot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{k}, \\\\ \frac{d \bar{H}_{C}}{d t}=\frac{\partial \bar{H}_{C}}{\partial t}+\bar{\omega}_{2} \times \bar{H}_{C}=-\frac{J}{R} \dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{i}+\frac{J}{R} \ddot{\gamma}_{1} \bar{j}+\frac{J}{2} \ddot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{k}. \end{array} (4)\)

Because \(\dot{\theta}=\dot{\gamma}_{1}\), substitution of Eqs. (2) and (3) into Eq. (1) for each \(j\) yields

\(\begin{array}{c} j=1: m_{1} \bar{a}_{G} \cdot \bar{i}+I \ddot{\theta}(0)+m_{2} \bar{a}_{C} \cdot \bar{i}+\frac{d \bar{H}_{C}}{d t} \cdot\left(\frac{1}{R} \bar{j}\right)=\bar{R} \cdot \bar{i}+\bar{M} \cdot \overline{0}, \\\\ j=2: m_{1} \bar{a}_{G} \cdot\left(-L \dot{\gamma}_{2} \bar{j}\right)+I \ddot{\theta}(1)+m_{2} \bar{a}_{C} \cdot(\overline{0})+\frac{d \bar{H}_{C}}{d t} \cdot(1 \bar{k})=\bar{R} \cdot \overline{0}+\bar{M} \cdot(1 \bar{k}).\end{array}\)

where \(\bar{R}\) and \(\bar{M}\) are the resultant force and moment about point \(C\) of the forces \(\bar{F}_{A}\) and \(\bar{F}_{B}\). The result of substituting Eqs. (2) and (4) into the preceding equations is

\(\begin{array}{c} m_{1}\left(\ddot{\gamma}_{1}+\dot{\gamma}_{2}^{2} L\right)+m_{2} \ddot{\gamma}_{1}+\frac{J}{R^{2}} \ddot{\gamma}_{1}=\bar{R} \cdot \bar{i}, \\\\ m_{1}\left(\dot{\gamma}_{1} \dot{\gamma}_{2}-\ddot{\gamma}_{2} L\right)\left(-L \dot{\gamma}_{2}\right)+I \ddot{\gamma}_{2}+\frac{J}{2} \ddot{\gamma}_{2}=\bar{M} \cdot \bar{k}. \end{array}\)

These equations of motion are the same as the result of .The kinematical quantities that must be described in each approach are essentially the same. The primary difference is that \(S\) is generated in a more automatic fashion, but the manipulations are more tedious. The operations in Kane’s equations are broken into smaller steps, and one can gain some physical insights by examining the forcing effects in the directions associated with the partial velocities.
