Guest Question.

In the sketch, \(xyz\) are centroidal axes of the 60-kg homogeneous orthogonal tetrahedron. Determine the principal moments of inertia and the rotation transformation for the principal axes whose origin also is the centroid.

Admin
Solution

This example addresses how mathematical software can be used to evaluate the principal inertia properties.† For the dimensions of the given body with m = 60 kg, we find from the Appendix that

\( [I]=\left[\begin{array}{lll}1.170 & 0.060 & 0.090 \\\\0.060 & 0.900 & 0.180 \\\\0.090 & 0.180 & 0.450\end{array}\right] \mathrm{kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2} .\)

Most mathematical software packages have functions or subroutines that can solve the standard eigenvalue problem \([[I]-\lambda[U]]\{e\}=\{0\}\). In Matlab, one obtains the desired result by writing [e_vecs, I_princ] = eigs(I); where I is the preceding \(3 \times 3\) array, I_princ is a \(3 \times 3\) diagonal array whose elements are the principal moments of inertia, and e_vecs is a \(3 \times 3\) array whose \(j\) th column is the eigenvector corresponding to I-princ \((j, j)\). The Matlab result is

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{-} \operatorname{princ}(1,1)=1.2059 \quad I_{-} \operatorname{princ}(2,2)=0.9327 \quad I_{-} \operatorname{princ}(3,3)=0.3814 \\\\e_{-} v e c s=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}-0.9395 & -0.3322 & -0.0836 \\\\-0.2908 & 0.9023 & -0.3181 \\\\-0.1811 & 0.2746 & 0.9444\end{array}\right] .\end{array} \)

The algorithm employed by Matlab returns e_vecs as an orthonormal transformation, that is, \(\left[e_{-} v e c s\right]\left[e_{-} v e c s\right]^{\mathrm{T}}=[U]\), but the principal values are not sequenced from smallest to largest, as we prefer. We therefore swap the first and third principal moments of inertia, and correspondingly swap the first and third columns of \(\left[e_{-v e c s}\right]\). However, swapping a pair of columns of a \(3 \times 3\) matrix reverses the sign of its determinant, which brings to the fore the question of whether the transformation initially computed by Matlab was right-handed. The raw output previously listed has a determinant of −1, so the result of swapping columns one and three yields the correct result. After these steps, the transformation matrix \([R]\) is readily obtained by recalling that the individual eigenvectors constituting the columns of \(\left[e_{-v e c s}\right]\) are the rows of \([R]\), so that \([R]=\left[e_{-} v e c s\right]^{\mathrm{T}} .\) After these adjustments, the results obtained from Matlab are

\(\begin{array}{c}I_{1}=0.3814, I_{2}=0.9327, I_{3}=1.2059 \mathrm{~kg}\text{-}\mathrm{m}^{2}, \\\\{[R]=\left[\begin{array}{rrr}-0.0836 & -0.3181 & 0.9444 \\\\-0.3322 & 0.9023 & 0.2746 \\\\-0.9395 & -0.2908 & -0.1811\end{array}\right] .}\end{array} \)

An excellent check for any solution, whether it is obtained by solving the characteristic equation or from computer software, is to verify that \([R][I][R]^{\mathrm{T}}\) yields a diagonal array whose nonzero elements are the principal moments of inertia in the correct sequence.

Mathcad uses two functions to solve the eigenvalue problem corresponding to a specified \(3 \times 3\) array \(I .\) The principal moments of inertia are computed by \(I_{\text {princ }}:=\) eigenvals (I); and the eigenvectors are obtained from e \(_{\text {princ }}:=\) eigenvecs \((\mathrm{I})\); where \(\mathrm{I}_{\text {princ }}\) is a three-element column vector of principal moments of inertia, and eprinc is a \(3 \times 3\) array whose columns are the eigenvectors sequenced to match \(\mathrm{I}_{\text {princ }}\). The result is

\(\left\{I_{\text {princ }}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}1.2059 \\\\0.9327 \\\\0.3814\end{array}\right\},\left[e_{\text {princ }}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}0.9395 & 0.3322 & -0.0836 \\\\0.2908 & -0.9023 & -0.3181 \\\\0.1811 & -0.2746 & 0.9444\end{array}\right] \text { . } \)

The eigenvectors returned by Mathcad’s algorithm, like Matlab’s, have the orthonormal property. Mathcad, like Matlab, does not address the handedness of the transformation, and it does not necessarily sequence the principal moments of inertia from smallest to largest. The preceding values are like the raw output from Matlab, except that the first two columns of \(\left[e_{\text {princ }}\right]\) are multiplied by \(-1\) relative to their Matlab correspondences, so \(\left|\left[e_{\text {princ }}\right]\right|=-1\). Because the principal moments of inertia are reversed from the desired order, as they were in Matlab, we swap the first and third values of \(\left\{I_{\text {princ }}\right\}\) and swap the first and third columns of \(\left[e_{\text {princ }}\right]\). This leads to \(\left|\left[e_{\text {princ }}\right]\right|=+1\), as required. The result is that the principal moments of inertia are the same as those obtained from Matlab, whereas the last two rows of \([R]\) obtained from Mathcad are the negative of the Matlab values.

 

† One should check the behavior of their software version. In particular, the manner in which eigenvectors are normalized is somewhat arbitrary. The results presented here were obtained from Matlab 7.1 and Mathcad 13.1.
