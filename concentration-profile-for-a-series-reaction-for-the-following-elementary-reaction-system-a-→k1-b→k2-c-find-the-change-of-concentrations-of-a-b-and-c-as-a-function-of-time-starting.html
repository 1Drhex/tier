Guest Question.

Concentration profile for a series reaction

For the following elementary reaction system,

\(A \quad \stackrel{k_{1}}{\rightarrow} \quad B \quad \stackrel{k_{2}}{\rightarrow} \quad C\)

Find the change of concentrations of A, B, and C as a function of time starting with pure A initially at \(C_{A}=C_{A 0}\) in a constant volume isothermal batch reactor.

Admin
Solution

This is a set of two reactions occurring in series. Fig. E4.3.1 shows a sketch of the reactor system. Mole balance for species j leads to

\(r_{j} V=\frac{d n_{j}}{d t}\). (E4.3.1)

Since the volume is constant, we have

\(\frac{d C_{j}}{d t}=r_{j}\). (E4.3.2)

We next find the rates of reaction. Applying Eq. (3.6) \(\nabla \longrightarrow=^{/} \) , we obtain

\(r_{ A }=v_{1 A } r_{1}+v_{2 A } r_{2}=-r_{1}=-k_{1} C_{ A }\) , (E4.3.3)

 

\(r_{ B }=v_{1 B } r_{1}+v_{2 B } r_{2}=r_{1}-r_{2}=k_{1} C_{ A }-k_{2} C_{ B }\) , (E4.3.4)

 

\(r_{ C }=v_{1 C } r_{1}+v_{2 C } r_{2}=r_{2}=k_{2} C_{ B }\). (E4.3.5)

Substituting Eq. (E4.3.3) \(r_{ A }=v_{1 A } r_{1}+v_{2 A } r_{2}=-r_{1}=-k_{1} C_{ A }\) , into Eq. (E4.3.2) \(\frac{d C_{j}}{d t}=r_{j}\). , we obtain

\(\frac{d C_{A}}{d t}=r_{A}=-k_{1} C_{A}\). (E4.3.6)

Separation of variables and integration,

\(\int_{C_{A0} }^{C_{A}} \frac{d C_{A}}{C_{A}}=-\int_{0}^{t} k_{1} d t.\) (E4.3.7)

We obtain

\(\ln \frac{C_{A}}{C_{A 0}}=-k_{1} t\) (E4.3.8)

or

\(C_{ A }=C_{ A 0} \exp \left(-k_{1} t\right)\). (E4.3.9)

Substituting Eqs. (E4.3.9) \(C_{ A }=C_{ A 0} \exp \left(-k_{1} t\right)\). and (E4.3.4) \(r_{ B }=v_{1 B } r_{1}+v_{2 B } r_{2}=r_{1}-r_{2}=k_{1} C_{ A }-k_{2} C_{ B }\) , into Eq. (E4.3.2) \(\frac{d C_{j}}{d t}=r_{j}\). , we obtain

\(\frac{d C_{B}}{d t}=r_{B}=k_{1} C_{A}-k_{2} C_{B}=k_{1} C_{A 0} e ^{-k_{1} t}-k_{2} C_{B}\). (E4.3.10)

Multiplying both sides by \(e ^{k 2 t}\), one can rearrange Eq. (E4.3.10) \(\frac{d C_{B}}{d t}=r_{B}=k_{1} C_{A}-k_{2} C_{B}=k_{1} C_{A 0} e ^{-k_{1} t}-k_{2} C_{B}\). to yield

\(e ^{k_{2} t}\left(d C_{B}+k_{2} C_{B} d t\right)=e^{k_{2} t} k_{1} C_{A 0} e ^{-k_{1} t} d t\) (E4.3.11)

or

\(e^{k_{2} t} d C_{B}+C_{B} d e^{k_{2} t}=d\left(C_{B} e^{k_{2} t}\right)=k_{1} C_{A 0} e^{\left(k_{2}-k_{1}\right) t} d t .\) (E4.3.12)

Integration of Eq. (E4.3.12) \(e^{k_{2} t} d C_{B}+C_{B} d e^{k_{2} t}=d\left(C_{B} e^{k_{2} t}\right)=k_{1} C_{A 0} e^{\left(k_{2}-k_{1}\right) t} d t .\) yields

\(C_{B} e^{k_{2} t}=\begin{cases}\frac{k_{1}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} C_{A 0}\left[e^{\left(k_{2}-k_{1}\right) t}-1\right], & k_{2} \neq k_{1} \\ k_{1} C_{A 0} t, & k_{2}=k_{1}\end{cases}\). (E4.3.13)

Thus the concentration of B in the isothermal constant volume reactor is given by

\(C_{B}=\left\{\begin{array}{cl}\frac{e^{-k_{1} t}-e^{-k_{2} t}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} k_{1} C_{A 0}, & k_{2} \neq k_{1} \\k_{1} C_{A 0} t e^{-k_{2} t}, & k_{2}=k_{1}\end{array}\right.\). (E4.3.14)

The concentration of C can be obtained either by substituting Eqs. (E4.3.14) \(C_{B}=\left\{\begin{array}{cl}\frac{e^{-k_{1} t}-e^{-k_{2} t}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} k_{1} C_{A 0}, & k_{2} \neq k_{1} \\k_{1} C_{A 0} t e^{-k_{2} t}, & k_{2}=k_{1}\end{array}\right.\). and (E4.3.5) \(r_{ C }=v_{1 C } r_{1}+v_{2 C } r_{2}=r_{2}=k_{2} C_{ B }\). into Eq. (E4.3.2) \(\frac{d C_{j}}{d t}=r_{j}\). , or via stoichiometry. Since there is only A in the reactor initially, the total concentration of A, B, and C is not going to change with time based on the stoichiometry as given by the series reaction (all the stoichiometry coefficients are unity). Thus

\(C_{ C }=C_{ A 0}-C_{ A }-C_{ B }\), (E4.3.15)

which gives

\(C_{B} e ^{k_{2} t}=C_{A 0}-\frac{k_{1} e^{-k_{2} t}-k_{2} e^{-k_{1} t}}{k_{1}-k_{2}} C_{A 0}\). (E4.3.16)

Fig. E4.3.2 shows the change of concentrations with time based on Eqs. (E4.3.9) \(C_{ A }=C_{ A 0} \exp \left(-k_{1} t\right)\). , (E4.3.14) \(C_{B}=\left\{\begin{array}{cl}\frac{e^{-k_{1} t}-e^{-k_{2} t}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} k_{1} C_{A 0}, & k_{2} \neq k_{1} \\k_{1} C_{A 0} t e^{-k_{2} t}, & k_{2}=k_{1}\end{array}\right.\). and (E4.3.16) \(C_{B} e ^{k_{2} t}=C_{A 0}-\frac{k_{1} e^{-k_{2} t}-k_{2} e^{-k_{1} t}}{k_{1}-k_{2}} C_{A 0}\). . One can observe that there is a maximum for concentration \(C_{B}\) that changes with \(k_{1}\) and \(k_{2}\) . This maximum can be obtained by setting

\(0=\left.\frac{d C_{B}}{d t}\right|_{t_{m}}=k_{1} C_{A}-k_{2} C_{B}\)

 

\(=k_{1} C_{A 0} e^{-k_{1} t_{m}}-k_{2} \frac{e^{-k_{1} t_{m}}-e^{-k_{2} t_{m}}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} k_{1} C_{A 0} \), (E4.3.17)

which can be rendered to

\(k_{1} \frac{k_{1}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} C_{A 0} e ^{-k_{1} t_{m}}=k_{2} \frac{k_{1}}{k_{2}-k_{1}} C_{A 0} e ^{-k_{2} t_{m}}\) (E4.3.18)

or

\(t_{m}=\frac{\ln \frac{k_{2}}{k_{1}}}{k_{2}-k_{1}}\) (E4.3.19)

and the corresponding concentration of B is given by

\(C_{B \max }=C_{A 0}\left(\frac{k_{2}}{k_{1}}\right)^{\frac{-k_{2}}{k_{2}-k_{1}}}\) (E4.3.20)
