Guest Question.

Find the expected rate of upcrossings for a covariant stationary Gaussian process \( \left\{X\left(t\right) \right\} \)

Admin
Solution

For a covariant stationary process, we know that the random variables \( X\left(t\right) \) and . \( \dot {X}\left(t\right) \) are uncorrelated. In addition, we know that Gaussian random variables are uncorrelated if and only if they are independent. Thus, we know that \( X\left(t\right) \) and . \( \dot {X}\left(t\right) \) are independent for the present situation. This allows us to use the unconditional distribution of . \( \dot {X}\left(t\right) \) in place of the conditional distribution in Eq.7.3

\(v_{X}^{+}(u,t)=p_{X(t)}(u)\int_{0}^{\infty }{vp_{\dot{X}(t) }[v \mid X(t)=u]dv} \)

, giving

\( \nu ^{+}_{X}(u,t)=p_{X(t)}(u)\int_{0}^{\infty }{\frac{\nu }{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma _{\dot {X}}} } \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\nu -\mu _{\dot{X}}(t)}{\sigma _{\dot{X}}} \right]^{2} \right) d\nu \)

which can be integrated to give

\( \nu ^{+}_{X}(u,t)=p_{X(t)}(u)\left( \mu _{\dot{X}}(t)\Phi\left[\frac{\mu _{\dot{X}}(t)}{\sigma _{\dot{X}}} \right]+\frac{\sigma _{\dot{X}}}{(2\pi)^{1/2}}\exp \left[\frac{-\mu ^{2}_{\dot{X}}(t)}{2\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}} \right] \right) \)

Or

\( \nu ^{+}_{X}(u,t)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1/2}\sigma _{X}} \exp \left(-\frac{1}{2} \left[\frac{u-\mu _{X}(t)}{\sigma _{X}} \right]^{2}\right) \times \left(\mu _{\dot{X}}(t)\Phi \left[\frac{\mu _{\dot{X}}(t)}{\sigma _{\dot{X}}} \right]+\frac{\sigma _{\dot{X}}}{(2\pi)^{1/2}}\exp \left[\frac{-\mu ^{2}_{\dot{X}}(t)}{2\sigma ^{2}_{\dot{X}}} \right] \right) \)

in which \( \Phi (\cdot ) \) denotes the cumulative Gaussian distribution function. Note that \( \mu _{X} \) and \( \mu _{\dot{X}} \) are written as functions of time, because covariant stationarity does not imply mean-value stationarity.

Next consider the special case in which \( \left\{X(t)\right\} \) is also mean-value stationary and thereby strictly stationary. In this case we know that \( \mu _{X} \) is a constant and \( \mu _{\dot{X}}=0 \) , so our expression can be simplified to

\( \nu ^{+}_{X}(u)=\frac{\sigma _{\dot{X}}}{2\pi\sigma _{X}}\exp \left(-\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{ u-\mu _{X}}{\sigma _{X}}\right]^{2} \right) \) (for mean and covariant stationary)

Recall that one definition of characteristic frequency of a narrowband process was given in Eq. 6.27

\(\omega _{c2}\equiv \frac{\sigma _{\dot{X} }}{\sigma _{X}} \)

as \( \omega _{c2}=\sigma _{\dot{X}}/\sigma _{X} \) . Thus, we can extend this definition of \( \omega _{c2} \) to apply to any stochastic process and say that

\( \nu ^{+}_{X}(u)=\frac{\omega _{c2}}{2\pi}\exp \left(-\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{ u-\mu _{X}}{\sigma _{X}}\right]^{2} \right) \)

The maximum value of this crossing rate occurs when \( u=\mu _{X} \) and it is simply \( \nu ^{+}_{X}(\mu _{X})=\omega _{c2}/(2\pi) \) . Thus, we see that the rate of upcrossings of \( u=\mu _{X} \) by a stationary Gaussian process is simply the energy-based average frequency divided by \( 2\pi \) . The factor of \( 2\pi \) , of course, comes from the fact that \( \omega _{c2} \) represents a frequency in radians per second, while the rate of meanupcrossings represents a frequency in cycles per second, or Hz. The interesting thing is that these two quite distinct definitions of process frequency are exactly equivalent for a stationary Gaussian process.
