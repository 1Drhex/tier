Guest Question.

The ceramic wall shown in Figure Example 4-15a is initially uniform in temperature at \(20^{◦} C\) and has a thickness of 3.0 cm. It is suddenly exposed to a radiation source on the right side at \(1000^{◦} C\) . The left side is exposed to room air at \(20^{◦} C\) with a radiation surrounding temperature of \(20^{◦} C\) . Properties of the ceramic are

\(k =3.0 W/m \cdot C^{◦} , ρ=1600 kg/m^{3},and\enspace c=0.8 kJ/kg \cdot C^{◦}, and\; \). Radiation heat transfer with the surroundings at Tr may be calculated from:

\(q_{r}=\sigma \epsilon A(T^{4}-T^{4}_{r} ) \quad W\hspace{30 pt} [a] \)

where: \(σ =5.669×10^{-8}, \epsilon =0.8 \) and T is in degrees Kelvin. The convection heat-transfer coefficient
from the left side of the plate is given by

\(h=1.92\Delta T^{1/4} \qquad W/m^{2} · ^{◦} C\hspace{30 pt} [b]\)

Convection on the right side is negligible. Determine the temperature distribution in the plate
after 15, 30, 45, 60, 90, 120, and 150 s. Also determine the steady-state temperature distribution.
Calculate the total heat gained by the plate for these times.

Admin
Solution

We divide the wall into five nodes as shown and must express temperatures in degrees Kelvin
because of the radiation boundary condition. For node 1 the transient energy equation is:

\(\sigma \epsilon\left(293^{4}-T_{1}^{p 4}\right)-1.92\left(T_{1}^{p}-293\right)^{5 / 4}+\frac{k}{\Delta x}\left(T_{2}^{p}-T_{1}^{p}\right)=\rho c \frac{\Delta x}{2} \frac{T_{1}^{p+1}-T_{1}^{p}}{\Delta \tau}\) ( c )

 

Similarly, for node

 

\(\sigma \epsilon\left(1273^{4}-T_{5}^{p 4}\right)+\frac{k}{\Delta x}\left(T_{4}^{p}-T_{5}^{p}\right)=\rho c \frac{\Delta x}{2} \frac{T_{5}^{p+1}-T_{5}^{p}}{\Delta \tau}\) (d)

Equations ( c ) and (d) may be subsequently written

 

\(T_{1}^{p+1}= \frac{\Delta \tau}{C_{1}}\left[\sigma \epsilon\left(293^{2}+T_{1}^{p 2}\right)\left(293+T_{1}^{p}\right)(293)-1.92\left(T_{1}^{p}-293\right)^{1 / 4}(293)+\frac{k}{\Delta x} T_{2}^{p}\right] +\left\{1-\frac{\Delta \tau}{C_{1}}\left[\sigma \epsilon\left(293^{2}+T_{1}^{p 2}\right)\left(293+T_{1}^{p}\right)-1.92\left(T_{1}^{p}-293\right)^{1 / 4}+\frac{k}{\Delta x}\right]\right\} T_{1}^{p}\) (e)

 

\(T_{5}^{p+1}= \frac{\Delta \tau}{C_{5}}\left[\sigma \epsilon\left(1273^{2}+T_{5}^{p 2}\right)\left(1273+T_{5}^{p}\right)(1273)+\frac{k}{\Delta x} T_{4}^{p}\right] +\left\{1-\frac{\Delta \tau}{C_{5}}\left[\sigma \epsilon\left(1273^{2}+T_{5}^{p^{2}}\right)\left(1273+T_{5}^{p}\right)+\frac{k}{\Delta x}\right]\right\} T_{5}^{p}\) (f)

where C1 =C5 =ρcΔx/2. For the other three nodes the expressions are much simpler:

\(T^{p+1}_{2} =\frac{\Deltaτ }{C_{2} } \frac{k}{\Delta x}(T^{p}_{1}+T^{p}_{3} ) +(1-\frac{2k}{C_{2} }\frac{\Delta τ}{\Delta x} )T^{p}_{2}\hspace{30 pt} [g] \)

 

\(T^{p+1}_{3} =\frac{\Deltaτ }{C_{3} } \frac{k}{\Delta x}(T^{p}_{2}+T^{p}_{4} ) +(1-\frac{2k}{C_{3} }\frac{\Delta τ}{\Delta x} )T^{p}_{3}\hspace{30 pt} [h] \)

 

\(T^{p+1}_{4} =\frac{\Deltaτ }{C_{4} } \frac{k}{\Delta x}(T^{p}_{3}+T^{p}_{5} ) +(1-\frac{2k}{C_{4} }\frac{\Delta τ}{\Delta x} )T^{p}_{4}\hspace{30 pt} [i] \)

 

whereC2 =C3 =C4 =ρcΔ x. So, to determine the transient response, we simply choose a suitable
value of Δτ and march through the calculations. The stability criterion is such that the coefficients
of the last term in each equation cannot be negative. For Equations (g), (h), and (i) the maximum
allowable time increment is

\(\Delta τ_{max}=\frac{C_{3} \Delta x}{2k} =\frac{(1600)(800)(0.0075)^{2} }{(2)(3)}=12\> s \)

For Equation ( f ), the worst case is at the start when \(T^{p}_{5}=20^{◦} C=293 K. \) We have

\(C_{5} =\frac{(1600)(800)(0.0075) }{2}=4800 \)

so that:

\(\Deltaτ_{max}=\frac{4800}{(5.669×10^{-8} )(0.8)(1273^{2} +293^{2} )(1273+293)+3.0/0.0075} = 9.43 s \)

For node 1 [Equation (e)] the most restrictive condition occurs when \(T^{p}_{1}=293 \).We have:

C1 =C5 =4800

so that:

\(\Deltaτ_{max}=\frac{4800}{(5.669×10^{-8} )(0.8)(293^{2} +293^{2} )(293+293)+3.0/0.0075} = 11.86\; s \)

 

So, from these calculations we see that node 5 is most restrictive and we must choose Δτ<9.43 s.
The calculations were performed with Δτ =3.0 s, and the results are shown in
Figure Example 4-15b, c. Note that a straight line is obtained for the steady-state temperature
distribution in the solid, which is what would be expected for a constant thermal conductivity. To
compute the heat added at any instant of time we perform the sum:

\( Q(τ)=\sum{C_{i} }(T_{i} -293) \hspace{30 pt} \hspace{30 pt} [j] \)

and plot the results in Figure Example 4-15c.
