Guest Question.

The initial voltages on capacitors\(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\) in the circuit shown in Fig. 7.14 have been established by
sources not shown. The switch is closed at t=0.

a) Find \(v_{1}\left(t\right)\),\(v_{2}\left(t\right)\), and \(v\left(t\right)\) for \(t \geq 0\) and \(i\left(t\right)\) for \(t \geq 0^{+}\).

b) Calculate the initial energy stored in the capacitors \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\).

c) Determine how much energy is stored in the capacitors as t→∞.

d) Show that the total energy delivered to the 250kΩ resistor is the difference between the
results obtained in (b) and (c).

Admin
Solution

a) Once we know \(v\left(t\right)\) we can obtain the current \(i\left(t\right)\) from Ohm’s law. After determining \(i\left(t\right)\), we can calculate \(v_{1}\left(t\right)\)and \(v_{2}\left(t\right)\) because the voltage across
a capacitor is a function of the capacitor current. To find \(v\left(t\right)\) , we replace the series-connected capacitors with an equivalent capacitor. It has a capacitance of \(4\mu F\) and is charged to a voltage of 20 V. Therefore, the circuit shown in Fig. 7.14 reduces to the one shown in Fig. 7.15, which reveals that the initial value of \(v\left(t\right)\) is 20 V, and that the time constant of the circuit is\( \left (4 \right)\left(250\right) \times 10^{-3}\), or 1 s. Thus the expression for \(v\left(t\right)\) is

 

\(v\left(t\right)=20 e^{-t} V, t \geq 0 \) .

 

The current \(i\left(t\right)\) is

 

\(i\left(t\right)=\frac{v\left(t\right)}{250,000}=80 e^{-t}\mu A , t \geq 0^{+} \) .

 

Knowing \(i\left(t\right)\) we calculate the expressions for \(v_{1}\left(t\right)\)and \(v_{2}\left(t\right)\):

 

\(v_{1}\left(t\right)=-\frac{10^{6}}{5}\int_{0}^{t}{80\times 10^{-6}e^{-x }dx-4} \)

 

\(= \left(16e^{-t}-20 \right) V, t \geq 0\),

 

\(v_{2}\left(t\right)=-\frac{10^{6}}{20}\int_{0}^{t}{80\times 10^{-6}e^{-x }dx+24} \)

 

\(= \left(4e^{-t}+20 \right) V, t \geq 0\).

 

b) The initial energy stored in \(C_{1}\) is

 

\(w_{1}=\frac{1}{2}\left(5\times 10^{-6}\right)\left(16\right)=40 \mu J \).

 

The initial energy stored in \(C_{2}\) is

 

\(w_{2}=\frac{1}{2}\left(20\times 10^{-6}\right)\left(576\right)=5760 \mu J \).

 

The total energy stored in the two capacitors is
\(w_{o}=40+5760 =5800 \mu J \).

 

c) As t→∞,

 

\(v_{1}→-20 V\)and\(v_{2}→+ 20 V\).

 

Therefore the energy stored in the two capacitors is

 

\(w_{\infty}=\frac{1}{2}\left(5+20 \right)\times 10^{-6}\left(400\right)=5000 \mu J \).

 

d) The total energy delivered to the 250kΩ resistor is

 

\(w=\int_{0}^{\infty}{p dt}=\int_{0}^{\infty}{\frac{400e^{-2t}}{250,000}dt}=800 \mu J\).

 

 

Comparing the results obtained in (b) and (c) shows that

 

\(800 \mu J=\left(5800 – 5000\right)\mu J\).

 

The energy stored in the equivalent capacitor in Fig. 7.15 is \(\frac{1}{2}\left(4\times 10^{-6}\right)\left(400\right) \), or \(800 \mu J.\) Because this capacitor predicts the terminal behavior of the original series-connected capacitors, the energy stored in the equivalent capacitor is the energy delivered to the 250kΩ resistor.
