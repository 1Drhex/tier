Guest Question.

Show that the second equation in Eq. 12.127 can be expressed in terms of the field tensor \(F^{\mu \nu}\) as follows:

\(\frac{\partial F^{\mu \nu}}{\partial x^{\nu}}=\mu_{0} J^{\mu}, \quad \frac{\partial G^{\mu \nu}}{\partial x^{\nu}}=0\) (12.127)

\(\frac{\partial F_{\mu \nu}}{\partial x^{\lambda}}+\frac{\partial F_{\nu \lambda}}{\partial x^{\mu}}+\frac{\partial F_{\lambda \mu}}{\partial x^{\nu}}=0\) (12.130)

Admin
Solution

We know that \(\partial_{\nu} G^{\mu \nu}=0\) is equivalent to the two homogeneous Maxwell equations, ∇·B = 0 and ∇×E = \( -\frac{\partial B }{\partial t}\) . All we have to show, then, is that \(\partial_{\lambda} F_{\mu \nu}+\partial_{\mu} F_{\nu \lambda}+\partial_{\nu} F_{\lambda \mu}=0\) is also equivalent to them. Now this equation stands for 64 separate equations \((\mu=0 \rightarrow 3, \nu=0 \rightarrow 3, \lambda=0 \rightarrow 3, \text { and } 4 \times 4 \times 4=64)\) . But many of them are redundant, or trivial .

Suppose two indices are the same \(\text { (say, } \mu=\nu) \text {. Then } \partial_{\lambda} F_{\mu \mu}+\partial_{\mu} F_{\mu \lambda}=\partial_{\mu} F_{\lambda \mu}=0 . \text { But } F_{\mu \mu}=0\) and \(F_{\mu \lambda}=-F_{\lambda \mu}\) , so this is trivial: 0 = 0. To get anything significant, then, \(\mu, \nu, \lambda\) must all be different. They could beall spatial \((\mu, \nu, \lambda=1,2,3=x, y, z\) — or some permutation). Let’s examine these two cases separately.

All spatial: say, \(\mu=1, \nu=2, \lambda=3\) (other permutations yield the same equation, or minus it.)

\(\partial_{3} F_{12}+\partial_{1} F_{23}+\partial_{2} F_{31}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(B_{z}\right)+\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(B_{x}\right)+\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(B_{y}\right)=0 \Rightarrow \nabla \cdot B =0\).

One temporal: say,\(\mu=0, \nu=1, \lambda=2\) (other permutations of these indices yield same result, or minus it).

\(\partial_{2} F_{01}+\partial_{0} F_{12}+\partial_{1} F_{31}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(-\frac{E_{x}}{c}\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial(c t)}\left(B_{z}\right)+\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(+\frac{E_{y}}{c}\right)=0\).

\(\text { or: }-\frac{\partial B_{z}}{\partial t}+\left(\frac{\partial E_{x}}{\partial y}-\frac{\partial E_{y}}{\partial x}\right)=0\) , which is the z-component of \(-\frac{\partial B }{\partial t}= \nabla \times E . \quad(\text { If } \mu=0, \nu=1, \lambda=2\) , we get the y component; for \(\nu=2, \lambda=3\), ) = 3 we get the x component.)

Conclusion: \(\partial_{\lambda} F_{\mu \nu}+\partial_{\mu} F_{\nu \lambda}+\partial_{\nu} F_{\lambda \mu}=0\) is equivalent to \(\nabla \cdot B =0 \text { and } \frac{\partial B }{\partial t}=- \nabla \times E\) , and hence to \( \partial_{\nu} G^{\mu \nu}=0 \text {. qed }\)
