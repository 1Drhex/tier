Guest Question.

This cantilever wall has moved sufficiently to create the active condition. Compute the lateral pressure distribution acting on this wall with the groundwater table at locations a and b, as shown in Figure 16.17. The soil properties are: \(c^{\prime } = 0\), \(Φ^{\prime } = 30°\), \(γ = 20.4 kN/m^{3}\), and \(γ_{sat} = 22.0 kN/m^{3}\)

Admin
Solution

Use Rankine’s Method.

\(K_{a}=\tan^{2}\left(45° – \Phi /2\right) =\tan^{2}\left(45° – 30°/2\right) =0.333\)

With the groundwater table at a:

\(\sigma =\sigma ^{\prime }_{z}K_{a}\cos \beta =\gamma zK_{a}\cos \beta =20.4z\left(0.333\right) \cos 0=6.79z\)

where z = depth below the top of the wall

\(β=0 ∴ V_{a}=0\) (per Rankine)

With the groundwater table at b:

\(\sigma @ z\geq 2.5m=\sigma ^{\prime }_{z}K_{a}\cos \beta=\left(\sum{\gamma H – u} \right) K_{a}\cos \beta =\left[\left(20.4 kN/m^{3}\right) \left(2.5 m\right) +\left(22.0 kN/m^{3}\right)\left(z – 2.5 m\right) – u\right] \left(0.333\right) \cos 0=7.33z – 0.33u – 1.33\)

 

\(u=9.80 kN/m^{3}(z – 2.5)≥0\)

demonstrates the profound impact of groundwater on retaining walls. If the groundwater table rises from a to b, the total horizontal force acting on the wall increases by about 30 percent. Therefore, the factor of safety against sliding and overturning could drop from 1.5 to about 1.0, and the flexural stresses in the stem would be about 30 percent larger than anticipated. There are two ways to avoid these problems:

1. Design the wall for the highest probable groundwater table. This can be very expensive, but it may be the only available option.
2. Install drains to prevent the groundwater from rising above a certain level. These could consist of weep holes drilled in the face of the wall or a perforated pipe drain installed behind the wall. Drains such as these are the most common method of designing for groundwater.

Further problems can occur if the groundwater becomes frozen and ice lenses form. The same processes that cause frost heave at the ground surface also produce large horizontal pressures on retaining walls. This is another good reason to provide good surface and subsurface drainage around retaining walls.
