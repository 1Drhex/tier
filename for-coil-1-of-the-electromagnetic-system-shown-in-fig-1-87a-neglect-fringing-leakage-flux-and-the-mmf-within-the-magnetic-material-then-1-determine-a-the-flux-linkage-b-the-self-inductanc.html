Guest Question.

For coil 1 of the electromagnetic system shown in Fig. 1-87(a), neglect fringing, leakage flux, and the mmf within the magnetic material, then:

1. Determine:

a. The flux linkage.

b. The self-inductance.

c. The mutual inductance.

2. If the current in coil 2 is reversed, repeat 1.

Admin
Solution

1. a. Flux linkage. By definition, the flux linkage of coil 1 is

\(\lambda _{1}=\lambda _{11}+\lambda _{12}\)

\(=N_{1}\phi _{11}+N_{1}\phi _{12}\) (I)

The term \(N_{1}\phi _{11}\) represents the flux linkage of coil 1 due to its own current. To find \(N_{1}\phi _{11}\), discard the current in coil 2 and draw the equivalent circuit, as shown in Fig. 1-87(b). Thus,

\(\lambda _{11}=N_{1}\phi _{11}=N_{1}\left(\frac{N_{1}I_{1}}{R} \right)\) (II)

The reluctance (as seen by the source of mmf \(N_{1}I_{1}\)) is

\(R=R_{1}+R_{2}//R_{3}\)

Since the air gaps have the same lengths, the reluctances \(R_{1} , R_{2}, and R_{3}\) are equal. Thus,

\(R=R_{1}+\frac{R_{1}}{2}=\frac{3}{2}R_{1}=\frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{l_{g}}{\mu _{0}A} \right)\) (III)

From Eq. (II) and (III), we obtain:

\(N_{1}\phi _{11}=N_{1}^{2}I_{1}\frac{2\mu _{0}A}{3l_{g}}\) (IV)

The term \(N_{1}\phi _{12}\) of relationship (I) represents the flux linkage of coil 1 due to the current in coil 2. To find the effects of the second magnetic voltage source, apply the superposition principle and draw the equivalent circuit as shown in Fig. 1-87(c).

From the equivalent circuit,

\(N_{1}\phi _{12}=N_{1}\phi _{22}\left(\frac{R_{3}}{R_{1}+R_{3}} \right)=N_{1}\frac{\phi _{22}}{2}\)

 

\(N_{1}\phi _{12}=\left(\frac{N_{1}}{2} \right)\frac{N_{2}I_{2}}{R_{2}+R_{1}//R_{3}}=\frac{N_{1}N_{2}I_{2}}{2\left(\frac{3}{2}R_{1} \right) } \)

=\(N_{1}N_{2}I_{2}\frac{\mu _{0}A}{3l_{g}}\) (V)

The total flux linkage of coil 1 is obtained by adding Eq. (IV) and (V). Thus,

\(\lambda _{1}=\underline{\underline{\frac{\mu _{0}A}{3l_{g}}\left(2N_{1}^{2}I_{1}+N_{1}N_{2}I_{2}\right)} }\) (VI)

b) Self-inductance. By definition, the self-inductance of coil 1 is

\(L_{11}=\frac{\lambda _{11}}{I_{1}}=\frac{N_{1}\phi _{11}}{I_{1}}\)

From the last relationship and from Eq. (IV) we obtain

\(L_{11}=\underline{\underline{N_{1}^{2}\frac{2}{3}\frac{\mu _{0}A}{l_{g}}} }\) (VII)

c. Mutual Inductance. The mutual inductance of coil 1, with respect to coil 2, is

\(L_{12}=\frac{\lambda _{12}}{i_{2}}=\frac{N_{1}\phi _{12}}{i_{2}}\)

Using rms values, from Eq. (V) and the last relationship, we obtain

\(L_{12}=\underline{\underline{N_{1}N_{2}\frac{\mu _{0}A}{3l_{g}}} }\) (VIII)

2. Reversing current \(I_{2}\)
a. If the current in coil 2 changes direction, its flux will oppose the flux produced by the current in coil 1. Thus,

\(\lambda_{1}=\underline{\underline{\frac{\mu _{0}A}{3l_{g}}\left(2N_{1}^{2}I_{1}-N_{1}N_{2}I_{2}\right) } }\) (IX)

b. and c. The self- and mutual inductances are scalar quantities, independent of current direction
