Guest Question.

Figure 3–22 shows a crank loaded by a force F = 300 lbf that causes twisting and bending of a \(\frac{3}{4}\)-in-diameter shaft fixed to a support at the origin of the reference system. In actuality, the support may be an inertia that we wish to rotate, but for the purposes of a stress analysis we can consider this a statics problem.
(a) Draw separate free-body diagrams of the shaft AB and the arm BC, and compute the values of all forces, moments, and torques that act. Label the directions of the coordinate axes on these diagrams.
(b) Compute the maxima of the torsional stress and the bending stress in the arm BC and indicate where these act.
(c) Locate a stress element on the top surface of the shaft at A, and calculate all the stress components that act upon this element.
(d) Determine the maximum normal and shear stresses at A.

Admin
Solution

(a) The two free-body diagrams are shown in Fig. 3–23. The results are

 

\(\text { At end } C \text { of } \operatorname{arm} B C: \quad F =-300 j \text { lbf, } T _{C}=-450 k lbf \cdot \text { in }\)

 

\(\text { At end } B \text { of arm } B C: \quad F =300 j lbf , M _{1}=1200 i lbf \cdot \text { in, } T _{1}=450 k lbf \cdot \text { in }\)

 

\(\text { At end } B \text { of shaft } A B: \quad F =-300 j lbf , T _{2}=-1200 i lbf \cdot in , M _{2}=-450 k lbf \cdot \text { in }\)

 

\(\text { At end } A \text { of shaft } A B: \quad F =300 j lbf , M _{A}=1950 k lbf \cdot in , T _{A}=1200 i lbf \cdot in\)

 

(b) For arm BC, the bending moment will reach a maximum near the shaft at B.If we assume this is 1200 lbf ? in, then the bending stress for a rectangular section will be

 

\(\sigma=\frac{M}{I / c}=\frac{6 M}{b h^{2}}=\frac{6(1200)}{0.25(1.25)^{2}}=18400 psi =18.4 kpsi\)

 

Of course, this is not exactly correct, because at B the moment is actually being transferred into the shaft, probably through a weldment.For the torsional stress, use Eq. (3–43). Thus

 

\(H=T \omega\) (3–43)

 

\(\tau_{\max }=\frac{T}{b c^{2}}\left(3+\frac{1.8}{b / c}\right)=\frac{450}{1.25\left(0.25^{2}\right)}\left(3+\frac{1.8}{1.25 / 0.25}\right)=19400 psi =19.4 kpsi\)

 

This stress occurs on the outer surfaces at the middle of the \(1 \frac{1}{4}\) -in side.
(c) For a stress element at A, the bending stress is tensile and is

 

\(\sigma_{x}=\frac{M}{I / c}=\frac{32 M}{\pi d^{3}}=\frac{32(1950)}{\pi(0.75)^{3}}=47100 psi =47.1 kpsi\)

 

The torsional stress is

 

\(\tau_{x z}=\frac{-T}{J / c}=\frac{-16 T}{\pi d^{3}}=\frac{-16(1200)}{\pi(0.75)^{3}}=-14500 psi =-14.5 kpsi\)

 

where the reader should verify that the negative sign accounts for the direction of \(\tau_{x z}\) (d) Point A is in a state of plane stress where the stresses are in the xz plane.Thus the principal stresses are given by Eq. (3–13) with subscripts corresponding to the x, z axes.

 

\(\sigma_{1},\sigma_{2}=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{y}}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (3–13)

 

The maximum normal stress is then given by

 

\(\sigma_{1}=\frac{\sigma_{x}+\sigma_{z}}{2}+\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{z}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x z}^{2}}\)

 

\(=\frac{47.1+0}{2}+\sqrt{\left(\frac{47.1-0}{2}\right)^{2}+(-14.5)^{2}}=51.2 kpsi\)

 

The maximum shear stress at A occurs on surfaces different than the surfaces containing the principal stresses or the surfaces containing the bending and torsional shear stresses. The maximum shear stress is given by Eq. (3–14), again with modified subscripts, and is given by

 

\(\tau_{1}, \tau_{2}=\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{y}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x y}^{2}}\) (3–14)

 

\(\tau_{1}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{x}-\sigma_{z}}{2}\right)^{2}+\tau_{x z}^{2}}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{47.1-0}{2}\right)^{2}+(-14.5)^{2}}=27.7 kpsi\)
