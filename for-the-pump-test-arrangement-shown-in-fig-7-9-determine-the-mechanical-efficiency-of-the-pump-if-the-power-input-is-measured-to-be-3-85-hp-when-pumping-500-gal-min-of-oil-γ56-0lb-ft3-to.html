Guest Question.

For the pump test arrangement shown in Fig. 7.9, determine the mechanical efficiency of the pump if the power input is measured to be 3.85 hp when pumping 500 gal/min of oil \(\left(\gamma = 56.0 lb/ft^3\right) \) . To begin, write the energy equation for this system.

Admin
Solution

Using the points identified as 1 and 2 in Fig. 7.9, we have

\( \frac{p_1}{\gamma} +z_1 + \frac{v^2_1}{2g} +h_A = \frac{p_2}{\gamma} +z_2 + \frac{v^2_2}{2g}\)

Because we must find the power delivered by the pump to the fluid, we should now solve for \( h_A \).

We use the following equation:

\( h_A = \frac{p_2 – p_1}{\gamma} +\left(z_2 – z_1\right) + \frac{v^2_2 – v^2_1}{2g}\) (7–7)

It is convenient to solve for each term individually and then combine the results. The manometer enables us to calculate \( \left(p_2 – p_1\right) /\gamma\) because it measures the pressure difference. Using the procedure outlined in Chapter 3, write the manometer equation between points 1 and 2.

Starting at point 1, we have

\( p_1 +\gamma_0 \Upsilon + \gamma_m \left(20.4in\right) – \gamma_0\left(20.4in\right) -\gamma_0 \Upsilon = p_2\)

where \( \Upsilon \) is the unknown distance from point 1 to the top of the mercury column in the left leg of the manometer. The terms involving \( \Upsilon \) cancel out. Also, in this equation \( \gamma_0 \) is the specific weight of the oil and \( \gamma_m \) is the specific weight of the mercury gage fluid.

The desired result for use in Eq. (7–7) is \( \left(p_2 – p_1\right)/\gamma_0\). Solve for this now and compute the result.

The correct solution is \( \left(p_2 – p_1\right)/\gamma_0 = 24.0ft.\) Here is one way to find it:

\( \gamma_m = \left(13.54\right) \left(\gamma_w\right) = \left(13.54\right) \left(62.4 lb/ft^3\right) = 844.9 lb/ft^3\)

\( p_2 = p_1 +\gamma_m \left(20.4in\right) – \gamma_0\left(20.4in\right) \)

\( p_2 -p_1 = \gamma_m \left(20.4in\right) – \gamma_0\left(20.4in\right) \)

\( \frac{p_2 – p_1}{\gamma_0} = \frac{\gamma_m\left(20.4in\right) }{\gamma_0} -20.4in = \left(\frac{\gamma_m}{\gamma_0} -1\right) 20.4in\)

\( = \left(\frac{844.9 lb/ft^3}{56.0 lb/ft^3}-1 \right) 20.4in = \left(15.1 – 1 \right)\left(20.4in\right) \)

\( \frac{p_1 – p_2}{\gamma_0} =\left(14.1\right) \left(20.4in\right) \left(\frac{1ft}{12in} \right) = 24.0 ft \)

The next term in Eq. (7–7) is \( z_2 – z_1 \). What is its value?

It is zero. Both points are at the same elevation. These terms could have been cancelled from the original equation. Now find \( \left(v^2_2 – v^2_1\right) /2g = 1.99ft\).

you should have \( \left(v^2_2 – v^2_1\right) /2g = 1.99ft\) = 1.99ft, obtained as follows. First, write

\( Q = \left(500gal/min\right) \left(\frac{1ft^3/s}{449gal/min} \right) =1.11ft^3/s\)

Using \( A_1 = 0.2006ft^2\) and \( A_2 =0.0884ft^2 \) from Appendix F, we get

\( v_1 = \frac{Q}{A_1} =\frac{1.11ft^3}{s} \times \frac{1}{0.2006ft^2} = 5.55ft/s \)

\( v_2 = \frac{Q}{A_2} =\frac{1.11ft^3}{s} \times \frac{1}{0.0884ft^2} =12.6ft/s\)

\(\frac{v^2_2 – v^2_1}{2g} =\frac{\left(12.6\right)^2 – \left(5.55\right)^2 }{\left(2\right)\left(32.2\right) } \frac{ft^2}{s^2} \frac{s^2}{ft} =1.99ft\)

Now place these results into Eq. (7–7) and solve for \( h_A \).

Solving for \( h_A \), we get

\( h_A = 24.0 ft +0+1.99ft=25.99ft \)

We can now calculate the power delivered to the oil, \( P_A \).

The result is \( P_A \) = 2.95 hp, found as follows:

\( P_A= h_a\gamma Q= 25.99ft \left(\frac{56.0lb}{ft^3} \right) \left(\frac{1.11ft^3}{s} \right) \)

\( P_A= 1620 lb–ft/s \left(\frac{1hp}{550 lb–ft/s} \right) = 2.95hp \)

The final step is to calculate \( e_M \), the mechanical efficiency of the pump.

From Eq. (7–6) we get

\( e_M = P_A/P_I = 2.95/3.85=0.77 \)

Expressed as a percentage, the pump is 77 percent efficient at the stated conditions.
This completes the programmed instruction.
