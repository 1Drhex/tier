Guest Question.

Return to the subsonic compressible flow over a wavy wall treated in . In that example we calculated the time rate of change of the volume of a fluid element per unit volume at the point \((x/l,y/l)=(\frac{1}{4},1)\)to be \(-0.7327S^{-1}\) That is, at the instant the fluid element was passing through this point, its volume was experiencing a rate of decrease of 73 percent per second, a substantial instantaneous rate of change. Moreover, we noted in that, because the volume was decreasing and hence the density increasing, the point \((\frac{1}{4},1)\) must be in a compression region. This would imply that the fluid element \((\frac{1}{4},1)\) is slowing down as it passes through point \((\frac{1}{4},1)\) ;i.e., it is experiencing a deceleration. Calculate the value of the deceleration at this point.

Admin
Solution

Acceleration (or deceleration) is physically the time rate of change of velocity. The time rate of change of velocity of a moving fluid element is, from the physical meaning of the substantial derivative, the substantial derivative of the velocity. Let us deal in terms of cartesian coordinates. For the two-dimensional flow considered in Example 2.1, the x and y components of acceleration are denoted by \(a_{x}\) and \(a_{y}\) , respectively, where

\(a_{x}=\frac{D_{u}}{_{t}}=u\frac{\partial _{u}}{\partial _{x}}+v\frac{\partial _{u}}{\partial _{y}} (E2.1)\)

and

\(a_{y}=\frac{D_{v}}{D_{t}}=u\frac{\partial _{v}}{\partial _{x}}+v\frac{\partial _{v}}{\partial y} (E2.2)\)

Equations for u and v are given in Example 2.1 as

\(u=V_{\infty }\left [ 1+\frac{h}{\beta }\frac{2\pi }{l}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} \right ] (2.35)\)

and

\(v=-V_{\infty }h\frac{2\pi }{l}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (2.36)\)

where

\(\beta =\sqrt{1-M_{\infty }^{2}}\)

From Equation (2.35),

\(u=V_{\infty }\left [ 1+\frac{h}{\beta }\frac{2\pi }{l}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} \right ] (2.35)\)

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-\frac{V_{\infty h}}{\beta }(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (E2.3)\)

and

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-V_{\infty }h(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (E2.4)\)

From Equation (2.36),

\(v=-V_{\infty }h\frac{2\pi }{l}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (2.36)\)

\(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}=-V_{\infty }h(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (E2.5)\)

and

\(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=V_{\infty }h\beta (\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} (E2.6)\)

For the wavy wall in , the wavelength is \(l=1.0\) m and the amplitude \(h=0.01\) m. Also given in Example 2.1 is \(M_{\infty }=240\) m/s and \(M_{\infty }=0.7.\) For these conditions, and remembering that we are making the calculation for a fluid element as it passes through \((x,y)=(\frac{1}{4},1),\)

\(\frac{2\pi }{l}=\frac{2\pi }{1.0}=6.283\)

\(\beta =\sqrt{1-M_{\infty }^{2}}=\sqrt{1-(0.7)^{2}}=0.714\)

\(\frac{2\pi \beta y}{l}=6.283(0.714)(1.0)=4.486\)

\(e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}=e^{-4.486}=0.01126\)

\(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{e}=\sin \frac{2\pi }{4}=\sin \frac{\pi }{2}=1\)

\(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l}=\cos \frac{\pi }{2}=0\)

From Equation (2.35),

\(u=V_{\infty }\left [ 1+\frac{h}{\beta }\frac{2\pi }{l}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l} \right ]\)

\(u=V_{\infty }=240 m/s\)

From Equation (2.36),

\(v=-V_{\infty }h\frac{2\pi }{l}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}\)

\(v=-(240)(0.01)(6.283)(1)(0.01126)\)

\(v=-0.1698m/s\)

From Equation (E2.3),

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-\frac{V\infty h}{\beta }(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}\)

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-\frac{(420)(0.01)}{0.714}(6.283)^{2}(1)(0.01126)\)

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-1.494s^{-1}\)

From Equation (E2.4),

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-V_{\infty }h(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}\)

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=0\)

From Equation (E2.5),

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=-V_{\infty }h(\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\cos \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}\)

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=0\)

From Equation (E2.6),

\(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=V_{\infty }h\beta (\frac{2\pi }{l})^{2}(\sin \frac{2\pi x}{l})e^{-2\pi \beta y/l}\)

\(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=(240)(0.01)(0.714)(6.283)^{2}(1)(0.01126)\)

\(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=0.7617s^{-1}\)

Substituting the above values into Equation (E2.1), we have

\(a_{y}=u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=(240)(-1.494)-(0.1698)(0)\)

\(a_{x}=-358.56m/s^{2}\)

From Equation (E2.2),

\(a_{y}=u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\)

\(a_{y}=(240)(0)-(0.1698)(0.7617)=-0.129m/s^{2}\)

The absolute magnitude of the acceleration is

\(|a|=\sqrt{a_{x}^{2}+a_{y}^{2}}=\sqrt{(-358.56)^{2}+(-0.129)^{2}}\)

\(|a|=358.6m/s^{2}\)

Note, however, that both ax and ay are negative, and hence the acceleration is negative, i.e., the fluid element is decelerating as it passes through point \((\frac{1}{4},1),\)with a value of

Deceleration \(= 358.6 m/s^{2}\)

Note also that, by far, the deceleration is greatest in the x direction, with the deceleration in the y direction being very small.

The acceleration of gravity at sea level on earth is \(9.8 m/s^{2}.\) Observe that the fluid element in this example is locally experiencing a deceleration with an absolute magnitude that is 36.6 times that of the acceleration of gravity; i.e., the fluid element as it passes through point \((\frac{1}{4},1)\) is experiencing a large deceleration of 36.6 g. This is totally consistent with the result from that the fluid element is simultaneously experiencing a very rapid change in volume of 73 percent per second.
(To relate to human experience, a human being can tolerate only up to 10 g acceleration or deceleration, and that for only a few seconds before life-threatening bodily injury.)
