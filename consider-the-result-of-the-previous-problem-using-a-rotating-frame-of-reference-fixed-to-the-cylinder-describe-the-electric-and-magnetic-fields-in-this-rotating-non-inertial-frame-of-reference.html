Guest Question.

Consider the result of the previous problem using a rotating frame of reference, fixed to the cylinder. Describe the electric and magnetic fields in this rotating (non-inertial) frame of reference.

(Assume that the angular velocity of rotation is much smaller than the cyclotron frequency, \( w_0 = eB /m, \) where \( e \) and \( m \) are the elementary charge and mass of the electron, respectively.)

Admin
Solution

The electric charge distribution at any point is the same in the rotating frame \( k^ \prime \) as in the laboratory (inertial) frame \( K \) , because the charge density is proportional to the number of electrons in unit volume, and both the number and the volume are clearly invariant. It thus follows that the electric charge density is homogeneous in the rotating frame and equal to

\( \rho ^\prime =\rho =2\varepsilon _0\left(\pm B w + \frac{m w^2}{e}\right) \approx \pm 2 \varepsilon _0 B w. \)

The force \( \pmb F \) acting on a charged particle must be the same in both frames of reference (as demonstrated by the fact that the elongation of a spring which measures the force is independent of the frame of reference). Thus \( \pmb F^\prime = \pmb F.\)

In the frame rotating with the cylinder the free electrons of the metal are at rest, and thus the net force exerted on them must be zero (otherwise they would move); if the centrifugal force is neglected this implies that the electric field must also be zero \( (\pmb E^ \prime = 0).\) Further,

\( Q(\pmb E + \pmb v \times \pmb B) = \pmb F = \pmb F^ \prime = Q[\pmb E^ \prime + (\pmb v + \pmb v _{rel}) \times \pmb B^ \prime ], \)

where \( \pmb v_{rel} \) is the relative velocity of the two frames, which is different at different points in the cylinder. Recalling that \( E = wBr \) and noting that \( \pmb v_{rel} \times \pmb B^\prime \) is directed radially with magnitude \( wB^ \prime r, \) we conclude that the magnetic field is (vectorially) the same in both frames, i.e. \( \pmb B^ \prime = \pmb B. \) It follows that in a rotating frame of reference electric charges can exist without an associated electric field, as illustrated in Fig. S174.1.

Similarly, it can be proved that in the frame of reference of an observer spinning in a homogeneous magnetic field, an inhomogeneous electric field exists, although there are no electric charges present (Fig. S174.2).

This means that Gauss’s law (the connection between electric flux and the charges responsible for it) is not obeyed in rotating frames of reference. This surprising result has to be allowed for, even in low-speed (non-relativistic) motion.
