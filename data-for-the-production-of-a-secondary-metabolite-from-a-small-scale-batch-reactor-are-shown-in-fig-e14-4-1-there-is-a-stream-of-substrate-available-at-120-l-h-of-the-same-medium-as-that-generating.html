Guest Question.

Data for the production of a secondary metabolite from a small-scale batch reactor are shown in Fig. E14.4.1. There is a stream of substrate available at 120 L/h of the same medium as that generating the batch-fermentation data. Assume that two reactors, each with 800 L working volume, are available. You will use exactly the same culture conditions (medium, pH, temperature, and so on) as in the batch reactor.

1. Determine the outlet concentration of the product and productivity.
2. Compare that obtained in part (a) to the value predicted if a single 1600 L reactor were used.

Admin
Solution

This is a chemostat problem. Mass balance for biomass over the i-th Chemostat at steady state yields:

\(Q X_{i-1}-Q X_{i}+r_{ Xi } V_{ i }=0\) (E14.4.1)

Dividing through by \(V_{i}\) and after rearrangement, we obtain

\(D_{i}=\frac{r_{X i}}{X_{i}-X_{i-1}}\) (E14.4.2)

where \(r_{ Xi }\) is the rate of biomass production inside the i-th chemostat, \(X_{ i }\) is the biomass concentration in the effluent stream of the i-th chemostat, which is the same as that inside the i-th chemostat, and \(D_{ i }\) is the dillustion rate of the i-th chemostat,

\(D_{i}=\frac{Q}{V_{i}}\) (E14.4.3)

Therefore, to solve the chemostat problem, we will need the rate of biomass production.

Similarly, mass balance of the secondary metabolite P over the i-th chemostat at steady state gives:

\(Q P_{ i -1}-Q P_{ i }+r_{ Pi } V_{ i }=0\) (E14.4.4)

Dividing through by \(V_{i}\) and after rearrangement, we obtain

\(D_{i}=\frac{r_{P_{i}}}{P_{i}-P_{i-1}}\) (E14.4.5)

Eqs. (E14.4.5) and (E14.4.2) are similar, in that the rate of formation divided by the difference in concentrations between the effluent and the feed equals the dilution rate. Therefore, if the rate functions are known, one can solve the problem easily (as we have been doing). In this problem, we do not know the rate function(s), however, we are given the kinetic data: concentration change with time.
Therefore, to solve the problem, we must find or relate the rate function(s) to the kinetic data. In batch operations, mass balance yields

\(r_{j}=\frac{d C_{j}}{d t}\) (E14.4.6)

In Eqs. (E14.4.2) and (14.4.5), we need the rate divided by the concentration difference. Rather than finding the rates and then do the conversion, we divide Eq. (E14.4.6) also by the concentration difference,

\(\frac{r_{j}}{C_{j}-C_{j F}}=\frac{1}{C_{j}-C_{j F}} \frac{d C_{j}}{d t}=\frac{d \ln \left(C_{j}-C_{j F}\right)}{d t}\) (E14.4.7)

Therefore, the left-hand sides of Eq. (E14.4.2) and (E14.4.5) are the same as the slope of the tangent on the batch experimental data having a value of \(C_{ j }-C_{ jF }\) if the batch data were plotted on a semilog paper of \(C_{ j }-C_{ jF }\) versus t. Thus, we can avoid determining the rates from the batch data. This has been illustrated in Section 5.11.

1. Two chemostats of equal volume in series. The dilution rates are: \(D_{1}=D_{2}=120 / 800 h ^{-1}=0.15 h ^{-1}\).
Fig. E14.4.2 shows the semilog plots for the cell concentration and secondary-metabolite concentration change with time from the batch data (replotted from Fig. E14.4.1). Since the feed to the first chemostat is sterile, therefore, we can determine the outlet concentration directly from this graph by plotting a line with a slope of 0.15 \(h ^{-1}\)/ln10 that is tangent to the batchgrowth data (for concentration higher than the initial culture). From this graph, we can read off the effluent conditions as \(X_{1}=6.66 g / L \text { and } P_{1}=0.73 g / L\) (at the tangent point).

The solution above is an estimate. The batch data did not give the substrate concentration, nor we know the stoichiometry.
This is a drawback. We know that for the secondary metabolite to be produced, there must be (1) large quantity of cell biomass, and (2) one of more of the key growth-required substrate concentration is low. Therefore, the first reactor is not really producing secondary metabolite as the substrates are all in excess and there is no cell biomass in the feed. Therefore, the value of \(P_{1} \) is correct as it reads off from Fig. E14.4.2.

For the second reactor, there is high cell biomass already in the feed, and the key substrate for cell growth is limiting or exhausted. Therefore, growth in the second reactor is suppressed, while secondary metabolite production is amplified. Therefore, we use Eq. (E14.4.5) to find the concentration for the secondary metabolite coming out of the second reactor with only minimum further cell biomass growth.

To determine the secondary metabolite concentration in the second chemostat, we replot the batch kinetic data for P − \(P_{1}\) on a semilog plot as shown in Fig. E14.4.3. One can follow the same procedure to plot the tangent on the product curve. We obtain from the tangent point at which \(P_{2}=P_{1}+\left(P_{2}-P_{1}\right)\) = 0.073 + 0.435 g/L = 0.508 g/L. The production rate of the secondary
metabolite is then Q \(P_{2}\) = 51.96 g/h.

2. Based on the discussions in part (a), a single chemostat would produce biomass rather than secondary metabolite. Fig. E14.4.4 shows a repetition of Fig. E14.4.2 with a different slope for the tangent on the biomass curve. D = 120/1600 \(h ^{-1}\) = 0.075 \(h ^{-1}\).
The pinch point is X = 7.3 g/L, and the corresponding secondary metabolite P = 0.113 g/L. Therefore, the production rate of the secondary metabolite is then Q P = 13.56 g/h.

Therefore, two stages of chemostats increase the secondary metabolite concentration from 0.113 g/L to 0.508 g/L, or the
secondary metabolite production from 13.56 to 51.96 g/h.
Looking at Example 14.4, we learned that a continuous culture problem can be solved based on batch data without explicitly obtaining rates. By examining the mass balance equations, one can devise simple procedures for solving continuous culture problems without obtaining the growth or reaction rates explicitly.
