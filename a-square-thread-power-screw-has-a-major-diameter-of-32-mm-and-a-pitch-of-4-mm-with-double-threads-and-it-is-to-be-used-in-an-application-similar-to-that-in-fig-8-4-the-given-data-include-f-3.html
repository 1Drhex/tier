Guest Question.

A square-thread power screw has a major diameter of 32 mm and a pitch of 4 mm with double threads, and it is to be used in an application similar to that in Fig. 8–4.
The given data include \(f = f_{c}= 0.08, d_{c}\) = 40 mm, and F = 6.4 kN per screw.
(a) Find the thread depth, thread width, pitch diameter, minor diameter, and lead.
(b) Find the torque required to raise and lower the load.
(c) Find the efficiency during lifting the load.
(d) Find the body stresses, torsional and compressive.
(e) Find the bearing stress.
( f ) Find the thread stresses bending at the root, shear at the root, and von Mises stress and maximum shear stress at the same location.

Admin
Solution

(a) From Fig. 8–3a the thread depth and width are the same and equal to half the pitch, or 2 mm. Also

\(d_{m} = d − p/2 = 32 − 4/2 = 30 mm\)

\(d_{r} = d − p = 32 − 4 = 28 mm\)
\(l = np = 2(4) = 8 mm\)

(b) Using Eqs. (8–1) and (8–6), the torque required to turn the screw against the load is

\(T_{R} =\frac{Fd_{m}}{2} \left( \frac {l + π f d_{m}}{πd_{m} − f l}\right)\) (8–1)

\(T_{c} =\frac {F f_{c}d_{c}}{2}\) (8–6)

\(T_{R} =\frac{Fd_{m}}{2} \left( \frac {l + π f d_{m}}{πd_{m} − f l}\right)+\frac {F f_{c}d_{c}}{2}\)

\(=\frac {6.4(30)}{2} \left[ \frac {8 + π(0.08)(30)}{π(30) − 0.08(8)} \right] +\frac {6.4(0.08)40}{2}\)

= 15.94 + 10.24 = 26.18 N · m

Using Eqs. (8–2) and (8–6), we find the load-lowering torque is

\(T_{L} =\frac{Fd_{m}}{2} \left( \frac { π f d_{m}-l}{πd_{m} + f l}\right)\) (8–2)

\(T_{L} =\frac{Fd_{m}}{2} \left( \frac { π f d_{m}-l}{πd_{m} + f l}\right) + \frac {F f_{c}d_{c}}{2}\)

\(=\frac {6.4(30)}{2} \left[ \frac { π(0.08)(30)-8}{π(30) − 0.08(8)} \right] +\frac {6.4(0.08)40}{2}\)

= −0.466 + 10.24 = 9.77 N · m

The minus sign in the first term indicates that the screw alone is not self-locking and would rotate under the action of the load except for the fact that the collar friction is present and must be overcome, too. Thus the torque required to rotate the screw “with” the load is less than is necessary to overcome collar friction alone.
(c) The overall efficiency in raising the load is

\(e =\frac {Fl}{2πT_{R}} =\frac {6.4(8)}{2π(26.18)} = 0.311\)

(d) The body shear stress τ due to torsional moment \(T_{R}\) at the outside of the screw body is

\(τ =\frac {16T_{R}}{πd^{3}_{r}} =\frac {16(26.18)(10^{3})}{π(28^{3}) }= 6.07 MPa\)

The axial nominal normal stress σ is

\(σ = − \frac {4F}{πd^{2}_{r}} = − \frac {4(6.4)10^{3}}{π(28^{2})} = −10.39 MPa\)

(e) The bearing stress \(σ_{B}\) is, with one thread carrying 0.38F,

\(σ_{B} = − \frac {2(0.38F)}{πd_{m}(1)p} = − \frac {2(0.38)(6.4)10^{3}}{π(30)(1)(4)} = −12.9 MPa\)

( f ) The thread-root bending stress \(σ_{b}\) with one thread carrying 0.38F is

\(σ_{b} = \frac {6(0.38F)}{πd_{r}(1)p} = \frac {6(0.38)(6.4)10^{3}}{π(28)(1)(4)} = 41.5 MPa\)

The transverse shear at the extreme of the root cross section due to bending is zero. However, there is a circumferential shear stress at the extreme of the root cross section of the thread as shown in part (d) of 6.07 MPa. The three-dimensional stresses, after Fig. 8–8, noting the y coordinate is into the page, are

\(σ_{x} = 41.5 MPa τ_{xy} = 0\)
\(σ_{y} = 0 τ_{yz} = 6.07 MPa\)
\(σ_{z} = −10.39 MPa τ_{zx} = 0\)

Equation (5–14) of Sec. 5–5 can be written as

\(σ^′ =\frac {1}{ \sqrt{2}} \left[(σ_{x} − σ_{y})^{2 }+ (σ_{y} − σ_{z})^{2} + (σ_{z} − σ_{x} )^{2} + 6(τ^{2}_{xy} + τ^{2}_{yz} + τ^{2}_{zx} ) \right]^{1/2}\) (5-14)

\(\sigma^{\prime}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\left\{(41.5-0)^{2}+[0-(-10.39)]^{2}+(-10.39-41.5)^{2}+6(6.07)^{2}\right\}^{1 / 2}\)

= 48.7 MPa

Alternatively, you can determine the principal stresses and then use Eq.(5–12) to find the von Mises stress. This would prove helpful in evaluating \(τ_{max}\) as well. The principal stresses can be found from Eq. (3–15); however, sketch the stress element and note that there are no shear stresses on the x face. This means that \(σ_{x}\) is a principal stress. The remaining stresses can be transformed by using the plane stress equation, Eq. (3–13). Thus, the remaining principal stresses are

\(σ^′ = \left[ \frac {(σ_{1} − σ_{2})^{2} + (σ_{2} − σ_{3)}^{2} + (σ_{3} − σ_{1})^{2}}{2}\right]^{1/2}\) (5–12)

\(σ_{1}, σ_{2} = \frac {σ_{x} + σ_{y}}{2} ± \sqrt{\left( \frac {σ_{x} – σ_{y}}{2} \right)^{2} +τ^{2}_{xy} }\) (3-13)

\(σ^{3} − (σ_{x} + σ_{y} + σ_{z})σ^{2} + \left( σ_{x}σ_{y} +σ_{x}σ_{z} + σ_{y}σ_{z} − τ^{2}_{xy} − τ^{2}_{yz} − τ^{2}_{zx}\right) σ − \left( σ_{x}σ_{y}σ_{z} + 2τ_{xy}τ_{yz}τ_{zx} −σ_{x} τ^{2}_{yz} − σ_{y}τ^{2}_{zx} − σ_{z}τ^{2}_{xy} \right)=0 \) (3-15)

\(\frac {−10.39}{2} ± \sqrt{ \left(\frac {−10.39}{2} \right)^{2}+6.07^{2} }= 2.79,−13.18 MPa\)

Ordering the principal stresses gives \(σ_{1}, σ_{2}, σ_{3}\) = 41.5, 2.79, −13.18 MPa. Substituting these into Eq. (5–12) yields

\(σ^′ = \left\{\frac {[41.5 − 2.79]^{2} + [2.79 − (−13.18)]^{2} + [−13.18 − 41.5]^{2}}{2} \right\}^{1/2}\)

= 48.7 MPa

The maximum shear stress is given by Eq. (3–16), where \(τ_{max} = τ_{1/3} \), giving

\(τ_{1/2 }=\frac {σ_{1} − σ_{2}}{2} τ_{2/3} =\frac {σ_{2} − σ_{3}}{2} τ_{1/3} =\frac {σ_{1} − σ_{3}}{2}\) (3–16)

\(τ_{max} =\frac {σ_{1} − σ_{3}}{2} =\frac {41.5 − (−13.18)}{2} =27.3 MPa \)
