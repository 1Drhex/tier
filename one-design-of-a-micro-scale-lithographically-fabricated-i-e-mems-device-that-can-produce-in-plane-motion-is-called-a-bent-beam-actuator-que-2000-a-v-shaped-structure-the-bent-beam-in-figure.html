Guest Question.

One design of a micro-scale, lithographically fabricated \(\left\lgroup \text{i.e., MEMS}\right\rgroup\) device that can produce in-plane motion is called a bent-beam actuator \(\left\lgroup \text{Que}\left\lgroup2000\right\rgroup \right\rgroup\). A V-shaped structure \(\left\lgroup \text{the bent-beam in Figure 1}\right\rgroup\) is suspended between two anchors. The anchors are thermally staked to the underlying substrate and therefore keep the ends of the bent-beam at room temperature \(\left\lgroup T_{a}=20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\right\rgroup\). An elevated voltage is applied to one pillar and the other is grounded. The voltage difference causes current \(\left\lgroup I\right\rgroup\) to flow through the bent-beam structure. The temperature of the bent-beam rises as a result of ohmic heating and the thermally induced expansion causes the apex of the bent-beam to move outwards. The result is a voltage-controlled actuator capable of producing in-plane motion.

a) Is it appropriate to treat the bent-beam as an extended surface?

b) Develop an analytical solution that can predict the temperature of one leg of the bent-beam as a function of position along the beam, s.

c) The thermally induced elongation of a differential segment of the beam \(\left\lgroup \text {of length ds}\right\rgroup\) is given by:

\(d L=C T E\left(T-T_{a}\right) d s\)

Estimate the displacement of the apex of the beam. Plot the displacement as a function of voltage.

Admin
Solution

The anchors of the bent-beam actuator are placed \(L_{a}=1\) mm apart and the beam structure has a cross-section of W = 10 μm by th = 5 μm. The slope of the beams \(\left\lgroup \text{with respect to a line connecting the two pillars}\right\rgroup\) is θ = 0.5 rad, as shown in Figure 2. The bent-beam material has conductivity k = 80 W/m-K, electrical resistivity \(\rho_{e}=1 \times 10^{-5}\) ohm-m and coefficient of thermal expansion \(CTE=3.5 \times 10^{-6}K^{-1}\). You may neglect radiation from the beam and assume all of the heat that is generated is convected to the surrounding air at temperature \(T_{\infty}=20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}\) with average heat transfer coefficient \(\bar h=100W/m^{2}-K\) or transferred conductively to the pillars \(\left\lgroup \text{which remains at}~T_{a}=20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\right\rgroup\). The actuator is activated with I = 10 mA of current.

th=5 [micron]∗convert(micron,m) “thickness of beam”
I=0.010 [Amp] “current”
theta=0.5 [rad] “slope of beam”
T_a=converttemp(C,K,20 [C]) “temperature of pillars”
T_infinity=converttemp(C,K,20 [C]) “temperature of air”
h_bar=100 [W/mˆ2-K] “heat transfer coefficient”
k=80 [W/m-K] “conductivity”
rho_e=1e-5 [ohm-m] “electrical resistivity”
CTE=3.5e-6 [1/K] “coefficient of thermal expansion”

The extended surface approximation requires that the 3-D temperature distribution within the bent-beam be approximated as 1-D; that is, temperature gradients within the beam that are perpendicular to the surface will be ignored so that the temperature may be approximated as a function only of s, the coordinate that follows the beam \(\left\lgroup \text{see Figure 2}\right\rgroup\). The resistance that must be neglected in order to use the extended surface approximation is conduction in the lateral direction \(\left\lgroup R_{cond,lat}\right\rgroup\). The extended surface approximation is justified provided that the lateral conduction resistance is small relative to the resistance that is being considered, convection from the outer surface \(\left\lgroup R_{conv}\right\rgroup\). The Biot number is therefore:

\(B i=\frac{R_{\text {cond,lat }}}{R_{\text {conv }}}\)

The heat transfer will take the shortest path to the surface and therefore it is appropriate to use the smallest lateral dimension \(\left\lgroup th/2\right\rgroup\) to compute the lateral conduction resistance.

\(B i=\left(\frac{t h}{2 k W L}\right)\left(\frac{\bar{h} W L}{1}\right)=\frac{t h \bar{h}}{2 k}\)

where L is the length of the beam from pillar to apex \(\left\lgroup \text{see Figure 2}\right\rgroup\).

Bi=th∗h_bar/(2∗k) “Biot number”

The Biot number is small \(\left\lgroup 3 \times 10^{-6}\right\rgroup\) and therefore the extended surface approximation is justified.

b) The general solution for an extended surface with a constant cross-sectional area and spatially uniform generation is derived in Section 1.7.2:

\(T=C_{1} \exp (m s)+C_{2} \exp (-m s)+T_{\infty}+\frac{\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime} A_{c}}{\bar{h}_{p e r}}\) (1)

For the bent-beam actuator, the perimeter \(\left\lgroup per\right\rgroup\), cross-sectional area \(\left\lgroup A_{c}\right\rgroup\), and fin parameter \(\left\lgroup m\right\rgroup\) are

\(\text { per }=2(W+t h)\)

 

\(A_{c}=W t h\)

 

\(m=\sqrt{\frac{\bar{h} \text { per }}{k A_{c}}}\)
per=2∗(W+th) “perimeter”
A_c=W∗th “area”
m=sqrt(h_bar∗per/(k∗A_c)) “fin parameter”

The volumetric generation, \(\dot g^{'''}\) is related to ohmic heating. The electrical resistance of the bent-beam structure \(\left\lgroup R_{e}\right\rgroup\) is:

\(R_{e}=\frac{\rho_{e} 2 L}{A_{c}}\)

where

\(L=\frac{L_{a}}{2 \cos (\theta)}\)

The volumetric rate of electrical dissipation is the ratio of ohmic dissipation to the volume of the structure:

\(\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime}=\frac{I^{2} R_{e}}{2 L A_{c}}\)
L=L_a/(2∗cos(theta)) “length of half-beam”
R_e=rho_e∗L∗2/A_c “resistance of beam structure”
g”’ _dot=Iˆ2∗R_e/(2∗L∗A_c) “volumetric generation”

The constants \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\) in Eq. \(\left\lgroup 1\right\rgroup\) are determined using the boundary conditions. The temperature of the beam where it meets the pillar is specified:

\(T_{s=0}=T_{a}\) (2)

Substituting Eq. \(\left\lgroup 1\right\rgroup\) into Eq. \(\left\lgroup 2\right\rgroup\) leads to:

\(C_{1}+C_{2}+T_{\infty}+\frac{\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime} A_{c}}{\bar h~per}=T_{a}\) (3)

A half-symmetry model of the bent-beam actuator considers only one leg. Because both legs of the bent-beam see identical conditions, there is nothing to drive heat from one leg to the other and therefore there will be no conduction through the end of the leg \(\left\lgroup \text {at s = L}\right\rgroup\):

\(\dot{q}_{s=L}=-\left.k \frac{d T}{d s}\right|_{s=L}=0\)

or

\(\left.\frac{d T}{d s}\right|_{s=L}=0\) (4)

Substituting Eq. \(\left\lgroup 1\right\rgroup\) into Eq. \(\left\lgroup 4\right\rgroup\) leads to:

\(C_{1}~m~\exp \left\lgroup m~L\right\rgroup- C_{2}~m ~\exp \left\lgroup -m~L\right\rgroup=0\) (5)

Equations \(\left\lgroup 3\right\rgroup\) and \(\left\lgroup 5\right\rgroup\) can be entered in EES and used to determine \(C_{1}\) and \(C_{2}\).

T_infinity+C_1+C_2+g”’_dot∗A_c/(h_bar∗per)=T_a “from boundary condition at s=0”
C_1∗m∗exp(m∗L)-C_2∗m∗exp(-m∗L)=0 “from boundary condition at s=L”

A variable s_bar is defined as s/L so that s_bar = 0 corresponds to the pillar and s_bar = 1 to the apex. The variable s_bar is defined for convenience, so that it is easy to generate a parametric table in which s is varied from 0 to L even if parameters such as θ and \(L_{a}\) change.

s_bar=s/L “non-dimensional position”

The temperature is evaluated using Eq. \(\left\lgroup 1\right\rgroup\).

T=T_infinity+C_1∗exp(m∗s)+C_2∗exp(-m∗s)+g”’_dot∗A_c/(h_bar∗per) “temperature”
T_C=converttemp(K,C,T) “in C”

A parametric table is generated that includes the variables s_bar and T_C. The temperature distribution through one leg of the beam is shown in Figure 3.

c) The total elongation of the beam \(\left\lgroup \Delta L\right\rgroup\) is obtained by integrating the differential elongation along the beam:

\(\Delta L=\int_{0}^{L} C T E\left(T-T_{a}\right) d s\) (6)

Substituting the solution for the temperature distribution, Eq. \(\left\lgroup 1\right\rgroup\) into Eq. \(\left\lgroup 6\right\rgroup\) leads to:

\(\Delta L=\int_{0}^{L} \operatorname{CTE}\left(C_{1} \exp \left\lgroup m~s\right\rgroup+C_{2} \exp \left\lgroup -m~ s\right\rgroup+T_{\infty}+\frac{\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime} A_{c}}{\bar h~per}-T_{a}\right) d s \)

Evaluating the integral:

\(\Delta L=C T E\left[\left(T_{\infty}-T_{a}+\frac{\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime} A_{c}}{\bar{h} \text { per }}\right) s+\frac{C_{1}}{m} \exp \left\lgroup m~s\right\rgroup-\frac{C_{2}}{m} \exp \left\lgroup -m~s\right\rgroup\right]_{0}^{L}\)

Substituting the integration limits:

\(\Delta L=C T~E\left\{\left(T_{\infty}-T_{a}+\frac{\dot{g}^{\prime \prime \prime} A_{c}}{\bar{h}~ per}\right) L+\frac{C_{1}}{m}[\exp \left\lgroup mL\right\rgroup -1]-\frac{C_{2}}{m}[\exp \left\lgroup -mL\right\rgroup -1]\right\}\)
DELTAL=CTE∗((T_infinity-T_a+g”’_dot∗A_c/(h_bar∗per))∗L+C_1∗(exp(m∗L)-1)/m-C_2∗(exp(-m∗L)-1)/m)
“displacement of beam”

Assuming that the joint associated with the apex does not provide a torque on either leg of the beam, the displacement of the apex can be estimated using trigonometry \(\left\lgroup\text {Figure 4}\right\rgroup\).

The original position of the apex \(\left\lgroup y\right\rgroup\) is given by:

\(y=\sqrt{L^{2}-\left(\frac{L_{a}}{2}\right)^{2}}\)

therefore, the motion of the apex \(\left\lgroup \Delta y\right\rgroup\) is:

\(\Delta y=\sqrt{(L+\Delta L)^{2}-\left(\frac{L_{a}}{2}\right)^{2}}-\sqrt{L^{2}-\left(\frac{L_{a}}{2}\right)^{2}}\)
DELTAy=sqrt((L+DELTAL)ˆ2-(L_a/2)ˆ2)-sqrt(Lˆ2-(L_a/2)ˆ2) “displacement of apex”
DELTAy_micron=DELTAy∗convert(m,micron) “in μm”

The voltage across the beam \(\left\lgroup V \right\rgroup\) is:

\(V=I R_{e}\)
V=I∗R_e “voltage”

Figure 5 illustrates the actuator displacement as a function of voltage. This plot was generated using a parametric table including the variables DELTAy_ micron and V; the variable I was commented out in order to make the table.
