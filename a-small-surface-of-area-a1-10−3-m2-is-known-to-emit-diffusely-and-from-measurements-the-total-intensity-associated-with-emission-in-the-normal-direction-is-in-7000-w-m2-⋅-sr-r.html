Guest Question.

A small surface of area \(A_{1}=10^{-3} m ^{2}\) is known to emit diffusely, and from measurements the total intensity associated with emission in the normal direction is \(I_{n}=7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot sr\) Radiation emitted from the surface is intercepted by three other surfaces of area \(A_{2}=A_{3}=A_{4}=10^{-3} m ^{2}\), which are 0.5 m from \(A_{1}\) and are oriented as shown. What is the intensity associated with emission in each of the three directions? What are the solid angles subtended by the three surfaces when viewed from \(A_{1}\) ? What is the rate at which radiation emitted by \(A_{1}\) is intercepted by the three surfaces?

Admin
Solution

Known: Normal intensity of diffuse emitter of area \(A_{1}\) and orientation of three surfaces relative to \(A_{1}\) .

Find:
1. Intensity of emission in each of the three directions.
2. Solid angles subtended by the three surfaces.
3. Rate at which radiation is intercepted by the three surfaces.

Assumptions:
1. Surface \(A_{1}\) emits diffusely.

2. \(A_{1}\), \(A_{2}\), \(A_{3}\), and \(A_{4}\) may be approximated as differential surfaces, \(\left(A_{j} / r_{j}^{2}\right) \ll 1\)

Analysis:
1. From the definition of a diffuse emitter, we know that the intensity of the emitted radiation
is independent of direction. Hence

\(I=7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot sr\)

for each of the three directions.

2. Treating \(A_{2}, A_{3}, \text { and } A_{4}\) as differential surface areas, the solid angles may be computed from Equation 12.7

\(d \omega \equiv \frac{d A_{n}}{r^{2}}\) (12.7)

where \(d A_{n}\) is the projection of the surface normal to the direction of the radiation. Since surfaces \(A_{3} \text { and } A_{4}\) are normal to the direction of radiation, the solid angles subtended by these surfaces can be directly found from this equation as

\(\omega_{3-1}=\omega_{4-1}=\frac{A_{3}}{r^{2}}=\frac{10^{-3} m ^{2}}{(0.5 m )^{2}}=4.00 \times 10^{-3} sr\)

 

Since surface \(A_{2}\) is not normal to the direction of radiation, we use \(d A_{n, 2}=d A_{2} \cos \theta_{2}\) , where \(\theta_{2}\) is the angle between the surface normal and the direction of the radiation. Thus

\(\omega_{2-1}=\frac{A_{2} \cos \theta_{2}}{r^{2}}=\frac{10^{-3} m ^{2} \times \cos 30^{\circ}}{(0.5 m )^{2}}=3.46 \times 10^{-3} sr\)

 

3. Approximating \(A_{1}\) as a differential surface, the rate at which radiation is intercepted by each of the three surfaces may be found from Equation 12.11, which, for the total radiation, may be expressed as

\(d q_{\lambda}=I_{\lambda, e}(\lambda, \theta, \phi) d A_{1} \cos \theta d \omega\) (12.11)

 

\(q_{1-j}=I \times A_{1} \cos \theta_{1} \times \omega_{j-1}\)

 

where \(\theta_{1}\) is the angle between the normal to surface 1 and the direction of the radiation. Hence

\(q_{1-2}=7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot \operatorname{sr}\left(10^{-3} m ^{2} \times \cos 60^{\circ}\right) 3.46 \times 10^{-3} sr\) \(=12.1 \times 10^{-3} W\)

 

\(q_{1-3}=7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot \operatorname{sr}\left(10^{-3} m ^{2} \times \cos 0^{\circ}\right) 4.00 \times 10^{-3} sr\) \(=28.0 \times 10^{-3} W\)

 

\(q_{1-4}=7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot \operatorname{sr}\left(10^{-3} m ^{2} \times \cos 45^{\circ}\right) 4.00 \times 10^{-3} sr\) \(=19.8 \times 10^{-3} W\)

 

Comments:
1. Note the different values of \(\theta_{1}\) for the emitting surface and the values of \( \theta_{2}, \theta_{3}, \text { and } \theta_{4}\) for the receiving surfaces.

2. If the surfaces were not small relative to the square of the separation distance, the solid angles and radiation heat transfer rates would have to be obtained by integrating Equations 12.8 and 12.11, respectively, over the appropriate surface areas.

\(d \omega=\sin \theta d \theta d \phi\) (12.8)

 

3. Any spectral component of the radiation rate can also be obtained using these procedures, if the spectral intensity \(I_{\lambda}\) is known.

4. Even though the intensity of the emitted radiation is independent of direction, the rate at which radiation is intercepted by the three surfaces differs significantly due to differences in the solid angles and projected areas. For example, consider moving surface \(A_{4}\) to various \(\theta_{1}\) positions, keeping \(A_{4}\) normal to the direction of radiation and \(r_{4}\) constant at 0.5 m as shown in Figure (a) below. Under these conditions \(\omega_{4-1}=4.00 \times 10^{-3}\) sr is constant and

\(q_{1-4}=I \times A_{1} \omega_{4-1} \cos \theta_{1}=\left[7000 W / m ^{2} \cdot sr \times 10^{-3} m ^{2} \times 4.00 \times 10^{-3} sr \right] \times \cos \theta_{1}\)

 

The energy that is emitted from \(A_{1}\) and subsequently intercepted by \(A_{4}\) is plotted in Figure (b) above. Consistent with our intuition, the energy intercepted by surface \(A_{4}\) is maximum at \(\theta_{1}=0^{\circ}\left(q_{1-4}=28.0 \times 10^{-3} W \right)\) since an observer on \(A_{4}\) would see the largest projected area of \(A_{1}\) Also consistent with our intuition, the energy intercepted by \(A_{4}\) is zero at \(\theta_{1}=\pm 90^{\circ}\) even though the intensity of the radiation emitted from \(A_{1}\) is independent of \(\theta \text { . At } \theta_{1}=\pm 90^{\circ}\) , an observer on \(A_{4}\) would be unable to see \(A_{1}\) and hence would intercept none of the energy emitted from \(A_{1}\) . Many real surfaces emit radiation in a manner that is approximately diffuse.
