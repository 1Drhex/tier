Guest Question.

Evaluate the terminal velocity of a 7.5-mm-diameter glass sphere falling freely through (a) air at 300 K, (b) water at 300 K, and (c) glycerin at 300 K. The density of glass is \( 2250 kg/m^{3} \).

Admin
Solution

The terminal (steady-state) velocity of a falling body is reached when the force due to fluid drag matches the body’s weight. In this case the weight of the glass sphere can be expressed as

\( \rho_{s} \frac{\pi d^{3}}{6} g \)

 

The fluid drag force is given by

\( C_{D} \frac{\rho_{f} \upsilon_{\infty }^{2}}{2} \frac{\pi d^{3}}{4} \)

 

and a force balance yields

\( C_{D} \upsilon_{\infty }^{2} = \frac{4 }{3} \frac{\rho_{x}}{\rho_{f}} dg \)

 

The drag coefficient, \( C_{D} \), is plotted as a function of Reynolds number, \( R e_{d} \), in Figure. As \( C_{D} \) is a function of \( \upsilon_{\infty} \), we are unable to solve explicitly for unless \( R e_{d} < 1\), which would permit the use of Stokes’ law in expressing \( C_{D},\) A trial-anderror solution is, thus, required. The conditions to be satisfied are our force balance expression and the graphical relation between \( C_{D},\) and \( R e_{D}\)in Figure.

For air at 300 K

\( \upsilon = 1.569 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s \)

 

\( \rho = 1.177 kg/ m^{3} \)

 

\( R e_{d} = \frac{d \upsilon_{\infty }}{\upsilon } = \frac{\left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \upsilon_{\infty }}{1.569 \times 10^{-5} m^{2}/s} = 478.0 \upsilon_{\infty} \)

 

Inserting known values into our force balance expression, we have

 

\( C_{D} \upsilon_{\infty}^{2} = \left ( \frac{4}{3} \right ) \frac{2250 kg/m^{3}}{1.177 kg/m^{3}} \left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \left ( 9.81 m/s^{2} \right ) = 187.5 m^{2}/s^{2} \)

 

Normally the trial-and-error procedure to achieve a solution would be straightforward. In this case, however, the shape of the \( C_{D} \) vs. \( R e_{d} \) curve, given in Figure 12.4, poses a bit of a problem. Specifically, the value of \( C_{D} \) remains nearly uniform— that is, \( 0.4 < C_{D} < 0.5 \) —over a range in \( R e_{d} \) between \( 500 < R e_{d} < 10^{5} : \) over three orders of magnitude!
In such a specific case, we will assume \( C_{D} \cong 0.4, \) and solve equation B for \( \upsilon_{\infty} : \)

 

\( \upsilon_{\infty} = \left [ \frac{187.5}{0.4} m^{2}/s^{2} \right ]^{1/2} = 21.65 m/s \)

 

Equation B then yields

\( R e_{d} = \left ( 478.0 \right ) \left ( 21.65 \right ) = 1.035 \times 10^{4} \)

 

These results are compatible with Figure, although the absolute accuracy is obviously not great.
Finally, for air, we determine the terminal velocity to be, approximately,

\( \upsilon_{\infty} \cong 21.6 m/s \)

 

For water at 300 K,

\( \upsilon = 0.879 \times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s \)

 

\( \rho = 996.1 kg/ m^{3} \)

 

\( R e_{d} = \frac{\left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \upsilon_{\infty }}{ 0.879 \times 10^{-6} m^{2}/s} = 8530 \upsilon_{\infty} \)

 

\( C_{D} \upsilon_{\infty}^{2} = \left ( \frac{4}{3} \right ) \frac{2250 kg/m^{3}}{996 kg/m^{3}} \left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \left ( 9.81 m/s^{2} \right ) = 0.2216 m^{2}/s^{2} \)

 

As in part (a), we will initially assume \( C_{D} \cong 0.4, \) and achieve the result that

\( \upsilon_{\infty} = 0.744 m/s \)

 

\( R e_{d} = 6350 \)

 

These results, again, satisfy Figure. Thus, in water

\( \upsilon_{\infty } = 0.744 m/s \)

 

Finally, for glycerin at 300 K,

\( \upsilon = 7.08 \times 10^{-4} m^{2}/s \)

 

\( \rho = 1260 kg/ m^{3} \)

 

\( R e_{d} = \frac{\left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \upsilon_{\infty }}{ 7.08 \times 10^{-4} m^{2}/s} = 10.59 \upsilon_{\infty} \)

 

\( C_{D} \upsilon_{\infty}^{2} = \left ( \frac{4}{3} \right ) \frac{2250 kg/m^{3}}{ 1260 kg/m^{3}} \left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) \left ( 9.81 m/s^{2} \right ) = 0.1752 m^{2}/s^{2} \)

 

In this case we suspect the Reynolds number will be quite small. As an initial guess we will assume Stokes’ law applies, thus \( C_{D} = 24 / Re. \) Solving for \( \upsilon_{\infty} \) for this case, we have

 

\( C_{D} \upsilon_{\infty}^{2} = \frac{24 \upsilon}{d \upsilon_{\infty }} \upsilon_{\infty}^{2} = 0.1752 m^{2}/s^{2} \)

 

\( \upsilon_{\infty} = \frac{\left (0.1752 m^{2}/s^{2}\right )\left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right ) }{ 24 \left ( 7.08 \times 10^{-4} m^{2} /s^{2} \right )} = 0.0773 m/s \)

 

To validate the use of Stokes’ law, we check the value of Reynolds number and get

 

\( R e_{d} = \frac{\left ( 7.5 \times 10^{-3} m \right )\left ( 0.0773 m/s \right ) }{ 7.08 \times 10^{-4} m^{2} /s} = 0.819 \)

 

which is in the allowable range. The terminal velocity in glycerin thus

\( \upsilon_{\infty} = 0.0773 m/s \)
