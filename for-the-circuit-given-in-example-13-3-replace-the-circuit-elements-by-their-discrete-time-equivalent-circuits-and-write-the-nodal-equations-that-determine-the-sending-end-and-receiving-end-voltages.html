Discrete-time equivalent circuit, single-phase lossless line transients, computer solution

For the circuit given in , replace the circuit elements by their discrete-time equivalent circuits and write the nodal equations that determine the sending-end and receiving-end voltages. Then, using a digital computer, compute the sending-end and receiving-end voltages for 0 ≦ t ≦ 9 ms. For numerical calculations, assume \(E=100V,Z_{c} =400\Omega ,C_{R}=5\mu F,\tau =1.0 \) ms, and Δt = 0.1 ms.

Admin
Solution

The discrete model is shown in Figure 13.18, where \(\upsilon _{k} \left(t\right) \) represents the sending-end voltage υ(0, t) and \(\upsilon _{m} \left(t\right) \) represents the receiving-end voltage υ(l, t). Also, the sending-end voltage source \(e _{G} \left(t\right) \) in series with \(Z _{G} \left(t\right) \) is converted to an equivalent current source in parallel with \(Z _{G} \left(t\right) \) Writing nodal equations for this circuit,

\(\left [ \begin{matrix}\left(\frac{1}{400}+\frac{1}{400} \right) & 0 \\ 0 &\left(\frac{1}{400}+\frac{1}{10} \right) \end{matrix} \right ] \left [ \begin{matrix} \upsilon _{k}\left(t\right) \\\upsilon _{m}\left(t\right) \end{matrix} \right ] =\left [ \begin{matrix}\frac{1}{4} -I_{k}\left(t-1.0\right) \\ I_{m}\left(t-1.0\right)+I_{C} \left(t-0.1\right) \end{matrix} \right ]\)

Solving,

\(\upsilon _{k} \left(t\right)=200\left[\frac{1}{4}-I_{k} \left(t-1.0\right) \right]\) (a)

\(\upsilon _{m} \left(t\right)=9.75610\left[I_{m} \left(t-1.0\right)+I_{C} \left(t-0.1\right) \right]\) (b)

The current sources in these equations are, from (13.4.9), (13.4.10), and (13.4.18), with the argument \(\left(t=\tau \right) \) replaced by t,

\(I_{m} \left(t-\tau \right)=I_{k} \left(t-2\tau \right) +\frac{2}{Z_{c} }\upsilon _{k} \left(t-\tau \right) \) (13.4.9)

\(I_{k} \left(t-\tau \right)=I_{m} \left(t-2\tau \right) -\frac{2}{Z_{c} }\upsilon _{m} \left(t-\tau \right)\) (13.4.10)

\(I_{C} \left(t-\Delta t\right)=i\left(t-\Delta t\right) +\frac{\upsilon \left(t-\Delta t\right) }{\left(\Delta t/2C\right) } =-I_{C} \left(t-2\Delta t\right)+\frac{\upsilon \left(t-\Delta t\right) }{\left(\Delta t/4C\right) } \) (13.4.18)

\(I_{m} \left(t\right)=I_{k}\left(t-1.0\right)+\frac{2}{400} \upsilon _{k}\left(t\right)\) (c)

\(I_{k} \left(t\right)=I_{m}\left(t-1.0\right)-\frac{2}{400}\upsilon _{m}\left(t\right)\) (d)

\(I_{C} \left(t\right)=-I_{C}\left(t-0.1\right)+\frac{1}{5}\upsilon _{m}\left(t\right)\) (e)

Equations (a) through (e) above are in a form suitable for digital computer solution. A scheme for iteratively computing \(\upsilon _{k} \) and \(\upsilon _{m} \) is as follows, starting at t = 0:

1. Compute \(\upsilon _{k} (t)\) and \(\upsilon _{m} (t)\) from equations (a) and (b).

2. Compute \(I _{m} (t)\), \(I _{k} (t)\) and \(I _{C} (t)\) from equations (c), (d), and (e). Store \(I _{m} (t)\) and \(I _{k} (t)\).

3. Change t to (t + Δt) = (t 1 0.1) and return to (1) above.

Note that since the transmission line and capacitor are unenergized for time t less than zero, the current sources \(I _{m} ( )\), \(I _{k} ( )\), and \(I _{C} ( )\) are zero whenever their arguments ( ) are negative. Note also from equations (a) through (e) that it is necessary
to store the past ten values of \(I _{m} ( )\) and \(I _{k} ( )\) .
