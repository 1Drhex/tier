Guest Question.

Replacement Analysis under an Infinite Planning Horizon

The Advanced Electrical Insulator Company is considering replacing a broken inspection machine which has been used to test the mechanical strength of electrical insulators with a newer and more efficient one.

  • Option 1: If repaired, the old machine can be used for another six years. However, the firm can sell it now to another firm in the industry for $5,000. If the machine is kept, it will require an immediate $1,200 overhaul to restore it to operable condition. The overhaul will neither extend the service life originally estimated nor increase the value of the inspection machine. The operating costs are estimated at $2,000 during the first year, and these are expected to increase by $1,500 per year thereafter. Future market values are expected to decline by 25% each year over the previous year’s value.
  • Option 2: The new machine costs $10,000 and will have operating costs of $2,200 in the first year, increasing by 20% per year thereafter. The expected salvage value is $6,000 after one year and will decline 15% each year. The physical life of the new machine would last eight years.

The company requires a rate of return of 15% before tax. Find the economic life for each option, and determine when the defender should be replaced.

Admin
Solution

Given: Financial data for both defender and challenger and i = 15%.
Find: (a) Economic service lives for both defender and challenger, (b) when to replace the defender.

(a) Economic service life.

  • Defender: If the company retains the inspection machine, it is in effect deciding to overhaul the machine and invest the machine’s current market value in that alternative. The opportunity cost of the machine is $5,000. Because an overhaul costing $1,200 is also needed to make the machine operational, the total initial cash outlay in the machine is $5,000 + $1,200 = $6,200. Other market (salvage) value and O&M cost data for the defender are summarized in Table 14.2. We can calculate the annual equivalent costs if the defender is to be kept for one year, two years, three years, and so forth, with the equation

\(AEC\left(15\%\right) _{N}\) = ($5,000 + $1,200)(A/P, 15%, N) + $2,000
+$1,500(A/G, 15%, N)

\(-\$5,000 \left(1 – 0.25\right) ^{N} \left(A/F, 15\%, N\right)\)

\(AEC\left(15\%\right) _{N} = \overbrace{\left[\$6,200-\$5,000\left(1 – 0.25\right) ^{N} \right] \left(A/P, 15\%, N\right) }^{CR\left(15\%\right) }\)

\(+0.15\left\{\$5,000\left(1 – 0.25\right) ^{N} \right\}\)

\(\underbrace{-\$2,000 + \$1,500\left(A/G, 15\%, N\right) }_{OC\left(15\%\right) }\)

For example, with N = 4 years, the annual cash flows looks like the one in Figure 14.9, and its annual equivalent costs are \(AEC\left(15\%\right) _{4}\) = $5,844. As shown in Table 14.2, we get the lowest AEC value when N = 2 years. Thus, the defender’s economic life is two years. Using the notation we defined in the procedure, we have

\(N_{D }*\) = 2 years

\(AEC_{D}*\) = $5,203

  • Challenger: The economic life of the challenger can be determined with the same procedure we used in . The equation is

\(AEC\left(15\%\right) _{N} = \$10,000 \left(A/P, 15\%, N\right)\)

\(+\$2,200 \left(P/A_{1},20\% , 15\% , N \right) \left(A/P, 15\%, N\right)\)

\(-\$6,000 \left(1 – 0.15\right) ^{N-1} \left(A/F, 15\%, N\right) .\)

We can also develop an Excel worksheet similar to the defender, as shown in Table 14.3, to determine the economic service life. Clearly, the economic life of the challenger is four years; that is,

\(N_{C }*\) = 4 years

Thus,

\(AEC_{C }*\) = $5,625

(b) Should the defender be replaced now? Since \(AEC_{D }* = \$5,203 \lt AEC_{C }* = \$5,625\) , the defender should not be replaced for now. If there are no technological advances in the next few years, the defender should be used for at least \(N_{D }*\) = 2 more years. However, it is not necessarily best to replace the defender right at the high point of its economic life, either. It depends on what type of challenger would be available in the future.

(c) When should the defender be replaced? If we need to find the answer to this question today, we have to calculate the cost of keeping and using the defender for the third year from today. That is, what is the cost of not selling the defender at the end of year 2, using it for the third year, and replacing it at the end of year 3?

The following cash flows are related to this question:

  • Opportunity cost at the end of year 2: equal to the market value then, or $2,813.
  • Operating cost for the third year: $5,000.
  • Salvage value of the defender at the end of year 3: $2,109.

Figure 14.10 represents these cash flows. The cost of using the defender for one more year from the end of its economic life is

$2,813 \(\times\) 1.15 + $5,000 – $2,109 = $6,126

Since \(AEC_{C }*\) = $5,625, it is more expensive to keep the defender for the third year than to replace it with the challenger. Accordingly, we conclude that we should replace the defender at the end of year 2. If this one-year cost is still smaller than \(AEC_{C }*\) , we need to calculate the cost of using the defender for the fourth year and then compare that cost with the \(AEC_{C }*\) of the challenger.
