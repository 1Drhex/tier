Guest Question.

For the system of Figure 6.33, the system parameters are given as \(m_{1}\) = \(m_{2}\) = 1 kg, \(c_{1}\) = \(c_{2}\) = 1 N-s/m, and \(k_{1}\) = \(k_{2}\) = 1 N/m. The equation of motion in matrixvector form is then

 

\(\begin{array}{r}{\left[\begin{array}{ll}1 & 0 \\0 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{c}\ddot{x}_{1} \\\ddot{x}_{2}\end{array}\right\}+\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}\dot{x}_{1} \\\dot{x}_{2}\end{array}\right\}} \\+\left[\begin{array}{cc}2 & -1 \\-1 & 1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}x_{1} \\x_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0\end{array}\right\} .\end{array}\)

Solve for the displacement response using the direct method.

Admin
Solution

Solution: Assuming the following solution,

 

\(\left\{\begin{array}{l}x_{1} \\x_{2}\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}A \\B\end{array}\right\} e^{\lambda t}\)

 

and taking the first and second derivatives and substituting these into the equation of motion, we find

 

\(\begin{aligned}\lambda^{2}[M]\left\{\begin{array}{c}A \\B\end{array}\right\} e^{\lambda t}+\lambda[C]\left\{\begin{array}{c}A \\B\end{array}\right\} e^{\lambda t} & \\+[K]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A \\B\end{array}\right\} e^{\lambda t} &=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0\end{array}\right\} \\\left[\lambda^{2}[M]+\lambda[C]+[K]\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A \\B\end{array}\right\} &=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0\end{array}\right\} .\end{aligned}\)

 

Substituting the mass, damping, and stiffness matrices leads to the characteristic matrix equation,

 

\(\left[\begin{array}{cc}\lambda^{2}+2 \lambda+2 & -(\lambda+1) \\-(\lambda+1) & \lambda^{2}+\lambda+1\end{array}\right]\left\{\begin{array}{l}A \\B\end{array}\right\}=\left\{\begin{array}{l}0 \\0\end{array}\right\}\) (6.73)

 

The determinant of the characteristic matrix is set equal to zero, leading to the characteristic equation,

 

\(\lambda^{4}+3 \lambda^{3}+4 \lambda^{2}+2 \lambda+1=0\)

 

that must be solved numerically.34 The roots are

 

\(\begin{aligned}&\lambda_{11}=-0.191+0.588 i \\&\lambda_{12}=-0.191-0.588 i \\&\lambda_{21}=-1.309+0.951 i \\&\lambda_{22}=-1.309-0.951 i\end{aligned}\)

 

Let us go through the steps used in proceeding from the value of \(\lambda_{11}\) to that of \(r_{11}\). The same steps would need to be followed for each root. Substituting \(\lambda_{11}\) into the first equation of matrix Equation 6.73 we find

 

\(\left(\lambda_{11}^{2}+2 \lambda_{11}+2\right) A_{11}-\left(\lambda_{11}+1\right) B_{11}=0\)

 

or

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{A_{11}}{B_{11}}=r_{11} &=\frac{\lambda_{11}+1}{\lambda_{11}^{2}+2 \lambda_{11}+2} \\&=\frac{0.809+0.588 i}{1.309+0.951 i},\end{aligned}\)

 

where \(A_{11}\), \(B_{11}\) are A, B, respectively, for \(\lambda_{11}\). Multiplying the top and bottom of Equation 6.74 by the complex conjugate of its denominator results in

 

\(r_{11}=0.618\)

 

where a small imaginary part may remain because of round-off error. The eigenvector in this case (not needed in this solution) can be written as \(\{0.6181\}^{T}\) . Similarly, we have

 

\(\begin{aligned}\frac{A_{22}}{B_{22}} &=r_{22}=\frac{\lambda_{22}+1}{\lambda_{22}^{2}+2 \lambda_{22}+2} \\&=-1.618 .\end{aligned}\)

 

The eigenvector in this case is \(\{0.6181\}^{T}\). Figure 6.34 is a sketch of these non-complex modes. There are two distinct eigenvalues.35

What does it mean physically that the imaginary parts drop out completely? It means that there is no phasing in the modes, and the system behaves as though there
is no damping. Since we do have damping, the damping matrix is linearly related to the \(M\) and \(K\) matrices. This is a special type of damping known as proportional damping. We will learn more about it in Section 6.6.3.

Using the notation introduced earlier,

\(a_{1}=1.309, \quad \omega_{d 1}=0.951 ; \quad a_{2}=0.191, \quad \omega_{d 2}=0.588,\)

and

\(\alpha_{1}=-1.618, \quad \beta_{1}=0 ; \quad \alpha_{2}=0.618, \quad \beta_{2}=0\)

 

These parameters are substituted into the general Equations 6.69 and 6.70. In order to ensure that the procedure is clear, \(\beta_{1}\) and \(\beta_{2}\) will be retained even though they are equal to zero,

 

\(\begin{aligned}x_{1}(t)=& e^{-a_{1} t}\left(r_{1} C_{1} \cos \omega_{c 1} t+r_{1}^{\prime} C_{2} \sin \omega_{c 1} t\right) \\&+e^{-a_{2} t}\left(r_{2} C_{3} \cos \omega_{d 2} t+r_{2}^{\prime} C_{4} \sin \omega_{d 2} t\right)\end{aligned}\) (6.69)

 

\(\begin{aligned}x_{2}(t)=& e^{-a_{1} t}\left(C_{1} \cos \omega_{d 1} t+C_{2} \sin \omega_{d 1} t\right) \\&+e^{-a_{2} t}\left(C_{3} \cos \omega_{d 2} t+C_{4} \sin \omega_{d 2} t\right)\end{aligned}\) (6.70)

 

\(\begin{aligned}x_{1}(t) &=e^{-1.509 t}\left(\left[C_{1}(-1.618)+C_{2}\left(\beta_{1}\right)\right] \cos 0.951 t\right.\\&\left.+\left[-C_{1}\left(\beta_{1}\right)+C_{2}(-1.618)\right] \sin 0.951 t\right) \\&+e^{-0.191 t}\left(\left[C_{3}(0.618)+C_{4}\left(\beta_{2}\right)\right] \cos 0.588 t\right.\\&\left.+\left[-C_{3}\left(\beta_{2}\right)+C_{4}(0.618)\right] \sin 0.588 t\right) .\end{aligned}\)

 

Simplifying and repeating similarly for \(x_{2}(t)\), we obtain the equations governing the motion of the two masses,

 

\(\begin{aligned}x_{1}(t) &=-1.618 e^{-1.309 t}\left(C_{1} \cos 0.951 t+C_{2} \sin 0.951 t\right) \\&+0.618 e^{-0.191 t}\left(C_{3} \cos 0.588 t+C_{4} \sin 0.588 t\right)\end{aligned}\) (6.75)

 

\(\begin{aligned}x_{2}(t) &=e^{-1.309 t}\left(C_{1} \cos 0.951 t+C_{2} \sin 0.951 t\right) \\&+e^{-0.191 t}\left(C_{3} \cos 0.588 t+C_{4} \sin 0.588 t\right) .\end{aligned}\) (6.76)

 

The constants of integration, \(C_{i},\) are obtained by satisfying the initial conditions. For initial conditions \(x_{1}(0)=\) 1, \(x_{2}(0)=0, \dot{x}_{1}(0)=0, \text { and } \dot{x}_{2}(0)=0,\) we find \(C_{1}\)

= -0.447 and \(C_{2}\)= 0.447. The initial velocities can be used to find the other two constants. The responses of Equations 6.75 and 6.76 are plotted in Figure 6.35 for these initial conditions.

It turns out in this example that the imaginary parts of the modal vectors equal zero. In the next example,

a more general damping case is considered where the ratios \(r_{i j}\) are fully complex conjugate.
