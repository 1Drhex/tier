Guest Question.

Before turning our attention to fully three-dimensional systems, let’s apply the results of Section 11.1 to our favorite one-dimensional system, the simple harmonic oscillator. We suppose that a particle with charge q is in a harmonic oscillator potential and that we perturb the system by applying a constant electric field E that points in the positive x direction. Since there is a constant force qE exerted on the particle, the additional contribution to the potential energy, which is the extra work that we must do to displace the particle by a distance x from the origin, is -q|E|x. Therefore the Hamiltonian of the system is given by

\(\hat{H}=\frac{\hat{P} _{x} ^{2} }{2m}+\frac{1}{2}mω^{2}\hat{x} ^{2}-q|E|\hat{x}\)

We break up the Hamiltonian into two parts:

\( \hat{H} _{0}=\frac{\hat{P} _{x} ^{2} }{2m}+\frac{1}{2}mω^{2}\hat{x} ^{2}\)

 

\( \hat{H} _{1}=-q|E|\hat{x}\)

Determine the corrections to the unperturbed energies through second order\(.^{3}\)

Solution

The energy eigenvalues of the unperturbed Hamiltonian are given by

\( E_{n} ^{(0)}=(n+\frac{1}{2} )\hslash ω\)

 

There are a number of easy ways to evaluate the first-order shift in the energy. Using

\( \hat{x}=\sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2mω} }(\hat{a} +\hat{a} ^{\dagger } )\)

 

from Chapter 7, we find that

\(E^{(1)} _{n}=\left\langle n|\hat{H} _{1}|n \right\rangle\)

 

\( =-q|E|\sqrt{\frac{\hslash }{2mω} }\left\langle n|(\hat{a} +\hat{a} ^{\dagger } )|n\right\rangle =0 \)

 

It is also instructive to evaluate the expectation value in position space:

\(E^{(1)} _{n}=-q|E|\left\langle n|\hat{x}|n \right\rangle=-q|E|\int_{-\infty }^{+\infty }{dx|\left\langle x|n\right\rangle |^{2}x }=0 \)

 

Here the integral vanishes because the energy eigenfunction 〈x|n〉 is an even or odd function (see Section 7.1.0) and hence \(|\left\langle x|n\right\rangle |^{2}\) is always even and \(x|\left\langle x|n\right\rangle |^{2}\) always odd. Since the first-order shift in the energy is just proportional to the expectation value of the electric dipole moment operator \(q\hat{x}\) , the vanishing of this first-order correction can be ascribed to the absence of a permanent electric dipole moment that can interact with the applied electric field.

The second-order shift in the energy is given by

\(E^{(2)} _{n}=\sum\limits_{k\neq n}{\frac{|\left\langle k|\hat{H} _{1}|n \right\rangle |^{2} }{(n+\frac{1}{2} )\hbar ω-(k+\frac{1}{2} )\hbar ω} }\)

Since

\(\left\langle k|\hat{H} _{1}|n \right\rangle=q|E|\sqrt{\frac{\hbar }{2mω} }\left(\sqrt{n+1} \left\langle k|n+1\right\rangle +\sqrt{n}\left\langle k|n-1\right\rangle \right)\)

 

there are contributions to the sum for the second-order shift in the energy when k = n + L and k = n – 1. Thus

\(E_{n} ^{(2)}=\frac{q^{2}|E|^{2}\hbar }{2mω}\left(\frac{n+1}{-\hbar ω}+\frac{n}{\hbar ω} \right)= -\frac{q^{2}|E|^{2} }{2mω^{2} }\)

What is the physical source of this nonvanishing higher order contribution?
On average, the electric field causes the particle to be displaced from the origin, inducing a dipole moment proportional to the magnitude of the electric field. This induced dipole moment itself interacts with the applied field, giving a contribution to the energy that is proportional to the magnitude of the field squared.

In this particular problem we have a simple way1o confirm the results. We really didn’t need to use perturbation theory for the full Hamiltonian because we can determine the eigenvalues and eigenstates exactly by “completing the square”:

\(\hat{H}=\frac{\hat{P} ^{2} _{x} }{2m}+\frac{1}{2}mω^{2}\hat{x} ^{2}-q|E|\hat{x}\)

 

\(=\frac{\hat{P} ^{2} _{x} }{2m}+\frac{1}{2}mω^{2}\left(\hat{x}-\frac{q|E|}{mω^{2} } \right)^{2}-\frac{q^{2}|E|^{2} }{2mω^{2} }\)

Figure 11.3 shows a graph of the potential energy for this Hamiltonian. It is a pure harmonic oscillator potential, just shifted along the x axis by \(q|E|/mω^{2}\) and shifted down in energy by \(q^{2}|E|^{2}/2mω^{2}\). In order to solve formally the quantum mechanical energy eigenvalue equation, we define the shifted position operator

\(\hat{x} _{s}=\hat{x}-\frac{q|E}{mω^{2} }\)

which satisfies the usual commutation relation \(\left[\hat{x} _{s},\hat{P} _{x} \right]=i\hbar\) with the momentum operator \(\hat{P} _{x}\)· Thus the exact eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian

\(\hat{H}=\frac{\hat{P}^{2} _{x} }{2m}+\frac{1}{2}mw^{2}\hat{x} ^{2} _{s}-\frac{q^{2}|E|^{2} }{2mω^{2} }\)

are given by

\(E_{n}=\left(n+\frac{1}{2} \right)\hbar ω-\frac{q^{2}|E|^{2} }{2mω^{2} }\)

in agreement with our earlier perturbative results. The exact eigenstates are those of the usual ha1monic oscillator, only shifted in position by \(q|E|/mω^{2}\)

These eigenstales can thus be expressed in terms of the translation operator by

\(|\psi 〉=\hat{T}(q|E|/mw^{2} )|n〉=x^{-iq|E|\hat{P} _{x}/mw^{2}\hbar } |n〉 \)

You can verify that this exact expression for the eigenstates agrees with the perturbative expansion (11.23)

\(|\psi _{n}〉=|\varphi ^{(0)} _{n}〉+\lambda \sum\limits_{k\neq n}{|\varphi ^{(0)} _{k}〉\frac{\left\langle \varphi ^{(0)} _{k}|\hat{H} _{1}|\varphi ^{(0)} _{n} \right\rangle }{E^{(0)} _{n}-E^{(0)} _{k} } } + O(λ^{2})\)

(see Problem 11.2).
