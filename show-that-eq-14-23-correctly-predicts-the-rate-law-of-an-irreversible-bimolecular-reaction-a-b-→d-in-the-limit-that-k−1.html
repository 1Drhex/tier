Guest Question.

ROBLEM Show that Eq. 14.23correctly predicts the rate law of an irreversible bimolecular reaction A + B →D in the limit that k−1 << k1.

\(\begin{aligned}&\frac{1}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}\left[\ln \left(\frac{[\mathrm{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}+[\mathrm{A}]^{\prime}}{[\mathrm{A}]^{\prime}}\right)-\ln \left(\frac{[\mathrm{B}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}}\right)\right]=k_{1}\left(1-\frac{k_{-1}}{k_{-1}+k_{2}}\right) t \\\\&\frac{1}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}\left[\ln \left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\left([\mathrm{~B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}+[\mathrm{A}]^{\prime}\right)}{[\mathrm{B}]_{0}[\mathrm{~A}]^{\prime}}\right)\right]=k_{1}\left(1-\frac{k_{-1}}{k_{-1}+k_{2}}\right) t \\\\&{[\mathrm{~A}]^{\prime}=[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\left(1-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right)\left\{\exp \left[\left([\mathrm{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\right)\left(1-\frac{k_{-1}}{k_{-1}+k_{2}}\right) k_{1} t\right]-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right\}^{-1} .}\end{aligned}\)
Admin
Solution

To solve this problem, we need to (i) find the correct rate law for the reaction \( A+B \xrightarrow[]{k_{1} } D\) and (ii) show that Eq. 14.23 yields the same result. (i) he concentrations of A and B are connected by the extent of reaction x, where

\(x= [A]_{0} – [A]= [B]_{0} – [B]\),

which allows us to write [B] in terms of\([A]:[B] = [A]+ [B]_{0} – [A]_{0}\equiv [A]+a\) where we have set \(a= [B]_{0} – [A]_{0}\) . the rate law we need to integrate is

\(\frac{d[A]}{dt} = – k_{1} [A][B]= – k_{1} [A]([A]+ a)\)

We can separate terms in the two variables, [A] and t, and then integrate to solve the differential equation:

\(\begin{gathered} \frac{d[\mathrm{~A}]}{[\mathrm{A}]([\mathrm{A}]+a)}=-k_{1} d t \\\\\int_{[A]_{0}}^{[\mathrm{A}]} \frac{d[\mathrm{~A}]^{\prime}}{[\mathrm{A}]^{\prime}\left([\mathrm{A}]^{\prime}+a\right)}=-\int_{0}^{t} k_{1} d t^{\prime}\end{gathered}\)

From a table of integrals, we learn that there is an analytical solution to the integral on the let side. The general formula is

\( \int{\frac{dx}{(x+a)(x+b)} }= \frac{1}{b-a}\ln \frac{x+a}{x+b} +C \)

Applying this formula with x = [A] and b = 0 gives the following:

\(-\frac{1}{a} \ln \frac{\acute{[A]}+ a}{\acute{[A]}} \mid ^{[A]}_{[A]_{0}} = – k_{1} t\)

 

\(-\frac{1}{a} \left\{ \ln \frac{[A]+ a}{[A]} – \ln \frac{[A]_{0} + a}{[A]_{0} } \right\} = – k_{1} t\)

the remaining work is to solve for [A], which we will summarize here in a few steps:

\(\begin{aligned}&-\frac{1}{a}\left\{\ln \left[\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]+a}{[\mathrm{~A}]}\right)\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}+a}\right)\right]\right\}=-k_{1} t \quad \ln x-\ln y=\ln (x / y) \\\\&\ln \left[\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]+a}{[\mathrm{~A}]}\right)\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}+a}\right)\right]=a k_{1} t \quad \text { multiply by }-a \\\\&\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]+a}{[\mathrm{~A}]}\right)\left(\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}+a}\right)=e^{i \mathrm{i} k_{1} t} & e^{x} \text { of both sides }\end{aligned}\)

 

\(\begin{aligned}[\mathrm{A}] &=\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0} a}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}+a}\left(e^{a k_{1} t}-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~A}]_{0}+a}\right)^{-1} \text { rearrange } \\\\ &=[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\left(1-\frac{\lceil\mathrm{A}\rceil_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right)\left\{\exp \left[-\left([\mathrm{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\right) k_{1} t\right]-\frac{\lceil\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right\}^{-1} \cdot \quad a=[\mathrm{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\end{aligned}\)

(ii) Setting $k_{-1}=k_{2}=0$, equation $14.23$ simplifies to

\(\begin{aligned}\lim _{k-1} \mathbb{k}_{1}[\mathrm{~A}]^{\prime} &=[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\left(1-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right)\left\{\exp \left[-\left([\mathrm{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\right)(1-0) k_{1} t\right]-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right\}^{-1} \\\\&=[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\left(1-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right)\left\{\exp \left[-\left([\mathbf{B}]_{0}-[\mathrm{A}]_{0}\right) k_{1} t\right]-\frac{[\mathrm{A}]_{0}}{[\mathrm{~B}]_{0}}\right\}^{-1}\end{aligned}\)

 

So the steady-state approximation, in the limit that the reverse reaction in the first step is negligible, predicts the correct time-dependent concentrations for the reactants.
