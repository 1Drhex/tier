Guest Question.

Perform a complete dynamic force analysis of the four-bar linkage in the posture shown in Fig. 12.10, using the principle of superposition. The input crank is operating at constant angular velocity ω2 = 60 rad/s ccw, and the applied force is \(F _{C}=40 \hat{ i } lb\). The weights of links 3 and 4 are w3 = 7.13 lb and w4 = 3.42 lb, respectively, and the mass moments of inertia of links 2, 3, and 4 are \(I_{G_{2}}=0.25 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2} I_{G_{3}}=0.625 in \cdot lbs ^{2}, \text { and } I_{G_{4}}= 0.037 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\), respectively.

ASSUMPTIONS

Gravity and friction effects are negligible.

Admin
Solution

STEP 1. All of the details of a complete kinematic analysis of the linkage are not included here, but the resulting acceleration polygon is shown in Fig. 12.10a (the numeric results are shown on the acceleration polygon). From the methods of Chap. 4, the angular accelerations of links 3 and 4 are

\(\alpha_{3}=148 rad / s ^{2} ccw \quad \text { and } \quad \alpha_{4}=604 rad / s ^{2} cw\)

STEP 2. Since the center of mass of link 2 is located at bearing O2, the force analysis is concerned primarily with links 3 and 4. Free-body diagrams of links 4 and 3 are shown in Figs. 12.11 and 12.12, respectively. Note that these diagrams are arranged in pseudo-equation form to emphasize the concept of superposition. Thus, in each figure, the forces in (a) plus those in (b) and (c) produce the results shown in (d). The action and reaction forces are also correlated; for example, \(F _{34}^{\prime}\) in Fig. 12.11a is equal to \(– F_{43}^{\prime}\) in Fig. 12.12a and so on. The following analysis is not difficult, but it is complex; therefore, it is important to read it slowly and to examine the figures carefully, detail by detail.

We start by considering the effects of the mass of link 4 alone, while ignoring the masses of links 2 and 3. Using Figs. 12.11a and 12.12a, we make the following calculations:

\(I_{G_{4}} \alpha_{4}=\left(0.037 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)\left(604 rad / s ^{2}\right)=22.3 in \cdot lb cw\)

 

\(m_{4} A_{G_{4}}=\frac{3.42 lb }{32.2 ft / s ^{2}}\left(349 ft / s ^{2}\right)=37.1 lb\)

 

\(h_{4}=\frac{I_{G_{4}} \alpha_{4}}{m_{4} A_{G_{4}}}=\frac{22.3 in \cdot lb }{37.1 lb }=0.602 in\)

 

Now, we position the inertia force \(-m_{4} A_{G_{4}}=37.1 lb\) on the free-body diagram of link 4 opposite in direction to \(A _{G_{4}}\) and offset from G4 by the distance h4 = 0.602 in. The direction of the offset is to the right of G4 so that the inertia force \(-m_{4} A_{G_{4}}\) produces a counterclockwise inertia moment \(-I_{G_{4}} \alpha_{4} \text { about } G_{4} \text {, }\) opposite to the clockwise sense of α4.

The line of action of \(F _{34}^{\prime}\) is along link 3 for part (a) of our superposition approach, since link 3 is a two-force member in this step. The intersection of the inertia force \(-m_{4} A _{G_{4}}\) and the line of action of \(F _{34}^{\prime}\) gives the point of concurrency and establishes the line of action of \(F _{14}^{\prime}\). The force polygon for part (a) for link 4 can be constructed and the magnitudes of \(F _{34}^{\prime}\) and \(F _{14}^{\prime}\) can be determined. These values are shown in the legend to Fig. 12.11.

Note that the forces \(F _{43}^{\prime}\) and \(F _{23}^{\prime}\) are now known, as shown in Fig. 12.12a.

STEP 3. Now, we consider the effects of the mass of link 3 alone. Using Fig. 12.12b, we make the following calculations:

\(I_{G_{3}} \alpha_{3}=\left(0.625 in \cdot lb \cdot s ^{2}\right)\left(148 rad / s ^{2}\right)=92.5 in \cdot lb ccw\)

 

\(m_{3} A_{G_{3}}=\frac{7.13 lb }{32.2 ft / s ^{2}}\left(758 ft / s ^{2}\right)=168 lb\)

 

\(h_{3}=\frac{I_{G_{3}} \alpha_{3}}{m_{3} A_{G_{3}}}=\frac{92.5 in \cdot lb }{168 lb }=0.550 in\)

 

Now, we position the inertia force \(-m_{3} A_{G_{3}}=168 lb\) on the free-body diagram of link 3 opposite in direction to \(A _{G_{3}}\) and offset from G3 by the distance h3 = 0.550 in. The direction of the offset is to the left of G3 so that the inertia force \(-m_{3} A _{G_{3}}\) produces a clockwise inertia moment \(-I_{G_{3}} \alpha_{3} \text { about } G_{3}\), opposite to the counterclockwise sense of α3. The line of action of \(F _{43}^{\prime \prime}\) 43 is along link 4 for part (b) of our superposition approach, since link 4 is a two-force member in this step. The intersection of the line of action of \(F _{43}^{\prime \prime}\) and the inertia force \(-m_{3} A _{G_{3}}\) gives the point of concurrency. Thus, the line of action of \(F _{23}^{\prime \prime}\) is known, and the force polygon for link 3 can be constructed. The resulting magnitudes of \(F _{43}^{\prime \prime}\) and \(F _{23}^{\prime \prime}\) are included in the legend of Fig. 12.12. Note that the forces \(F _{34}^{\prime \prime}\) and \(F _{14}^{\prime \prime}\) are now known, as shown in Fig. 12.11b. The results of the static force analysis with the applied force Fc = 40 lb as the specified loading, but with no inertia forces, as shown in Fig. 12.11c.

STEP 4. Recognizing that link 3 is again a two-force member, the force polygon in Fig. 12.11c determines the values of the forces acting on link 4. From these forces, the magnitudes and directions of the forces \(F _{43}^{\prime \prime}\) and \(F _{23}^{\prime \prime}\) acting on link 3 can be determined, as shown in Fig. 12.12c.

STEP 5. Now we perform the superposition of the results obtained in Steps 2, 3, and 4; this is accomplished by the vector additions shown in part (d) of each figure. The analysis is completed by taking the resultant force F23 from Fig. 12.12d and applying the negative value, F32, to link 2. This is shown in the free-body diagram of link 2, Fig. 12.13. There are no inertia force or inertia moment on link 2 because the center of mass is stationary and the angular velocity is constant. The offset distance, h2, is measured as h2 = 1.56 in Therefore, the external torque applied to link 2 is

\(T_{12}=h_{2} F_{32}=(1.56 in )(145 lb )=226 in \cdot lb cw\)

Note that this torque is opposite in sense to the direction of rotation of link 2; this is not true for the entire cycle of operation, but it can occur at particular crank angles. The torque must sometimes be in the reverse direction to maintain the constant angular velocity of link 2.
