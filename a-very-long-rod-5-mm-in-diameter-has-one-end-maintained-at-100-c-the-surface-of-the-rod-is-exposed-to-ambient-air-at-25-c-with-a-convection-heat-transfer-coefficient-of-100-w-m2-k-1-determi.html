Guest Question.

A very long rod 5 mm in diameter has one end maintained at 100 C. The surface of the rod is exposed to ambient air at 25 °C with a convection heat transfer coefficient of 100 W/m² .K.

1. Determine the temperature distributions along rods constructed from pure copper, 2024 aluminum alloy, and type AISI 316 stainless steel. What are the corresponding heat losses from the rods?

2. Estimate how long the rods must be for the assumption of infinite length to yield an accurate estimate of the heat loss.

Admin
Solution

Known: A long circular rod exposed to ambient air.
Find:
1. Temperature distribution and heat loss when rod is fabricated from copper, an aluminum alloy, or stainless steel.
2. How long rods must be to assume infinite length.

Assumptions:
1. Steady-state conditions.
2. One-dimensional conduction along the rod.
3. Constant properties.
4. Negligible radiation exchange with surroundings.
5. Uniform heat transfer coefficient.
6. Infinitely long rod.

Properties: copper \( [T = (T_b + T_∞)/2 = 62.5°C ≈ 335 K]: k = 398 W/m. K. \) Table A.1, 2024 aluminum (335 K): k = 180 W/m. K. Table A.1, stainless steel, AISI 316 (335 K): k = 14 W/m. K.

Analysis:
1. Subject to the assumption of an infinitely long fin, the temperature distributions are determined from Equation 3.84, \( \frac{θ}{θ_b} = e^{-mx} \) which may be expressed as

\( T = T_∞ +(T_b – T_∞) e^{-mx} \)

where \( m = (hP/kA_c)^{1/2} = (4h/kD)^{1/2}. \) Substituting for h and D, as well as for the thermal conductivities of copper, the aluminum alloy, and the stainless steel, respectively, the values of m are 14.2, 21.2, and 75.6 \( m^{-1} \) . The temperature distributions may then be computed and plotted as follows:

 

From these distributions, it is evident that there is little additional heat transfer associated with extending the length of the rod much beyond 50, 200, and 300 mm, respectively, for the stainless steel, the aluminum alloy, and the copper.

From Equation 3.85, the heat loss is

\( q_f = \sqrt{hPkA_c}θ_b \)

Hence for copper,

\( q_f = \left[100 W/m^2 . K \times \pi \times 0.005 m \times 398 W/m .K \times \frac{\pi }{4}(0.005 m )^2 \right] ^{1/2} (100-25) ^\circ C = 8.3 W \)

Similarly, for the aluminum alloy and stainless steel, respectively, the heat rates are \( q_f = 5.6 W \text{ and } 1.6 W. \)

2. Since there is no heat loss from the tip of an infinitely long rod, an estimate of the validity of this approximation may be made by comparing Equations 3.81 \( q_f =\sqrt{hPkA_c} θ_b \tanh mL \) and 3.85 \( q_f = \sqrt{hPkA_c}θ_b \). To a satisfactory approximation, the expressions provide equivalent results if tanh mL ≥ 0.99 or mL ≥ 2.65. Hence a rod may be assumed to be infinitely long if

\(L \geq L_{\infty} \equiv \frac{2.65}{m}=2.65\left(\frac{k A_{c}}{h P}\right)^{1 / 2}\)

For copper,

\(L_{\infty}=2.65\left[\frac{398 \mathrm{~W} / \mathrm{m} \cdot \mathrm{K} \times(\pi / 4)(0.005 \mathrm{~m})^{2}}{100 \mathrm{~W} / \mathrm{m}^{2} \cdot \mathrm{K} \times \pi(0.005 \mathrm{~m})}\right]^{1 / 2}=0.19 \mathrm{~m} \)

Results for the aluminum alloy and stainless steel are \( L_∞ = 0.13 m \text{ and } L= 0.04 m, \) respectively.

Comments:
1. The foregoing results suggest that the fin heat transfer rate may accurately be predicted from the infinite fin approximation if mL ≥ 2.65. However, if the infinite fin approximation is to accurately predict the temperature distribution T(x), a larger value of mL would be required. This value may be inferred from Equation 3.84 and the requirement that the tip temperature be very close to the fluid temperature. Hence, if we require that θ(L)/θ_b = exp(- mL) < 0.01, it follows that mL > 4.6, in which case L_∞ ≈ 0.33, 0.23, and 0.07 m for the copper, aluminum alloy, and stainless steel, respectively. These results are consistent with the distributions plotted in part 1.
