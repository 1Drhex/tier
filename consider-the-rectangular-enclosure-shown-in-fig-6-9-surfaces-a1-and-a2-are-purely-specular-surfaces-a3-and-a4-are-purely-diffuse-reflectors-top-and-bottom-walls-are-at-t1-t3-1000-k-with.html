Guest Question.

Consider the rectangular enclosure shown in Fig. 6-9. Surfaces \(A_{1}\) and \(A_{2}\) are purely specular, surfaces \(A_{3}\) and \(A_{4}\) are purely diffuse reflectors. Top and bottom walls are at \(T_{1} = T_{3}=1000K\), with \(ε1 = 1 - \rho ^{s}_{1} = ε3 = 1 -\rho ^{d}_{3} = 0.3\) ; the side walls are at \(T_{2} =T_{4}=600K\) with emittances \(ε2 = 1- \rho ^{s}_{2} = ε4 = 1 - \rho ^{d}_{4} = 0.8\). Determine the net radiative heat flux for each surface.

Admin
Solution

Looking at Fig. 6-9a, one sees that \(F^{s}_{1-1} =F^{s}_{2-2}=0\) , while all other specular view factors are nonzero.
Again, with \(H^{s}_{oi}=0\) , we have from equation (6.22) \(\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{\left[\delta _{ij} – (1- \rho ^{s}_{j}) F^{s}_{i-j} \right] } E_{bj}= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{\left\lgroup\frac{\delta _{ij}}{\epsilon _{j} } -\frac{ \rho ^{d}_{j}}{\epsilon _{j}} F^{s}_{i-j} \right\rgroup } q_{j} + H^{s}_{oi} , i = 1,2,…N\)

\(E_{b1}- \epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{1-2}E_{b2} – F ^{s}_{1-3}E_{b3} – F ^{s}_{1-4}E_{b4} = \frac{q_{1} }{\epsilon _{1} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{3} }-1 \right) F ^{s}_{1-3} q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{4} }-1 \right) F ^{s}_{1-4} q_{4},\)

\(-\epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{2-1}E_{b1}+E_{b2} – F ^{s}_{2-3}E_{b3} – F ^{s}_{2-4}E_{b4}= \frac{q_{2} }{\epsilon _{2} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{3} }-1 \right) F ^{s}_{2-3} q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{4} }-1 \right) F ^{s}_{2-4} q_{4},\)

\(-\epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{3-1}E_{b1} – \epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{3-2}E_{b2}+(1- F ^{s}_{3-3})E_{b3}- F ^{s}_{3-4}E_{b4}= \left[\frac{1}\epsilon _{3} – \left\lgroup\frac{1}{\epsilon _{3} }-1\right\rgroup F^{s}_{3-3}\right]q_{3} – \left\lgroup\frac{1}{\epsilon _{4} }-1\right\rgroup F ^{s}_{3-4}q_{4}\)

\(-\epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{4-1}E_{b1} – \epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{4-2}E_{b2} – F ^{s}_{4-3}E_{b3}+ (1- F ^{s}_{4-4})E_{b4} = -\left\lgroup\frac{1}{\epsilon _{3} }-1\right\rgroup F ^{s}_{4-3}q_{3} + \left[\frac{1}\epsilon _{4} – \left\lgroup\frac{1}{\epsilon _{4} }-1\right\rgroup F^{s}_{4-4}\right]q_{4}\)

Again, we have only two simultaneous equations to solve for the two (diffuse) heat fluxes \(q_{3}\) and \(q_{4}\): The first two equations are explicit expressions for \(q_{1}\) and \(q_{2}\), respectively (once \(q_{3}\) and \(q_{4}\) have been determined). Checking the various images in Fig. 6-9a, we find that the specular view factors for surface \(A_{1}\) are

\(F ^{s}_{1-1}=0 ,\)
\(F ^{s}_{1-2}= F _{1-2} ,\)
\(F ^{s}_{1-3}= F _{1-3} + +\rho ^{s}_{2}F_{1(2)-3} ,\)
\(F ^{s}_{1-4}= F _{1-4} + +\rho ^{s}_{2}F_{1(2)-4} .\)

Checking the summation rule, we find

\((1-\rho ^{s}_{1} ) F ^{s}_{1-1} + (1-\rho ^{s}_{2}) F ^{s}_{1-2}+ F ^{s}_{1-3} + F ^{s}_{1-4} =0 + F _{1-2} +F _{1-3} +F _{1-4} -\rho ^{s}_{2} (F _{1-2} – F _{1(2)-3} – F _{1(2)-4})=1\)

or

\(F _{1(2)-3} – F _{1(2)-4}=F_{1-2}\).

Indeed, by checking Fig. 6-9a, we find

\(F _{1(2)-3} – F _{1(2)-4}=F_{1(2)-(3+4)}= F _{1(2)-2} =F_{1-2} .\)

Similarly, we have

\(F ^{s}_{2-2}=0\) ,
\(F ^{s}_{2-1}= F _{2-1} ,\)
\(F ^{s}_{2-3}= F _{2-3} + +\rho ^{s}_{1}F_{2(1)-3} ,\)
\(F ^{s}_{2-4}= F _{2-4} + +\rho ^{s}_{1}F_{2(1)-4}\)

For surfaces \(A_{3}\) and \(A_{4}\) dual specular reflections are possible:

\(F ^{s}_{3-1}=F _{3-1}+ \rho ^{s}_{2} F _{3(2)-1},\)

\(F ^{s}_{3-2}= F _{3-2}+ \rho ^{s}_{1} F _{3(1)-2},\)

\(F ^{s}_{3-3}= \rho ^{s}_{1} F _{3(1)-3} + \rho ^{s}_{1}\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{3(12)-3}+ \rho ^{s}_{2}\rho ^{s}_{1}F _{3(21)-3},\)

\(F ^{s}_{3-4}=F _{3-4}+ \rho ^{s}_{1} F _{3(1)-4}+\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{3(2)-4} + \rho ^{s}_{1}\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{3(12)-4} +\rho ^{s}_{2}\rho ^{s}_{1}F _{3(21)-4},\)

\(F ^{s}_{4-1}= F _{4-1}+ \rho ^{s}_{2} F _{4(2)-1},\)

\(F ^{s}_{4-2}= F _{4-2}+ \rho ^{s}_{1} F _{4(1)-2},\)

\(F ^{s}_{4-3}=F _{4-3}+ \rho ^{s}_{1} F _{4(1)-3}+\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{4(2)-3} + \rho ^{s}_{1}\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{4(12)-3} +\rho ^{s}_{2}\rho ^{s}_{1}F _{4(21)-3},\)

\(F ^{s}_{4-4}= \rho ^{s}_{2}F _{4(2)-4} + \rho ^{s}_{1}\rho ^{s}_{2}F _{4(12)-4} +\rho ^{s}_{2}\rho ^{s}_{1}F _{4(21)-4},\)

It is tempting to assume that \(F _{4(12)-4} = F _{4(21)-4},\) etc. Closer inspection of Figs. 6-9b and c reveals, however, that these view factors are partially obstructed: For example, for \(F _{4(21)-4}\) all rays from \(A _{4(21)}\) to \(A _{4}\) must pass through the image \(A _{2(1)}\) as well as \(A _{1}\), i.e., all rays must stay below the comer between \(A _{1}\) and \(A _{2}\) (center pomt of Fig. 6-9b). On the other hand, for \(F _{4(12)-4}\) all rays from \(A _{4(21)}\) must stay above the comer between \(A _{1}\) and \(A _{2}\), and both together add up to the unobstructed view factor from the image to \(A _{4}\). The same is true for \(F _{3(12)-3} + F _{3(21)-3}\). However, the geometry is such that \(F _{3(21)-4}=0\), while \(F _{4(12)-3}\) is unobstructed (thus, still adding up to the unobstructed view factor). Similarly, \(F _{3(12)-4}=0\) , while \(F _{3(21)-4}=0\) is unobstructed.
Simplifications for partially obstructed view factor were found for this particular simple geometry.
Care must be taken before extrapolating these results to other configurations.
Before actually evaluating view factors one should take advantage of the fact that there are only two different surface temperatures, i.e., \(E_{b3}=E_{b1}\) and \(E_{b4}=E_{b2}\), and only two emittances, \(ε_{3}=ε_{1}\) and \(ε_{4}=ε_{2}\):

\((1- F ^{s}_{1-3})E_{b1} – (\epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{1-2}+ F ^{s}_{1-4} )E_{b2} = \frac{q_{1} }{\epsilon _{1} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1}}-1 \right) F ^{s}_{1-3}q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2}}-1 \right) F ^{s}_{1-4}q_{4},\)

\(– (\epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{2-1}+ F ^{s}_{2-3} )E_{b1} + (1- F ^{s}_{2-4})E_{b2} = \frac{q_{2} }{\epsilon _{2} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1}}-1 \right) F ^{s}_{2-3}q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2}}-1 \right) F ^{s}_{2-4}q_{4},\)

\((1- \epsilon _{1}F ^{s}_{3-1}-F ^{s}_{3-3} )E_{b1} – (\epsilon _{2}F ^{s}_{3-2}-F ^{s}_{3-4} )E_{b2}= \left[\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1}}-1 \right)F ^{s}_{3-3} \right]q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2}}-1 \right)F ^{s}_{3-4}q_{4},\)

\(-(\epsilon _{1}F ^{s}_{4-1}+F ^{s}_{4-3} )E_{b1}) + (1- \epsilon _{2}F ^{s}_{4-2}-F ^{s}_{4-4} )E_{b2} = -\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{1}}-1 \right) F ^{s}_{4-3}q_{3} + \left[\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2} } – \left(\frac{1}{\epsilon _{2}}-1 \right)F ^{s}_{4-4} \right]q_{4}.\)

The necessary view factors are readily found from the crossed-strings method [equation (4.49)], reciprocity, and the summation rule [equation (6.21) \(\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N}{(1-\rho ^{s}_{j} ) F ^{s}_{i-j}}=1 , i = 1,2,…,N\) ], as well as from for the diffuse view factors:

\(F ^{s}_{1-2} = F _{1-2} = 0.25;\)
\(F _{1-3}=0.5, F _{1(2)-3}= (\sqrt{64+9}+3-2\times 5 )/2\times 4= 0.1930 :\)
\(F ^{s}_{1-3} = 0.5+0.2\times 0.1930=0.5386;\)
\(F _{1-4} = 0.25, F _{1(2)-4}=(5+8-4-\sqrt{73} )/8 = 0.0570:\)
\(F ^{s}_{1-4}=0.25 +0.2\times 0.0570 = 0.2614;\)
\(F ^{s}_{2-1}= F _{2-1} = 0.3333;\)
\(F _{2-3}= 0.3333 , F _{2(1)-3}=(5+6-\sqrt{52}-3 )/6 = 0.1315:\)
\(F ^{s}_{2-3}= 0.3333+0.7\times 0.1315=0.4254;\)
\(F _{2-4} = 0.3333 , F ^{s}_{2(1)-4}= (\sqrt{52}+4-2\times 5 )/6 = 0.2019:\)
\(F ^{s}_{2-4}=0.3333 + 0.7\times 0.2019 = 0.4746;\)
\(F ^{s}_{3-1}= F ^{s}_{1-3} = 0.5386;\)
\(F ^{s}_{3-2 }= A_{2}F ^{s}_{2-3}/A_{3} =0.75\times 0.4254 = 0.3191;\)
\(F _{3(1)-3}= (\sqrt{52}-6 )/4 = 0.3028,\)
\(F _{3(12)-3}+ F _{3(21)-3}= (10+6-2\sqrt{52} )/8=0.1972:\)
\(F ^{s}_{3-3 } = 0.7\times 0.3028 + 0.2\times 0.7\times 0.1972=0.2396;\)
\(F ^{s}_{3-4 } = 1- \epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{3-1 } – \epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{3-2 } – F ^{s}_{3-3 }\)
\(= 1-0.3\times 0.5386-0.8\times 0.3191 -0.2396=0.3436;\)
\(F ^{s}_{4-1 }= A_{1}F ^{s}_{1-4}/ A_{4} = 0.2614/0.75=0.3485;\)
\(F ^{s}_{4-2 }=F ^{s}_{2-4 }=0.4746;\)
\(F ^{s}_{4-3 }=A_{3}F ^{s}_{3-4 }/A_{4} = 0.3436/0.75=0.4581;\)
\(F ^{s}_{4-4}=1-\epsilon _{1} F ^{s}_{4-1 }- \epsilon _{2} F ^{s}_{4-2 } – F ^{s}_{4-3 }\)
\(= 1-0.3\times 0.3485-0.8\times 0.4746-0.4581=0.0576.\)

Substituting these values into the heat flux equations and realizing, from the summation rule, that the two coefficients in front \(E_{b1} and E_{b2}\) are the same for each equation, we obtain

\((1-0.5386)(E_{b1}-E_{b2})= \frac{q_{1} }{0.3}- \left(\frac{1}{0.3}-1 \right)0.5386 q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{0.8}-1 \right)0.2614 q_{4},\)

\(-(1-0.4746)(E_{b1}-E_{b2}) = \frac{q_{2} }{0.8}- \left(\frac{1}{0.3}-1 \right)0.4254 q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{0.8}-1 \right)0.4746 q_{4},\)

\((0.8\times 0.3191+0.3436)(E_{b1}-E_{b2}) = \left[\frac{1}{0.3}- \left(\frac{1}{0.3}-1 \right)0.2396 \right] q_{3} – \left(\frac{1}{0.8}-1 \right)0.3436 q_{4},\)

\(-(0.3\times 0.3485 + 0.4581) (E_{b1}-E_{b2})=\left(\frac{1}{0.3}-1 \right)0.4581 q_{3} + \left[\frac{1}{0.8}- \left(\frac{1}{0.8}-1 \right)0.0576 \right] q_{4}\)

After a little cleaning up these equations become

\(2.7743 q_{3} – 0.0859 q_{4} = 0.5989(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)
\(-1.0689 q_{3}+ 1.2356 q_{4}=-0.5627 (E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)
\(q_{1}= 0.3770 q_{3}+ 0.0196 q_{4}+ 0.1384(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)
\(q_{2}= 0.7941 q_{3}+ 0.0949 q_{4}- 0.4203(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)

Solving the first two equations leads to

\(q_{3} = \frac{0.5989\times 1.2356-0.5627\times 0.0859}{2.7743\times 1.2356-1.0689\times 0.0859} (E_{b1}-E_{b2}) = 0.2073(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)

\(q_{4}= \frac{0.5989\times 1.0689 -0.5627\times 2.7743}{2.7743\times 1.2356-1.0689\times 0.0859} (E_{b1}-E_{b2}) = -0.2761(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)

and

\(q_{1}= [0.3770\times 0.2073+0.0196\times (-0.2761)+0.1384](E_{b1}-E_{b2})=0.2111(E_{b1}-E_{b2}),\)
\(q_{2}= [0.7941\times 0.2073+0.0949\times (-0.2761)-0.4203](E_{b1}-E_{b2})=-0.2819(E_{b1}-E_{b2}).\)

To determine the net surface heat fluxes we evaluate

\(E_{b1}-E_{b2}=\sigma (T^{4}_{1}-T^{4}_{2} ) = 5.670\times 10^{-8} (1000^{4} – 600^{4} )W/m^{2}=4.935 W/cm^{2}\)

and multiply by the respective surface areas. Thus,

\(Q^{\prime }_{1}= 40cm \times 0.2111\times 4.935 W/cm^{2} =41.7W/cm,\)
\(Q^{\prime }_{2}= 30cm \times (-0.2819)\times 4.935 W/cm^{2} =-41.7W/cm,\)
\(Q^{\prime }_{3}= 40cm \times 0.2073\times 4.935 W/cm^{2} =40.9W/cm,\)
\(Q^{\prime }_{4}= 30cm \times (-0.2761)\times 4.935 W/cm^{2} =-40.9W/cm.\)

Checking our results, we note that the four heat fluxes add up to zero as they should.
The results of the present example—an enclosure with two adjacent specular reflectors—should be compared with those of Example 5.4, dealing with the identical problem except that all four surfaces were perfectly diffuse reflectors. For , we had found \(Q^{\prime }_{1}= -Q^{\prime }_{2} = 1^ =Q^{\prime }_{3} =-Q^{\prime }_{4}\)= 42.3 W/cm.
For the present configuration the heat fluxes of the specular surfaces are reduced by 1%, while the heat fluxes of the diffuse surfaces are reduced a litde more, by approximately 3%. Overall, the eff’ects of specularity are found to be rather minor.
In the last two examples only two simultaneous equations had to be solved, even though there were three and four unknown surface heat fluxes, respectively, because for any purely specular surface with known temperature the radiosity is not unknown, but is given as J=ε\(E_{b}\), Thus, for an enclosure consisting of N surfaces, of which n are purely specular with known temperature, only N-n simultaneous equations need to be solved. While this fact simplifies specular enclosure analysis as compared with diffuse enclosures, one should remember that, in general, specular view
factors are considerably more difficult to evaluate.
