Guest Question.

A relatively new design for a water heater consists of 3 m of 1 standard type K water tubing that is surrounded with a heating element. The element provides a constant heat flux at the tube wall. Water is heated from a temperature of 20 to 80°C. The volume flow rate of 20°C water to be heated is 80 oz in 120 s. Determine the power required to do this and the wall temperature at the outlet.

Admin
Solution

As the water is heated, its properties change. The volume flow of water at 20°C will therefore be different from the volume flow at 80°C.
Assumptions
1. The flow is steady.
2. The water is incompressible.
3. The liquid properties are constant within the tube and are evaluated at the mean temperature
of (80 + 20)/2 = 50°C.

From Appendix for 1 standard type K copper water tubing
OD = 2.858 cm
ID = 2.528 cm
A = 5.019 \(cm^{2} \)
The volume flow rate of water (at 20°C) is

 

\(Q=\frac{80 {oz}}{120 {~s}} \times 2.957 \times 10^{-5} \frac{{m}^{3}}{{oz}}=1.97 \times 10^{-5} {~m}^{3} / {s}\)

 

where the conversion factor is obtained from Appendix . From Appendix , for water at \(20^{\circ} {C}\)

 

\(\rho=1.000(1000) {kg} / {m}^{3}\)

 

The mass flow rate is constant through the tube and is found to be

 

\(\dot{m}=\left.(\rho Q)\right|_{2 \sigma^{\circ} {C}}=1000\left(1.97 \times 10^{-5}\right)=0.0197 {~kg} / {s}\)

 

From Appendix for water at \(50^{\circ} {C}\) we interpolate to find

\(\rho=0.990(1000) {kg} / {m}^{3} \)

 

\(c_{{p}}=4181 {~J}({~kg} \cdot {K}) \)

 

\({v}=0.586 \times 10^{-6} {~m}^{2} / {s} \)

 

\(k_{{f}}=0.640 {~W} /({m} \cdot {K})\)

 

\(\alpha=1.533 \times 10^{-7} {~m}^{2} / {s} \)

 

\(\operatorname{Pr}=3.68\)

The power required is calculated as

\(q_{{w}}^{\prime \prime}=\frac{q}{A_{{s}}}=\frac{\dot{m} c_{{p}}}{A_{{s}}}\left(T_{{bo}}-T_{{bi}}\right)\)

 

where \(A_{{s}}=\pi( ID ) L\). By substitution

\(q_{{w}}^{\prime \prime}=\frac{0.0197(4181)(80-20)}{\pi(0.02528)(3)}\)

 

or

\(q_{{w}}^{\prime \prime}=2.074 \times 10^{4} {~W} / {m}^{2}\)

Also

\(q=q_{{w}}^{\prime \prime} A_{{s}}=4942 {~W}\)

So a \(5 {~kW}\) heater should be sufficient.

To find the wall temperature, we use the local convection coefficient.

\(q_{{w}}^{\prime \prime}=\left.h_{z}\left(T_{{w}}-T_{{b}}\right)\right|_{{any} z}\)

 

We have made no assumptions regarding the developed condition of the flow. We can use Figure 6.12 to obtain the information needed on Nusselt number and to find \(h_{z}\) provided the flow is laminar. The average velocity based on 50°C conditions is

 

\(V=\frac{\dot{m}}{\rho A}=\frac{0.0197}{990\left(5.019 \times 10^{-4}\right)}=3.97 \times 10^{-2} {~m} / {s}\)

 

The Reynolds number is

\(\operatorname{Re}_{{D}}=\frac{V D}{v}=\frac{0.0397(0.02528)}{0.568 \times 10^{-6}}=1765\)

Thus, the flow is laminar.

The inverse Graetz number at tube end, based on \(50^{\circ} {C} \)conditions, is

\(\frac{1}{{Gz}}=\frac{z}{D \operatorname{Re}_{{D}} \operatorname{Pr}}=\frac{3}{0.02528(1765)(3.68)}=0.0183\)

 

From Figure 6.12, in which the location of \(\operatorname{Pr}=3.68 \) is estimated, we find

\({Nu}_{{D}}=\frac{h_{z} D}{k_{{f}}}=6.9\)

 

The local convection coefficient is

 

\(h_{z}=\frac{6.9(0.640)}{0.2528}=174.7 {~W} /\left({m}^{2} \cdot {K}\right)\)

 

The outlet wall temperature is now calculated as

\(T_{wo} =\frac{q^{''}_{w} }{h_{z} } +T_{bo} =\frac{2.074\times 10^{4} }{174.7} +80\)

 

\(T_{wo} =199^{\circ } C\)

 

It is oft en impractical to have graphs from which to obtain the Nusselt number for the types of problems discussed here. Alternatively, it is desirable to have an equation, especially if a computer or calculator is available. For the combined problem of developing velocity and temperature profiles, the Seider–Tate equation is a useful correlation.

 

\(\overline{{Nu}_{{D}}}=\frac{\overline{h} _{L} D}{k_{{f}}}=1.86\left(\frac{D \operatorname{Re}_{{D}} \operatorname{Pr}}{L}\right)^{1 / 3}\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_{{w}}}\right)^{0.14}\)

 

(constant wall temperature; hydrodynamically and thermally developing laminar flow)

in which \(\mu_{w}\) is the viscosity of the fluid evaluated at the wall temperature, and the remaining fluid properties are all evaluated at the average of the bulk temperatures \(\left(T_{\text {bo }}+T_{\text {bi }}\right) / 2\). The Seider-Tate equation applies to steady laminar flow of a Newtonian fluid in a tube having constant wall temperature under the following conditions:

\(\begin{matrix} T_{{w}}=a \text { constant } \\ 0.48<\operatorname{Pr}<16700 \\ 0.004 \quad 4<\left(\mu / \mu_{{w}}\right)<9.75 \end{matrix} \)

In addition to having heat-transfer correlations as in the preceding discussion, it is important also to be able to calculate a pressure drop within a circular duct under laminar conditions. From Equations 6.3 and 6.4, we have, for the wall shear stress

\(\tau_{w}=\tau_{rz}\mid _{r=R}=+\mu \frac{dV_{z}}{dr} \mid_{r=Rl}=(-\frac{dp}{dz} )(\frac{R}{2} )\) ( 6.3)

\(V=\frac{R^{2}}{8\mu } (-\frac{dp}{dz} )\) (6.4)

\(\tau_{{w}}=\left(-\frac{d p}{d z}\right) \frac{R}{2}\)

 

and for the average velocity

\(V=\left(-\frac{d p}{d z}\right) \frac{R^{2}}{8 \mu}\)

 

We now introduce a friction factor that is defined in terms of the wall shear stress and average velocity as*

\(f=\frac{4 \tau_{w} g_{c}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}}\)

 

* The friction factor defined in Equation 6.35 is known as the Darcy–Weisbach friction factor. The Fanning friction
factor, f´, is used in some texts and is defined as

 

\(f^{'} =\frac{ \tau_{w} g_{c}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}}\)

 

The Darcy–Weisbach equation is preferred here because the 4 in Equation 6.35 becomes included in f, which yields a simpler formulation for the equations that follow.

 

Substituting Equation 6.3 gives

 

\(f=\frac{4(-d p / d z)(R / 2) g_{c}}{\frac{1}{2} \rho V^{2}}\)

or

\(d p=-\frac{\rho V^{2}}{2 g_{c}} \frac{f d z}{D}\)

 

where D=2 R is the pipe diameter. Integrating from point 1 (where\( p=p_{1} \)and z=0 ) to point 2 downstream (where \(p=p_{2}\) and z=L ) gives

 

\(\int_{{p} 2}^{{P} 1} d p =-\frac{\rho V^{2}}{2 g_{{c}}} \frac{f}{D} \int_{0}^{L} d z \)

 

\(p_{1}-p_{2} =+\frac{\rho V^{2}}{2 g_{{c}}} \frac{f L}{D}\)

 

The pressure drop due to friction is thus a fraction of the kinetic energy of the flow. To determine the friction factor, solve Equation 6.4 for dp / dz and combine with Equation 6.36; from Equation 6.4

 

\(\frac{d p}{d z}=-\frac{8 \mu V}{R^{2}}\)

 

Combining with Equation 6.36

\(-\frac{d p}{d z}=\frac{\rho V^{2}}{2 g_{c}} \frac{f}{D}=\frac{8 \mu V}{R^{2}}\)

 

Rearranging, with 2 R=D, we obtain

 

\(\frac{\rho V^{2}}{2 g_{c}} \frac{f}{D}=\frac{8 \mu V}{D^{2} / 4}\)

or

\(f=\frac{64 \mu g_{c}}{\rho V D}=\frac{64}{\operatorname{Re}_{{D}}}\)

This result applies to steady laminar flow of a Newtonian fluid in a circular duct.
