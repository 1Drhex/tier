GIVEN Consider the steady, two-dimensional flow field discussed in . FIND Determine the acceleration field for this flow

Admin
Solution

In general, the acceleration is given by

\(a = \frac{DV}{Dt} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} +(V.\nabla)(V)\)

 

\(= \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} + w \frac{\partial V}{\partial z} \) (1)

where the velocity is given by \(V = (V_{0}/l)(-x\hat{\imath} + y\hat{\jmath} ) \) so that \(u = -(V_{0}/l) x\) and \(v = (V_{0}/l) y\) . For steady \([{\partial ()}/{\partial t}=0 ]\) two dimensional [ w = 0 and \({\partial ()}/{\partial z} = 0\) flow, Eq. l becomes

\(a = u\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial V}{\partial y} \)

 

\(= (u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} )\hat{\imath} + (u\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial v}{\partial y})\hat{\jmath} \)

Hence, for this flow the acceleration is given by

\(a = [(-\frac{V_{0}}{l} )(x)(-\frac{V_{0}}{l} ) + (\frac{V_{0}}{l} )(y)(0)]\hat{\imath} \)

 

\(= [(-\frac{V_{0}}{l} )(x)(0)+(\frac{V_{0}}{l} )](y)(\frac{V_{0}}{l} )]\hat{\jmath} \)

or

\(a_{x} = \frac{V^{2}_{0} x }{l^{2} }\) , \(a_{x} = \frac{V^{2}_{0} y }{l^{2} }\)

COMMENTS The fluid experiences an acceleration in both the x and y directions. Since the flow is steady, there is no local acceleration—the fluid velocity at any given point is constant in time. However, there is a convective acceleration due to the change in velocity from one point on the particle’s pathline to another. Recall that the velocity is a vector—it has both a magnitude and a direction. In this flow both the fluid speed (magnitude) and flow direction change with location (see Fig. E4.1a). For this flow the magnitude of the acceleration is constant on circles centered at the origin, as is seen from the fact that

\(\left|a\right| = (a^{2}_{x}+a^{2}_{y}+a^{2}_{z})^{1/2} = (\frac{V_{0}}{l} )^2(x^2+y^2)^{1/2}\) (2)

Also, the acceleration vector is oriented at an angle from the x axis, where

\(\tan \theta = \frac{a_{y}}{a_{x}} = \frac{y}{x} \)

This is the same angle as that formed by a ray from the origin to point ( x , y) . Thus, the acceleration is directed along rays from the origin and has a magnitude proportional to the distance from the origin. Typical acceleration vectors (from Eq. 2) and velocity vectors (from) are shown in Fig. E4.5 for the flow in the first quadrant. Note that a and V are not parallel except along the x and y axes (a fact that is responsible for the curved pathlines of the flow), and that both the acceleration and velocity are zero at the origin ( x = y = 0 ). An infinitesimal fluid particle placed precisely at the origin will remain there, but its neighbors (no matter how close they are to the origin) will drift away.
