Guest Question.

Orbital Debris Collision

The International Space Station has hundreds of shields made of aluminum and bulletproof composite materials that are intended to offer protection against impact with debris present in low Earth orbit (Figure 3.5). With sufficient advance warning, the station’s orbit can even be adjusted slightly to avoid close approaches of larger objects. Over 13,000 pieces of debris have been identified by the United States Space Command, including paint chips, spent booster casings, and even an astronaut’s glove. (a) Calculate the kinetic energy \(U_{k}=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2} \text { of an } m=1 g\) particle of debris traveling at v =8 km/s, which is a typical velocity in low Earth orbit. (b) How fast would a 0.31 lb baseball have to be thrown to have the same kinetic energy?
We first convert the debris particle’s mass and velocity to the dimensionally consistent units of kg and m/s, respectively, using the definition of the “kilo” prefix (Table 3.3). The conventional SI unit for energy in Table 3.2 is the joule, defined as 1 N . m. In part (b), we will convert the kinetic energy to the USCS using the factor 1J = 0.7376 ft . lb from Table 3.6. Since the baseball’s weight is specified in the problem statement, we will make an intermediate calculation for its mass.

Solution

(a) With m =0.001 kg and v =8000 m/s, the kinetic energy of the debris particle is

 

\(\begin{aligned}U_{k} &=\frac{1}{2}(0.001 kg )\left(8000 \frac{ m }{ s }\right)^{2} \leftarrow\left[U_{k}=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}\right] \\&=32,000( kg )\left(\frac{ m ^{2}}{ s ^{2}}\right) \\&=32,000\left(\frac{ kg \cdot m }{ s ^{2}}\right)( m ) \\&=32,000 N \cdot m \\&=32,000 J\end{aligned}\)

 

Applying an SI prefix to suppress the trailing zeroes, the kinetic energy of the particle is 32 kJ.

 

(b) Expressed in the USCS, the particle’s kinetic energy is

 

\(\begin{aligned}U_{k} &=(32,000 J )\left(0.7376 \frac{ ft \cdot lb }{ J }\right) \\&=23,603( J )\left(\frac{ ft \cdot lb }{ J }\right) \\&=23,603 ft \cdot lb\end{aligned}\)

 

For a dimensionally consistent calculation of kinetic energy in the USCS, we will determine the baseball’s mass in the units of slugs:

 

\(\begin{aligned}m &=\frac{0.31 lb }{32.2 ft / s ^{2}} \leftarrow\left[m=\frac{w}{g}\right] \\&=9.627 \times 10^{-3} \frac{ lb \cdot s ^{2}}{ ft } \\&=9.627 \times 10^{-3} \text { slugs }\end{aligned}\)

 

since \(1 \text { slug }=1\left( lb \cdot s ^{2}\right) / ft\) in Equation (3.2). To have the same kinetic energy as the debris particle, the baseball must be thrown with velocity

 

\(\begin{aligned}v &=\sqrt{\frac{2(23,603 ft \cdot lb )}{9.627 \times 10^{-3} \text { slugs }}} \leftarrow\left[v=\sqrt{\frac{2 U_{k}}{m}}\right] \\&=2214 \sqrt{\frac{ ft \cdot lb }{\text { slug }}} \\&=2214 \sqrt{\frac{ ft \cdot\left(\text { slug } \cdot ft / s ^{2}\right)}{\text { slug }}} \\&=2214 \frac{ ft }{ s }\end{aligned}\)

 

\(1 \text { slug }=1 \frac{ lb \cdot s ^{2}}{ ft }\) Equation (3.2)

Discussion

Even though orbiting dust and debris particles may be small in size, they can convey large amounts of kinetic energy because their velocities are so great.

The equivalent speed of a baseball is some 1500 mph, or about fifteen times the speed of a major league fastball pitch or the fastest professional cricket bowl

\(\begin{aligned} U_{k} &=32 \mathrm{~kJ} \\ v &=2214 \frac{\mathrm{ft}}{\mathrm{s}} \end{aligned}\)
